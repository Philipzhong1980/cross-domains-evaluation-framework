### Overview
The briefing begins with Karine Jean-Pierre addressing a deadly school shooting in Perry, Iowa, expressing presidential concern, outlining federal coordination with state and local authorities, and using the tragedy to press Congress for expanded gun safety measures beyond the 2022 bipartisan law and existing executive actions. Admiral John Kirby then delivers a detailed intelligence update showing that Russia has obtained several dozen ballistic missiles and launchers from North Korea and has already used them against Ukraine, while simultaneously pursuing close-range ballistic missiles from Iran; he outlines likely reciprocal military benefits for Pyongyang, warns of destabilizing effects in the Indo-Pacific, and announces plans to raise these illicit deals at the U.N. Security Council, expand sanctions, and keep exposing such transfers publicly, all while urging Congress to pass supplemental funding so the U.S. can continue supplying Ukraine—especially air defenses. In extensive Q&A, Kirby defends the effectiveness of sanctions, explains Russia’s ongoing reliance on foreign munitions, and answers a wide array of foreign-policy questions: he acknowledges ISIS-K’s claim for the Tehran attack but cautions against overinterpreting one incident; discusses Israel’s campaign in Gaza and Lebanon, stressing both Israel’s right to target Hamas and U.S. expectations regarding international law and civilian protection, while admitting there is no formal U.S. legal assessment of Israel’s conduct; highlights U.S.-driven humanitarian efforts and tough diplomacy with Israel to expand aid to Gaza; rejects arguments that humanitarian financing for Iran funds malign activity; underscores U.S. respect for Iraqi sovereignty even as Washington protects its forces from Iran-aligned militias; confirms ongoing efforts to evacuate Americans from Gaza; and reiterates support for Taiwan’s democratic elections amid concerns about Chinese interference. After Kirby departs, Jean-Pierre fields domestic and political questions, affirming the seriousness of the charges against Senator Menendez while deferring to Senate leadership on his future, defending the administration’s border strategy by pointing to Biden’s Day One immigration proposal and current bipartisan Senate negotiations while criticizing House Republicans for leaving town and blocking additional border agents, and describing Biden’s ongoing engagement with historians and his upcoming Valley Forge speech as part of a broader effort to confront threats to democracy exemplified by January 6. She also highlights economic gains for Black Americans and targeted investments in Black communities, defends the administration’s electric vehicle push amid questions about consumer demand, responds cautiously to internal dissent over Gaza policy, underscores local control and individual choice on masking as respiratory illnesses rise, and repeatedly returns to the theme that on issues from gun violence to Ukraine and border security, the administration has acted within its authority but needs further, often bipartisan, congressional action to fully achieve its objectives.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### White House response to Iowa school shooting
Karine Jean-Pierre opens the substantive briefing by addressing a tragic school shooting at Perry Middle and High School in Iowa. She stresses the administration’s concern for victims, notes that federal officials are coordinating with state and local authorities, and frames the event within a broader pattern of recurring gun violence early in the new year. She reiterates the administration’s position that existing actions are insufficient and calls on Congress to enact additional gun safety measures.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that the President is tracking the tragic school shooting in Perry, Iowa, and that senior White House staff are in touch with the governor’s office.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre explains that federal officials are working with local law enforcement to support the investigation into the shooting.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre emphasizes that it is only the fourth day of the new year and the country is already facing another horrific school shooting, asking rhetorically, "When will enough be enough?"
- Ms. Jean-Pierre calls on Congress to pass universal background checks, ban assault weapons and high-capacity magazines, require safe firearm storage, end gun industry immunity from liability, and enact a national red flag law.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says students and teachers deserve safe schools where they focus on learning rather than “duck-and-cover drills.”

#### Russia’s use of North Korean ballistic missiles
Admiral John Kirby details new U.S. intelligence that Russia has obtained ballistic missile launchers and several dozen ballistic missiles from North Korea and has already used them against Ukraine. He notes launches on December 30, 2023, and January 2, 2024, describing the attacks as part of Russia’s larger winter campaign targeting Ukrainian civilian infrastructure. Kirby frames the transfers as a serious escalation, highlights the missiles’ 900-kilometer range, and signals that Russia is likely to use more North Korean systems in future strikes.
- Mr. Kirby reports that North Korea recently provided Russia with ballistic missile launchers and several dozen ballistic missiles.
- Mr. Kirby says that Russia launched at least one North Korean ballistic missile into Ukraine on December 30, 2023, which landed in an open field in Zaporizhzhia.
- Mr. Kirby notes that on January 2, Russia launched multiple North Korean ballistic missiles into Ukraine as part of an overnight aerial attack, whose impacts are still being assessed.
- Mr. Kirby explains that the missiles have ranges of approximately 900 kilometers, calling this a significant and concerning escalation in DPRK support for Russia.
- Mr. Kirby presents a graphic showing launch areas inside Russia and impact points in Ukraine for the North Korean-supplied missiles.

#### Russia’s arms partnerships with North Korea and Iran
Kirby broadens the discussion to Russia’s efforts to obtain weapons from both North Korea and Iran to sustain its war on Ukraine. He states that Pyongyang seeks advanced military assistance and production technology from Moscow in return, which could destabilize the Korean Peninsula and Indo-Pacific. He also describes Iranian exhibitions of close-range ballistic missile systems to Russian delegations and assesses that Russia intends to buy such systems, though no deal is yet consummated. This highlights a growing network of authoritarian states trading arms and technology despite sanctions.
- Mr. Kirby says Pyongyang is seeking Russian military assistance in exchange for missiles, including fighter aircraft, surface-to-air missiles, armored vehicles, ballistic missile production equipment, and advanced technologies.
- Mr. Kirby warns that such Russia–North Korea cooperation would have concerning security implications for the Korean Peninsula and the Indo-Pacific region.
- Mr. Kirby states that Russia is seeking to acquire close-range ballistic missiles from Iran but that the United States does not believe Iran has yet delivered them.
- Mr. Kirby recounts that Iran’s Revolutionary Guard Corps showcased the Ababil close-range ballistic missile and other systems to Russian Defense Minister Shoigu in September 2023.
- Mr. Kirby notes that in mid-December the IRGC Aerospace Force displayed multiple ballistic missile and support systems to a visiting Russian delegation, and he assesses that Russia intends to purchase missile systems from Iran.

#### Planned U.S. responses to Russia–DPRK–Iran arms deals
Kirby outlines how the U.S. and partners plan to respond to Russia’s illicit weapons procurement from North Korea and its negotiations with Iran. He emphasizes that these deals violate multiple U.N. Security Council resolutions and announces plans to raise the issue at the Security Council, impose additional sanctions on facilitators, and publicize intelligence to expose the transfers. He argues that the most impactful response remains sustained military support to Ukraine, which requires new supplemental funding from Congress.
- Mr. Kirby says Russia’s ballistic missile procurement from North Korea directly violates multiple U.N. Security Council resolutions and that the United States will raise these deals at the Security Council to seek accountability.
- Mr. Kirby announces that the United States will impose additional sanctions on individuals and entities facilitating arms transfers between Russia and North Korea and between Russia and Iran.
- Mr. Kirby commits to continuing to publicly release information about these arms deals so that countries cannot secretly aid Russia’s war effort.
- Mr. Kirby asserts that the most effective response to Russian attacks is to provide Ukraine with air defense capabilities and other military equipment.
- Mr. Kirby urges Congress to approve the administration’s supplemental funding request for Ukraine without delay so the United States can continue to supply needed assistance.

#### Intelligence sourcing and Russian dependence on foreign munitions
In response to questions, Kirby carefully describes the intelligence process behind the missile revelations without divulging sensitive sources, characterizing it as a mosaic of corroborated inputs. He affirms that Russia remains reliant on North Korean munitions, including artillery shells previously disclosed, and that the Russia–DPRK relationship is ongoing. Kirby disputes the notion that Russian domestic missile production has fully neutralized sanctions, arguing that foreign systems are additive to Russia’s capabilities and help sustain attacks on Ukrainian civilians.
- Mr. Kirby says the information about North Korean missiles comes from a wide range of intelligence sources knit together over time, but he declines to provide specifics.
- Mr. Kirby states that the United States has seen nothing indicating Russia is no longer reliant on munitions and missiles from North Korea.
- Mr. Kirby recalls that U.S. intelligence previously downgraded information about Russia’s procurement of North Korean artillery shells, underscoring an ongoing relationship.
- Mr. Kirby pushes back on reporting that Russian missile production has fully recovered, saying U.S. officials still believe sanctions and export controls have harmed Russia’s defense industrial capacity.
- Mr. Kirby explains that North Korean and Iranian-supplied systems do not give Russia new types of capabilities but are additive to an already sophisticated missile arsenal, enabling continued attacks on civilian infrastructure.

He maintains that Israel continues to face a viable threat from Hamas and is responsible for its own strategic decisions, while the U.S. focuses on advising how operations are conducted. He reiterates that zero civilian casualties is the U.S. goal, acknowledges concerns about proportionality, and cites Israeli steps—such as reduced reliance on airstrikes, smaller-unit operations, safe areas, and corridors—as evidence of efforts to mitigate harm, while conceding more must be done.
- Mr. Kirby rejects the idea that the continued presence of Hamas fighters means Israel’s campaign is ineffective, noting militaries can degrade leadership while many members survive.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that assessing the effectiveness of Israel’s strategy is for Israel to decide, not the United States.
- Mr. Kirby states that zero civilian casualties is the right answer and that the United States does not want to see a single more civilian killed in Gaza.
- Mr. Kirby affirms that Hamas still poses a viable threat to Israel and has vowed to repeat October 7-style attacks, which drives Israel’s continued operations.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Israel has taken measures such as reducing reliance on airstrikes, conducting more precise smaller-unit operations, publicizing safe areas, and opening humanitarian corridors, though he emphasizes that more can and should be done to protect civilians.

He describes ISIS-K as a viable terrorist threat primarily based in Afghanistan, affecting both the Afghan population and the broader region. However, he cautions against drawing sweeping conclusions about the organization’s overall strength from a single attack and says the U.S. cannot yet assess whether the threat is increasing.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges ISIS-K’s public claim of responsibility for the attack in Iran and says the United States is in no position to doubt it.
- Mr. Kirby describes ISIS-K as largely headquartered in Afghanistan and as a continued viable terrorist threat to people in Afghanistan and the region.
- Mr. Kirby says it would be imprudent to conclude from one event that ISIS-K’s capabilities or resources have exponentially improved.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that the U.S. government has long maintained that ISIS-K remains a viable terrorist threat.

He maintains that Washington has seen no basis to alter its approach to assisting Israel’s self-defense and says Israeli mitigation measures support the view that it is making an effort, even as concerns persist.

He rejects the argument that such arrangements indirectly free up regime resources for malign activities, insisting the regime already prioritizes military programs over its population and that helping ordinary Iranians does not finance the mullahs.

#### Jackson, Mississippi judicial appointment dispute and democracy
Jean-Pierre answers a question about a controversial ruling allowing state leaders in Mississippi to appoint a judge and prosecutors for a jurisdiction including Jackson, a predominantly Black city. She notes the case is in active litigation and avoids detailed comment but stresses in principle that all states must respect democratic processes and the will of the people. She says denial of self-governance undercuts foundational democratic values and affirms that Jackson residents, like all Americans, deserve to have their voices heard.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre declines to comment directly on the Mississippi case because litigation is ongoing but notes that a federal appeals court has temporarily halted the decision.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre asserts that every state must respect the democratic process and the will of the people.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre calls denial of self-governance an affront to the democratic values on which the United States was founded.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the people of Jackson, Mississippi, deserve to have their voices heard like any other Americans.

#### Black economic progress and persistent disparities
Jean-Pierre discusses indicators of economic conditions for Black Americans amid questions about the racial wealth gap, homeownership, and jobs. She cites gains since 2019, including millions of jobs for Black workers, falling Black unemployment, increased Black wealth, and a doubling of Black business ownership, attributing progress in part to the American Rescue Plan and other Biden policies. She acknowledges that challenges like the wealth gap and homeownership persist but argues that investments in Black communities, small businesses, and clean energy jobs, plus health measures such as insulin caps, reflect a sustained effort to ensure Black communities are not left behind.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says more than 2.5 million Black Americans have gained jobs and Black unemployment has fallen from 9.2 percent to 5.8 percent since 2019.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that Black wealth is up a record 60 percent since 2019.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reports that the number of Black households owning a business has more than doubled since 2019 and that Black business ownership is growing at the fastest pace in 30 years.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre points out that about two-thirds of new clean energy jobs are in communities of color, including roughly 60,000 in Black communities.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre highlights the insulin price cap under the Inflation Reduction Act as another policy with significant benefits for Black communities.

#### Electric vehicle adoption and regulatory approach
Jean-Pierre is challenged on whether EV mandates should be delayed given relatively low EV shares in Ford and GM sales. She responds by citing a 51% overall increase in electric vehicle sales as evidence of strong growth under the administration’s climate and clean energy agenda. She does not engage in detail on consumer preference arguments or specific mandates, instead reiterating that EV uptake is rising and is a key part of climate strategy.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that electric vehicle sales have increased by 51 percent, which she presents as a significant overall gain.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre frames EV growth as important for climate change and clean energy goals.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre does not commit to adjusting EV mandates despite questions about relatively low EV shares in individual automakers’ total sales.

#### Internal dissent over Gaza policy within the administration
Jean-Pierre responds to reports of administration officials, including at the Department of Education, resigning or protesting over President Biden’s Israel–Gaza policy. She affirms that people have a right to voice their opinions and acknowledges the emotional nature of the moment but declines to discuss individual resignations in detail. She directs reporters to the relevant agencies for specific personnel matters and avoids indicating any change in policy stemming from internal dissent.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says administration officials, like all people, have the right to express their opinions, especially in emotional times.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre acknowledges that the conflict is highly personal for many but declines to comment on each individual resignation or protest.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre refers questions about a specific Department of Education resignation to that department for further comment.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre does not suggest that internal dissent has prompted any change in Gaza policy.

#### Public attitudes on democracy and campaign speech audience
Asked whether Biden’s democracy-focused speech aims to convince Republicans and independents who view him as weakening democracy, Jean-Pierre draws a line between governing and campaigning. She says she cannot discuss campaign strategy or target audiences from the podium, referring such questions to the campaign. She reiterates only the administration’s substantive view that January 6 was an unprecedented attack requiring ongoing public engagement.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre declines to say whether the President’s speech is aimed at persuading Republicans and independents, citing restrictions on discussing campaign activity from the White House podium.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre refers questions about the speech’s political strategy and audience to the Biden campaign.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre restates that January 6 was an unprecedented attack on democracy and that the President will continue to speak out about it.

#### Respiratory illness surge, masking, and COVID tools
Jean-Pierre addresses rising levels of respiratory illness, including COVID, RSV, and flu, and renewed masking rules in some hospitals. She reiterates that decisions on hospital masking are local and not dictated by the White House. For the general public, she emphasizes that individuals should choose for themselves but notes that vaccines, at-home tests, and treatments are widely available tools that Americans should consider using to protect themselves and others.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says masking policies in hospitals are local decisions made by facilities, communities, states, or cities, not by the White House.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre asserts that it is up to individual Americans to decide whether to wear masks amid rising respiratory illness levels.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre highlights the availability of vaccines, at-home tests, and treatments as tools to mitigate the impact of COVID-19.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre encourages Americans to use these tools but says the administration will not regulate individual masking choices.

#### Impact of 2022 bipartisan gun bill on mass shootings
Closing the loop on gun policy, Jean-Pierre is asked whether the 2022 bipartisan gun safety law has reduced mass shootings. She responds that the legislation is important, particularly for enabling state-level initiatives, but insists that it is insufficient on its own. Reiterating previous points, she calls for further federal measures such as an assault weapons ban, limits on high-capacity magazines, and a national red flag law, emphasizing that the administration believes more sweeping action is needed to address the gun violence epidemic.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the 2022 bipartisan gun law was important and helps states take actions against gun violence.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre argues that the law and existing executive actions are not enough to curb mass shootings.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre repeats that an assault weapons ban, limits on high-capacity magazines, and a national red flag law are needed to meaningfully address the epidemic of gun violence.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre underscores that Congress must act further and that “enough is enough” on mass shootings.
