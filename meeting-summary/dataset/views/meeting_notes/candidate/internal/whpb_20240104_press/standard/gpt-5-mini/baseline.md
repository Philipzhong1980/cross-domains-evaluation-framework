### Overview
  The briefing covered a mix of urgent foreign policy and domestic topics. Admiral John Kirby briefed on new U.S. intelligence indicating that North Korea recently provided Russia with ballistic missile launchers and several missiles that Russia launched into Ukraine on December 30 and January 2, prompting the administration to announce steps including raising the matter at the U.N., imposing sanctions on facilitators, and publicly exposing the transfers while urging Congress to approve supplemental funding so the U.S. can prioritize air defense for Ukraine. Reporters probed the limits of public disclosure, Russia’s domestic production, and possible Iranian involvement; Kirby said negotiations with Iran appear to be advancing but the U.S. has not seen confirmed transfers from Iran, and he emphasized the intelligence is compiled from multiple sources. The briefing also addressed Middle East developments: Kirby urged Israel to operate within international law while noting U.S. engagement to reduce civilian harm, acknowledged ISIS-K’s claimed attack in Iran, defended UNRWA’s humanitarian role amid allegations about civilian infrastructure use, and declined to attribute a Baghdad strike without DoD confirmation. On domestic issues, Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre expressed condolences for the Iowa school shooting and reiterated the administration’s call for stronger gun laws, defended the Department of Education’s staged FAFSA rollout, summarized economic indicators for Black Americans and EV sales data, discussed immigration negotiations (noting White House engagement with Senate negotiators and criticism of House Republicans’ December adjournment), and declined to expand on personnel or litigation matters such as Senator Menendez’s status and ongoing Jackson litigation. The administration framed its foreign responses and domestic initiatives as ongoing efforts that require congressional action, allied cooperation, and continued diplomatic engagement.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Iowa school shooting
  The briefing opens with the administration expressing sorrow over a tragic school shooting in Perry, Iowa and calling attention to the recurring crisis of gun violence. The White House emphasizes support for victims and first responders and frames the event as another example prompting legislative action. The speaker reiterates specific policy priorities the administration wants Congress to enact to reduce gun violence.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President and administration are heartbroken for the families of the victims and grateful for first responders.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said students and teachers deserve safe schools where they can focus on learning rather than drills.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said Congress must enact universal background checks, ban assault weapons and high-capacity magazines, require safe storage, end gun industry immunity, and pass a national red flag law.
  #### DPRK missiles to Russia
  Admiral John Kirby briefed on U.S. intelligence indicating that North Korea recently provided Russia with ballistic missile launchers and several ballistic missiles, some of which Russia launched into Ukraine on December 30 and January 2. The U.S. released a graphic showing launch areas and the impact location of the missile that landed in Zaporizhzhia, and warned that these transfers represent a significant escalation. Officials are still assessing impacts from subsequent launches.
- MR. KIRBY said U.S. information indicates the DPRK provided Russia with ballistic missile launchers and several ballistic missiles.
- MR. KIRBY said Russia launched at least one North Korean ballistic missile on December 30 and multiple on January 2, and that one landed in an open field in Zaporizhzhia.
- MR. KIRBY said the North Korean missiles have a range of approximately 900 kilometers and that Russia may use additional DPRK-supplied missiles to target civilian infrastructure.
  #### U.S. response and actions
  The administration announced a multi-pronged response to arms transfers between Russia and third parties, including raising the issue at the U.N. Security Council, imposing additional sanctions on facilitators, and publicly exposing these transfers. Officials framed these steps as necessary but said the most effective answer is continued provision of air defense capabilities to Ukraine, contingent on Congress approving supplemental funding. The White House urged prompt congressional action to replenish U.S. assistance stocks.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. will raise Russia’s procurement of DPRK missiles at the U.N. Security Council and demand accountability.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration will impose additional sanctions on those facilitating arms transfers between Russia and the DPRK and between Russia and Iran.
- MR. KIRBY said the best response is continued provision of air defense and other military equipment to Ukraine and that Congress must approve the supplemental funding without delay.
  #### Intelligence sourcing limits
  Reporters asked how the U.S. obtained the information on DPRK transfers; Admiral Kirby explained the intelligence is built from a wide range of sources and has been downgraded for public release. He declined to provide operational specifics, saying intelligence is a mosaic and many sources were corroborated over time. He emphasized the conclusion while withholding granular sourcing details.
- MR. KIRBY said the information was derived from a wide range of intelligence sources and has been carefully downgraded for public release.
- MR. KIRBY said he cannot go into great detail about the pieces that helped construct the intelligence mosaic.
- MR. KIRBY said the relationship between Russia and DPRK appears ongoing and remains a concern.
  #### Russian domestic production question
  Reporters pressed whether Russia’s domestic munitions production has recovered and whether it still needs DPRK or Iranian supplies. Admiral Kirby said international sanctions and export controls have had a detrimental effect overall and that foreign supplies are additive even if Russia strives to increase its own production. He cautioned against definitive public judgments about Russia’s internal industrial recovery while underscoring the escalation risk from foreign-supplied systems.
- MR. KIRBY said international sanctions and export controls have been effective in degrading Russia’s defense industrial capacity, even if Moscow is attempting to rebuild production.
- MR. KIRBY said the DPRK- and Iran-supplied systems are additive to Russia’s capabilities rather than necessarily filling a single capability gap.
- MR. KIRBY said Russia is weaponizing winter and continuing efforts to target civilian infrastructure.
  #### Russia-Iran missile talks
  Kirby addressed reports that Russia seeks close-range ballistic missiles from Iran, saying the U.S. has not seen evidence that Iran has delivered such missiles to Russia but that negotiations appear to be advancing. He referenced public displays in September and December where Iran showed missile systems to visiting Russian delegations. The administration expressed concern about the implications for regional security if transfers occur.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. does not believe Iran has delivered close-range ballistic missiles to Russia at this time.
- MR. KIRBY said press reporting shows Iranian displays of missile systems to senior Russian visitors in September and December 2023.
- MR. KIRBY said U.S. officials are concerned that Russian negotiations to acquire missiles from Iran are actively advancing.
  #### Israel-Hamas civilian casualties
  Reporters questioned how Israel can still hold a significant Hamas presence in Gaza while civilian casualties remain very high. Admiral Kirby responded that a campaign can be effective against leadership while many members survive, and he repeatedly urged efforts to reduce civilian harm. He emphasized the U.S. expectation that Israel operate within international law and noted U.S. engagement with Israeli counterparts about minimizing civilian impact.
- MR. KIRBY said it is possible to be effective against an organization’s leadership while many members continue to survive.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. expects Israel to operate within the boundaries of international humanitarian law and continues to press Israeli counterparts on how they conduct operations.
- MR. KIRBY said Israel has taken steps such as reducing reliance on airstrikes, conducting smaller-unit operations, opening humanitarian corridors, and publicizing safe areas.
  #### ISIS-K attack in Iran
  When asked about claims that ISIS-K carried out an attack in Iran, Kirby acknowledged ISIS-K’s public credit and said the U.S. sees them as a viable terrorist threat, largely based in Afghanistan. He cautioned against interpreting a single attack as proof of a larger trend in strength or resourcing. The administration continues to monitor the group’s capabilities and regional threat.
- MR. KIRBY said ISIS-K has publicly claimed credit for the attack in Iran and the U.S. does not dispute that claim.
- MR. KIRBY said a single attack does not provide enough basis to conclude the group’s overall strength is increasing substantially.
- MR. KIRBY said ISIS-K remains a viable terrorist threat, primarily based in Afghanistan.
  #### Houthis and Red Sea threats
  A reporter asked whether warnings to the Houthis signaled imminent military action; Kirby described the international statement as a clear demonstration of partners’ concern about Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping. He declined to enumerate possible responses and said the coalition will hold the Houthis accountable while protecting commerce and U.S. interests. The administration emphasized seriousness but would not telegraph specific options.
- MR. KIRBY said the international statement reflects deep concern about Houthi actions in the Red Sea and their impact on international commerce.
- MR. KIRBY said the coalition takes the attacks very seriously and will protect U.S. interests and free shipping.
- MR. KIRBY said the statement warned that the Houthis will be held accountable for consequences without detailing operational options publicly.
  #### Iran frozen funds transactions
  Reporters asked about two transactions withdrawing previously frozen funds in Oman and whether waivers that enable humanitarian flows indirectly finance Iran’s proxies. Kirby said he did not have transaction details and would follow up, and he reiterated that the administration intends those funds solely for humanitarian goods for the Iranian people. He rejected the fungibility argument that humanitarian transfers free regime resources for malign activity as an adequate rationale to stop aid.
- MR. KIRBY said he did not have immediate details on the two transactions and would get back to reporters.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration’s policy is that these funds do not go to the Iranian regime but to approved vendors for food, water, medicine, and agricultural products.
- MR. KIRBY said the fungibility argument does not justify withholding humanitarian assistance from the Iranian people.
  #### Strike in Baghdad inquiry
  When asked whether the U.S. conducted a strike in Baghdad that killed a pro-Iranian commander, Kirby referred the question to the Department of Defense and declined to confirm responsibility. He reiterated the U.S. commitment to defending its personnel in Iraq and noted the advisory relationship with Iraqi security forces. Kirby emphasized respect for Iraqi sovereignty while stressing the need to protect U.S. forces.
- MR. KIRBY referred questions about responsibility for the Baghdad strike to the Department of Defense.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. values its partnership with the Iraqi government and operates in an advise-and-assist capacity to help defeat ISIS.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. has a responsibility to protect its service members and will continue to do so.
  #### Shifa Hospital and UNRWA
  Reporters asked whether new intelligence changed assessments about Al-Shifa Hospital being used by Hamas; Kirby said he was not aware of any new information beyond what was reported in November. He defended UNRWA’s humanitarian role, saying the agency works in harm’s way and cannot be held responsible for Hamas’s use of civilian infrastructure. The administration reiterated concerns about Hamas hiding within civilian facilities.
- MR. KIRBY said he was not aware of new information about Al-Shifa beyond prior assessments from November.
- MR. KIRBY said UNRWA performs important humanitarian work in Gaza and operates in harm’s way.
- MR. KIRBY said Hamas has a history of using hospitals, schools, and tunnels to hide and to store weapons, complicating humanitarian operations.
  #### Evacuation of Americans from Gaza
  The briefing covered a recent rescue that freed the mother and uncle of a U.S. service member from Gaza; Kirby described this as part of ongoing diplomatic efforts with Israeli and Egyptian counterparts to secure safe passage for Americans. He confirmed there was no U.S. military involvement and framed the rescue as consistent with broader efforts to protect U.S. citizens. The administration welcomed the safe return and considered those individuals Americans for assistance purposes.
- MR. KIRBY said the freed relatives were the result of diplomatic engagement with Israeli and Egyptian counterparts to allow safe passage for Americans.
- MR. KIRBY said there was no U.S. military involvement in the rescue operation.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration is glad the individuals are out and considered them Americans for consular purposes.
  #### Ukraine supplemental urgency
  Multiple reporters asked what options exist if Congress does not approve the administration’s supplemental request for Ukraine; Kirby emphasized that U.S. leadership matters and allies are watching, and he said there are no remaining replenishment funds after the last distribution. He avoided discussing hypotheticals and reiterated that Congress must act to sustain Ukraine’s air defense and other needs. Kirby identified air defense as the top priority for any replenishment funds.
- MR. KIRBY said allies and global competitors are watching whether the U.S. will remain a reliable partner and that U.S. leadership matters.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. exhausted available replenishment funds with the December 27 distribution and needs the supplemental to continue assistance.
- MR. KIRBY said additional funding would prioritize air defense capabilities for Ukraine.
  #### Senator Menendez and access
  Reporters asked whether the President believes Senator Menendez should retain access to sensitive information amid serious allegations; Jean-Pierre said the situation is serious and affirmed that it was appropriate for the senator to step down from his chairmanship, but she declined to go further. She framed any decisions about congressional membership or resignation as matters for Senate leadership. The White House maintained a cautious, limited public posture.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration views the allegations against Senator Menendez as very serious and that stepping down from the chair was the right action.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said decisions about resignation or Senate status are for Senate leadership to decide.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said she would not comment further to be careful given the situation’s gravity.
  #### Domestic gun policy follow-up
  Jean-Pierre returned to gun policy after earlier remarks, reiterating that the President has taken more executive actions than any previous president in the first two years and that the White House continues to push Congress for more legislation. She highlighted the Safer States Initiative, creation of an anti-gun-violence office, and convenings of state legislators. The administration framed legislative action as necessary to end the epidemic of gun violence.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President has signed more than two dozen executive actions addressing gun violence and created the first anti-gun-violence office.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the White House hosted the largest-ever convening of state legislators on gun violence and launched the Safer States Initiative.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration believes Congress must do more, including legislation on high-capacity magazines and assault weapons.
  #### Child Tax Credit and taxes
  Reporters asked whether the President would accept a tax deal that expanded the Child Tax Credit and renewed research tax credits; Jean-Pierre affirmed the President supports tax cuts for working families and the Child Tax Credit specifically and said she would consult the team about renewing expired research credits. She emphasized that any tax bill that benefits corporations should also benefit working families. The response left some specifics to follow-up.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President strongly believes tax cuts for big corporations should be matched with tax cuts for working families and supports the Child Tax Credit.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the White House will consult its team regarding renewing expired research tax credits before taking a position.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the Child Tax Credit in the American Rescue Plan substantially reduced child poverty and the President supports measures that help families.
  #### Border negotiations and politics
  Multiple exchanges covered whether administration policies contributed to record border crossings and whether the White House is engaging House Republicans on the supplemental that includes border funding. Jean-Pierre said the President proposed comprehensive immigration reform on day one and that the White House is engaged with Senate negotiators; she criticized House Republicans for leaving mid-December and described their actions as obstructive. The administration declined to negotiate specifics from the podium and emphasized ongoing Senate-level talks.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President proposed comprehensive immigration legislation on his first day in office and that the system has been broken for decades.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the White House is in regular touch with Senate negotiators and appreciates bipartisan Senate efforts to find a solution.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said House Republicans left in mid-December and that their actions have impeded progress on a bipartisan deal.
  #### President meets historians
  Jean-Pierre described a White House meeting in which the President convened a diverse group of historians ahead of January 6 commemorations to hear perspectives on democracy and the rule of law. She said the President has done this before and values hearing diverse voices on important national moments. She also noted the President will speak publicly on January 6 at a campaign event moved to avoid inclement weather.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President regularly meets historians to hear diverse perspectives and prepared for the January 6 anniversary.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President values diverse voices on democracy and wanted to hear from historians ahead of the national moment.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President’s January 6 remarks are part of a campaign event and the campaign will provide additional details.
  #### FAFSA rollout issues
  Reporters raised concerns about the soft launch of the new FAFSA form; Jean-Pierre defended the Department of Education’s staged rollout as best practice to monitor site performance. She reported that more than 400,000 applications were completed during the soft launch and said the Department worked through the weekend to improve the user experience. The administration framed the effort as intended to make college more affordable and manageable for families.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the Department of Education used a soft launch to ramp up volume and monitor site performance as a best practice.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said more than 400,000 FAFSA applications had been completed during the initial launch period.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the Department of Education worked over the weekend to improve performance and user experience.
  #### Jackson, Mississippi litigation
  A question about a court ruling in Jackson, Mississippi that would allow state appointment of a judge and prosecutors prompted Jean-Pierre to emphasize deference to ongoing litigation. She said the President believes in respecting democratic processes and that residents deserve self-governance. The White House declined further comment because the matter remains in litigation.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the litigation in Jackson is ongoing and the White House will be careful in commenting.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President believes every state should respect democratic processes and the will of the people.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said residents of Jackson deserve to have their voices heard regarding local governance.
  #### Black economic indicators
  Jean-Pierre summarized administration economic achievements relevant to Black Americans, citing job gains, declines in unemployment, increases in Black wealth, and growth in Black business ownership since 2019. She attributed these trends to Biden administration policies such as the American Rescue Plan and investments in clean energy and small business. The administration presented these indicators as evidence of progress while acknowledging remaining disparities.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said over 2.5 million Black Americans have gained jobs and Black unemployment fell from 9.2 percent to 5.8 percent since 2019.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said Black wealth is up about 60 percent since 2019 and Black business ownership has more than doubled.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said two-thirds of new clean energy jobs are in communities of color, including nearly 60,000 in Black communities.
  #### Electric vehicle market
  When asked whether the administration would ease EV mandates after lower-than-expected manufacturer EV sales, Jean-Pierre highlighted a 51 percent increase in EV sales overall and framed that growth as positive for clean energy goals. She did not announce policy changes and reiterated the administration’s support for the EV transition. The answer emphasized high-level sales growth rather than automaker-specific targets.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said EV sales increased by 51 percent overall and presented that as a significant market shift.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration views increased EV adoption as important to climate and clean-energy goals.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE declined to discuss changing mandates and instead focused on the observed growth in EV sales.
  #### DOE employee protest resignation
  Reporters asked about internal resignations and protests within the administration over Israel and Gaza policies; Jean-Pierre said people have a right to voice their opinions and that resignations are personal decisions. She referred detailed questions to the Department of Education for specifics. The White House acknowledged emotions are high but did not elaborate on personnel actions.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said employees have the right to voice their opinions and the administration understands these are emotional times.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said she would refer questions about the Department of Education resignations to that agency for details.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said she would not comment further on individual resignations from the podium.
  #### Respiratory illness guidance
  On rising respiratory viruses and hospital masking policies, Jean-Pierre said decisions about masking are localized to hospitals, communities, and states. She highlighted available federal tools — vaccines, at-home tests, and treatments — and encouraged Americans to use them while leaving masking choices to individuals and local authorities. The White House did not advocate new federal mask mandates.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said decisions about hospital masking are made locally by hospitals, communities, and states.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said federal tools such as vaccines, at-home testing, and treatments are available to help people protect themselves.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration encourages Americans to use available tools but will not regulate personal masking choices from the federal level.
