### Overview
The Public Safety Advisory Board meeting centers on an in‑depth briefing and discussion about Mountain View’s response to residents living in vehicles, including human services programs, safe parking, and parking enforcement practices. Assistant City Manager Kimberly Thomas and Human Services Manager Parneet Dhindsa outline the city’s continuum‑of‑care approach, report that the 2022 Point‑in‑Time count found 346 homeless individuals (a 43% decrease from 2019), and describe extensive partnerships and nearly $11 million in recent investments, with safe parking and basic hygiene services as current focal points. Police leaders Captain Scott Nelson and Lieutenant Matthew Atkins explain the legal framework for the public right‑of‑way, the history and settlement terms of Measure C’s narrow‑streets RV restrictions, and a three‑phase, education‑first process for enforcing the 72‑hour parking ordinance, presenting fall 2022 citation and tow data while acknowledging the high resource demands of chalking and follow‑up. Board members and public commenters raise persistent concerns about restroom access and dignity for vehicle residents, debate the interpretation and equity of 72‑hour enforcement for inhabited vehicles, scrutinize the impacts of towing on vulnerable people, and question whether enforcement resources could be reallocated toward services and infrastructure like neighborhood Porta potties or inclusive residential parking permits. Throughout, staff emphasize attempts to balance neighborhood expectations, safety, legal obligations, and limited capacity, while several board members, including Chair Cleave Frink, explicitly commend Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson for her empathetic, relationship‑based outreach and coordination with community partners to support unhoused residents living in vehicles.
  
### Detailed Summary

#### Human services approach to homelessness and vehicle residency
Assistant City Manager Kimberly Thomas and Human Services Manager Parneet Dhindsa present an overview of Mountain View’s human services strategy for homelessness, with emphasis on residents living in vehicles. They describe a multi‑department “continuum of care” and note that local investments and programs exceed what is typical for a city of Mountain View’s size. They frame vehicle residents as unsheltered homeless within county data and stress that outreach and case management are central to pathways toward more stable housing.
- Kimberly Thomas explains that homelessness in Mountain View requires a coordinated team effort across the Police, Fire, Public Works, Community Services, Community Development, City Attorney’s Office, and the City Manager’s multilingual engagement program.
- Kimberly Thomas states that residents living in vehicles are treated as unsheltered homeless for planning purposes and are counted as such in the County of Santa Clara’s biannual Point‑in‑Time (PIT) count.
- Parneet Dhindsa reports that the 2022 PIT count found 346 homeless individuals in Mountain View, a 43% decrease from 606 in 2019.
- Parneet Dhindsa notes that the city conducts its own annual street‑by‑street vehicle counts, which consistently show 200–300 vehicles used for living in the public right of way, more than half of which are RVs since 2018.
- Kimberly Thomas highlights that Mountain View has committed nearly $11 million over six years in capital and operating funds across homelessness services, including safe parking, interim housing, shelter, prevention, and basic needs support.
#### Homelessness services, safe parking, and COVID‑era programs
Staff detail specific programs serving unhoused residents, particularly those living in vehicles, and clarify which programs remain active post‑COVID. They emphasize safe parking, basic hygiene services through safety‑net providers, and partnerships with county and community‑based organizations. Board members challenge whether current restroom and hygiene offerings adequately support vehicle residents, especially at night and near large clusters of RVs.
- Parneet Dhindsa explains that Mountain View works within a continuum of coordinated care that includes homelessness prevention, rapid rehousing, emergency shelter, interim housing, permanent supportive housing, and basic health and safety services.
- Parneet Dhindsa notes that services for vehicle dwellers have been a City Council priority since 2016 and lists actions such as funding cold‑weather shelter, biohazard cleanup, hygiene programs, and an RV sanitary waste voucher pilot.
- Kimberly Thomas clarifies that several COVID‑era expanded hygiene programs and the RV waste voucher pilot have ended, while current core efforts focus on safe parking, basic hygiene services, and showers through providers like Community Services Agency (CSA) and Hope’s Corner.
- In response to Member Kavita Aiyar, Kimberly Thomas confirms that the city recently reinstated portable restrooms at CSA and Hope’s Corner but does not plan to place standalone Porta potties in RV‑dense neighborhoods due to resource limits and neighborhood impacts.
- Member Kavita Aiyar strongly disagrees with limiting Porta potties to provider locations, arguing that residents on streets like Independence and Pioneer cannot reasonably access distant restrooms at night and that this undermines dignity for vehicle residents.
- Member Kalwant Sandhu and public speakers later echo the request for more distributed restroom facilities, including at Klein Park and near large RV concentrations.
#### Safety, outreach, and capacity constraints in homelessness response
The discussion addresses safety and capacity issues in serving vehicle residents, including fire risks, shelter and safe parking capacity, and the need for outreach to connect people to county systems. Staff describe proactive fire‑safety education and acknowledge that overall system capacity remains insufficient to meet demand. Board members explore barriers to safe parking participation and the balance between voluntary engagement and program requirements like case management.
- Kimberly Thomas reports that following several fires in safe parking lots and in the public right of way, the Fire Department conducted in‑person outreach to vehicle residents and shared multilingual brochures on safe living and fire prevention in vehicles.
- Kimberly Thomas reiterates that Mountain View, like other cities, faces a structural shortage of shelter and housing slots, estimating that historically roughly two to three people seek assistance for every available placement in the county system.
- In response to Vice Chair Jeannette Wang, Kimberly Thomas says the most critical need is outreach that gets vehicle residents assessed and entered into the county queue for case management, medical care, and eventual access to permanent supportive or affordable housing (e.g., projects like Crestview).
- Member Derek Langton asks why some residents remain on the street rather than enter safe parking lots, and Kimberly Thomas suggests a combination of trust, preference, and capacity constraints, noting that safe parking requires case management participation.
- Kimberly Thomas emphasizes that Mountain View’s safe parking capacity—approaching about 120 spaces after planned expansion—already far exceeds that of other cities in the region.
#### Overview of safe parking sites and upcoming expansion
Staff outline the city’s existing safe parking locations, amenities, and a planned expansion at Shoreline Lot B. Board members seek clarity on how many new spaces will be added and whether more of the shoreline lot could be used, while staff explain logistical and partnership constraints with Live Nation. The board also probes what services are provided inside safe parking compared with on‑street conditions.
- Kimberly Thomas confirms that Mountain View operates three city‑controlled safe parking sites—Shoreline, Evelyn, and Terra Bella—plus faith‑based lots such as Lord’s Grace Church, with St. Timothy’s previously participating.
- Parneet Dhindsa explains that each safe parking site provides ADA‑accessible restrooms, access to mobile showers and laundry via Dignity on Wheels, water service, and onsite case management, with additional support from CSA, Valley Homeless Healthcare, Hope’s Corner, and other nonprofits.
- In response to Member Kavita Aiyar, Kimberly Thomas states that the city plans to bring a February 14 Council item to expand Shoreline Lot B by roughly 17 additional spaces adjacent to the existing Move Mountain View operation, subject to agreement with Live Nation.
- When Member Aiyar questions why only 17 additional spaces are pursued, Kimberly Thomas replies that the city seeks a contiguous, manageable program footprint and must negotiate separately with Live Nation for any further expansion higher up in the lot.
- Member Derek Langton notes that he frequently sees water, restrooms, and mobile shower services at the shoreline site and asks whether those amenities have been reduced; staff confirm those services remain in place for program participants.
#### Legal framework for public right‑of‑way and parking
Police leadership explain the legal and operational framework governing use of the public right‑of‑way, including safety, access, and utility maintenance requirements. They stress that on‑street parking is a limited, shared public resource regulated by the California Vehicle Code and Mountain View City Code. This context underpins later discussion of Measure C, narrow streets restrictions, and 72‑hour parking enforcement.
- Lieutenant Matthew Atkins defines the public right‑of‑way as real property where the city holds a right‑of‑way interest, intended for public use such as streets, utilities, and limited on‑street parking, rather than exclusive occupation by individuals.
- Matthew Atkins explains that right‑of‑way management aims to maintain sight lines, turning clearance, bike and pedestrian access, parking widths, and fire hydrant access, which drives various parking restrictions and time limits.
- Atkins notes that temporary no‑parking restrictions are used for maintenance like street paving, sidewalk and curb repairs, tree work, storm drain and utility work, and that street sweeping supports environmental compliance by keeping pollutants out of storm drains, creeks, and the Bay.
- Police emphasize that parking rules, including time limits and restrictions on oversized vehicles, are authorized by the California Vehicle Code and implemented through city ordinances.
#### Measure C, narrow streets ordinance, and settlement terms
Captain Scott Nelson and Lieutenant Atkins review the history of Measure C (the “narrow streets” oversized vehicle ordinance), its voter approval, subsequent litigation, and the settlement conditions that narrowly define how the NES unit may enforce it. They explain that enforcement resumed in October 2022 after a tentative settlement and is largely limited to specialized officers who use a warning‑first approach. Board and community questions probe towing thresholds and protections for vulnerable residents.
- Scott Nelson recounts that Mountain View passed Ordinance 14.19 in 2019 restricting oversized vehicles on streets 40 feet wide or less for safety reasons, voters approved Measure C in November 2020, and a lawsuit in 2021 delayed enforcement until a tentative settlement in September 2022.
- Scott Nelson explains that under the settlement, only the Neighborhood and Event Services (NES) unit normally enforces Measure C and bike‑lane street restrictions, although patrol officers may respond if NES is unavailable and then refer non‑urgent cases back to NES.
- Nelson outlines the settlement steps: NES provided warnings and parking maps to oversized vehicles before October 1, 2022; the city posted no‑parking signs on all restricted narrow and bike‑lane streets; NES then began issuing citations accompanied by explanation forms and maps; and vehicles may be towed if they remain in place 72 hours after a citation.
- Scott Nelson notes that immediate towing is only allowed if a vehicle obstructs traffic or blocks a driveway under state law, or in future for repeat Measure C offenders with three qualifying citations once the settlement is fully executed.
- Members and public commenters repeatedly stress that towing vehicle homes can be devastating for unhoused residents, and staff acknowledge that in at least one case fees were waived and community partners helped a towed vehicle resident obtain a replacement vehicle.
#### Police outreach and NES unit structure
The Police Department describes the Youth and Community Services Unit and the Neighborhood and Event Services (NES) team that lead outreach and enforcement related to homelessness and vehicle residency. NES is staffed with a sergeant, community service officer, and Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson, who focuses on relationship‑based outreach and connecting residents to services. Board members and the chair praise McPherson’s work and ask how communication across units is organized.
- Scott Nelson explains that the Youth and Community Services Unit includes the School Resource Officer unit and the NES unit, with NES created in 2017 to address the growing homeless and vehicle‑resident population.
- Scott Nelson states that the NES team—one sergeant, one community service officer, and Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson—is the primary group for education, outreach, and enforcement with unhoused and vehicle‑resident individuals.
- Lieutenant Atkins says field operations, traffic, NES, and community service officers meet regularly to coordinate on cases and processes, especially when cases involve vehicle residents known to NES.
- In response to Member Eva Tang’s concern about an elderly man whose RV was towed, Nelson and Atkins describe efforts to improve cross‑unit communication so officers can quickly consult NES or supervisors when they encounter complex situations involving unhoused residents.
- Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson details how, after learning of the elderly resident’s tow, she coordinated with LifeMoves, Hope’s Corner, the Day Worker Center, and case managers to offer shelter, temporary lodging in a friend’s van, and ultimately a donated replacement vehicle, while continuing to offer safe parking or shelter placements that the resident declined.
#### 72‑hour parking enforcement process and data
Lieutenant Atkins and Captain Nelson describe Mountain View’s three‑phase approach to enforcing the 72‑hour parking ordinance and present citation data from October–December 2022. They emphasize an education‑first strategy, with chalking, pamphlet warnings, and delayed ticketing, while noting that enforcement is resource‑intensive. Board members and public commenters question the legal interpretation, equity impacts on vehicle residents, and whether resources could be better spent on services rather than enforcement.
- Matthew Atkins explains the three‑phase process: officers first chalk vehicles; after 72 hours they return and place pamphlet warnings about possible citations and tows; after another 72 hours they return and issue citations to vehicles still in violation.
- Atkins reports that from October 13–31, 2022, officers left pamphlets on about 211 passenger vehicles and 127 oversized vehicles, resulting in 8 passenger and 23 oversized vehicle citations and one tow for 72‑hour violation, plus 17 additional abandoned‑vehicle citations via the county VASA program.
- Atkins notes that in November 2022, 116 passenger and 89 oversized vehicles received pamphlets, with zero passenger and 14 oversized citations and no 72‑hour tows, and in December 2022, 12 passenger and 115 oversized vehicles received pamphlets, with 2 passenger and 34 oversized citations and three 72‑hour tows.
- Atkins emphasizes that community service officers and police assistants chalk roughly 200–700 vehicles per week, making the process highly time‑consuming and requiring significant resource allocation; he adds that NES has begun skipping the pamphlet phase in some areas where residents are clearly familiar with the 72‑hour rule.
- Captain Nelson observes that complaint volume and enforcement needs were high in October and November but have decreased recently as more residents voluntarily comply, reducing the time spent on 72‑hour enforcement.
- Member Kavita Aiyar questions whether the cost and neighbor‑driven complaint dynamic of 72‑hour enforcement aligns with city priorities, suggesting those resources could be redirected to services that reduce complaints by improving conditions for vehicle residents.
#### Interpretation and fairness of the 72‑hour ordinance
A substantial portion of the discussion, including public comment, challenges how the city interprets and applies the 72‑hour rule, particularly for people living in vehicles. Police leadership state that legal counsel has advised that vehicles must move at least 1,000 feet and not return to the same spot, while public commenters argue that the ordinance text does not prohibit returning and was intended for abandoned vehicles, not inhabited homes. Board members probe edge cases such as residents’ own cars and vehicles that cannot be moved due to mechanical issues.
- Captain Scott Nelson says city legal counsel has advised that vehicles must move 1,000 feet and not return to the same parking spot, even though the current ordinance text does not explicitly state the prohibition on returning, and acknowledges that future ordinance updates may clarify this.
- In response to Member Eva Tang’s scenario about a resident’s personal car that is chalked in front of their home, Nelson suggests the safest approach is to move the vehicle beyond the chalk marks or 1,000 feet away before returning, and notes that citations can be contested through a departmental review process.
- Public commenters Tim McKenzie and “Brown” argue that the ordinance’s reference to odometer readings and “consecutive” hours implies that moving and even returning to the same spot is allowed and that enforcement against inhabited vehicles contradicts both the letter and spirit of a law aimed at abandoned vehicles.
- Member Kavita Aiyar asks how officers determine expired registration for tow decisions, and Nelson explains that officers may run license plates through their systems when they see expired tags, with tows used in limited circumstances such as severely expired registration rather than for lack of insurance.
- Public speakers and board members stress that inhabited vehicles are by definition not abandoned, and they question the equity of applying a “72‑hour” abandoned‑vehicle framework to people whose vehicle is their home.
#### Towing of inhabited vehicles and mitigation efforts
Specific cases of towing inhabited vehicles, especially an elderly RV resident, become a focal point for concerns about harms to vulnerable people. Police and outreach staff explain the tow process, associated costs, and steps taken to mitigate impacts, including fee waivers and coordination with community partners. The board seeks clarity on whether towing effectively results in people losing their homes and how often service‑oriented alternatives are pursued.
- Chair Cleave Frink raises a personal concern about the financial burden of towing, asking whether people whose vehicles are their homes effectively lose those homes if they cannot afford retrieval fees.
- Captain Scott Nelson explains that to recover a towed vehicle, owners must first obtain a release from the Police Department (paying a statutory storage fee, roughly in the $150 range) and then pay the tow company’s charges, which accrue daily, making quicker retrieval less expensive.
- Nelson adds that in some limited cases, such as communication errors, the department has waived its own fees, and that community organizations have sometimes stepped in to pay tow‑yard costs for unhoused residents.
- Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson describes how, in the elderly RV resident’s case, she quickly engaged LifeMoves, Hope’s Corner, and the Day Worker Center to offer shelter and ultimately secure a donated working van for him, while noting that he declined shelter and safe parking placements even when available.
- Member Joan Brodovsky presses for concrete details about what happened to the elderly man after his vehicle was towed, and McPherson confirms he now lives in a different van on the street but with continued access to case management and offers of services.
#### Restroom access, hygiene, and dignity
Board members and public commenters repeatedly highlight bathroom access and hygiene as central dignity issues for vehicle residents. While staff emphasize restrooms and showers at safety‑net providers and safe parking sites, multiple speakers argue this is insufficient for residents parked far away, especially at night. The discussion surfaces tension between neighborhood concerns, vandalism, resource limits, and the need to “meet people where they are.”
- Kimberly Thomas explains that during COVID‑19 the city placed Porta potties in 8–11 locations across the community, but post‑COVID it has reduced standalone units and now focuses restroom access at CSA and Hope’s Corner, along with existing park and facility restrooms during daylight hours.
- Member Kavita Aiyar strongly criticizes the pullback of neighborhood Porta potties, asserting that vehicle residents on streets like Independence cannot reasonably reach distant facilities at night and that lack of restrooms pushes people toward unsanitary options that then fuel neighborhood conflict.
- Member Kalwant Sandhu and Member Joan Brodovsky support expanding restroom access, with Brodovsky referencing her own difficulties finding open bathrooms during COVID closures and connecting that experience to vehicle residents’ ongoing challenges.
- Public speaker Marguerite, who lives on Ortega Avenue, states that as a housed neighbor she supports more facilities—such as a Porta potty or permanent restroom at Klein Park—and believes her neighborhood benefits when vehicle residents have dignified access to restrooms.
- Member Aiyar later reiterates her call for Porta potties near Independence/Pioneer and Klein Park and asks staff to distinguish between legitimate restroom concerns and assumptions that unhoused people are responsible for restroom vandalism.
- Captain Mike Canfield notes that the Parks Department is concerned about vandalism and hate graffiti in park bathrooms and agrees to follow up on whether reduced hours or other measures have been enacted and how they relate to restroom access decisions.
#### Residential parking permits and alternative policy ideas
Public commenters and board members explore potential policy alternatives, including residential parking permits that would include vehicle residents and reduce enforcement burdens. Staff note that traditional residential permit programs exist for fixed‑housing neighborhoods but say past analysis concluded that permitting specific streets for mobile dwellings was not legally viable. The board requests historical documentation to understand prior legal rationale before reconsidering such options.
- Public commenter Dana PD asks whether the city ever considered a residential parking permit program that includes vehicle residents and non‑vehicle residents alike, suggesting such a program could incentivize vehicle residents to be assessed for services while reducing 72‑hour enforcement workload for police.
- Kimberly Thomas confirms that Mountain View has a residential parking permit program for fixed‑housing residents and that in 2017–2018 staff studied the idea of permitting specific locations for people living in mobile units, but legal analysis at the time found that approach was not permissible.
- Member Kavita Aiyar requests access to those prior staff reports and legal analyses so board members can understand what has already been explored and avoid repeating dead‑end proposals, and Thomas agrees to compile relevant public materials and consult the City Attorney’s Office as needed.
- Public commenters suggest that, rather than relying on neighbors’ complaints and repeated chalking cycles, a permit‑based approach could manage parking more predictably while linking vehicle residents to supportive services.
#### Community perspectives on enforcement versus services
Throughout board questions and public comment, there is a recurring tension between enforcement activities (chalking, citations, tows) and investment in services (restrooms, safe parking, outreach). Several board members and residents argue that enforcement resources might be more effectively spent on supportive infrastructure and that current practices risk criminalizing poverty. Staff acknowledge the resource intensity of enforcement but emphasize legal obligations and some evidence of increased voluntary compliance.
- Member Kavita Aiyar questions whether the high labor investment in 72‑hour enforcement is an optimal use of city resources and suggests reallocating some of that effort toward sanitation, sewage management, and support services that would reduce complaints by improving conditions for all neighbors.
- Public commenters Tim McKenzie and Marguerite express discomfort with the volume of complaints driving enforcement, arguing that the 72‑hour rule was intended for abandoned vehicles and that applying it aggressively to inhabited vehicles effectively punishes people for being poor.
- Public commenter Brown asserts that tying access to services—like safe parking—to participation in case management and other program requirements can feel coercive and may not respect the autonomy of people who choose to live in vehicles but still need basic amenities like toilets.
- Kimberly Thomas maintains that the city tries to balance neighborhood needs, legal requirements, and limited resources while prioritizing programs—such as safe parking and interim housing—that can move residents toward long‑term stability.
- Captain Nelson notes that as residents become more aware of the 72‑hour rule and comply voluntarily, complaint volume and enforcement demands have recently decreased, suggesting some shift away from the intense enforcement period in late 2022.
#### Board reflections and commendation of outreach efforts
As the discussion concludes, board members reflect on the complexity of balancing safety, neighborhood expectations, legal constraints, and compassion for vehicle residents. Several members commend staff, especially Community Outreach Officer McPherson, for person‑centered, relationship‑based work. The board signals ongoing interest in restroom access, parking policy alternatives, and ensuring enforcement practices do not undermine dignity for unhoused neighbors.
- Member Derek Langton describes living on a wide street where neighbors differ sharply on RV presence, with some aggressively reporting vehicles and others favoring flexible, dignity‑centered responses, and acknowledges the difficulty for staff in balancing these competing values.
- Member Langton raises broader concerns about uninsured drivers based on a personal experience and asks about enforcement of insurance requirements; Captain Nelson explains that California generally uses correctable citations and does not tow vehicles solely for lack of insurance.
- Member Aiyar reiterates her intention to advocate for Porta potties near RV clusters and to distinguish restroom vandalism from assumptions about vehicle residents’ behavior, while also pressing for more information on past residential permit analyses.
- Chair Cleave Frink explicitly praises Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson for her compassionate, resource‑focused street outreach, saying he has personally observed her working with vehicle residents to solve problems such as non‑functional vehicles and connecting people to services.
