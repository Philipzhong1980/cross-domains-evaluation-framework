### Overview
  The Public Safety Advisory Board meeting focuses on services for residents living in vehicles and enforcement of parking regulations. City staff led by Assistant to the City Manager Kimberly Thomas, Human Services Manager Parneet Dhindsa, Lieutenant Matthew Atkins, Captain Scott Nelson, and Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson present an overview of the Human Services continuum of care, recent data showing reduced unsheltered counts, and existing programs including safe parking, mobile hygiene, and medical outreach. Police staff explain the Neighborhood and Event Services (NES) unit’s role in outreach and the Measure C settlement conditions that limit enforcement, and they detail the three-phase 72‑hour enforcement process and recent citation/tow data. The board and public press for more decentralized restroom access, question the cost and effectiveness of chalking/enforcement, and raise concerns about towing impacts and interdepartmental communication after an instance of an elderly man’s vehicle being towed; staff describes follow-up outreach and ongoing improvements to coordination. Staff commits to presenting a Shoreline Lot B safe-parking expansion item on February 14, to compile prior staff reports and legal analyses on residential parking permit options, and to continue strengthening cross-unit communication while maintaining safe parking, outreach, and case-management efforts.

#### Human Services overview
  Assistant to the City Manager Kimberly Thomas presents the Human Services division role, coordination across city departments, and the continuum of care for people experiencing homelessness. She highlights partnerships, multilingual engagement, and that the division absorbed a vacant police officer position to lead policy and response strategies. The City positions safe parking, outreach, hygiene services, and housing pipeline work as core priorities supported by nearly $11 million in recent funding.
- Kimberly Thomas explains the Human Services division coordinates with police, fire, public works, planning/housing, and the City Attorney to address homelessness.
- Kimberly Thomas reports the city has invested nearly $11 million in funding, capital, and operations to serve people who are homeless or unstably housed over the last six years.
- Kimberly Thomas states that safe parking and outreach are core ongoing priorities and that the Human Services division leads policy formulation and response strategies.
#### Vehicle counts and trends
  Parneet Dhindsa presents local data showing a decline in the county point-in-time (PIT) count for Mountain View and a separate city vehicle count that trends downward. The county PIT reports 346 people in 2022 versus 606 in 2019, and the city's street-by-street vehicle counts have ranged 200–300 with recent decreases and an increased RV share historically. Staff frames living-in-vehicle residents as unsheltered for planning and service eligibility.
- Parneet Dhindsa reports the County of Santa Clara 2022 PIT count shows 346 homeless individuals in Mountain View, down 43% from 2019.
- Parneet Dhindsa says the city's street-by-street vehicle count ranges from 200 to 300 vehicles used for living purposes and is trending downward.
- Parneet Dhindsa notes that since December 2018 more than half of counted vehicles have been recreational vehicles.
#### Services and safe parking
  Staff details the City's range of services for residents living in vehicles including safe parking, mobile hygiene, medical outreach, and pilot programs for RV waste disposal. The City funds portable restrooms, mobile showers and laundry, and collaborates with CSA, Valley Homeless Healthcare, Move Mountain View, and others to provide on-site resources. Staff emphasizes safe parking as a long-standing council priority and points to county partnerships and legislative advocacy to expand options.
- Parneet Dhindsa summarizes city-funded services including portable restrooms, mobile showers, washers/dryers, and outreach through CSA and Valley Homeless Healthcare.
- Parneet Dhindsa explains the city created a Safe Parking Ordinance, funded Move Mountain View to run programs, and helped create the county's largest safe parking program.
- Kimberly Thomas highlights pilot initiatives such as the RV sanitary waste disposal voucher program and ongoing efforts to identify new safe parking lots.
#### Right-of-way regulation
  Lieutenant Matthew Atkins outlines the legal framework for public right-of-way management and the City's responsibility to balance parking access, safety, and utility maintenance. He explains parking restrictions, time limits, temporary no-parking notifications for maintenance, and environmental requirements such as street sweeping. Staff frames these rules as essential to protecting mobility, sight lines, pedestrian/bicycle access, and infrastructure obligations.
- Lieutenant Matthew Atkins explains the public right-of-way exists for public use and supports streets, utilities, and limited on-street parking.
- Lieutenant Matthew Atkins states the city enforces restrictions to preserve sight lines, turning clearance, pedestrian and bicycle access, and fire hydrant access.
- Lieutenant Matthew Atkins notes temporary no-parking restrictions support maintenance and regulatory requirements such as street sweeping to protect stormwater systems.
#### Police outreach structure
  Captain Scott Nelson presents the Police Department's Youth and Community Services Unit structure and the Neighborhood and Event Services (NES) Unit's role in outreach and enforcement for people living in vehicles. NES consists of a sergeant, a community service officer, and a community outreach officer and focuses on education, outreach and measured enforcement. Nelson highlights community involvement in hiring and cross-unit coordination when NES is unavailable.
- Captain Scott Nelson says the NES unit, formed in 2017, includes a sergeant, a community service officer, and a community outreach officer focused on outreach and enforcement.
- Captain Scott Nelson states the NES unit leads education, outreach, and enforcement for the living-in-vehicle population and that community members participated in hiring.
- Captain Scott Nelson explains that if NES cannot respond to a call, a patrol officer will assess the situation and forward non-urgent incidents to NES for follow-up.
#### Measure C and settlement
  Captain Nelson summarizes Measure C (narrow streets ordinance) history, voter approval, subsequent lawsuit, and the settlement terms that limit enforcement to NES members after a tentative agreement. Enforcement of Measure C and bicycle-lane restrictions resumes October 1, 2022 under the settlement, with steps for outreach and signage completed before citations. The settlement also restricts who may enforce and outlines specific post-citation towing processes.
- Captain Scott Nelson reports Measure C (narrow streets ordinance) passed in 2019, was approved by voters in 2020, and faced litigation that delayed enforcement.
- Captain Scott Nelson states the settlement requires initial warnings and signage and allows NES officers to issue citations after the pre-enforcement notification period.
- Captain Scott Nelson explains one settlement term restricts enforcement of Measure C and bicycle-lane streets to NES personnel, with patrol officers assisting when NES is unavailable.
#### Citation and towing rules
  Staff walks through the settlement-mandated enforcement steps: warnings and maps, posted signage, citation issuance, and the possibility of towing after citations if a vehicle remains in violation. Immediate tow circumstances include obstruction, repeat offenders with three citations 72 hours apart, and other code-based safety hazards. The settlement defines an effective period and certain towing exceptions that will apply once fully executed.
- Captain Scott Nelson outlines the five-step process in the settlement that begins with warnings and maps and leads to citations and potential tow after 72 hours.
- Captain Scott Nelson notes immediate tow authority applies for vehicles obstructing traffic or driveways and for repeat offenders meeting specific citation criteria.
- Captain Scott Nelson clarifies the settlement's effective period begins on the settlement date and lasts four years, and some tows cannot occur until the settlement is fully executed.
#### 72‑hour enforcement process
  Lieutenant Atkins explains a three-phase 72‑hour enforcement approach: chalking, pamphlet warnings after 72 hours, and citations if vehicles remain after an additional 72 hours, though staff is reducing phase-two pamphlets in some areas. Atkins presents October–December enforcement metrics showing hundreds of vehicles contacted, a rising number of oversized vehicle citations in December, and a small number of tows. Staff emphasizes the process is resource intensive and has required significant officer time.
- Lieutenant Matthew Atkins describes the three-phase approach of chalking, returning with pamphlets after 72 hours, and issuing citations after another 72 hours.
- Lieutenant Matthew Atkins reports October–December data showing hundreds of vehicles contacted, with oversized vehicle citations increasing in December and three tows for 72‑hour violations.
- Lieutenant Matthew Atkins states the enforcement process consumes substantial staff time and that the NES team and community service officers observe 200–700 vehicles per week during phase one.
#### Current program status
  Board members ask which services remain active; Kimberly Thomas clarifies the city focuses on the continuum of care with active safe parking and targeted hygiene services at CSA and Hope's Corner while some COVID-era programs have scaled back. Staff confirms ongoing funding for outreach, case management, and safe parking and notes limitations due to resource constraints. Kimberly offers to supply prior staff reports and additional documentation when requested.
- Member Kavita Aiyar asks which programs remain active and Kimberly Thomas replies that safe parking and targeted hygiene services at CSA and Hope's Corner are current priorities.
- Kimberly Thomas states many COVID-era specialty programs have been reduced but that the city reinstated some hygiene services at key safety-net locations.
- Kimberly Thomas offers to gather and share prior staff reports and legal advisement related to prior analyses when board members request background documentation.
#### Porta‑potty placement debate
  Board members and the public debate placing portable restrooms closer to vehicle residents versus concentrating facilities at safety-net locations. Staff explains past placements during COVID and current strategy to site restrooms at providers for 24/7 access while acknowledging neighborhood concerns and resource limits. Several speakers press for more decentralized restroom placement to preserve dignity and reduce public sanitation problems.
- Member Kavita Aiyar urges locating portable restrooms near where vehicle residents live, arguing many cannot reach centralized sites at night.
- Kimberly Thomas explains the city's strategy concentrates 24/7 restroom services at key safety-net providers and cites resource and neighborhood-balancing considerations.
- Public speaker Marguerite and others request a permanent restroom at Klein Park and additional porta‑potties at locations like Independence and Pioneer.
#### Safety and fire outreach
  Board members ask about safety risks from cooking and other hazards inside vehicles; Kimberly Thomas and Captain Nelson describe outreach and safety education efforts. The fire department conducted direct site visits after recent fires and distributed safety brochures, and the outreach officer distributes materials and liaises with case managers. Staff frames prevention education as ongoing and coordinated with response partners.
- Member Kalwant Sandhu asks about fire and cooking safety and Kimberly Thomas confirms the city distributes multilingual safety materials and that the fire department conducts outreach after incidents.
- Captain Scott Nelson indicates outreach includes education on living safely in vehicles and that NES and fire personnel coordinate site visits following fires.
- Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson confirms she distributes safety information and communicates with case managers and service providers for follow-up.
#### Enforcement interpretation debate
  Speakers question the legal interpretation of the 72‑hour rule and whether the code requires moving 1,000 feet or prohibits returning to the same spot; staff cites legal counsel's guidance that moving 1,000 feet and not returning to the same location is the enforced interpretation. Community members and advocates argue the ordinance text emphasizes consecutive time at a space and that enforcement practices may conflict with the code's letter and intent. Staff acknowledges ambiguity and notes possible future ordinance updates to clarify language.
- Captain Scott Nelson states legal counsel advises a vehicle must move 1,000 feet and not return to the same spot as the working enforcement interpretation.
- Public commenters Tim McKenzie and Mr. Brown argue the ordinance language emphasizes consecutive time and odometer distance and question whether current enforcement matches the code's text and intent.
- Captain Scott Nelson and Lieutenant Atkins acknowledge discussion and indicate the city may consider clarifying ordinance language in the future.
#### Communication and towing incident
  Board members raise concerns about interdepartmental communication after an elderly man's vehicle was towed; NES and outreach staff describe follow-up efforts and casework. Officer McPherson confirms she conducts outreach after incidents, offers shelter and safe parking referrals, and coordinates with community-based case managers, and notes some clients decline shelter offers; staff reports they are improving redundancy and internal communication. The board discusses towing impacts, fee processes, and community assistance to recover vehicles.
- Member Eva Tang describes an elderly man whose vehicle was towed and asks how cross-unit communication failed, and Lieutenant Atkins says the department is improving internal updates and redundancy.
- Community Outreach Officer Janleah McPherson recounts outreach after the towing, offers made to shelter and safe parking, and indicates the client refused shelter and later accepted a donated vehicle.
- Captain Scott Nelson explains the towing-release process and notes community groups have sometimes helped pay tow fees and staff has waived fees in limited circumstances.
#### Residential permit discussion
  Members ask whether a residential parking permit program could allow vehicle residents to remain without enforcement pressure; staff explains prior legal reviews concluded permitting mobile units as residential was legally problematic. Kimberly Thomas offers to retrieve and share past staff reports and city attorney advice about prior analyses. The board expresses interest in revisiting the concept with legal guidance and historical documentation.
- Public speaker Dana PD proposes a residential parking program that includes vehicle residents to reduce enforcement burdens and incentivize assessment.
- Kimberly Thomas states staff previously reviewed residential permits for mobile units and that prior analyses found legal constraints, and she offers to pull past staff reports and city attorney guidance.
- Member Kavita Aiyar asks staff to provide any prior reports from 2016–2018 that examined residential permitting for mobile units.
#### Safe parking lot details
  Staff provides specifics on safe parking operations, services provided onsite, and a planned Shoreline Lot B expansion to add approximately 17 contiguous spaces. Parneet confirms three city-operated safe parking lots (Shoreline, Evelyn, Terra Bella) with restrooms, water, Dignity on Wheels shower/laundry service, medical outreach, and case management. Kimberly and Parneet stress capacity constraints and that the city will present a February 14 agenda item proposing the Shoreline expansion.
- Parneet Dhindsa confirms all three safe parking sites (Shoreline, Evelyn, Terra Bella) provide on-site ADA restrooms and have mobile shower and laundry services provided by Dignity on Wheels.
- Kimberly Thomas states the city will present a February 14 agenda item to consider adding approximately 17 contiguous spaces at Shoreline Lot B.
- Kimberly Thomas reports the city-managed safe parking program approaches roughly 120 spaces countywide when combined with partner lots and remains the largest program in the county.
#### Public recommendations
  Multiple public commenters urge redirecting enforcement funds into services, placing portable or permanent restrooms in neighborhood locations like Klein Park, relaxing 72‑hour restrictions for oversized vehicles, and building dignity-centered solutions. Speakers argue enforcement-driven approaches risk criminalizing poverty and recommend more decentralized services and financial support for repairs and access. Staff hears these proposals without making immediate commitments beyond offering to provide background and pursue internal follow-up.
- Public speaker Marguerite requests a permanent restroom at Klein Park, suspension or reinterpretation of the 72‑hour rule for oversized vehicles, and reallocation of enforcement funds to services.
- Public commenter Brown urges the city not to gate services behind case management and to respect vehicle residents' autonomy while providing basic sanitary access.
- Dana PD suggests a residential parking permit as a possible mutually beneficial solution to reduce enforcement and encourages exploring that option further.

Staff agrees to assemble prior staff reports on residential permitting, present a Shoreline Lot B expansion item on February 14, and continue improving interdepartmental communication. The meeting transitions to closing comments without new policy decisions.
