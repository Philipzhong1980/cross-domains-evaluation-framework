### Overview
The Rental Housing Committee meeting centers on three substantive areas: a quasi‑judicial appeal over rent concessions, the program’s current operational metrics, and a forward‑looking communications and outreach strategy. Chair Haynes‑Livesay leads the committee in unanimously approving consent items and then convenes an appeal in which landlord Steve challenges a hearing officer’s decision granting tenant Ron Dillon a downward rent adjustment and partial refund after a one‑month free‑rent concession in 2021; staff (Nas, Karen, and Anky) explain that the appeal turns on legal interpretation of CSFRA rent concession regulations adopted in mid‑2022, not disputed facts, and recommend against de novo review. Steve argues the rules are being applied retroactively in a way he reasonably did not foresee, while Ron supports the decision as a straightforward application of base‑rent principles. After probing the legislative history of the one‑year lookback and September 1, 2022 cutoff, and voicing both sympathy for fairness concerns and reminders that the CSFRA’s base rent language has been in place since 2015, committee members—including Greenawald, Ramos, Rosas, and Almond—vote unanimously to adopt the tentative appeal decision and affirm the hearing officer. The committee then reviews October status metrics showing substantial inquiry volume, outreach efforts, mediations, and stable but softening rental market conditions, and hears a detailed communications plan in which staff outline expanded data reporting, targeted mailers, rent registry enhancements, and deeper engagement with vulnerable communities and realtors; members broadly praise staff’s work and discuss budget, metrics, and mailer effectiveness, with Ramos citing research that postcards best reach hard‑to‑serve populations. Finally, staff highlight ongoing Housing and Eviction Help Center clinics, weekly virtual office hours, upcoming workshops—including a citywide virtual mobile home rent stabilization session ahead of in‑park events—and recent cultural outreach at Dia de Muertos, before the meeting adjourns at 9:01 PM with the next session set for December 5, 2022.

Chair Haynes‑Livesay outlines the quasi‑judicial nature of the proceeding, the appeal structure, and the roles of the appellant landlord and respondent tenant. #### Staff legal analysis of rent concession appeal
Staff presents the legal and procedural framework for the appeal, focusing on whether the hearing officer correctly applied the rent concession regulations to the tenant’s petition. Nas explains that staff does not recommend a de novo hearing because the facts are not in dispute; the appeal turns on legal interpretation of when and how the rent concession regulations apply. Staff’s tentative appeal decision concludes the hearing officer properly applied the regulations, which clarify base rent calculation for tenancies with concessions and limit retroactive recovery, and recommends the RHC affirm that decision.
- Nas explains that the RHC’s role is to review the hearing officer’s decision on the appealed elements only, determining whether the decision is supported by substantial evidence rather than re‑trying the case.
- Nas advises that a de novo hearing is not recommended because the parties do not contest the factual record and the appeal concerns legal application of existing regulations, not new evidence.
- Nas summarizes the hearing officer’s findings that the tenant is entitled to a downward rent adjustment because annual general adjustments for 2021 and 2022 were applied to an incorrect base rent that failed to account for a one‑month concession, and that overpaid rent must be refunded.
- Nas states that the landlord’s single issue on appeal is the claim that CSFRA Regulation Chapter 2, Section B(2)(i) on rent concessions should not apply to concessions granted before September 1, 2022.
- Nas reviews the rent concession regulations, noting they apply to tenancies beginning after October 19, 2015 and require calculating base rent as the average rent actually charged and paid during the initial term when temporary concessions are given, with a specific carve‑out for first‑month‑only concessions.
- Nas highlights that Chapter 4, Section G(6) limits tenant refunds for concessions in tenancies commencing before September 1, 2022 to overpayments made within one year before filing, while allowing full recovery for tenancies commencing on or after that date, subject to state statutes of limitation.
- Nas concludes that the rent concession rules do not retroactively change base rent but clarify how it has always been defined under the CSFRA; therefore, applying them to earlier tenancies is not improper retroactivity, and the hearing officer did not err.
#### Landlord appellant argument on retroactivity
The appellant landlord, Steve, challenges the application of the rent concession regulations to a concession granted before September 1, 2022. He argues that discussion at the July 18, 2022 RHC meeting and staff slides led him to reasonably believe the amendments would only apply prospectively from September 1, 2022, and that applying them to his earlier decision to offer a one‑month free rent concession unfairly penalizes him after the fact. He characterizes this as retroactive rulemaking that undermines common‑sense expectations and likens it to ticketing drivers for parking in a spot that was legal at the time.
- Steve contends that the July 18, 2022 meeting discussion and staff slides, including language about ensuring landlords and tenants understand concessions’ impact on base rent, implied that the concession regulations would be effective beginning September 1, 2022.
- Steve asserts that based on that discussion, he reasonably assumed only concessions starting on or after September 1, 2022 would be affected and that no one explained that earlier concessions would be re‑evaluated under the new framework.
- Steve argues that his tenant was charged the full lease rate of $1,545 each month during the initial term and that one month was effectively paid by him, similar to third‑party rental assistance, so the base rent should remain $1,545.
- Steve states that before the July 18 action he was fully compliant with the CSFRA, but that making the concession rule apply backwards now penalizes landlords for having provided rent assistance in prior years.
- Steve likens the situation to the city retroactively declaring a previously legal parking spot illegal and then issuing tickets for earlier parking, arguing this fails a “common sense” test.
- Steve questions whether under the current interpretation tenants whose full rent was paid by the state during the pandemic could claim a base rent of zero, underscoring his view that the regulation was not drafted with all consequences in mind.
- Steve concludes that the rule should instead apply only to concessions for tenancies commencing after September 1, 2022 and states he will accept that prospective application but objects to retroactive impact on past business decisions.
#### Tenant respondent support for decision
The respondent tenant, Ron Dillon, supports the hearing officer’s and tentative appeal decisions and emphasizes that he is simply applying the base rent rules as written. He explains that he moved in during 2021 with one free month in the second month of the tenancy and filed his petition when he believed the base rent used for later increases did not properly account for that concession. He distinguishes between the effective date of higher AGA caps and the rent concession rules and argues that future landlord concerns about concessions fall outside the narrow scope of his case.
- Ron Dillon states he began his tenancy on May 1, 2021 at $1,545 per month, with June 2021 designated as a free month for which he did not pay any rent.
- Ron Dillon explains that when it came time to renew, he questioned whether his base rent should be calculated as 11 paid months at $1,545 averaged over 12 months, rather than simply treating 12 months at $1,545 as the base with the concession ignored.
- Ron Dillon notes his understanding that base rent has always been defined as what tenants actually pay over the first 12 months of tenancy, and that the rent concession amendments clarified application to concessions rather than changing that core principle.
- Ron Dillon recalls that he understood the September 1 date primarily as the effective date for increasing the AGA cap to 5% and did not see it as limiting application of the concession clarification to earlier tenancies.
- Ron Dillon states that he believes the tentative decision correctly applies the rules to his lease and that any broader policy concerns raised by landlords about future concessions extend beyond the specific legal question in his appeal.

#### Committee questions on retroactivity and legislative history
Committee members question staff about the origin and intent of the one‑year lookback and September 1, 2022 cutoff, probing whether the RHC contemplated a zero‑retroactivity option and how the compromise was reached. Staff and legal counsel explain that the RHC discussed concessions over multiple meetings, including June and July 2022, and adopted the one‑year limitation partly in response to landlord concerns, while maintaining that the regulations simply clarify base rent rules that have been in the CSFRA since 2015. The conversation surfaces both the policy tradeoffs and the limits of the committee’s discretion in this appeal posture.
- Committee Member Greenawald asks Karen to explain the rationale behind limiting tenant rent credit recovery to a one‑year lookback for pre‑September 1, 2022 concessions.
- Karen clarifies that the one‑year limitation was adopted specifically to address landlord concerns that clarifying the base rent rules could otherwise open many years of retroactive refunds for older concessions.
- Anky recalls that landlords requested some form of limitation in prior meetings and that the one‑year lookback emerged as a compromise supported by the RHC.
- Karen and Anky emphasize that the concession issue had been discussed at several meetings prior to July 18, 2022 and that the regulations adopted that night, including the lookback rule, were debated on the record rather than quietly inserted via consent calendar.
- Nas points out that a straw poll at an earlier meeting helped guide staff in drafting the compromise regulations the RHC adopted in July.
- Vice Chair Ramos notes he served as a swing vote in favor of the one‑year lookback, describing it as a landlord‑favorable compromise compared to allowing refunds back to October 19, 2015, the CSFRA base date, and adds that a zero‑recovery option was not seriously on the table.
#### Committee deliberation on fairness and legal constraints
The committee deliberates on the tension between legal requirements and perceived fairness to landlords. Several members express sympathy for the appellant’s reliance and fairness arguments, acknowledging that even the RHC needed multiple meetings to work through concession rules, but conclude that the law as adopted clearly governs the case and must be followed. Members reiterate that the RHC sought to mitigate landlord impacts by carving out first‑month concessions and limiting retroactive refunds, and that the underlying CSFRA language on base rent, not the July regulations, ultimately constrained their choices.
- Committee Member Greenawald states he will vote to affirm the tentative decision because the law is clear in this case, but he also expresses discomfort with the retroactive effect and notes that it took the committee multiple meetings to fully understand concessions, making it hard to say landlords should have known all along.
- Chair Haynes‑Livesay emphasizes that the CSFRA’s base rent definition predates the concession regulations and that prior legal advice suggested the CSFRA left little flexibility for concessions, prompting the committee to search for lawful ways to limit landlord exposure via the one‑year lookback and first‑month carve‑out.
- Vice Chair Ramos notes he originally pushed to allow one month free rent (the first month) as a landlord accommodation and expresses frustration that an attempt at compromise is now characterized as insufficiently landlord‑friendly.
- Committee Member Rosas forcefully rejects the characterization that tenants received a “free ride” during the pandemic, highlighting health risks, job loss, potential homelessness, and the importance of rental assistance programs in averting a deeper crisis, while noting that landlords generally received back rent through those programs.
- Committee Member Almond describes the appeal as a predictable test case of the new concession regulations and argues that the decision is straightforward under the adopted framework, which now provides clear parameters for future concessions and rollbacks.
- Committee Member Greenawald reiterates that while the RHC made a compromise choice, many residents might view the retroactive aspect as unfair, and he urges the committee to be candid about that tension in future policy discussions.
#### Decision to affirm tentative appeal decision
After deliberation, the committee moves and votes to adopt the tentative appeal decision, thereby affirming the hearing officer’s ruling in favor of the tenant’s downward adjustment and partial refund. Members acknowledge the policy concerns raised by the landlord but emphasize that their role in this appeal is to apply the existing regulations to the record, not to rewrite the rules. The unanimous vote finalizes the RHC’s decision on petition 21220016, leaving any further challenge to the courts.
- Committee Member Almond moves to accept the tentative appeal decision as the RHC’s final decision on the appeal.
- Chair Haynes‑Livesay seconds the motion, setting up final discussion and roll‑call vote.
- Committee Member Greenawald asks to provide comments before the vote and then confirms he will vote aye despite his earlier fairness concerns.
- Andrea conducts roll call and announces that all members present vote “aye,” so the motion to adopt the tentative appeal decision passes unanimously.

#### Communications and outreach plan overview
Staff presents a comprehensive communications and outreach plan for fiscal year 2022‑23, reflecting lessons learned during COVID‑19 and the addition of the mobile home rent stabilization program. The plan aims to increase awareness and understanding of tenant protections, improve landlord compliance, and reach vulnerable populations through multimodal engagement, including printed mailers, digital tools, pop‑up events, workshops, and targeted outreach. Committee members largely praise the plan’s breadth, detail, and community focus while probing budget implications and metrics for measuring effectiveness.
- Staff explains that outreach efforts since 2020 shifted heavily toward COVID‑19 eviction protection education, rent relief access, and implementation of the Mobile Home Rent Stabilization Ordinance, including opening the Eviction Help Center (now Housing and Eviction Help Center).
- Staff reports that from August 2021 to June 2022, 61 Eviction Help Center clinics served 832 clients across 343 tenant households, representing roughly 40–45% of Mountain View households that applied for state rent relief.
- Staff notes that 18 pop‑up events reached approximately 830 community members at venues including the city’s Monster Bash, school district Cafecitos, CSA food distributions, and a summer kickoff event with community partners.
- Staff outlines three key objectives of the new communications plan: creating consistent and clear materials on rights and responsibilities, increasing engagement through multiple communication channels, and improving program efficiency and transparency via better metrics and timely updates.
- Staff proposes enhancing the monthly status report with new data points such as registration and fee compliance, average regulated rents by unit size, historical termination data, mobile home metrics, Housing/Eviction Help Center activity, and trends in public inquiries.
- Staff describes expanded targeted mailings, including banked rent increase follow‑ups to tenants, continued termination notice packets, information packets to new property owners, and paper registration surveys mailed to small landlords with five or fewer units to assist those less comfortable with online systems.
- Staff details upcoming rent registry improvements, including a bulk upload feature targeted for mid‑to‑late December, a public search function by December 1, online filing for landlord MNOI petitions by mid‑January, and a simplified unit amendment process by late January.
- Staff highlights planned efforts to reach vulnerable and hard‑to‑reach groups, including expanded translated materials and webpages, distribution at laundromats and grocery stores, engagement with Chinese‑speaking residents through the Multicultural Engagement program, and new realtor‑focused trainings to reduce instances of buyers unknowingly acquiring rent‑controlled properties.
- Committee Member Almond commends staff for the outreach program’s depth and breadth and notes that similar work in her own career underscores how resource‑intensive such efforts are.
- Committee Member Greenawald asks about outreach costs and budget impact; staff replies that efforts fit within the existing budget previously approved, with supplemental ARPA funds supporting language access, and estimates that major mailings, like program newsletters, cost roughly $10,000 per mass print run.
- Anky notes that ARPA funds are time‑limited and that staff will likely return to the committee during the next budget cycle to address ongoing funding needs for multicultural and language‑access work.
- Committee Member Rosas praises staff’s presence at late‑night community meetings, the focus on Spanish‑language outreach, and the new outreach to the Chinese‑speaking community, while urging attention to staff workload and work‑life balance.
- Vice Chair Ramos cites academic research indicating postcards are particularly effective for reaching older, lower‑income, less‑educated, and foreign‑born populations and strongly supports continuing postcard campaigns as a key equity tool.
- Chair Haynes‑Livesay emphasizes the importance of educating realtors and suggests exploring continuing education credit, noting past appeals where buyers appeared unaware of CSFRA coverage at purchase.
