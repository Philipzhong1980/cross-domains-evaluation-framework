### Overview
The meeting featured a senior administration official providing a comprehensive briefing on the upcoming January 13th Taiwan presidential and legislative elections. The official emphasized the United States' full confidence in Taiwan's democratic processes and bipartisan support for free and fair elections, while maintaining a neutral stance on candidates. The discussion highlighted the U.S. commitment to the One China policy and the importance of peace and stability in the Taiwan Strait, which is critical to global trade and economic stability. The official detailed the longstanding practice of sending unofficial delegations of former U.S. officials to Taiwan post-election to reinforce the U.S. One China policy and support cross-Strait stability, noting plans to continue this tradition after the upcoming election. Concerns about Beijing's attempts at election interference were addressed, with confidence expressed in Taiwan's ability to manage such challenges and clear U.S. messaging to China against interference. The briefing also covered ongoing diplomatic engagement with Beijing, contingency planning for potential military reactions, and the continuation of trade initiatives with Taiwan regardless of election outcomes. Throughout, the official underscored the importance of open communication channels with both Beijing and Taipei to manage tensions and support peaceful resolution of cross-Strait differences.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Taiwan Election Overview
The senior administration official provided an overview of Taiwan's upcoming presidential and legislative elections scheduled for January 13th, highlighting that this election marks the island's fourth democratic transition since 1996. The United States expresses full confidence in Taiwan's democratic processes and emphasizes bipartisan support for free and fair elections. The official stressed that the U.S. does not take sides in the election and will maintain consistent policy regardless of the outcome.
- Senior administration official states Taiwan's election is a routine democratic process marking the fourth transition since 1996.
- The U.S. has full confidence in Taiwan's democratic processes and bipartisan support for free and fair elections.
- The U.S. does not favor any candidate and will maintain its policy and unofficial relationship regardless of election results.
#### U.S. Policy on Taiwan and China
The official reiterated the U.S. commitment to the One China policy guided by the Taiwan Relations Act, the Three Joint Communiqués, and the Six Assurances. The U.S. opposes unilateral changes to the status quo by either side and supports peaceful resolution of cross-Strait differences without coercion. The official noted that Beijing would be the provocateur if it responds to the election with military pressure or coercion.
- President Biden reaffirmed U.S. policy toward Taiwan remains unchanged during his meeting with President Xi.
- The U.S. opposes unilateral changes to the status quo and does not support Taiwan independence.
- Peaceful resolution of cross-Strait differences is expected, free from coercion and acceptable to people on both sides.
- Senior administration official notes Beijing would be the provocateur if it escalates military pressure post-election.
#### Importance of Taiwan Strait Stability
The official emphasized the critical importance of peace and stability in the Taiwan Strait for global economies, noting that about half of global trade flows through the strait. Disruptions would seriously damage the global economy and have widespread spillover effects. The U.S. plans to maintain open communication channels with Beijing and Taiwan throughout the election and transition period to support stability.
- Taiwan Strait is vital to global supply chains with about half of global trade passing through it.
- Disruption to peace and stability in the strait would seriously damage the global economy.
- The U.S. will keep diplomatic and military-to-military communication channels open with Beijing and maintain close contact with Taiwan through AIT.
#### Unofficial U.S. Delegations to Taiwan
The official discussed the longstanding practice of sending high-level unofficial delegations of former U.S. government officials to Taiwan after elections, a tradition spanning about 20 years. These delegations are consistent with the One China policy and the status quo, and are not viewed as escalatory by China. The U.S. intends to send such a delegation after the upcoming election but has not confirmed timing or participants yet.
- Sending unofficial delegations of former officials to Taiwan post-election is a decades-long tradition.
- Previous delegations included former Cabinet secretaries, national security advisors, and members of Congress.
- President Biden has sent such delegations twice before, in 2021 and 2022, without escalation from China.
- The upcoming delegation is consistent with precedent and will occur after the election to avoid endorsing any candidate.
#### Election Interference Concerns
In response to questions, the official acknowledged concerns about Beijing's attempts to interfere in Taiwan's elections through disinformation and coercion but expressed confidence in Taiwan's ability to manage and expose such interference. The U.S. has communicated clearly to Beijing that interference is unacceptable and would be provocative. The official declined to speculate on specific interference actions but emphasized ongoing monitoring and communication with Taiwan.
- The U.S. has clearly communicated to Beijing that election interference is unacceptable and provocative.
- Taiwan is diligent in identifying and exposing disinformation and interference attempts.
- The U.S. remains confident in the integrity of Taiwan's democratic processes despite interference concerns.
- Channels of communication with Beijing and Taiwan remain active to monitor and address interference.
#### Post-Election Diplomatic Engagements
The official confirmed that the U.S. maintains channels of communication with Beijing and Taiwan and that sending delegations to Taiwan is part of managing cross-Strait tensions. While the official did not confirm whether a delegation would visit Beijing post-election, they noted that such visits have varied over time. The purpose of delegations is to reinforce the U.S. One China policy and support peace and stability through direct communication.
- Unofficial delegations facilitate direct communication with Taiwan's incoming and outgoing officials.
- Sending delegations is a key tool for managing cross-Strait tensions and supporting peace.
- The U.S. has not confirmed if a delegation will visit Beijing post-election, noting this varies by circumstance.
- The delegations clarify U.S. One China policy and reinforce commitment to stability.
#### U.S.-China Relations and Cross-Strait Tensions
The official emphasized that the health of the U.S.-China relationship is not solely dependent on cross-Strait issues and that diplomacy continues to manage difficult situations. The U.S. prepares for various contingencies regarding potential Chinese military reactions to the election but focuses on avoiding unintended conflict through clear communication. The official downplayed the idea that cross-Strait tensions define the broader bilateral relationship.
- U.S.-China relations are managed independently of cross-Strait tensions.
- The U.S. engages in ongoing diplomacy to manage difficult issues and avoid conflict.
- Contingency planning for potential Chinese military reactions is underway but details are not disclosed.
- Clear communication aims to prevent unintended escalation during the election period.
#### Trade Relations with Taiwan
The official noted the importance of the trade relationship with Taiwan, highlighting the 21st Century Trade Initiative Phase One signed last year. The U.S. intends to continue advancing Phase Two of the trade initiative regardless of the election outcome, reflecting bipartisan support in both the U.S. and Taiwan. No specific timeline was provided for the next phase.
- The 21st Century Trade Initiative Phase One was signed last year between the U.S. and Taiwan.
- The U.S. intends to proceed with Phase Two of the trade initiative independent of election results.
- There is bipartisan support in both countries for continuing trade relations.
- No specific details or timelines for Phase Two were provided.
