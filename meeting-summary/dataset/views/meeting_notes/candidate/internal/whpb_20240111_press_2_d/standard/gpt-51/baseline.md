### Overview
A senior U.S. administration official conducts a background briefing on Taiwan’s January 13 presidential and legislative elections, emphasizing that they represent a routine democratic process in which the United States has full confidence, does not favor any candidate, and maintains its longstanding Taiwan policy based on the One China framework, the Taiwan Relations Act, the Three Joint Communiqués, and the Six Assurances. The official underscores that Washington opposes any external interference, has repeatedly warned Beijing against coercion in high-level engagements from the Biden–Xi Woodside summit to talks with Wang Yi and Ambassador Xie Feng, and would view any post-election military or coercive response as provocative, particularly given the Taiwan Strait’s central role in global trade and supply chains. She describes an ongoing strategy of open diplomatic and military-to-military channels with Beijing, close coordination with Taipei via AIT, and a decades-old practice of sending post-election unofficial delegations of former senior officials—consistent with precedent and timed after the vote—to meet the president-elect and other candidates, reaffirm the strong unofficial partnership, and clarify U.S. policy as a means to support peace and stability rather than escalate tensions. In response to press questions, she characterizes PRC efforts to shape Taiwan’s election—through information operations and economic pressure—as consistent with past patterns and not expected to alter the election outcome, affirms that trade initiatives such as the U.S.-Taiwan 21st Century Trade Initiative will continue regardless of who wins, explains that U.S. agencies are conducting prudent contingency planning for a range of possible Chinese reactions, and stresses that the broader U.S.-China relationship is managed through sustained diplomacy aimed at avoiding unintended conflict and keeping intentions clear, rather than being solely driven by cross-Strait developments.

She emphasizes that Taiwan serves as a model democracy globally and that Washington opposes any outside interference in the electoral process. She also reiterates that the United States does not take sides and that U.S. policy toward Taiwan will remain consistent regardless of the election outcome.

She underscores U.S. opposition to unilateral changes to the status quo, non-support for Taiwan independence, and support for peaceful cross-Strait dialogue acceptable to both sides. She clarifies that the United States takes no position on the ultimate resolution of cross-Strait differences as long as they are resolved peacefully and without coercion.
