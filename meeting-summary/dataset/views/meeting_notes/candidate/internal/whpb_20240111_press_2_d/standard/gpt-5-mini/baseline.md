### Overview
  Senior administration officials hold an embargoed background briefing to outline U.S. policy and posture ahead of Taiwan’s January 13 presidential and legislative elections and to take questions. The United States affirms confidence in Taiwan’s democratic processes, reiterates continuity in U.S. policy guided by the One China framework and related instruments, and opposes outside interference or unilateral changes to the status quo, with Senior Administration Official noting President Biden reaffirmed these positions in his meeting with President Xi. The administration says it will keep diplomatic and reopened mil-to-mil channels with Beijing open, maintain close engagement through the American Institute in Taiwan, and intends to send an unofficial delegation to Taipei after the election consistent with longstanding precedent, though timing and participants are not yet confirmed. Officials describe Beijing’s efforts to shape the information environment and apply economic pressure as part of a consistent pattern, say they are monitoring those actions closely in coordination with Taiwan, and note that U.S. agencies are conducting contingency planning for a range of scenarios while emphasizing diplomacy to manage tensions and avoid unintended conflict.

#### Taiwan election overview
The senior administration official summarizes the election timeline: Taiwan holds presidential and legislative elections on January 13 with the president-elect inaugurated on May 20. She underscores that this is the island’s fourth democratic transition since 1996 and emphasizes U.S. confidence in and bipartisan support for Taiwan’s free and fair elections. She reiterates U.S. opposition to outside interference and clarifies the U.S. does not take sides among candidates.
- Senior Administration Official states Taiwan will hold presidential and legislative elections on January 13 and inaugurate the president-elect on May 20.
- Senior Administration Official emphasizes this is Taiwan’s fourth transition between democratically elected administrations since 1996.
- Senior Administration Official says the United States has full confidence in Taiwan’s democratic processes and opposes outside interference.
#### U.S. policy and cross‑Strait principles
The senior administration official reiterates that U.S. policy toward Taiwan remains unchanged and is guided by the One China policy, the Taiwan Relations Act, the Three Joint Communiqués, and the Six Assurances. She confirms the U.S. does not support Taiwan independence, opposes unilateral changes to the status quo by either side, and supports peaceful cross‑Strait dialogue. President Biden’s recent meeting with President Xi in San Francisco reaffirmed continuity in U.S. policy toward Taiwan.

#### Risks to stability and economy
The official warns that unilateral coercive actions by Beijing would make it the provocateur and could threaten peace and stability in the Taiwan Strait. She highlights Taiwan’s central role in global supply chains and estimates that roughly half of global trade flows through the Taiwan Strait, meaning disruptions would have worldwide economic impacts. The official frames maintenance of stability as a global economic and security priority.
- Senior Administration Official says Beijing would be the provocateur if it responds to the election with additional military pressure or coercion.

#### Channels of communication and AIT engagement
The administration commits to keeping diplomatic and recently reopened military-to-military channels with Beijing open during the election and transition period. It also commits to ensuring the American Institute in Taiwan (AIT) and the AIT chair remain in close contact with Taiwan interlocutors to reinforce support for democratic processes and the status quo. These channels are presented as necessary mechanisms to manage tensions and avoid unilateral changes.

#### Delegation precedent and purpose
The official provides historical context, noting the U.S. has a decades-long tradition of sending high‑level former officials to Taiwan after elections, dating back about 20 years. She lists the types of former officials who have traveled in prior delegations and cites examples from this administration and earlier ones, including delegations in 2016, 2021, and 2022. She presents these visits as consistent with the One China policy, the status quo, and as a useful means of direct, face-to-face communication to manage cross‑Strait tensions.
- Senior Administration Official says the United States has for decades sent high-level unofficial delegations of former Cabinet secretaries, national security advisors, members of Congress, governors, and other officials to Taipei.
- Senior Administration Official notes President Biden sent unofficial delegations of former U.S. government officials to Taipei in April 2021 and February 2022.
- Senior Administration Official cites the 2016 visit by former Deputy Secretary Bill Burns and the AIT chair as an example of precedent for post-election engagement.
