### Overview
The Mountain View Downtown Committee meets virtually to approve prior minutes, set future agenda priorities, and receive status updates on downtown projects, operations, and membership. Members raise near-term agenda requests focused on downtown cleanliness, sidewalk and restroom conditions, police presence, unlicensed busking, holiday-season programming, and the downtown ambassadors, with John Lang agreeing to bring forward a consolidated maintenance and events briefing and to schedule the police update and busking discussion in November. Planning staff, led by Krisha Penollar, report limited new development activity but highlight Council approval of a major commercial project at 590 Castro Street, new office use at the former Tide House site, and a forthcoming Spanish tapas restaurant at the former Zonz location, while John Lang outlines the precarious status of the Hope Street hotel, which risks losing its entitlements if it cannot secure a building permit by November 27. The committee receives a brief Castro Street pedestrian mall update, learning that Council hearings on the ordinance and the business improvement assessment area are underway and that overhead lighting is nearly complete except where mature trees block certain spans; members also request a future Parks presentation on long-term tree maintenance. The committee unanimously renews its AB 361 resolution to continue virtual meetings, then reviews proposed revisions to the personal delivery device pilot program that would require a local operator presence, enhanced device labeling, expanded data reporting, and additional safety rules, with members questioning business impacts from Starship’s exit, PDD interactions with major arterials and signals, and the need to eventually move from pilots to a permanent permitting program. Finally, John Lang outlines upcoming vacancies and reappointments across the committee’s membership categories and confirms that, pending Council adoption of updated bylaws and barring last-minute changes, there appear to be enough candidates to fill all seats except the intentionally vacant chamber/DBA slot; the meeting closes with announcements of a historical walking tour, Halloween and business meet-and-greet events, an update on efforts to secure a small downtown fund via the mid-year budget, and a discussion of downtown aesthetics in which staff stress a light-touch beautification approach while many businesses remain below pre-pandemic sales levels.

#### Personal delivery devices (PDD) pilot program revisions
John Lang presents proposed revisions to the city’s personal delivery device pilot program, incorporating prior feedback from the Downtown Committee and the Bicycle/Pedestrian Advisory Committee (BPAC) and reflecting lessons from Starship’s departure from Mountain View. The proposed changes would require PDD providers to maintain a physical presence in the city, add device-level contact information, distinguish between fully autonomous and remotely piloted operations, and significantly expand data reporting on performance, incidents, and infrastructure conditions. Committee members ask about business impacts from losing Starship, PDD interactions with major arterials and signal technology, and the long-term path from pilot projects toward a standardized program, while also noting the devices’ potential value as both a service and a downtown attraction.
- John Lang recounts that Starship, the prior PDD operator downtown, ceased operations over the summer and that other providers have expressed interest but are waiting for updated program rules before committing.
- John Lang states that the pilot program aims to support business opportunities and test PDD use on sidewalks, and that the new revisions reflect Downtown Committee and BPAC feedback.
- John Lang proposes requiring PDD companies to establish a tangible presence in Mountain View beyond the devices themselves, such as an office, operations team, or maintenance facility.
- John Lang recommends adding clear contact information for both the city and the vendor directly on each device so the public can report issues quickly.
- John Lang explains that the revised rules would still require human handlers for fully autonomous devices but remove the handler requirement for devices that are actively controlled remotely by an operator.
- John Lang outlines expanded data requirements, including performance measures that quantify trip reduction or emissions benefits, regular reporting, documentation of all public inquiries or conflicts, and infrastructure condition reporting based on what devices encounter on sidewalks.
- John Lang highlights that PDD vendors are particularly receptive to sharing infrastructure-related data—such as sidewalk uplift, dips, and misaligned pedestrian push buttons—because these issues affect device performance and city safety.
- John Lang notes the proposed safety rule that devices may not stop in curb ramps or in front of pedestrian push buttons, addressing pedestrian accessibility concerns.
- Member David Lin asks whether PDD companies see the requested data as proprietary, and John Lang answers that vendors generally accept the requirements because the city seeks aggregated route patterns and incident data rather than detailed proprietary information.
- Member Jamil Shaikh asks whether businesses have been affected by Starship’s departure, and John Lang admits staff have not yet measured the impact but agrees this is important to investigate.
- Chair Mike Kasperzak mentions prior conversations with a local business (Ava’s) where Starship deliveries were important, especially during the pandemic, underscoring the value of assessing business impacts.
- Chair Mike Kasperzak questions the city’s reluctance to allow PDDs to cross large arterials like Shoreline Boulevard and asks for clarification on potential designated crossing routes.
- John Lang responds that staff are discussing predefined crossing routes over major arterials, limiting PDD crossings to one or two specific streets to address Public Works’ concerns about signal timing and safety.
- Chair Mike Kasperzak suggests the city should consider transitioning from ad-hoc pilots to a standardized PDD permitting framework with clear criteria, so companies can deploy under known rules rather than negotiate bespoke pilot agreements.
- John Lang replies that the city has kept the program in “pilot” mode due to the rapidly evolving technology and a potential shift of devices from sidewalks to bike lanes as speeds increase, but agrees the broader programmatic question is worth exploring.
- Member Eric Cormier asks whether any vendors have proposed aerial delivery devices, and John Lang says the city has had only preliminary conversations on aerial concepts with no formal proposals yet, while acknowledging that this is an emerging space.
- Member David Lin raises whether companies have requested new infrastructure such as automated walk-signal triggers, and John Lang notes there is industry interest in enabling PDDs and vehicles to interact with traffic signals, potentially through sensors or communication tech, though solutions range from simple additional push buttons to more advanced signal upgrades.
- Member Kira Pascoe observes that the PDD robots themselves became a minor tourist attraction and downtown experience, drawing interest at the tech showcase and from visitors, and suggests this “innovation feature” adds value to Castro Street.
- John Lang concludes that he will refine the revisions based on the committee’s comments, return to BPAC for final feedback, and then take the updated pilot program to City Council for approval.
