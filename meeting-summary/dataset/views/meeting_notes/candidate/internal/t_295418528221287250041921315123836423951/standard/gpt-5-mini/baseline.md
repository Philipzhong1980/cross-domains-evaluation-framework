### Overview
  The Mountain View Downtown Committee meeting covers administrative approvals, upcoming agenda items, development and operations updates, and planning matters affecting downtown. The committee unanimously adopts the minutes and passes an AB 361 resolution to authorize virtual meetings for a 30-day period (motion by Merry Yen). Staff delivers development updates including Council approval of 590 Castro (four-story mixed-use with public plaza), temporary occupancy at 954 Villa for office use, and a new restaurant at 19110 Castro, while flagging that the Hope Street lot hotel project has exercised financing extensions but faces entitlement expirations on November 27 that could force re-entitlement if building permits are not issued. John Lang outlines Castro Street pedestrian mall milestones—an October 11 public hearing and an October 25 adoption hearing—reports substantial lighting completion with targeted fixes where tree canopies block cross lighting, and details ongoing cleanup of tent remnants and damaged planters. The committee examines proposed augmentations to the personal delivery device pilot (local operator presence, device contact and reporting requirements, revised human-handler rules for remotely controlled devices, and infrastructure feedback), hears that providers are generally receptive to non‑proprietary data reporting, and discusses whether to continue a flexible pilot or move toward standardized permitting. Staffing and governance topics include bylaw changes approved by CPPC to resolve the chamber/DBA seat, interviews scheduled for November 21, and Council appointments in December, with several incumbents seeking reappointment while others are stepping down. Members also discuss holiday programming, downtown events (including Halloween on October 29 and a walking tour on October 23), beautification concerns and a measured city enforcement approach given ongoing business recovery, and a mid-year budget request that staff is preparing to support committee activities.

  #### Personal delivery devices
  Staff presents proposed augmentations to the city’s personal delivery device (PDD) pilot, reflecting committee and BPAC feedback and provider discussions. Key proposed changes include requiring a local presence or investment by operators, stronger device contact and reporting requirements, adjusted human-handler rules for remotely controlled devices, expanded non-proprietary data reporting (heat maps, incident logs, infrastructure condition reports), and safety rules to prevent devices from blocking curb ramps or pedestrian push buttons; providers have generally been receptive and staff will take revisions to BPAC and then Council. Committee members discuss business impacts of Starship's departure, whether to standardize a permanent permitting program versus continuing a pilot, crossing/boundary restrictions, potential signal interaction technology, and future aerial delivery interest.
- John Lang: summarizes proposed PDD pilot augmentations including a requirement for operators to have a physical presence or investment in Mountain View beyond just devices.
- John Lang: proposes adding clear device contact information and more robust reporting requirements, including documenting all public inquiries and incidents.
- John Lang: proposes adjusting the human-handler requirement for remotely controlled devices so handlers are not mandatory when a person is actively controlling a device remotely.
- John Lang: requests infrastructure feedback from operators so devices can report sidewalk uplift, damaged push buttons, and other hazards to the city.
- David Lin: asks whether companies treated data requests as proprietary and John Lang: reports providers are generally receptive to non-proprietary aggregated data such as heat maps.
- John Lang: notes Starship ceased downtown operations over the summer and agrees to ask businesses about any business impacts from Starship's departure.
- Mike Kasperzak: encourages the city to consider establishing clear standards for PDD operation rather than prolonged, uncertain pilots to attract operators.
- Kira Pascoe: observes that delivery droids previously served as a downtown attraction and could help distinguish and draw people to the area.
- David Lin: asks whether devices could trigger walk signals and John Lang: confirms staff has begun exploratory conversations about integrating sensors and newer signal technology.
- John Lang: says staff will present the refined program to BPAC and then pursue Council approval for any changes to the pilot.
