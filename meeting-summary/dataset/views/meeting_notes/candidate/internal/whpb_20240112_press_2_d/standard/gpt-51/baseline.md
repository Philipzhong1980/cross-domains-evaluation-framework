### Overview
Senior administration and military officials brief reporters on joint U.S.-UK strikes against Houthi-controlled targets in Yemen, explaining that the operation, backed by support and endorsements from a broad coalition of countries, aims to significantly degrade Houthi missile, radar, and UAV capabilities that have threatened commercial and military shipping in the Red Sea, Bab el-Mandeb, and Gulf of Aden. They situate the strikes within a month-long sequence of UN Security Council actions, multilateral statements, and the creation of the 22-country Operation Prosperity Guardian, and stress that the offensive strikes are legally grounded, separate from that defensive mission, and designed with precision to minimize collateral damage. The officials attribute the Houthi campaign to substantial Iranian intelligence and material support, reject Houthi claims that their attacks are limited to Israel-related shipping, and frame the U.S. response as a measured effort to defend global commerce and freedom of navigation without seeking regional escalation. In response to questions, they characterize the impact on Houthi capabilities as significant while withholding detailed metrics, report no confirmed retaliatory attacks or verified related incidents in Iraq as of the briefing, acknowledge that Iran-backed proxy activity across the region is likely to continue, and underscore President Biden’s willingness to order further measures if needed to protect U.S. personnel and the free flow of international trade.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Joint U.S.-UK strikes in Yemen
  A senior administration official announces that the United States and the United Kingdom, supported by Australia, Bahrain, Canada, and the Netherlands, have conducted joint strikes on Houthi-controlled areas in Yemen. The stated purpose is to disrupt and degrade Houthi capabilities that threaten commercial shipping and freedom of navigation in the Red Sea and surrounding waterways. Targets focus on missile, radar, and UAV assets directly tied to attacks on international shipping. The action follows a major Houthi attack on January 9 against U.S. ships and forms part of a broader international response to escalating threats to global trade.
- A senior administration official states that U.S. and UK armed forces conducted joint strikes against Houthi-controlled areas in Yemen with support from Australia, Bahrain, Canada, and the Netherlands.
- A senior administration official explains that the strikes specifically target Houthi missile, radar, and UAV capabilities used in attacks against commercial shipping.
- A senior administration official links the strikes to an earlier large-scale Houthi attack on January 9 involving nearly 20 drones and multiple missiles against U.S. ships.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that Operation Prosperity Guardian forces defeated the January 9 attack and likely prevented ships, including a commercial jet-fuel carrier, from being struck or sunk.
#### Impact on global commerce and UN response
  The senior administration official frames Houthi attacks as a global economic and security issue affecting over 50 countries and prompting rerouting of commercial traffic. These disruptions raise shipping and insurance costs and threaten critical maritime chokepoints. In response, the UN Security Council has condemned the attacks and those arming the Houthis, particularly Iran, while affirming states’ rights to defend their vessels. This diplomatic backdrop underpins and legitimizes the collective military action as a defense of global commerce and international law.
- A senior administration official notes that Houthi attacks have affected citizens, cargo, and commercial interests from more than 50 countries and forced over a dozen shipping companies to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope.
- A senior administration official says the rerouting has increased shipping and insurance costs and negatively impacted the global economy.
- A senior administration official cites a recent UN Security Council resolution condemning more than two dozen Houthi attacks on commercial vessels since November 19 and those who arm the Houthis, naming Iran as the primary supplier.
- A senior administration official highlights that the UN resolution acknowledges the right of states to defend their vessels in accordance with international law.
#### Timeline of diplomatic and military preparations
  The senior administration official lays out a timeline of escalating diplomatic efforts and operational planning leading to the strikes. Since early December, the UN Security Council and coalitions of states have issued statements condemning Houthi actions and stressing freedom of navigation, while Secretary Austin established Operation Prosperity Guardian as a 22-country defensive coalition. President Biden has been closely engaged, directing both diplomatic initiatives and the development of military options, including a final multilateral warning to the Houthis on January 3. After the large January 9 attack, the President authorized Secretary Austin to execute a collective military response, culminating in the current strikes.
- A senior administration official recalls that on December 1 the UN Security Council condemned Houthi attacks in the Red Sea and called for an immediate end to them.
- A senior administration official explains that on December 18 Secretary Austin announced Operation Prosperity Guardian, a 22-country defensive coalition under Combined Maritime Forces and CTF 153.
- A senior administration official notes that on December 19, 44 countries issued a statement condemning Houthi interference with navigational rights and freedoms in the Red Sea.
- A senior administration official reports that President Biden has been engaged on a near-daily basis, directing the initial diplomatic response and the formation of Operation Prosperity Guardian.
- A senior administration official describes how, after attacks on the Maersk Hangzhou on New Year’s Day, President Biden convened his national security team to accelerate UN efforts, build a multinational coalition, and refine potential military targets.
- A senior administration official states that on January 3 the United States and 13 other major shipping nations warned the Houthis they would bear the consequences of further attacks on Red Sea commercial vessels.
- A senior administration official explains that following the large January 9 Houthi attack, President Biden again convened his national security team and then directed Secretary Austin to carry out the joint strikes.
#### International coalition support and endorsements
  Officials describe the breadth of international military and political support for the strikes. The United States and the United Kingdom conduct the kinetic operations, while Australia, Canada, the Netherlands, and Bahrain provide additional, non-operational support. Several countries that signed the January 3 warning, including Denmark, Germany, New Zealand, and the Republic of Korea, publicly endorse the action. The administration underscores the global nature of the coalition to demonstrate legitimacy and shared interest in securing maritime commerce.
- A senior administration official reiterates that the United States and the United Kingdom executed the strikes, with Australia, Canada, the Netherlands, and Bahrain providing additional support.
- A senior administration official notes that Denmark, Germany, New Zealand, and the Republic of Korea, among others from the January 3 warning, have endorsed the strikes.
- A senior administration official says the administration expects more supportive statements from countries overnight.
- A senior administration official declines to detail which other countries were asked to participate in the strikes, citing the sensitivity of operational consultations, but stresses broad international support for the action.
#### Regional context and U.S. strategic framing
  The senior administration official situates the operation within broader Middle East tensions and longstanding U.S. commitments to secure global trade routes. The administration rejects Houthi claims that their attacks are justified by or targeted solely at Israel-related shipping, calling them indiscriminate and legally baseless. The U.S. emphasizes that most affected vessels have no ties to Israel and that the strikes aim to protect lives and commerce, not escalate regional conflict. Officials present the operation as consistent with U.S. national security and defense strategies and as a measured effort to degrade capabilities while avoiding wider war.
- A senior administration official stresses that the issue centers on global commerce, freedom of navigation, and threats to commercial vessels in international waterways.
- A senior administration official says the United States has a historic obligation to protect key arteries of global trade, and that the strikes align with the National Security Strategy and National Defense Strategy.
- A senior administration official calls Houthi claims that their attacks are tied to Gaza or limited to Israeli-linked shipping completely baseless and illegitimate.
- A senior administration official asserts that most ships attacked have no connection to Israel and that such claims do not justify illegal attacks in international waters.
- A senior administration official states that the selected targets focus only on Houthi capabilities and that there is no intent to escalate the situation, only to degrade the Houthis’ ability to continue attacks.
#### Strike execution and separation from defensive coalition
  The senior military official provides an operational overview of how the strikes are conducted and clarifies their relationship to Operation Prosperity Guardian. U.S. and UK forces employ air, surface, and subsurface platforms, using precision-guided munitions to hit specific Houthi facilities while minimizing collateral damage. The military stresses that this offensive action is distinct from Operation Prosperity Guardian, which remains a separate 22-country defensive effort to protect shipping. Forces involved in the strikes remain postured to defend themselves and continue coalition maritime security missions in surrounding waters.
- A senior military official states that the joint strikes use air, surface, and subsurface platforms to hit Houthi-controlled facilities in Yemen.
- A senior military official reports that manned aircraft from the U.S. Navy, U.S. Air Force, and the UK participate in the operation.
- A senior military official emphasizes that precision-guided munitions are used to destroy targets and minimize collateral damage.
- A senior military official clarifies that the strikes are separate from Operation Prosperity Guardian, which remains a defensive 22-country coalition operating in the Red Sea, Bab el-Mandeb, and Gulf of Aden.
- A senior military official says U.S. and UK forces involved in the strikes remain prepared to defend themselves and to continue safeguarding maritime traffic and military vessels in the region.
#### Effect on Houthi capabilities and partner roles
  In response to questions, officials characterize the impact of the strikes on Houthi capabilities as significant but withhold specific metrics for operational security reasons. The military reiterates that the core objective is to remove the Houthis’ ability to target commercial and military vessels in key waterways. The UK’s role in providing fighter aircraft for the strikes is acknowledged, while the nature of other partners’ support is left to those governments to describe. The administration also notes that a broad range of countries express political backing for the operation beyond those directly engaged in the strike package.
- A senior military official says the aim of the strikes is to remove Houthi capability to target maritime vessels in the Red Sea, Bab el-Mandeb, and Gulf of Aden.
- A senior military official characterizes the degradation of Houthi capability as significant but declines to provide percentages due to operational security and intelligence sensitivities.
- A senior military official confirms that the UK contributes fighter aircraft that materially participate in the strikes.
- A senior military official declines to detail the support roles of Australia, Bahrain, Canada, and the Netherlands and instead refers questions to those governments.
- A senior administration official reiterates that there is broad international support for the actions taken, beyond those directly executing the strikes.
#### Expected Houthi response and immediate security picture
  Officials address questions about potential retaliatory actions by the Houthis and current threat assessments. The administration concedes they expect some form of Houthi response, though the intent is that sustained pressure will reduce both capacity and willingness to attack over time. The military reports that, as of the call, there are no observed direct retaliatory attacks against U.S. or coalition forces in the region. U.S. naval crews’ capabilities to detect and track incoming threats are cited as a key factor in defending vessels and determining targeting patterns.
- A senior administration official says they would not be surprised to see some form of Houthi response to the strikes, even as they expect the action to diminish Houthi capability over time.
- A senior military official reports that, at the time of the briefing, there have been no direct retaliatory actions by the Houthis against U.S. or coalition forces in the Red Sea, Bab el-Mandeb, or Gulf of Aden.
- A senior military official states that U.S. crews use advanced sensors and tracking systems to determine with high precision where Houthi weapons are headed.
- A senior military official notes that even when targeting is ambiguous, weapons that threaten ships justify self-defense responses from U.S. and coalition forces.
#### Iran’s role and potential consequences
  The senior administration official discusses Iran’s central role in enabling Houthi activities and addresses questions about broader actions against Tehran. The U.S. assesses that Iran provides intelligence, weapons, and operational support to the Houthis and is involved at every phase of their attacks. The official references an ongoing U.S. pressure campaign against Iran over its regional proxy activities, including in Yemen, but declines to preview any new measures. The administration underscores that it holds Iran responsible for its support to the Houthis and other groups attacking U.S. forces and has conveyed this directly to Tehran.
- A senior administration official states that Iran is the primary enabler and sponsor of the Houthis, providing intelligence and the capabilities used in the Red Sea attacks.
- A senior administration official asserts that Iran has been involved operationally in Houthi attacks and is involved at every phase of these operations.
- A senior administration official notes that the United States has a longstanding pressure campaign against Iran over its activities in Yemen and sponsorship of regional proxies.
- A senior administration official declines to telegraph future actions against Iran but says the United States holds Iran responsible for its role in supporting groups that attack U.S. forces and has communicated that responsibility to Iran.
#### Regional spillover risks, including Iraq and Israel
  Officials respond to questions about possible immediate spillover, including reports of an embassy attack in Iraq and potential Iranian-directed escalations against Israel. The senior administration official reports no information confirming an embassy strike in Iraq at the time of the briefing but acknowledges dynamic conditions. On Israel, the official observes that Iran already sponsors multiple groups attacking Israel regularly, such as Hamas, Hezbollah, and various militias, and says those activities may continue irrespective of the Yemen operation. The administration does not see evidence of an imminent new Iran-driven escalation tied specifically to the Red Sea strikes but does not rule out continued proxy attacks in the region.
- A senior administration official says they have seen no information indicating an embassy in Iraq has been hit, based on reporting available before the briefing.
- A senior administration official notes that Iran already sponsors Hamas, Hezbollah, and Shia militias in Iraq and Syria, as well as the Houthis, all of which conduct attacks on Israel or U.S. forces.
- A senior administration official states that there is no current indication of an imminent new Iranian-directed attack on Israel specifically linked to the Red Sea strikes, but they would not be surprised if Iran-backed attacks continue as before.
- A senior military official explains more generally that U.S. combatant commands routinely maintain kinetic response options against a variety of targets, including Houthi-related threats, though specific timelines for target development are not disclosed.
#### Targeting process, collateral damage, and future actions
  In closing exchanges, officials outline how targets are selected and hint at potential follow-on operations. The military emphasizes that the strike package focuses on specific Houthi capabilities, not civilian areas, and uses precision munitions to minimize collateral damage; detailed battle damage assessment will follow from CENTCOM. The administration describes the operation as a significant option from among several and reiterates confidence that it will significantly degrade Houthi attack capabilities. At the same time, the President signals readiness to take further measures if necessary to protect U.S. personnel and ensure the free flow of international commerce, indicating this may not be the final operation if attacks persist.
- A senior military official says details on the number of targets, locations, and collateral damage assessments will be released by U.S. Central Command in the coming hours and days.
- A senior military official emphasizes that the strikes deliberately avoid civilian population centers and focus on specific Houthi capabilities using precision munitions to minimize collateral damage.
- A senior military official notes that U.S. combatant commanders routinely maintain kinetic response options, including target lists, as part of standard planning, without disclosing timelines for target development.
- A senior administration official characterizes the strikes as a significant option intended to substantially degrade Houthi capabilities to launch recent types of attacks.
- A senior administration official quotes President Biden’s stance that he will not hesitate to direct further measures to protect U.S. personnel and international commerce, and cautions that this may not be the last action if threats continue.
