### Overview
  Senior administration and senior military officials briefed reporters on a coordinated U.S.- and U.K.-led strike against Houthi-controlled targets in Yemen intended to disrupt missile, radar, and UAV systems used to attack commercial shipping in the Red Sea. Officials tie the strikes to a large January 9 Houthi attack that launched nearly 20 drones and multiple missiles at U.S. and commercial vessels and describe the action as part of a broader diplomatic and coalition effort that included Operation Prosperity Guardian and multilateral warnings. The military says the strikes used air, surface, and subsurface platforms with precision-guided munitions, that the U.K. contributed fighter aircraft, and that additional partners provided support while operational details remain limited for security reasons. Officials characterize the degradation of Houthi capability as significant but decline to provide quantitative assessments, report no observed direct retaliatory strikes against coalition forces as of the briefing, and refer detailed damage assessments to Central Command; they also reiterate that Iran is a primary enabler of the Houthis and that the United States holds Iran responsible while not telegraphing further actions.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Announcement of strikes
  Senior administration official announces that U.S. and U.K. forces conducted joint strikes against Houthi-controlled areas in Yemen with support from several partners. The action is framed as aimed at disrupting Houthi capabilities that threaten commercial shipping and freedom of navigation in the Red Sea. The official emphasizes the collective nature of the response and situates it within ongoing attacks against international shipping.
- Senior administration official says the United States and the United Kingdom conducted joint strikes against Houthi-controlled areas in Yemen with support from Australia, Bahrain, Canada, and the Netherlands.
- Senior administration official states the action aims to disrupt and degrade Houthi capabilities that threaten global trade and freedom of navigation in the Red Sea.
  #### Targeting rationale
  Officials describe the strikes as focused specifically on Houthi missile, radar, and UAV capabilities that enable attacks on commercial shipping. They stress selection of targets intended to remove the means by which Houthis threaten vessels rather than to escalate the conflict. The stated goal is to degrade the Houthis' ability to carry out further attacks on maritime traffic.
- Senior administration official explains the targets focused on Houthi missile, radar, and UAV capabilities because those systems are essential to attacks on commercial shipping.
- Senior administration official says there is no intent to escalate and that the aim is to degrade the Houthis' ability to continue these attacks.
  #### Recent attack context
  Officials recount a major Houthi attack on January 9 that launched nearly 20 drones and multiple missiles directed at U.S. ships and commercial vessels and was defeated by U.S. and U.K. naval forces. They assert that without defensive measures ships could have been struck, potentially including a fuel-laden commercial vessel. This event directly precipitated the decision to conduct the retaliatory strikes.
- Senior administration official reports that on January 9 nearly 20 drones and multiple missiles were launched in multiple salvos directly against U.S. ships and were defeated by U.S. and U.K. forces.
- Senior administration official warns that without the defensive mission ships could have been struck, including a commercial ship carrying U.S.-owned jet fuel.
  #### Shipping impacts and condemnation
  Officials highlight the broader economic impact of Houthi attacks, noting that more than a dozen shipping companies have rerouted vessels around the Cape of Good Hope and that over 50 countries have been affected. They point to broad international condemnation, including U.N. Security Council and multilateral statements calling for an end to the attacks. This diplomatic backdrop is presented as support for the collective military action.
- Senior administration official states that Houthi attacks have affected citizens and cargo of more than 50 countries and led over a dozen shipping companies to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope.
- Senior administration official notes that the U.N. Security Council issued a resolution condemning the attacks and recognized states' right to defend their vessels under international law.
  #### Formation of coalitions
  Officials review the diplomatic and coalition-building steps taken before the strikes, including the creation of Operation Prosperity Guardian and multilateral warning statements. They describe Operation Prosperity Guardian as a 22-country defensive coalition organized under Combined Maritime Forces to protect maritime traffic in the region. The strikes are presented as coordinated with and endorsed by a range of partners who previously joined warning statements.
- Senior administration official recounts the establishment of Operation Prosperity Guardian as a 22-country defensive coalition led by Coalition Task Force 153.
- Senior administration official notes that the January 3 multilateral warning statement involved the United States and 13 other major shipping countries and warned the Houthis they would bear full consequences for further attacks.
  #### Presidential decision process
  Senior administration official outlines President Biden's near-daily engagement and direction to pursue diplomatic efforts, build a coalition, and refine military options. The President convened his national security team after recent attacks and ultimately directed Secretary Austin to carry out the collective strikes after assessing options. Officials emphasize that the decision followed warnings and coordination with partners.
- Senior administration official says President Biden directed the initial diplomatic response and formation of Operation Prosperity Guardian and remained engaged through his national security team.
- Senior administration official states the President directed Secretary Austin to carry out the response after being presented military options following the January 9 attack.
  #### Military execution details
  Senior military official describes the strikes as launched from air, surface, and subsurface platforms using a variety of manned aircraft from U.S. Navy, U.S. Air Force, and U.K. forces, and employing precision-guided munitions to minimize collateral damage. He clarifies that these strikes are separate from Operation Prosperity Guardian, which remains a defensive coalition in the region. The military reiterates readiness to defend maritime traffic and participating forces' self-defense capabilities.
- Senior military official reports the strikes were launched from air, surface, and subsurface platforms and used manned aircraft from the U.S. Navy, U.S. Air Force, and the U.K.
- Senior military official says precision-guided munitions were used to destroy targets and minimize collateral damage and that the strikes are distinct from Operation Prosperity Guardian.
  #### Assessment of degradation
  When asked about how much Houthi capability the strikes degraded, the senior military official characterizes the effect as significant but declines to provide quantitative measures for operational security. He frames the stated aim as removing the Houthis' ability to target maritime vessels across the Red Sea, Bab el-Mandeb, and Gulf of Aden. The administration official echoes that the action is intended to reduce capability and propensity to attack over time.
- Senior military official characterizes the degradation of Houthi capability as significant but declines to provide exact percentages due to operational security.
- Senior administration official says the action is expected to diminish Houthi capability over time and would not be surprised to see some form of response.
  #### Coalition contributions
  Officials confirm that the U.K. materially participated with fighter aircraft and that Australia, Bahrain, Canada, and the Netherlands provided additional support, while offering limited public operational details. The senior administration official declines to discuss the full slate of consultations or which other partners were asked to participate. Officials point reporters to partners for specifics and note broader international endorsements of the action.
- Senior military official confirms that the U.K. participated materially with fighter aircraft in the strikes.
- Senior administration official declines to discuss operational consultations beyond the publicly listed participants and says partners may reveal their levels of support.
  #### Houthi response observed
  Reporters ask about Houthi claims of retaliatory attacks after the strikes; the senior military official states that as of the briefing they have not observed direct retaliatory action against U.S. or coalition forces in the region. Officials reiterate readiness to defend forces and maritime traffic and acknowledge they would not be surprised by some form of Houthi response. The administration official defers to the Pentagon for real-time operational observations.
- Senior military official says they have not seen any direct retaliatory action directed toward U.S. or coalition members in the Red Sea, Bab el-Mandeb, or Gulf of Aden as of the briefing.
- Senior administration official says they would not be surprised to see some response but defers to the Pentagon for observed developments.
  #### Targeting determination and ISR
  Officials explain that U.S. forces use professional crews and sensors to detect, track, and determine where weapons are headed, which underpins self-defense actions against threats to vessels. The military official emphasizes detection and tracking capabilities but declines to disclose intelligence sources and methods. Reporters are referred to Central Command for more detailed assessments of targets and damage.
- Senior military official says professional crews with advanced equipment can detect, track, and nearly precisely determine where incoming weapons are headed.
- Senior military official refers reporters to Central Command for detailed assessments on specific targets and strike outcomes.
  #### Iran's role and consequences
  Senior administration official reiterates that Iran is a primary enabler and sponsor of the Houthis, providing intelligence and capabilities used in the attacks, and says the U.S. holds Iran responsible for that role. He declines to detail sources or to telegraph future measures but notes an ongoing pressure campaign against Iran and that the administration will act as necessary to protect U.S. interests and international commerce. The official also relates Iran's sponsorship to broader proxy activity in the region.
- Senior administration official states that Iran is a primary sponsor and enabler of the Houthis and provided information, intelligence, and capabilities used in the attacks.
- Senior administration official says the U.S. has a longstanding pressure campaign against Iran and holds Iran responsible for its role with the Houthis, while declining to announce additional actions.
  #### Collateral damage and follow-ups
  Reporters press for numbers of targets, locations, and collateral damage assessments; the senior military official says those specifics will be released by Central Command in the coming hours and days and reiterates that targets were selected to minimize risk to civilians. The senior administration official frames the strikes as a significant action intended to degrade capability and cautions that further measures may follow if necessary. Officials commit to forthcoming detailed assessments from relevant commands.
- Senior military official says details on the number of targets, locations, and assessments of collateral damage will be forthcoming from Central Command.
- Senior military official emphasizes that targets were selected to minimize the risk of collateral damage and were not civilian population centers.
