### Overview
  The advisory board receives the Youth Services Unit (YSU) annual report from Sergeant David Shen and Lieutenant McGee, with Chief Canfield and staff present, and reviews program activity, training, partnerships, and operational data for the 2022–2023 school year. YSU emphasizes its values—trust, safe schools, restorative justice, and youth empowerment—reports roughly 280 school visits (with program visits comprising about half), details programs including Dreams and Futures and PAL boxing, and highlights training (about 154 hours) and partnerships such as the Stanford Reach Lab for vaping/cannabis harm-reduction work. The unit reports three arrests (3% of school incidents), approximately 61 home visits, and anecdotally about 200 vaping devices seized (50 devices shared with Stanford; others retained and destroyed per evidence protocols), and identifies data-collection gaps and a plan to improve outcome reporting and parent-facing outreach. Board members and public commenters press for clearer demographic tracking, more Spanish-language parent engagement, expanded high-school and female-officer involvement, and national documentation of YSU practices; staff note capacity to implement demographic capture and plan to pursue NASRO and other outreach. The meeting also includes a PSAB subcommittee update on North County Trust and mobile crisis coordination: Chief Canfield explains technical limits of the statewide 988 routing (currently tied to caller area code) and describes a temporary pause in local dispatch-procedure rollouts while the state upgrades 988 capabilities, with the tri-city group continuing coordination; staff confirm naloxone is available in schools and reiterate plans to clarify subcommittee names and memberships after City Council adopts workplans. Public safety concerns raised include a historical park shooting sentencing and a campus weapon incident, and staff reiterate commitments to improved data, enhanced prevention education (especially on vaping), trauma-informed practices, and regional collaboration on alternative mental-health responses.
  
  ### Detailed Summary

#### YSU overview and goals
  Sergeant David Shen and Lieutenant McGee present the Youth Services Unit (YSU) structure, core values, and primary goals for school engagement. They outline a small unit (one sergeant, three SROs, a program coordinator, and police assistants) and emphasize values of building trust, safe school communities, restorative justice, and youth empowerment. The unit focuses on reducing referrals to the criminal justice system through proactive engagement and partnerships.
- Sergeant David Shen explains the YSU staffing and introduces the unit's four core values as the foundation for programming.
- Sergeant David Shen states the unit's primary goals include creating safe school environments, reducing criminal referrals, preventing juvenile delinquency, and fostering positive engagement with families and schools.
- Lieutenant McGee supports the presentation and notes historical continuity in YSU work and manuals previously developed with board input.
#### Training and partnerships
  YSU reports 154 hours of SRO-specific training during the school year, including active shooter response, crisis intervention, de-escalation, internet crimes against children (ICAC) topics, and a harm-reduction curriculum for nicotine/cannabis. The unit highlights partnerships—most notably with Stanford Reach Lab on youth substance education and with organizations supporting neurodiverse engagement and Safe Routes to Schools. Those partnerships inform curriculum, prevention work, and evidence-based approaches to trending youth issues.
- Sergeant David Shen reports SROs completed about 154 hours of specialized training covering active shooter response, crisis intervention, de-escalation, and social-media safety.
- Sergeant David Shen describes the Stanford Reach Lab partnership as providing current research to shape harm-reduction curriculum for vaping and cannabis.
- Sergeant David Shen says YSU collaborates with neurodiversity-focused organizations and Safe Routes to Schools to expand outreach and safety programming.
#### YSU programs and activities
  The unit reviews recurring programs such as Dreams and Futures (summer program), PAL mentoring and boxing, ROAR mentoring, run-hide-defend training, and vaping education presentations, reporting participation metrics for the year. Many programs are referral-based for at-risk youths; Dreams and Futures had 128 participants and a large number of peer counselors. The unit emphasizes mentoring continuity and peer-led approaches as program strengths.
- Sergeant David Shen reports Dreams and Futures provided 18 daily summer sessions with 128 participants and 40 counselors, 24 of whom were past participants.
- Sergeant David Shen states YSU ran about 54 mentoring sessions and introduced the ROAR mentoring program at Graham Middle School with 30 participants.
- Sergeant David Shen highlights PAL boxing, run-hide-defend training, vaping education sessions, and annual community distribution events as core YSU activities.
#### School visit classifications
  YSU analyzed 280 school visits and categorized them by purpose: programming, school-initiated requests, administrative meetings, dispatched calls, incident follow-ups, and proactive visits. Programming visits represented about half of recorded visits, with school-initiated requests and administrative/program meetings making up much of the remainder, while proactive unscheduled visits appear underreported. Sergeant Shen acknowledges limitations in the current data capture and expects the proactive visit count to be higher than recorded.
- Sergeant David Shen reports about 280 recorded school visits and explains categories used: YSU programming, school-initiated, administrative, dispatched, incident follow-up, and proactive visits.
- Sergeant David Shen states approximately 50% of visits were YSU programming, 37% were school-initiated, and proactive visits were recorded at about 2% but likely undercounted.
- Sergeant David Shen acknowledges data-collection gaps and says proactive visits—especially at elementary schools—are likely higher than the system currently reports.
#### Juvenile arrests and outcomes
  The presentation details three school-related custodial incidents during the 2022–2023 school year: a 15-year-old possession case resulting in a juvenile contact report, a 17-year-old attempted murder/stabbing resulting in custodial arrest, and an 18-year-old possession of an egregious dagger resulting in custodial arrest. YSU emphasizes that arrests and JCRs represent a small fraction of incidents and that SROs retain statutory responsibility for arrest decisions, exercised within unit policy and restorative justice principles.
- Sergeant David Shen states there were three arrests in 2022–2023: a 15-year-old cannabis possession with a JCR, a 17-year-old attempted murder via stabbing leading to custodial arrest, and an 18-year-old possessing a spring-loaded dagger leading to custodial arrest.
- Sergeant David Shen reports those three arrests represent approximately 3% of the 113 school-related incidents that required SRO response.
- Sergeant David Shen emphasizes SROs make arrest decisions under state law and departmental procedures, with a preference for counseling and restorative approaches except when safety requires arrest.
#### Home visits and interventions
  YSU conducted 61 home visits classified as truancy-related, support, wellness checks, or intervention. The largest category was truancy (27), and interventions (3) were used where there was concern about potential threats or imminent safety issues. The unit stresses home visits are parent-centered and are curtailed if the parent or juvenile requests them to stop.
- Sergeant David Shen reports 61 home visits: 27 for truancy, 15 for support, 16 for wellness checks, and 3 for intervention-related concerns.
- Sergeant David Shen explains intervention visits target higher-risk situations such as potential threats to school safety and are used to assess household risk and ensure safety.
- Sergeant David Shen emphasizes home visits are conducted with sensitivity to parental wishes and are ended if parents or juveniles withdraw consent.
#### Vaping device seizures
  YSU estimates seizing approximately 200 vaping devices during the year and is prioritizing vaping and substance education for the coming school year. About 50 devices were provided to Stanford for research; the department retains other seized devices as evidence and subsequently destroys them if not needed for a case. The unit frames vaping as a rising trend requiring parent-facing education and harm-reduction strategies.
- Sergeant David Shen reports anecdotally seizing about 200 vaping devices during the school year and identifies vaping as a nationwide youth trend requiring increased education.
- Sergeant David Shen states roughly 50 devices were donated to Stanford Reach Lab for study and the department processes and destroys other seized devices per evidence protocols.
- Sergeant David Shen commits to expanding vaping and substance-prevention education using the Reach Lab harm-reduction curriculum.
#### Data improvements and next steps
  YSU identifies data collection and outcome reporting as priority areas for improvement, including automatic classification of visit types, better recording of seizures/searches, and capturing demographic data. The unit plans to implement more robust reporting, pursue trauma-informed and environmental-design training, and increase quarterly surveys and meetings with school administrators. Sergeant Shen indicates a desire to track year-over-year outcomes and present more comprehensive data in future reports.
- Sergeant David Shen says the unit will improve data collection to automatically capture visit classifications, seizures, and other outcome measures.
- Sergeant David Shen notes the unit intends to implement regular surveys and quarterly meetings with school administrators to collect collaborative feedback.
- Sergeant David Shen states the YSU will pursue additional trauma-informed training and document year-over-year data to define success metrics.

#### Mental health response update
  The PSAB subcommittee provides a verbal update on coordination with North County Trust and MCRT and announces biannual Trust updates and an upcoming Trust presentation to PCOP. Chief Canfield explains efforts to divert appropriate 911 mental-health calls to the statewide 988 system and highlights a temporary pause in local procedure changes while the state updates 988 capabilities. Tri-city coordination continues, and staff await state guidance on data and dispatch capabilities before finalizing local protocols.

#### 988 routing technical issues
  Chief Canfield details technical limitations of the 988 system: routing is currently based on caller area code, which can misroute calls and lacks the data transfer capabilities of 911 systems. The department is temporarily pausing implementation of local transfer procedures to avoid redundant work until state requirements and upgrades are finalized. Staff emphasize they will route non-violent, non-medical calls to 988 when feasible and continue tri-city coordination.
- Chief Canfield explains 988 currently receives only minimal data (effectively the caller area code) and that calls can be misrouted if the caller's phone displays an out-of-area number.
- Chief Canfield says the tri-city effort is pausing local procedure rollout to align with forthcoming state standards and avoid rework.
- Chief Canfield asserts the department's goal is to route calls without weapons or medical needs to behavioral-health responders via 988 once systems and guidelines permit.
#### EMS role and clarifications
  Board members ask whether EMS or fire personnel will be part of alternative mental-health responses. Staff clarify that county EMS typically does not respond to purely mental-health calls without apparent medical need, and tri-city planning focuses on behavioral-health responders, Trust, and MCRT. The city continues to advocate for sustainable, well-integrated alternative response models while monitoring state guidance.
- Chief Canfield states that if there is no apparent medical need, county EMS typically does not respond to purely mental-health calls in the current regional model.
- Chief Canfield emphasizes the city is motivated to develop sustainable local diversion pathways and will continue to partner with county and regional stakeholders.
- Subcommittee members confirm Trust and MCRT remain central to non-police crisis response strategies as local procedures evolve.
