### Overview
The meeting centers on an in-depth annual report from the Mountain View Police Department’s Youth Services Unit and School Resource Officer program, followed by a shorter update on mental health crisis response coordination. Sergeant David Shen and Chief Canfield outline a youth-focused model staffed by one sergeant, three SROs, and a program coordinator that emphasizes trust-building, restorative justice, and reduced justice-system referrals. They report 264 program sessions reaching more than 2,000 participants, 61 home visits primarily tied to truancy and wellness, and only three justice actions (two arrests and one Juvenile Contact Report) out of 113 SRO-involved incidents, while highlighting partnerships with Stanford’s REACH Lab, neurodiversity advocates, Challenge Team, and Safe Routes to School. Board members probe school visit patterns, the limited but likely undercounted proactive presence on elementary campuses, and the reduced presence at the high school, and they press for clearer demographic tracking, expanded female officer involvement, Spanish-language outreach, and careful balancing of structured vs. informal campus visits to avoid reinforcing school-based bias. Public commenters from Mothers Against Murder and MVCPRA raise concerns about a light sentence in a local shooting case, SRO oversight after the subcommittee’s dissolution, and safety issues in specific SRO activities, while staff clarify that SRO oversight now occurs via MOUs and annual reports to PSAB. In the second half, the Mental Health Crisis Response subcommittee reports ongoing collaboration with the county’s TRUST program and the Mobile Crisis Response Team, and Chief Canfield explains tri-city efforts to integrate 988 and 911 routing for non-violent mental health calls, which are temporarily paused pending state-level upgrades to 988’s location and transfer capabilities, underscoring Mountain View’s broader commitment to alternative crisis response and data-informed policing reforms.

#### Youth Services Unit and SRO program structure
Sergeant David Shen and Lieutenant (later referenced with Chief) present the annual report on the Youth Services Unit (YSU) and School Resource Officer (SRO) program for FY 2022–23. They describe the unit’s staffing, guiding values, and overarching goals focused on safety, trust-building, restorative justice, and reduced justice-system referrals. The presentation frames the SRO function as programming- and relationship-focused rather than security-guard–style campus policing.
- Sergeant David Shen introduces himself as the new Youth Services Unit sergeant, succeeding Scott Thomas, and explains that he assumed the role in March near the end of the school year.
- Sergeant Shen states that the YSU consists of one sergeant, three SROs (Officers Guevara, Lowe, and Delira), a program coordinator (Liz Mendez), and supporting police assistants.
- Sergeant Shen outlines four core values for the unit—building trust, safe school community, restorative justice, and empowering youth—describing them as interdependent ‘table legs’ that all must be present for the program to work.
- Sergeant Shen emphasizes that a key goal is to reduce referrals of youth into the criminal justice system by looking beyond a “Band-Aid” arrest-or-citation model toward root causes, relationships, and program referrals.
- Sergeant Shen notes that the unit aims to prevent juvenile delinquency proactively by engaging at-risk youth early and by partnering closely with families, school administration, and community experts.
#### YSU quantitative outcomes and vaping trend
The YSU reports a mix of quantitative activity data, highlighting both broad engagement and a small number of enforcement actions. They document hundreds of program sessions, dozens of home visits, and only three justice-system referrals out of 113 SRO-involved incidents. A major trend is the widespread presence of youth vaping, which the unit addresses with seizures, education, and collaboration with public health researchers.
- Sergeant Shen reports 264 YSU-led session events reaching just over 2,000 participants during the 2022–23 school year.
- Sergeant Shen states that SROs handled 113 SRO-assisted or SRO-involved school incidents, which led to only two custodial arrests and one Juvenile Contact Report (JCR) notice to appear.
- Sergeant Shen notes that the unit conducted 61 home visits—27 truancy-related, 15 support-related, 16 wellness checks, and 3 intervention-related—and that these categories often overlap.
- Sergeant Shen estimates that approximately 200 vaping devices were seized during the school year and describes youth vaping (nicotine and cannabis) as an alarming, widespread issue the unit will focus on more heavily in the coming year.
- Sergeant Shen characterizes many YSU outcomes as qualitative—improved relationships and behavior changes—that are not fully captured by the quantitative numbers presented.
#### SRO training and specialized skills
The presenters detail the substantial training SROs receive, emphasizing school safety, de‑escalation, mental health response, and youth-specific issues like substance use and online exploitation. The training portfolio aims to keep officers current with evolving youth risk factors, including social media, cannabis, and vaping. The board appears generally supportive of the training depth and asks how it has evolved over time.
- Sergeant Shen reports that SROs completed approximately 154 hours of SRO-related training in 2022–23, including school safety, de-escalation, and crisis intervention courses.
- He describes a school safety symposium that covers connecting with students, reducing bullying, and behavioral threat assessment on campus.
- He explains that SROs receive annual active shooter training that goes beyond tactics to include emergency medical care, coordination with fire personnel, and recognizing early warning signs for prevention.
- Sergeant Shen highlights the Stanford Cannabis Conference and Tobacco Toolkit Symposium and notes that YSU has partnered with Stanford’s REACH Lab to apply a harm reduction approach to youth cannabis and nicotine use rather than a purely abstinence-focused DARE-style model.
- He notes that SROs attend crisis intervention training (40 hours) to recognize mental health disorders, practice de-escalation, prevent suicide, and distinguish neurodiversity from mental illness, and they also receive training from the Silicon Valley ICAC Vigilant Parent Initiative focused on social media exploitation risks.
- Member Langton asks how the training program has evolved, and Sergeant Shen responds that the unit intentionally selects courses that reflect current youth stressors, especially social media and newer substances, to keep the program relevant.
#### Community partnerships and harm-reduction collaboration
The YSU describes several external partnerships that support its mission, including research institutions, neurodiversity advocates, community coalitions, and school safety programs. These collaborations help the unit stay aligned with best practices in harm reduction, inclusivity, and traffic safety for students. The board hears that many of these relationships are two-way, with YSU providing field insights while receiving expert guidance.
- Sergeant Shen explains that YSU partners with Stanford’s REACH Lab (Research and Education to Empower Adolescents and Young Adults to Choose Health) to address youth substance use with up-to-date research and harm reduction strategies.
- He notes that YSU shares “boots on the ground” insights about current vape device types, access methods, and youth behaviors with Stanford, while receiving curriculum support on safer decision-making around cannabis and nicotine.
- Sergeant Shen reports collaborations with the group P.R.A.G.N.Y.A, which helps neurodiverse youth interact more comfortably with law enforcement and, reciprocally, trains officers to better understand and engage neurodiverse community members.
- He states that SROs regularly attend monthly Challenge Team meetings to listen to school and community stakeholders, including a recent panel of high school students discussing contemporary stressors, and that the unit uses these insights to refine programming.
- He describes Safe Routes to School as a key partner in bike safety, explaining that SROs provide bike safety and enforcement presentations and distribute helmets to students and families in need, especially after local bike-related tragedies.
#### YSU programs: Dreams and Futures, mentoring, and boxing
The unit highlights its flagship and ongoing youth programs, which target at-risk students identified by schools. These programs emphasize mentorship, leadership development, structured activities, and long-term relationship-building rather than one-off events. Board members with long familiarity with PAL and similar programs note their longevity and apparent impact on participants’ lives.
- Sergeant Shen describes Dreams and Futures as the unit’s largest annual program, with 18 daily sessions each summer: two weeks for elementary students and two weeks for middle school students, totaling 128 participants and 40 youth counselors.
- He notes that at-risk counselors, SCEFs, and assistant principals refer students for Dreams and Futures, and that 24 of the 40 counselors this year were former Dreams and Futures participants, demonstrating a ‘pay it forward’ effect.
- He explains that PAL mentoring held 54 weekly sessions at Monta Loma and Castro Elementary Schools with about 38 participants referred for behavioral or social concerns, often later feeding into Dreams and Futures.
- Sergeant Shen outlines PAL boxing as a weekly program with about 54 sessions and 30 participants, emphasizing that it is not about fighting but about channeling energy, building discipline, and opening avenues for youth to talk and connect with officers.
- Member Frank recalls that PAL boxing programs have existed since at least the 1980s and notes that similar programs nationally have produced Junior Olympic and Olympic-level athletes, underscoring the program’s long-term potential.
#### New ROAR peer-mentoring program at Graham Middle
YSU piloted the ROAR mentoring program at Graham Middle School to leverage student leadership in supporting at-risk peers. Rather than relying solely on officers or adult mentors, the program positions selected student leaders to mentor classmates flagged by school staff. The unit hopes this peer-based approach will create sustainable leadership pipelines and deeper peer influence.
- Sergeant Shen introduces ROAR mentoring as a new program at Graham Middle School with 30 participants and weekly meetings launched in 2022–23.
- He explains that school administrators identify student leaders with strong positive behavior and pair them with at-risk students, shifting the mentoring role toward peers rather than relying solely on SROs.
- He says the goal is for ROAR participants to eventually become counselors and leaders in other YSU programs, thereby expanding youth leadership and ownership of the program culture.
- Member Schauer asks how at-risk students are identified, and Sergeant Shen clarifies that SCEFs and at-risk counselors make referrals based on behavior, performance, or social concerns.
#### Vaping education, Run-Hide-Defend, and other school presentations
Beyond core mentoring programs, YSU delivers several schoolwide educational offerings. These include vaping and substance-use education sessions, active shooter response training, and participation in events like bike rodeos and school fun runs. The unit frames these as both safety interventions and opportunities for positive contact, particularly in elementary and middle schools.
- Sergeant Shen reports that YSU delivered about eight vaping education presentations to roughly 350 attendees across middle and elementary schools, focusing on nicotine and cannabis risks via vape pens.
- He states that SROs provided approximately 15 Run-Hide-Defend active shooter response presentations across K–12 schools, the adult school, and the Mountain View Operations Center.
- He notes that YSU participates in events such as bike rodeos, school fun runs (e.g., Vargas), and summer camps, often at schools that do not generate many enforcement-related calls, to ensure those campuses also receive positive attention.
- Member Schauer, a high school teacher, comments that vaping is effectively an epidemic in schools and supports increased education and parent-focused outreach on both marijuana and nicotine.
- Sergeant Shen indicates that YSU aims to expand parent-facing education, including potential Spanish-language offerings, especially around social media risks and substance harm reduction.
#### School visit data and SRO presence patterns
The presentation includes a breakdown of 280 school visits by purpose, revealing that most SRO presence is either for YSU programs or school-initiated requests, with a small number of purely proactive visits. Board members probe how frequently SROs appear unannounced, especially at the high school, and what drives the distribution of presence across school levels. Staff acknowledge data limitations and commit to improved tracking.
- Sergeant Shen explains that school visit purposes are categorized as school-initiated, dispatched, administrative meetings, YSU programs, incident follow-ups, and proactive visits, with proactive visits largely at elementary schools.
- He reports that about 50% of visits are for YSU programs, 37% are school-initiated, 10% are administrative meetings, and about 2% each are dispatched, follow-up, or proactive visits.
- Sergeant Shen concedes that the recorded number of proactive visits (about 10) is likely an undercount due to incomplete data entry, especially for unscheduled elementary school drop-ins.
- Member Schauer asks whether SRO presence at Mountain View High School is mostly scheduled or unannounced, and Shen replies that high school presence is now significantly less than at middle and elementary schools and is largely program-based (e.g., Dreams and Futures).
- In response to questions, Shen attributes the reduced high school presence primarily to focusing programming on younger students rather than to community feedback, while Chief Canfield clarifies that an earlier rumor about officers handing out pizza at the high school was inaccurate and that a pizza-related appearance had been at a middle school event by invitation.
#### Juvenile arrests, JCRs, and restorative approach
YSU presents details on the three youth-related justice actions taken in the school year and situates them within a broader restorative framework. Out of 113 SRO-involved incidents, only three resulted in a JCR or arrest: one repeat cannabis possession case, one attempted murder stabbing, and one adult student with a dangerous dagger. The low enforcement rate is highlighted as evidence of the unit’s commitment to counseling and restorative responses.
- Sergeant Shen explains that when SROs respond to an incident, options include admonishment/counseling, issuance of a Juvenile Contact Report (JCR) notice to appear before juvenile probation, or custodial arrest for criminal matters.
- He reports three enforcement outcomes in 2022–23: one JCR for a 15-year-old repeatedly caught with cannabis in a vape pen (with 7.7 grams found during a school-led search), one custodial arrest of a 17-year-old for attempted murder in a school stabbing, and one custodial arrest of an 18-year-old fifth‑year student for possessing a spring-loaded dagger on campus.
- Shen notes that in the dagger case, school staff initially searched for vaping contraband and then learned from the student that a weapon was also present, prompting SRO involvement to recover the weapon for safety reasons.
- He emphasizes that the three cases represent roughly 3% of the 113 SRO-involved incidents, and that the vast majority of cases are resolved through counseling and school administrative handling rather than criminal justice referrals.
- In response to Member Frank’s concerns about potential disparities, Shen acknowledges that demographic data on contacted students is not currently automatically tracked but states that improving data collection—including demographics—is a priority going forward.
#### Home visits: truancy, wellness, and intervention
The unit describes 61 home visits conducted in the school year, with overlapping reasons including truancy, academic or behavioral support, wellness checks, and rare intervention related to potential threats. The board asks how these categories are defined and how voluntary participation is ensured. YSU stresses that home visits are collaborative, not coercive, and stop if families withdraw consent.
- Sergeant Shen reports 61 home visits: 27 primarily truancy-related, 15 support-related (e.g., school performance or behavior), 16 wellness checks, and 3 classified as intervention for potential safety threats.
- He explains that these categories overlap significantly and were manually parsed by reviewing individual visit summaries, with “intervention” reserved for more serious concerns such as statements about possible school violence.
- Shen states that home visits are always conducted with parental involvement and consent, and that if a parent or youth asks to stop, officers do not force continued engagement.
- In response to board questions, Shen notes that many support and wellness visits originate from truancy concerns but evolve into broader checks on student and family well-being.
- Member Langton asks for further clarification on category distinctions, and Shen reiterates that truancy may be the trigger but the dominant purpose (e.g., verifying a threat’s credibility) determines whether a visit is labeled intervention.
#### Future improvements: data, vaping response, and training
Looking ahead, YSU outlines planned improvements in data collection, program emphasis, and training. They aim to better capture visit purposes, searches, and contraband; expand vaping and substance education; invest in trauma-informed practices; and maintain robust active shooter preparedness. Formalized feedback loops with schools and more diverse officer participation in youth programs are also priorities.
- Sergeant Shen identifies improved data collection and outcome reporting as a key goal, aiming to automate fields such as visit type (school-initiated vs. dispatched), search type, and contraband seized to avoid manual parsing.
- He states that YSU will increase vaping and substance-use education, particularly parent-facing sessions, and continue leveraging Stanford REACH Lab’s harm-reduction curriculum in light of fentanyl and other risks.
- Shen notes that SROs are pursuing trauma-informed practice training through Mountain View High School’s educator-focused sessions and are exploring 40-hour crime prevention through environmental design and student threat assessment trainings.
- He mentions planned more formal quarterly meetings with school administrators and regular surveys to gather structured feedback on SRO performance and program effectiveness, acknowledging this as an area that needs strengthening.
- Shen and Chief Canfield discuss a strategy to involve more non‑SRO officers in youth programming—initially in a collateral, mentored capacity—to broaden relationships (including with female officers) and build a pipeline of future SROs without diluting the program’s culture.

#### Narcan deployment and overdose preparedness in schools
Board members ask how the district and YSU handle opioid overdose risks on campuses. Staff describe a proactive push to place naloxone (Narcan) in schools, with particular attention during a countywide shortage. The unit’s harm reduction approach and training on fentanyl risks aligns with this effort to mitigate overdose deaths among youth.
- A board member asks whether Narcan is being used or available at high schools and which drugs it counteracts, expressing uncertainty about its effectiveness for fentanyl.
- Sergeant Shen clarifies that Narcan (naloxone) is effective for opioid overdoses, including fentanyl, and confirms that it is available in high schools.
- Chief Canfield adds that the SRO program proactively advocated for placing Narcan across schools—from high schools and middle schools down to elementary campuses—especially during a countywide supply shortage.
- The chief notes that the county ultimately provided Narcan in response to these expressed needs, underscoring YSU’s preventive role beyond traditional policing.
#### Restorative justice implementation and school collaboration
In response to board inquiries, YSU explains how restorative justice principles guide responses to student misconduct and clarify the division of responsibility between police and school administration. SROs focus on criminal matters and counseling; schools independently administer discipline. The Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) between the city and school districts codifies these boundaries.
- A board member asks how YSU practices and models restorative justice and how districts support this, prompting Shen to describe a counseling-first approach aimed at helping youth understand harm and repair relationships instead of defaulting to arrest or citation.
- Sergeant Shen states that JCRs and arrests are considered last-resort options after other interventions are exhausted, and the low 3% enforcement rate reflects this philosophy.
- He notes that SROs may refer students to programs like PAL boxing as part of restorative responses, using structured, positive outlets to address underlying issues.
- Shen stresses that SROs do not participate in school disciplinary decisions; administrative consequences are handled solely by school staff.
- Chief Canfield emphasizes that the MOU between the city and school districts formally defines that SROs handle criminal matters and that school discipline remains separate, aligning operational practice with restorative goals.
#### Concerns about demographic impacts and school-driven bias
A board member voices concern that school administrators’ decisions about when to call SROs may reflect bias and disproportionately affect certain student groups, and asks how this might shape YSU’s enforcement data. YSU and the chief respond that while schools initiate many contacts, the police department retains independent authority over enforcement decisions and is open to adding demographic tracking to monitor impacts.
- Member Frank notes from personal experience that some school discipline practices appear to lean on certain student populations more than others and worries that SRO involvement may inherit those biases.
- He asks whether YSU tracks demographics for students contacted through enforcement and home visits and whether this is part of the current data set.
- Sergeant Shen replies that demographic data is not yet automatically captured but can be derived manually from case records, and he identifies building demographic tracking into future systems as an important improvement area.
- Chief Canfield underscores that MVPD does not simply execute school preferences; if patterns emerge that conflict with city ethical standards, the department can and will intervene, including by raising concerns with district leadership.
- Frank reiterates that since these are negative interactions for youth, understanding which groups are most affected is essential and urges the department to make the experience as constructive as possible for impacted students.

#### Mental Health Crisis Response subcommittee and TRUST/MCRT coordination
The Mental Health Crisis Response subcommittee provides a verbal update on its work supporting implementation of the county’s TRUST program, the Mobile Crisis Response Team (MCRT), and 988/behavioral health services. They report ongoing engagement with TRUST leadership and upcoming presentations to PSAB, while the chief outlines efforts to better integrate 988 and 911 dispatch processes for mental health calls. This work aims to increase appropriate non-police responses to crises.

- Assistant City Manager Rampart emphasizes that Mountain View has taken a leadership role in supporting alternative crisis response—through PSAB’s work, federal earmark funding for TRUST, and regular county coordination—and that the state’s current efforts mirror regional work that Mountain View and neighboring cities had already begun.
#### Dispatch integration challenges between 988 and 911
The chief provides more technical detail on the obstacles to seamless routing between 988 and local 911 dispatch, which currently limit real-time coordination. Because 988 only receives the caller’s area code, it cannot reliably determine or transfer local calls, complicating efforts to triage mental health crises away from police. Tri-City police departments are pausing their locally designed integration plan pending state-level technology and policy upgrades.
- Chief Canfield explains that calls to the national 988 number are routed to county call centers based on information entered by callers, but the center only reliably receives the phone’s area code rather than precise location.
- He notes that this design prevents 988 from transferring calls directly to Mountain View’s 911 center with accurate location data and likewise prevents MVPD dispatch from handing calls off to 988 with full call metadata.
- He reports that Mountain View, Palo Alto, and Los Altos jointly use a shared dispatch system and had been developing procedures to transfer non-violent, non-weapons, non-medical mental health calls from 911 to 988 via a dedicated law enforcement line.
- Because the state is now developing standardized guidance and upgraded capabilities for 988 centers, the tri-city group is pausing local procedure development to avoid building systems that will soon be obsolete or inconsistent with state standards.
- In response to Member Schauer, the chief confirms that they are effectively in a waiting period for state direction on data and funding, but he reiterates that if the state process becomes too slow or entangled, the cities remain motivated to resume local solutions.

- He frames this as an opportunity to ensure “the right tool for the job” across the full spectrum of calls, not only mental health crises.
- The board does not directly debate this broader proposal during the meeting but acknowledges the value of the ongoing mental health routing work.
