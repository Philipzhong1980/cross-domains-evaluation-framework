### Overview
  The City Council holds a special meeting to interview seven candidates for two four-year seats on the Environmental Planning Commission (EPC), piloting Zoom automated captioning and following Council Policy K2 for a randomized round-robin interview format. The Council adjusts the first question to ask incumbents what they have contributed and non-incumbents what they would bring, then hears one-minute introductions and two-minute answers on the General Plan and on balancing private property rights with community benefits. Candidates emphasize a mix of local experience, environmental expertise, housing and small-business perspectives, and consistent support for robust public comment; several recommend targeted updates to the General Plan or better alignment between precise plans and zoning. Councilmembers raise a desire for a Commission training '101' to clarify EPC advisory roles and hybrid meeting procedures, and after deliberation the Council recommends reappointing incumbent Bill Cranston and appointing Jose Gutierrez to the two available seats, with staff scheduled to bring a formal resolution to the Council on December 13.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Meeting setup and notices
  The mayor opens the special City Council meeting and explains procedures including a pilot of Zoom automated captioning and public comment options. He reads the statutory announcement required under California Government Code Section 54953(e) and provides instructions for accessing captions and the live YouTube stream. The City Clerk takes roll call and confirms a quorum with one councilmember absent.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez announces the City's pilot use of Zoom automated captioning and instructs attendees how to enable and configure captions.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez reads the meeting's legal authorization under California Government Code Section 54953(e).
- City Clerk Heather Glaser performs roll call and confirms a quorum with Council Member Schwalter absent.
#### Interview process rules
Council reviews the Environmental Planning Commission (EPC) interview format, which follows Council Policy K2 and uses a randomized round-robin with equal time limits per question. The proposed time allocations are one minute for question one and two minutes each for questions two and three. Council discusses fairness about asking incumbents the same questions and agrees to a wording modification that asks incumbents what they have contributed and non-incumbents what they would bring.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez explains interviews will follow Council Policy K2 with randomized round-robin order and equal time per question.
- Council Member Lisa Matichak recommends asking incumbents a substituted question focused on their contributions rather than a general 'tell us about yourself' prompt.
- City Clerk Heather Glaser confirms the policy generally requires asking the same questions to all applicants and notes adding clarifying follow-ups is acceptable.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez records a consensus to modify question one to ask incumbents what they have personally contributed and non-incumbents what they would uniquely bring.
#### Applicant introductions
Seven applicants each provide one-minute statements about what they would contribute to the EPC, emphasizing a mix of local experience, professional backgrounds, and perspectives. The candidates highlight community involvement, technical expertise, small-business and landlord viewpoints, environmental stewardship, and interests in affordable housing and active transportation. All applicants complete their initial responses and thank the Council for the opportunity to apply.
- Anne Cavanaugh says she brings 22 years as a Mountain View resident, downtown landlord experience, and a talent for perspective-taking.
- Bill Cranston says he brings a small-business owner perspective focused on keeping employees in the area and staying current on state law.
- Paul Donahue says he brings 16 years of civic advisory experience and nine years of land-use experience on an airport land-use commission.
- Jose Gutierrez says he brings over 22 years in the community, experience promoting affordable housing partnerships, and consensus-building skills.
- Celia Paymer says she brings 15 years as a resident, experience as an environmental science teacher, and hands-on knowledge of active-transportation infrastructure.
- Molly Smith says she brings an environmental studies degree, recent relocation experience, and commuter perspective after two years in the area.
- Li (Lee) Zhang says she brings 20-plus years living in Mountain View, a PhD in chemistry, and a strong focus on sustainability and outreach.
#### Views on General Plan
Applicants answer a two-minute question about the city's General Plan and precise plans, with common themes that the General Plan contains strong goals but may need targeted updates for post-COVID conditions and implementation gaps. Several applicants recommend focused amendments or aligning zoning and precise plans rather than a full rework, while others emphasize ensuring new developments create complete neighborhoods with services, parks, and transportation. Concerns include implementation of the North Bayshore precise plan and the need for clarity on zoning and displacement strategies.
- Bill Cranston says the General Plan set change-area expectations and that some precise plans have had significant impacts while others like North Bayshore have not yet realized expected benefits.
- Jose Gutierrez supports revising the General Plan to reflect changed needs and strengthen the city's negotiating position with developers.
- Molly Smith says the General Plan should be reviewed because COVID changed development needs and goals over a short time frame.
- Li (Lee) Zhang cautions that reworking the General Plan is a multi-year effort and stresses aligning zoning and precise plans to implement the housing element.
- Celia Paymer praises the General Plan's themes on sustainability and quality of life and urges focusing on implementation that supports active transportation and park space.
- Paul Donahue prefers targeted amendments over a full rewrite and emphasizes designing new neighborhoods to be complete with services, parks, and transit.
#### Balancing property rights
Applicants respond to how they would balance private property rights with community benefits and treat public comment, with most emphasizing strong public engagement and a generally community-oriented leaning as density increases. Respondents note legal constraints such as state density bonus rules and the need to be fair to property owners while pursuing shared public amenities like open space. Candidates consistently underscore listening to public comment and incorporating neighborhood perspectives into deliberations.
- Paul Donahue emphasizes listening to public comment, leaning toward public open space when it benefits the whole community.
- Celia Paymer says she leans toward public benefit as density increases and stresses respecting public speakers' time and thought.
- Li (Lee) Zhang commits to broadening outreach to ensure a diverse set of public comments rather than comments from a single group.
- Anne Cavanaugh advocates balancing both private and public interests and values public comment as a source of new perspectives.
- Jose Gutierrez highlights the democratic process of listening and negotiating and describes the EPC role as identifying community benefits the city can seek from developers.
- Molly Smith stresses protecting private property rights while urging that new developments retain airflow, greenery, and internal open spaces.
- Bill Cranston supports property owners using existing rules but says the Council and EPC should review and update rules when they no longer make sense.
#### Interview close and logistics
Council concludes the interview portion, thanks applicants, and explains that applicants may remain during deliberation but will be contacted by staff after the selection process. The mayor reminds the council that two four-year EPC seats are available and that the selection process can be changed by majority vote. Applicants are informed that the City Clerk's office will follow up after the Council makes recommendations.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez thanks all applicants for participating and notes that the Council will now deliberate and recommend appointments.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez states that two four-year terms are available and that tie votes will require revotes on tied applicants.
- City Clerk Heather Glaser states staff will contact applicants at the conclusion of the process and that a formal resolution will follow.
#### Council deliberation and training
Council members discuss procedural and administrative improvements for the EPC, notably proposing a Commission training '101' to clarify advisory roles, questions, and hybrid/hands-on meeting logistics. Several members express support for better onboarding and clarity about EPC's advisory scope and how hybrid participation will work. The discussion sets a tone of wanting to strengthen EPC effectiveness and community outreach.
- Council Member Ellen Kamei proposes a Commission or board 'training 101' to answer procedural questions and clarify the EPC's advisory role.
- Vice Mayor Alison Hicks and other council members express support for Kamei's proposal for training and clearer hybrid meeting procedures.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez emphasizes process adherence and signals preference to follow the established selection method unless the Council votes to change it.
#### Voting and appointment outcome
The Council conducts sequential voting and discusses candidate preferences, ultimately recommending reappointment of incumbent Bill Cranston and appointing Jose Gutierrez to the second available four-year seat. Councilmembers explain votes based on incumbency, demonstrated service, subject-matter expertise, representation, and outreach to underrepresented communities. The Council instructs staff that a formal resolution confirming the appointments will be brought to the December 13 meeting.
- Vice Mayor Alison Hicks moves to reappoint Bill Cranston and expresses support for Celia Paymer as well, noting Cranston's thoughtful public participation.
- Multiple council members, including Margaret Abekoga and Ellen Kamei, state votes for Bill Cranston based on his incumbency and demonstrated contributions.
- Council Members split on the second seat but ultimately a majority support Jose Gutierrez and the Council recommends him for appointment.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez states his votes support incumbents when performance is strong and emphasizes the value of representation from R3-zoned residents.
- City Clerk Heather Glaser confirms that a formal resolution to reappoint Bill Cranston and Jose Gutierrez will be presented to the Council on December 13.
