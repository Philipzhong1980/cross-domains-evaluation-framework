### Overview
The Mountain View City Council held a special meeting to interview seven applicants for two four-year seats on the Environmental Planning Commission (EPC), first adjusting its standard interview questions so that both incumbents and new applicants could describe their unique contributions under a uniform, policy-compliant format. Applicants then answered three core questions about their backgrounds and value to the EPC, their views of the city’s General Plan and precise plans in light of COVID-era changes and the Housing Element, and how they would balance private property rights, community benefits, and public comment; common themes included support for environmentally sustainable, transit-oriented and walkable development, careful alignment of plans and zoning, and deep respect for diverse resident input. During deliberations, Council Member Ellen Kamei called for future boards-and-commissions training and clearer guidance on EPC’s advisory role, while Vice Mayor Alison Hicks and others praised the overall strength of the applicant pool. Adhering to the Council’s prescribed tally-based selection method, members cast two rounds of votes: the first established strong majority support to reappoint incumbent chair Bill Cranston, and the second produced a majority for reappointing incumbent commissioner Jose Gutierrez, with Silja (Celia) Paymer and Paul Donahue also receiving notable support. City Clerk Heather Glaser explained that a formal resolution to confirm Cranston and Gutierrez will return at a subsequent regular meeting, and Mayor Lucas Ramirez closed by thanking all applicants, noting that some are being recommended for other advisory bodies, and adjourning the session.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Interview process and question adjustments
Mayor Lucas Ramirez opens the special City Council meeting to conduct Environmental Planning Commission (EPC) applicant interviews and outlines the standardized panel format, timing, and fairness rules. Council discusses whether incumbents should receive different questions, but the City Clerk reminds them that policy requires the same questions and time for all candidates. To accommodate both incumbents and new applicants, Council Member Lisa Matichak proposes modifying the first question to focus on each applicant’s unique contributions to or potential value for the EPC. The council reaches consensus on the revised wording and maintains the original time allocations for all three questions.
- Mayor Ramirez explains that all seven EPC applicants will be interviewed in a panel format under Council Policy K-2, with identical questions and time limits for each candidate.
- Council Member Matichak states that asking incumbents to simply "tell us about yourself and the role of the EPC" feels redundant and seeks a more contribution-focused question.
- City Clerk Heather Glaser advises that the adopted policy requires the same questions and timing for all candidates, but suggests adding or amending wording to cover both incumbents and new applicants.
- Council Member Matichak proposes revising Question 1 to ask what incumbents have personally contributed to the EPC and what unique contributions non-incumbents would bring.
- Mayor Ramirez confirms consensus to modify Question 1 as suggested and keeps the one-minute allocation for that question and the existing two-minute allocations for Questions 2 and 3.
#### Applicant contributions and perspectives (Question 1)
Each applicant answers the modified first question, describing their background and what they have contributed or would contribute uniquely to the EPC. Themes include long-term residency in Mountain View, professional and lived experience with housing, business, and environmental issues, and skills in consensus-building, governance, and technical subject matter. The answers establish a benchmark of subject-area familiarity and community commitment that Council later cites during deliberations. This segment sets a clear contrast among candidates’ perspectives: small landlord, small business owner, environmental educator, commuter, parent, minority community advocate, and scientist.
- Anne Cavanaugh explains that as a 22-year downtown resident and small landlord, she brings dual perspectives as both tenant-oriented resident and property owner, along with a talent for balancing multiple constituencies’ needs.
- Bill Cranston states that he contributes a small business owner’s perspective, focusing on employees who earn below-market or moderate incomes and on staying current with state legislation that affects Mountain View land use.
- Paul Donahue cites 16 years of city advisory service and nine years on the County Airport Land Use Commission, emphasizing his passion for good governance, land use experience, and attention to detail.
- Jose Gutierrez highlights his 22-plus years in Mountain View, his work on boards, his advocacy for affordable housing projects and partnerships, and his ability to bridge strong differing viewpoints to help Council make governance decisions.
- Silja (Celia) Paymer notes her 15 years in Mountain View, background as an environmental science teacher, daily bike commuting, parenting experience, and insight into city–school interactions as unique contributions she would bring.
- Molly Smith identifies as a newer resident with an environmental studies degree, rail commuter experience, and interest in applying her environmental knowledge and getting more involved in local planning.
- Li (Lee) Zhang emphasizes over 20 years living on both sides of El Camino, raising a family in Mountain View, her PhD in chemistry, strong sustainability orientation, and desire to help remove constraints to housing development while engaging the community.
#### Views on General Plan and precise plans (Question 2)
Applicants provide their assessments of Mountain View’s General Plan and various precise plans, with many agreeing that the existing framework is generally strong but needs updating or better alignment with current realities. Several cite the age of the 2012 General Plan, the impact of COVID-19 on land use patterns, and the partial implementation of major precise plans such as North Bayshore, El Camino, San Antonio, and downtown. A key tension emerges between those favoring a substantial General Plan revision and those warning against reopening such a major effort, instead advocating targeted amendments and careful implementation of precise plans and the Housing Element.
- Bill Cranston states that the General Plan’s change areas and precise plans (Bayshore, San Antonio, El Camino, East Whisman) have significantly shaped city development, but notes that the plan predates recent economic cycles and COVID, leaving some areas potentially ripe for re-examination and additional precise plans.
- Bill Cranston points out that North Bayshore has not yet delivered its intended benefits and argues that downtown planning, including issues highlighted by the Sobrato project, needs particular attention given the age of prior plans.
- Jose Gutierrez argues that the General Plan is outdated given changing needs and COVID impacts, and urges that Council undertake a revision to strengthen the city’s negotiating position with developers and close loopholes in areas such as the Downtown Precise Plan.
- Jose Gutierrez references EPC support for a parking cap in the Downtown Precise Plan as an example of creating time and space for Council to revisit critical policy choices and avoid repeating problematic situations like the Sobrato case.
- Molly Smith, acknowledging she is still learning, supports a review of the General Plan because societal and economic conditions have shifted drastically, especially since COVID, making pre-pandemic assumptions feel misaligned with current needs.
- Li Zhang notes that the 2012 General Plan is visionary but misaligned in places with current precise plans and zoning, citing a village shopping center site whose zoning remains commercial despite a mixed-use designation in the General Plan.
- Li Zhang cautions that a full General Plan update could take three to four years and require significant resources, and instead emphasizes aligning zoning and precise plans with the General Plan and providing Council direction on R3 upzoning and displacement strategy to fulfill Housing Element obligations.
- Silja (Celia) Paymer supports the themes of the General Plan around quality of life, sustainability, active transportation, parks, and health, but observes conflicts between projects, precise plans, and overarching goals that require careful design-stage integration.
- Silja (Celia) Paymer says she is excited by the potential redevelopment of neighborhood centers like Blossom into denser and more walkable spaces, which she views as currently not very walkable.
- Paul Donahue opposes fully reopening the General Plan due to the heavy lift of the last four-year update, instead favoring targeted amendments and leveraging transit-oriented precise plans such as El Camino and downtown around the train station.
- Paul Donahue stresses that new neighborhoods like North Bayshore must be complete communities with services, transportation, schools, and parks rather than isolated housing enclaves separated by Highway 101.
- Paul Donahue highlights the importance of implementing the Housing Element update and notes rising concerns about the state’s Builders Remedy, which could affect local control over land use.
- Anne Cavanaugh praises the General and precise plans for emphasizing environmental sustainability and demographic diversity, and argues that implementation challenges reflect land scarcity and changing circumstances rather than flawed planning goals.
- Anne Cavanaugh credits the EPC with adapting plan implementation to evolving conditions, such as COVID’s impact on downtown and the idea of a pedestrian mall, while continuing to use the plans as a guiding framework.
#### Balancing property rights, community benefits, and public comment (Question 3)
In the final question, applicants discuss how they would weigh private property rights against community-wide benefits and how they approach public comment. Several emphasize the importance of ensuring property owners can rely on existing rules while also using community benefit negotiations when projects seek extra entitlements. Others lean toward prioritizing public benefits—especially shared open space and parks—as density increases. All candidates underscore that public comment is critical, both for legitimacy and for surfacing perspectives that decision-makers might otherwise miss, though some note the need to broaden whose voices are heard.
- Paul Donahue says public comment is essential because residents must feel heard and respected even when not everyone gets their preferred outcome.
- Paul Donahue indicates that, when weighing private and public benefits, he tends to favor public open space over private open space because it benefits the entire community rather than just one development.
- Silja (Celia) Paymer states that in a denser city she leans toward public benefit, arguing that shared public and semi-public spaces become increasingly important as fewer residents have private yards.
- Silja (Celia) Paymer references the El Camino bike lane and parking debate as an example of friction when some residents view public streetside parking as private, and she stresses listening to all sides while still reclaiming space for broader public use where appropriate.
- Silja (Celia) Paymer emphasizes that public commenters invest considerable time and thought, and she pledges to take their input seriously and look for common ground, even when she ultimately disagrees.
- Li (Lee) Zhang observes that housing element public comments often come from a narrow set of voices and expresses a desire to proactively reach out to more diverse communities so people do not feel excluded after decisions are made.
- Li (Lee) Zhang acknowledges limited familiarity with some technical aspects of the public–private balance but notes issues such as shared condo space and schoolyard access, indicating a need for more study.
- Anne Cavanaugh argues that both private rights and public benefits must be prioritized in a balanced way, recognizing owners’ investment-backed expectations while ensuring sufficient shared spaces for non-owners.
- Anne Cavanaugh reiterates that public comment consistently brings new perspectives and impact considerations she had not previously thought of, which she views as vital to sound decision-making.
- Jose Gutierrez frames public input as central to democratic local government, sharing that residents approach him on Castro Street and he values listening deeply even when he must agree to disagree.
- Jose Gutierrez explains that EPC’s role is to identify how much community benefit—such as additional units, parkland, or green space—can reasonably be secured from each project within the constraints of applicable laws.
- Molly Smith affirms the importance of hearing all perspectives, including those she might not like, and stresses preserving breaks, airflow, and plantings within new developments to enhance livability and visual appeal even when adding density.
- Molly Smith cites a nearby redevelopment on Middlefield that removed large trees and urges that future projects maintain internal open spaces and greenery despite high-density goals.
- Bill Cranston contends that property owners should be able to rely on the city’s existing rules and pursue projects consistent with those rules, with public-benefit tradeoffs primarily arising when projects seek to exceed current zoning or when the city reevaluates outdated standards.
- Bill Cranston notes that state density bonus law constrains how much community benefit cities can demand, since requirements perceived as impairing project feasibility may be prohibited, necessitating a fair and reasonable approach.
- Bill Cranston values both in-meeting testimony and written comments, particularly highlighting that League of Women Voters letters often provide thought-provoking analysis that informs his deliberations.
#### Council reflections on EPC operations and training
After the applicant interviews, the Council transitions to deliberations and uses the opportunity to reflect briefly on how the EPC functions. Council Member Ellen Kamei notes observations from watching recent EPC meetings and suggests that commissioners would benefit from structured training and clearer guidance on their advisory role. She and others also anticipate EPC’s eventual transition to hybrid meetings and link that change to broader discussions around public comment processes and expectations. These reflections provide context for the Council’s desire to support, not just select, EPC commissioners.
- Council Member Kamei reports that after reviewing multiple EPC meetings, she sees value in developing a Commission or Board Training 101 to clarify roles, expectations, and procedural questions.
- Council Member Kamei recalls that both the City Clerk and City Manager previously mentioned training, and she expresses interest in pursuing it, particularly as EPC transitions from Zoom-only to in-person or hybrid formats.
- Council Member Kamei notes potential ambiguity for EPC in whether to base recommendations strictly on commission-level judgment or on anticipating what Council needs, and she suggests a training could better define this advisory function.
- Council Member Kamei connects potential changes in hybrid public comment procedures at Council with likely similar changes at EPC, emphasizing the need to manage this shift carefully.
#### Council voting methodology and process clarification
As deliberations begin, Vice Mayor Alison Hicks attempts to appoint candidates via a traditional motion and second, but Mayor Ramirez and the City Clerk remind the Council that existing policy prescribes a tally-based voting method. The Council decides to adhere to the established process, using multiple voting rounds to identify candidates with majority support. This preserves procedural consistency and avoids ad hoc changes that could complicate the outcome or appear unfair.
- Vice Mayor Hicks initially proposes a motion to appoint Bill Cranston and Celia Paymer based on her evaluation of their qualifications and past contributions.
- Mayor Ramirez reminds the Council that the EPC appointment policy specifies a structured tally-based voting process, and that any deviation would require majority support to change the procedure mid-meeting.
- City Clerk Heather Glaser advises that if Council wishes to depart from the policy, they should at least confirm majority support for altering the process, but leaves the decision to the Council.
- Mayor Ramirez expresses a preference to retain the existing tally method to avoid multiple overlapping motions and potential confusion, and Vice Mayor Hicks agrees to treat her earlier remarks as comments while the Council uses the prescribed process.
#### Council evaluation of candidates and first-round voting
Council members each articulate their reasoning and then cast first-round votes for two candidates, with strong consensus emerging around incumbent Chair Bill Cranston and diverging preferences for the second seat. Many emphasize experience, question quality, commitment to public input, and representation of underrepresented communities. The first round yields majority support for reappointing Bill Cranston but splits the second seat primarily among Jose Gutierrez, Celia (Silja) Paymer, and Paul Donahue, prompting a second round focused on the remaining top contenders.
- Council Member Margaret Abe-Koga follows her tradition of reappointing incumbents who have done a good job and votes for Bill Cranston and Jose Gutierrez, noting the overall high quality of the applicant pool.
- Council Member Kamei supports both incumbents, praising Bill Cranston’s unprecedented back-to-back EPC chairmanship during the pandemic and Jose Gutierrez’s leadership through the Spanish Language Leadership Academy and outreach to underrepresented communities.
- Council Member Sally Lieber emphasizes that many applicants show a strong public service ethic, particularly around valuing public comment, and casts her votes for Bill Cranston and Silja (Celia) Paymer.
- Council Member Lisa Matichak notes that she frequently watches EPC meetings and appreciates Cranston’s thoughtful, well-informed questions, prompting her to support him along with Paul Donahue, whose detailed land use understanding she observed on the Airport Land Use Commission.
- Council Member Pat Showalter remarks on the high caliber of candidates and her positive conversations with all applicants, then uses her first-round votes for Silja (Celia) Paymer and Paul Donahue.
- Vice Mayor Hicks reiterates that she sees this as a strong field but maintains her support for reappointing Bill Cranston and appointing Silja (Celia) Paymer, citing Paymer’s extensive engagement across Council, DRC, green space, and other meetings.
- Mayor Ramirez states a general philosophy of reappointing incumbents who are performing well and notes Chair Cranston’s long-standing contributions as a neighborhood leader and EPC member, leading him to support Cranston.
- Mayor Ramirez explains that he supports reappointing Jose Gutierrez partly because he lives in R3-zoned housing, aligning with major upcoming R3 policy work, and because Jose brings important Latino representation and Spanish-language skills to a historically underrepresented commission.
- After tallying first-round votes, Mayor Ramirez announces that Bill Cranston has clear majority support for reappointment and that a second round is needed to decide the remaining four-year EPC seat.
#### Runoff voting for second EPC seat and outcome
With one seat secured for Bill Cranston, the Council conducts a second voting round to choose the remaining four-year EPC appointee. The runoff focuses on Jose Gutierrez and Silja (Celia) Paymer, who have attracted significant but not majority support. After the second tally, Jose Gutierrez secures the majority, confirming both incumbents for reappointment. The City Clerk explains that a formal resolution will follow at a later regular meeting, and the Mayor closes by thanking applicants and confirming next steps.
- In the second voting round, Council Member Abe-Koga reaffirms her support for Jose Gutierrez for the remaining EPC seat.
- Council Member Kamei again votes for Jose Gutierrez, reinforcing her earlier comments about his community outreach and perspective.
- Council Member Lieber maintains her support for Silja (Celia) Paymer in the runoff, continuing to view Paymer as a strong candidate.
- Council Member Matichak shifts to or maintains support for Jose Gutierrez, giving him additional momentum in the second round.
- Council Member Showalter casts her runoff vote for Silja (Celia) Paymer, indicating ongoing council interest in Paymer’s service even though it does not reach a majority.
- Vice Mayor Hicks continues to support Silja (Celia) Paymer in the second round, consistent with her earlier statements.
- Mayor Ramirez again votes for Jose Gutierrez, citing his earlier reasons related to performance, R3 residency, and representation, resulting in a majority for Jose.
- City Clerk Heather Glaser clarifies that since this is a study session, no formal motion is required tonight, and that a resolution to reappoint Bill Cranston and Jose Gutierrez will be brought forward at the December 13 regular meeting.
- Mayor Ramirez reiterates his appreciation for all applicants, notes that some, including Molly Smith and Anne Cavanaugh, are being recommended for other advisory bodies, and confirms that Council will finalize EPC appointments alongside other board and commission appointments at a future meeting.
- Mayor Ramirez adjourns the special meeting at 6:38 PM and announces the next regular City Council meeting date.
