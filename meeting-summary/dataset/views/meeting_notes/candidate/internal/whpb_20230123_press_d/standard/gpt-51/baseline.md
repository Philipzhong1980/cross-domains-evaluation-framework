### Overview
The call, led by White House spokesperson Ian Sams with multiple national reporters, centers on the Biden classified documents investigation, the administration’s cooperation with the Department of Justice, and its posture toward congressional and media scrutiny. Sams opens by highlighting a new White House Counsel letter to House Oversight Chair James Comer that stresses the White House no longer possesses the documents at issue, affirms respect for DOJ’s independent investigation, and pledges to accommodate legitimate oversight requests while protecting investigative integrity and separation of powers. He repeatedly underscores that President Biden’s personal lawyers, acting at Biden’s direction, voluntarily and proactively offered DOJ a thorough, 13-hour “planned consensual search” of the Wilmington residence, during which agents accessed every room and removed six items containing classified-marked documents and related materials from Biden’s Senate and vice-presidential years, as well as handwritten notes. Throughout extensive questioning from outlets including CBS, AP, Bloomberg, ABC, NPR, Politico, and others, Sams refuses to specify total document or page counts, the physical form of the materials, or details about potential future searches of other locations, consistently directing reporters to DOJ and Biden’s personal attorneys and explaining that the legal team promptly turns material over without reviewing it to avoid influencing the investigation. He also delineates the separate roles of the White House Counsel’s Office and the President’s personal lawyers, defends the administration’s level of transparency and non-televised briefings as appropriate under investigative constraints, and notes that key Democrats like Senator Dick Durbin have emphasized Biden’s responsible cooperation even as some criticism mounts. Sams closes by reaffirming that the White House is in communication with House Oversight and intends to work in good faith, all while maintaining that DOJ controls investigative decisions and that President Biden will continue advancing his policy agenda alongside full legal cooperation.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### White House letter to House Oversight
Ian Sams opens by describing a new White House Counsel letter to House Oversight Chairman James Comer responding to his demands for documents related to the Biden classified documents matter. He explains that the White House emphasizes it does not physically possess the records now held by the National Archives and the Department of Justice and underscores the need to protect the integrity of ongoing law enforcement investigations. The administration signals it will engage with the committee and seek to accommodate legitimate oversight interests while observing constitutional and investigative constraints. This frames the administration’s posture toward congressional scrutiny as cooperative but bounded by legal obligations.
- Mr. Sams says the White House Counsel sent a letter to Chairman Comer explaining that the White House does not have possession of the documents now held by the Archives and DOJ.
- Mr. Sams notes the letter stresses the importance of protecting the integrity and independence of ongoing law enforcement investigations.
- Mr. Sams states that the response is consistent with President Biden’s stated willingness to work in good faith with Congress.
- Mr. Sams explains that the White House will engage in conversations with the House Oversight Committee to determine next steps and seek to accommodate legitimate oversight interests.
#### DOJ search of Wilmington residence
Sams details the voluntary DOJ search of President Biden’s Wilmington home, emphasizing that it was initiated by the President’s team and that DOJ has characterized it as a “planned consensual search.” He explains that the search lasted about 13 hours, covered every room, and included decades of personal papers, handwritten notes, and memorabilia. DOJ removed additional materials, including items with classified markings and related documents, which are now in its custody. Sams repeatedly uses this episode to illustrate Biden’s proactive cooperation and seriousness in ensuring government records are returned.
- Mr. Sams says the President’s personal lawyer publicly disclosed that DOJ conducted a voluntary search of the Wilmington residence on Friday that lasted about 13 hours.
- Mr. Sams emphasizes that the President offered DOJ access to his home, describing the search as voluntary and unprecedented for a sitting President’s personal residence.
- Mr. Sams notes that DOJ described the operation as a “planned consensual search” and confirms agents had access to every room and decades’ worth of personal materials.
- Mr. Sams explains that DOJ identified and took additional material from the home and that the President’s team waited to disclose the search until DOJ asked that it be completed.
