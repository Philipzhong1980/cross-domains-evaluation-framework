### Overview
The Council Neighborhoods Committee convenes a fully virtual neighborhood meeting focused largely on mobile home park issues, rent stabilization, and neighborhood infrastructure concerns, led by Chair Pat Showalter, Vice Mayor Alison Hicks, and staff from Public Works, Community Services, Housing and Neighborhood Services, Rent Stabilization, Transportation, Sustainability, Police, and the City Manager’s Office. Staff first outline the Community Emergency Response Team (CERT) program and the Mobile Home Rent Stabilization Ordinance (MHRSO), explaining how CERT builds volunteer disaster capacity and how the MHRSO stabilizes rents, protects tenants, and preserves park owners’ fair returns. During pre-submitted and live Q&A, Public Works Director Dawn Cameron and Traffic Engineer Lorenzo Lopez address repeated complaints about poor internet, submetered utilities, and unsafe traffic conditions near Sylvan Avenue, Highway 237, and Sahara Mobile Park’s El Camino Real driveway, while committing to AT&T outreach, site inspections, Caltrans coordination, and directing residents to the Neighborhood Traffic Management Program and Ask Mountain View portals. Community Services Director John Marchant and Police Captain Michael Canfield respond to concerns about Live Nation concert traffic and noise by describing existing private traffic management, tweaks made during the season, plans for postseason debriefs, and the availability of a dedicated noise hotline. Rent Stabilization Program Manager Anky van Deursen and Special Counsel Karen Tiedemann explain how the MHRSO’s CPI-based 2–5% annual rent adjustment, fair return petition process, and eviction protections work, underscore that changes to rent formulas or retroactivity are policy questions for elected officials, and, with mobile home advisor Martin Eichner, outline residents’ rights under state Mobilehome Residency Law to organize, meet with management, challenge billing practices, and potentially purchase their park, albeit with limited enforcement mechanisms. Additional topics include cannabis planting and consumption rules, solar feasibility on mobile homes (with a commitment to further research), city funding for CSA and Rebuilding Together, upcoming Safe Routes to School and bike safety events, and the consolidation of key resource links for rent stabilization, PG&E oversight, traffic complaints, and general city requests. The meeting closes with expressions of appreciation to residents and the Spanish interpreter, and with elected officials emphasizing the importance of continued resident engagement through the provided channels to shape city responses and improvements.

#### Forming homeowner associations and retaliation concerns
Residents ask how to form a homeowners association (HOA) in a park where the owner opposes it and some residents fear retaliation or eviction. Martin Eichner explains that the Mobilehome Residency Law (MRL) gives homeowners explicit rights to meet with park management individually or as an organized group, subject to 30 days’ written notice, and protects them from retaliation for exercising those rights. He adds that the MRL also allows residents to form a nonprofit to attempt to purchase the park if it is for sale, obligating the owner to negotiate with them. However, Eichner cautions that enforcement under the MRL is weak because it often requires residents to sue in court, though a new state Mobilehome Residency Protection Program may soon provide some complaint support. Karen Tiedemann adds that the MHRSO provides limited, cause-based eviction protections for mobile home occupants but does not contain explicit anti-harassment or anti-retaliation provisions beyond those in state law.
- Martin Eichner explains that the Mobilehome Residency Law guarantees mobile home owners the right to meet with park management individually or as an organized group with 30 days’ written notice.
- Martin Eichner notes that participation in HOA-type organizing and meetings with management is explicitly protected from retaliation under state law.
- Martin Eichner states that if a park is put up for sale, residents can form a nonprofit corporation and the park owner must negotiate in good faith with them about purchasing the park.
- Martin Eichner cautions that enforcement of the MRL usually depends on homeowners bringing civil court actions, which can be costly and complex, though a new Mobilehome Residency Protection Program may help route some complaints to legal aid.
- Karen Tiedemann clarifies that while the MHRSO provides eviction protections for mobile home occupants, it does not include explicit anti-harassment provisions; protections from retaliation primarily come from the MRL’s limits on allowable eviction grounds.

#### Submetered gas billing in mobile home parks
A resident asks why gas service at their mobile home park is submetered by park management instead of being billed directly by PG&E, and what rules govern that practice. Martin Eichner explains that under the Mobilehome Residency Law, parks have discretion to use master metering and submetering so long as residents pay only for utilities they actually use plus a fair share of common area costs. He emphasizes that park owners cannot add their own profit or unrelated costs into utility charges and must provide enough detail on bills so residents can verify that rates match those charged by the utility and that common-area allocations are reasonable. Eichner notes that if a resident believes they are being overcharged, they may need to challenge the park’s billing practices under state law.
- Martin Eichner explains that mobile home parks are allowed to use master meters and submetering for gas and other utilities under the Mobilehome Residency Law.
- Martin Eichner states that residents may be charged for their own usage plus a proportionate share of common area utility costs but cannot be billed for the park owner’s unrelated expenses or profit.
- Martin Eichner notes that utility bills must show the rates and components so residents can verify that charges match PG&E’s rates and fair allocations.
- Martin Eichner advises that overbilling concerns would need to be pursued under state mobile home law, potentially requiring residents to challenge the park’s practices.
