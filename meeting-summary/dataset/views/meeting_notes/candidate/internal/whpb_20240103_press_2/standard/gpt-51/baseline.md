### Overview
The briefing brings together National Security Council spokesman Admiral John Kirby and Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre to address escalating crises abroad and contentious issues at home. Kirby outlines an expanded U.S. military posture in the Middle East, emphasizing carrier and amphibious groups, missile defenses, and a 13-nation statement backing Operation Prosperity Guardian to counter Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping, while insisting these moves are defensive and aimed at preventing regional escalation, including in Lebanon and along Israel’s northern border. He repeatedly underscores U.S. support for Israel’s right to neutralize the threat from Hamas—by degrading its leadership and infrastructure rather than erasing its ideology—rejects South Africa’s genocide case against Israel as baseless, and confirms ongoing but currently stalled hostage negotiations; he also warns that Ukraine faces intensified Russian attacks even as U.S. Ukraine aid reaches the limits of current funding, and describes Russia’s mixed military position, combining munitions shortages with enduring manpower and hardware. Jean-Pierre then pivots to domestic policy, warning of an impending government shutdown driven, in the administration’s view, by House Republican inaction and impeachment “stunts” against DHS Secretary Mayorkas, even as bipartisan Senate talks seek a border security deal aligned with Biden’s broader immigration reform and supplemental funding request. She defends Vice President Harris’s role, criticizes Governor Abbott’s border razor wire and GOP votes to cut Border Patrol staffing, and frames record migration numbers as part of long-standing, smuggler-driven fluctuations requiring both policy change and resources. Throughout, she highlights Biden’s priorities for 2024—implementing major laws on infrastructure, chips, and climate; advancing his Unity Agenda; safeguarding democracy ahead of the next election—and contrasts those goals with Republican tax and spending plans she blames for most debt growth, all while fielding questions on issues from offshore wind and East Palestine rail safety to antisemitism on campus, the Menendez indictment, and concerns over bias-motivated violence such as the New Jersey imam shooting.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Impending government funding deadline
Karine Jean-Pierre opens substantive remarks by warning that parts of the U.S. government are 16 days away from a shutdown due to inaction by House Republicans. She argues that instead of staying in Washington to negotiate funding, House Republicans left town in December and are now focused on impeachment-related activities. The White House frames funding the government as Congress’s most basic duty and urges Republicans to honor prior bipartisan budget agreements. No concrete breakthrough or new proposal is described; the administration’s position is that Congress must return and do its job.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that the U.S. is just 16 days away from parts of the government shutting down and criticizes House Republicans for leaving Washington in mid-December.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says House Republicans are prioritizing what she calls baseless impeachment stunts rather than addressing government funding.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre emphasizes that funding the government is Congress’s basic duty and calls on House Republicans to get back to work and honor the bipartisan deal reached in 2023.
#### U.S. force posture in Middle East
Admiral John Kirby provides an extensive overview of U.S. military deployments and objectives in the Middle East since the Israel-Hamas war began. He underscores that the U.S. supports Israel’s self-defense, seeks to surge humanitarian aid to Gaza, protect U.S. interests, and safeguard commercial shipping, particularly in the Red Sea. Kirby details carrier and amphibious groups, aircraft, and missile defense assets deployed, stressing that the posture is meant to deter escalation, not start a wider war. He reiterates that while the U.S. does not seek conflict, it will defend its forces, partners, and the free flow of commerce.
- Mr. Kirby says President Biden received regular Middle East updates over the holidays from National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan and others, including on calls with Prime Minister Netanyahu.
- Mr. Kirby explains that the U.S. currently has the USS Dwight D. Eisenhower carrier strike group and an amphibious ready group led by USS Bataan, with more than 4,000 sailors, Marines, and over 50 aircraft in the Eastern Mediterranean.
- Mr. Kirby notes that U.S. deployments include additional fighter squadrons ashore and destroyers configured for ballistic missile defense, some operating in the Red Sea.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that the U.S. does not seek conflict or a wider Israel-Hamas war but will not shrink from defending U.S. interests, partners, or international commerce.
#### Red Sea Houthi attacks and coalition response
Kirby focuses on Houthi attacks on commercial shipping in the Red Sea and the U.S.-led response. He announces a new joint statement, now supported by 13 nations, condemning Houthi actions and demanding they cease, while also referencing Operation Prosperity Guardian, a multinational maritime security effort. He argues the Houthi threat endangers freedom of navigation and global trade and justifies the expanded naval presence as defensive, not escalatory. Questions about the smaller number of signatories compared to a prior statement lead him to stress that some states contribute quietly and that more governments are becoming alarmed as shipping reroutes around Africa.
- Mr. Kirby says U.S. and coalition warships and aircraft in the Red Sea provide strong air and missile defense, ISR, and offensive and defensive power to counter Houthi threats.
- Mr. Kirby announces a joint statement with 12 other countries (later including Singapore, bringing the total to 13) condemning Houthi attacks on Red Sea commercial vessels and demanding they stop.
- Mr. Kirby argues these Houthi actions directly threaten freedom of navigation and global trade and put innocent lives at risk.
- Mr. Kirby responds to concerns about fewer signatories than in a December statement by noting that some countries quietly contribute capabilities to Operation Prosperity Guardian without seeking public attribution.
- Mr. Kirby rejects the premise that U.S.-led Red Sea patrols are escalatory, asserting that the provocation comes from Houthi missile and drone attacks on merchant shipping using Iranian-supplied weapons.

Kirby says the U.S. has limited information on the Iran attack, offers condolences, and states there is no indication Israel was involved. On the Beirut strike, he refuses to confirm details, notes al-Arouri is a U.S.-designated global terrorist, and says no one should mourn his death if confirmed. Kirby reiterates that the U.S. is not assessing responsibility for the Beirut strike and frames U.S. deployments as aimed at preventing broader regional escalation.

- Mr. Kirby states that the U.S. has no indication that Israel was involved in the Iran bombing “in any way whatsoever.”
- Mr. Kirby notes that al-Arouri is a designated global terrorist and says that if he is indeed dead, no one should be shedding a tear over his loss.
- Mr. Kirby says he is not aware of U.S. efforts to assess who carried out the airstrike in Lebanon that killed al-Arouri and stresses the U.S. was not involved.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that recent U.S. force posture changes are designed to prevent escalation or widening of the conflict beyond Israel and Hamas.
#### Risk of regional escalation and Hezbollah posture

Asked about Hezbollah leader Hassan Nasrallah’s statement that his group is not afraid of war, Kirby downplays rhetoric and says the U.S. will judge by actions, noting Hezbollah has not fully entered the war on Hamas’s behalf. The administration continues private and public messaging to regional actors to avoid widening the conflict.

- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. will judge Hezbollah by its actions, not words, and notes that Hezbollah has not jumped in with both feet to assist Hamas.
- Mr. Kirby confirms the U.S. continues to message regional partners that widening the conflict is bad for the region and for the Israeli people.
#### Israeli campaign against Hamas and feasibility of ‘eradication’
Multiple reporters ask if Israel’s goal to eliminate Hamas is realistic and whether the U.S. endorses it. Kirby draws a distinction between destroying a group’s operational capacity and erasing its ideology or every last fighter. He says the U.S. believes it is militarily attainable for Israel to significantly degrade Hamas’s capabilities and neutralize its threat to Israelis by targeting leadership, infrastructure, and resources, citing U.S. experiences against ISIS and al-Qaeda. He declines to provide numeric estimates or a ‘report card’ on Israel’s progress but says Hamas’s command, resourcing, and leadership capacity have been notably degraded.
- Mr. Kirby says Israel has the right and responsibility to eliminate the threat Hamas poses to Israelis, while noting that only Israel should define its exact military goals.
- Mr. Kirby explains that targeting leadership, tunnels, command nodes, and infrastructure is similar to the U.S. approach against terrorist groups in Iraq and Afghanistan.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that although militaries can significantly degrade terrorist groups’ capabilities, they rarely eliminate the underlying ideology or every member, citing al-Qaeda and ISIS as examples.
- Mr. Kirby clarifies that the U.S. believes Israel can eradicate Hamas’s ability to conduct attacks against Israel, even if the ideology persists and the group is not erased from existence.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges Hamas retains a significant force posture in Gaza but says Israel has had a clear effect on Hamas’s ability to command, resource, and lead its fighters.
#### Hostages in Gaza and negotiations
Reporters seek updates on efforts to secure the release of remaining hostages held by Hamas. Kirby says talks, led by the U.S. in coordination with Qatar and others, remain active and substantive, though there is no new public progress to report. He notes that the U.S. retains hope and pursues the issue with the same energy that yielded a previous release of around 50 hostages. He avoids commenting on Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu’s reference to an alleged Hamas ultimatum and confirms U.S. envoys like Brett McGurk and David Satterfield remain engaged.
- Mr. Kirby says conversations on hostage releases are ongoing, real, and pursued with the same energy that previously freed about 50 hostages.
- Mr. Kirby notes there is no specific new progress to announce but insists the U.S. will not stop talking, working, or engaging partners on the hostage issue.
- Mr. Kirby confirms ongoing communications involving U.S. officials such as Brett McGurk and David Satterfield, though he does not detail their travel schedules.
- Mr. Kirby says he does not know what ultimatum Prime Minister Netanyahu was referring to when he mentioned Hamas and hostages.
#### Economic impact of Red Sea disruption
Kirby addresses questions about major shipping companies, such as Maersk, suspending or rerouting traffic away from the Red Sea due to Houthi attacks. He calls the Red Sea a vital global trade corridor and notes that rerouting ships around the Cape of Good Hope adds weeks and costs, and poses additional risks. He says the U.S. has not yet seen a direct pocketbook impact on American consumers but acknowledges the global economic risk if the threat persists or intensifies. This uncertainty underlines the strategic importance of Operation Prosperity Guardian and coalition maritime defense.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges concern over shipping companies pausing or rerouting around the Red Sea, citing added weeks to voyages and higher costs when going around the Cape of Good Hope.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. has not yet observed a specific effect on the U.S. economy or inflation from Red Sea disruptions but warns that continued threats could affect the global economy.
- Mr. Kirby frames Operation Prosperity Guardian as evidence that the U.S. takes the threat to commerce seriously, involving more than 20 nations protecting shipping.

No indication is given that this filing will alter U.S. support for Israel or bilateral relations with South Africa beyond rhetorical disagreement.

On judicial reform, he avoids commenting on internal Israeli politics but reiterates President Biden’s longstanding view that any governance changes in Israel should be pursued with broad public consensus in a vibrant democracy.

#### Ukraine war situation and U.S. aid funding gap
Kirby provides an update on the war in Ukraine, describing continued Russian missile and drone barrages, including against civilian infrastructure, as an attempt to ‘weaponize winter.’ He criticizes portrayals of Vladimir Putin as ready to negotiate, arguing Russia’s actions signal the opposite. Kirby underscores that the U.S. has just issued its last security assistance package under current funding and that new supplemental appropriations from Congress are essential to sustain Ukraine’s defense. He later details Russia’s depleted munitions, reliance on Iranian drones and foreign shells, and ongoing structural weaknesses, but warns Russia still retains substantial air, ground, naval, and manpower capacity.
- Mr. Kirby says Russia continues to launch drones and ballistic missiles at both military and civilian targets in Ukraine, especially infrastructure, to weaponize winter.
- Mr. Kirby argues Putin’s continued attacks contradict media narratives that he is eager to negotiate.
- Mr. Kirby confirms that the U.S. has used up its existing replenishment authority and that the December 27 package was the last security assistance to Ukraine absent new supplemental funding.
- Mr. Kirby warns that without a supplemental, shipments of weapons to Ukraine would have to stop once the last package is delivered, as there is no other ‘magical pot’ of funds.
- Mr. Kirby notes Russia has suffered significant losses of munitions and relies on foreign supplies, including Iranian-designed drones, but still has sizable air, ground, naval, and manpower advantages.
- Mr. Kirby points out persistent Russian weaknesses in command, control, morale, and leadership, stating Russia has achieved little through its own touted offensives in recent months.

He defers detailed accounting of losses and Ukrainian capabilities to Kyiv.

- Mr. Kirby notes that many weapons are transferred to Ukrainian troops and quickly expended in combat, making detailed public accounting of every item difficult.

#### Debt, spending, and Republican criticism
Confronted with questions about rapid recent debt growth and whether spending cuts are on the table, Jean-Pierre reiterates that Republican tax cuts are the primary historical driver of debt, repeating prior statistics. She highlights Biden’s deficit-reduction measures and attacks GOP plans she labels ‘MAGAnomics’ for proposing further tax cuts that she says would add $3 trillion to the debt. She points to actions like taking on ‘Big Pharma’ to cut drug costs as examples of fiscally responsible policymaking. She does not discuss new spending cuts or entitlement reforms, instead focusing the debate on revenue and distributional choices.

#### Border razor wire, impeachment, and House Speaker relations
Jean-Pierre returns to the razor wire dispute and clarifies that its primary problem is operational, making it harder for Border Patrol to apprehend or assist migrants, rather than purely a safety issue for agents themselves. Asked why Biden has not yet held a one-on-one meeting with new House Speaker Mike Johnson, she points to Johnson’s track record of treating the border as a political issue and obstructing solutions. She contrasts this with what she calls productive bipartisan work in the Senate and declines to announce any planned Biden–Johnson meeting, effectively signaling that the White House is prioritizing Senate negotiations over direct engagement with House leadership on immigration.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre clarifies that Governor Abbott’s razor wire primarily hampers Border Patrol’s ability to apprehend migrants or provide assistance rather than directly injuring agents, though it still complicates operations.

- Ms. Jean-Pierre offers no timeline for a Biden–Johnson one-on-one meeting, pointing instead to the broader ‘playing field’ of current negotiations and conflicts.
