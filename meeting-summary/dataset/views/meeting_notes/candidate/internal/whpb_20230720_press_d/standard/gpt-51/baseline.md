### Overview
During an in-flight press gaggle en route to Philadelphia, White House spokesperson Olivia Dalton previews President Biden’s visit to the Philly Shipyard, where he will highlight “Bidenomics” through a steel-cutting ceremony for the Acadia offshore wind vessel, announce the first-ever offshore wind sale in the Gulf of Mexico, and showcase how more than $500 billion in private-sector clean energy and manufacturing investments are creating American union jobs. Reporters press Dalton on several security and foreign policy issues, including the still-unclear status of Private King in North Korea, where she says the U.S. is working through DOD, State, the U.N., and Swedish and South Korean partners and has reached out to the Korean People’s Army to determine his whereabouts and well-being, as well as Iraq’s expulsion of Sweden’s ambassador over a Quran burning, on which she withholds comment pending review. Dalton raises strong concern about Russia’s recent missile and drone attacks on Ukrainian Black Sea ports and possible targeting of grain-carrying civilian vessels, noting the destruction of 60,000 tons of grain and worsening global food insecurity, and says the U.S. will continue supporting Ukraine. On domestic economic policy, she defends the impact of the CHIPS and Science Act amid TSMC’s delay of its Arizona plant, arguing that CHIPS-driven investments and workforce provisions will enable companies to follow through on their U.S. commitments, and she contends that while polls show skepticism about Biden’s economic stewardship, individual initiatives like the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, Inflation Reduction Act, and CHIPS Act enjoy broad support and will take time to be fully felt. Dalton declines to confirm a reported Camp David summit with Japanese and South Korean leaders, and she sharply criticizes Senator Tuberville’s ongoing hold on military promotions for undermining military readiness and the historically bipartisan NDAA process, citing the absence of a Marine Commandant for the first time in a century and pledging continued pressure on him and on congressional Republicans to end the blockade.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Bidenomics and clean energy agenda
Ms. Dalton previews President Biden’s trip to Philadelphia, where he will highlight how “Bidenomics” supports America’s clean energy future. She explains that the administration is driving more than $500 billion in private-sector manufacturing and clean energy investments that aim to create American-made projects, in American factories, with American workers. The President will visit the Philly Shipyard for a steel-cutting ceremony for the Acadia, a vessel that will support offshore wind farm construction, which is expected to generate hundreds of new union jobs. Dalton frames this event as a tangible example of the administration’s industrial and climate policies creating jobs and boosting U.S. clean energy leadership.
- Ms. Dalton states that President Biden will deliver remarks in Philadelphia on how Bidenomics is fueling America’s clean energy future through American-made projects and workers.
- Ms. Dalton notes that more than $500 billion in private-sector manufacturing and clean energy investments have been attracted under the administration’s policies.
- Ms. Dalton explains that in Philadelphia the President will visit the Philly Shipyard for a steel-cutting ceremony for the Acadia, a vessel that will be used to help build offshore wind farms.
- Ms. Dalton says the Acadia and the offshore wind farms it will help build are expected to support hundreds of new union jobs.
- Ms. Dalton reports that Great Lakes Dredge & Dock, which is building the Acadia, has said it would not have entered the U.S. offshore wind market without the Biden-Harris administration’s clean energy policies.
- Ms. Dalton adds that the President will also announce the first-ever offshore wind sale in the Gulf of Mexico during the Philadelphia visit.
- Ms. Dalton notes that the President will be joined in Philadelphia by Congresswoman Mary Gay Scanlon, Mayor Jim Kenney, and Representatives Brendan Boyle, Madeleine Dean, Chrissy Houlahan, Donald Norcross, and Joe Courtney.

#### Russian attacks on Black Sea grain routes
Darlene then raises concerns about potential Russian attacks on further grain shipments in the Black Sea and whether such actions could draw in other countries to the conflict. Ms. Dalton declines to speculate on hypothetical escalations but stresses that the administration is deeply concerned about current Russian missile and drone attacks on Black Sea port cities. She notes that Russia appears intent on reinstating its blockade of Ukrainian ports, has signaled it may target civilian vessels by labeling them potential military cargo carriers, and has already caused the destruction of 60,000 tons of grain, worsening global food insecurity. Dalton says the U.S. will continue to do everything it can to support Ukraine and mitigate global impacts of these actions.

#### Public perception of Bidenomics
Akayla questions why the President’s economic message, branded as “Bidenomics,” does not appear to resonate with the public, pointing to polling that shows his economic approval underwater. Ms. Dalton responds that the underlying policies—such as the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, the Inflation Reduction Act, and the CHIPS and Science Act—enjoy broad, bipartisan support among Americans. She acknowledges that Americans have endured a difficult period marked by the pandemic and global inflation following Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, which has shaped perceptions. Dalton argues that despite these headwinds, the U.S. shows strong economic performance, including low inflation, low unemployment, rising wages, and world-leading recovery, but it will take time for Americans to fully feel these gains, and the Philadelphia trip aims to demonstrate how Bidenomics is delivering in concrete ways.
- Akayla observes that polls show President Biden’s economic approval ratings remain underwater and asks why his Bidenomics message has not resonated with the public.
- Ms. Dalton says that individual policies like the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, the Inflation Reduction Act, and the CHIPS and Science Act have large bipartisan majorities of support from the American people.
- Ms. Dalton explains that part of the Philadelphia trip is to communicate these accomplishments directly to Americans.
- Ms. Dalton notes that President Biden took office at the tail end of a pandemic and that Russia’s invasion of Ukraine contributed to global inflation that hurt Americans.

- Ms. Dalton acknowledges that it will take time for Americans to feel the full impact of these economic gains, which is why the administration is highlighting real-world examples like the Philadelphia visit.

#### Senator Tuberville’s hold on military promotions
Eugene then asks about next steps for the administration in response to Senator Tuberville’s continued hold on military promotions, noting Tuberville has said a failed Senate vote will not change his position. Ms. Dalton strongly criticizes Tuberville for “playing politics” with the military and the historically bipartisan National Defense Authorization Act process. She highlights concrete consequences, including the lack of a Marine Commandant for the first time in 100 years and ripple effects on hundreds of military families. Dalton says the administration will keep pressing Tuberville to lift his hold and will also call on Republicans in both chambers to oppose his actions and protect military readiness.

- Ms. Dalton characterizes these consequences as deeply impactful and dangerous and says the administration will also call on Senate and House Republicans to do everything they can to stand up to Tuberville’s behavior.
