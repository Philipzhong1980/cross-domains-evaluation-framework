### Overview
The Human Relations Commission meeting centers on two major areas: an in‑depth update on the City of Mountain View’s Multicultural Engagement Program (MEP) and internal planning for the HRC’s new work plan and administrative requirements. Community Outreach Coordinator Nancy Ducos and Chief Communications Officer Lenka Wright describe how MEP, now a seven‑person team in the Communications and Outreach Division, supports non‑English‑speaking residents by translating hundreds of documents, providing interpretation for housing, eviction, rent stabilization, and council processes, organizing Spanish and Chinese language Civic Leadership Academies and an Ambassador Program, supplying devices and digital literacy training to participants, and facilitating culturally responsive events and surveys, all guided by emerging language access policy work and American Community Survey data. Commissioners probe language demand trends, the leadership pipeline from these academies into commissions and grassroots groups, and ways to elevate program visibility, including via the city website and possibly the city’s YouTube channel. In its business segment, the HRC confirms membership for three subcommittees—Mental Health (Nwokidu, Rakestraw, Sylvester), Bystander Intervention Training (Ball, Rakestraw, Webb), and Housing Insecurity Storytelling (Sylvester, Lin, Webb)—balancing Brown Act limits with the impending departures of Commissioners Ball, Lin, and Solomon while preserving room for new appointees in January. Commissioner Solomon outlines remaining tasks to close the prior Discrimination and Inclusion in Housing project, including securing a final interview release, following up with four interested community members, and working with staff to enhance the housing history web page, while she and Vice Chair Sylvester also coordinate to respond to a police request for LGBTQ+ community input. Staff then brief commissioners on a new requirement to file Form 700 Statements of Economic Interests because of the HRC’s funding recommendations, on potential penalties for non‑filing, and on the current extension of virtual meeting authorization, as well as flagging a citywide celebration event for commissioners and volunteers at Shoreline.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Multicultural Engagement Program overview
Community Outreach Coordinator Nancy Ducos, joined by Chief Communications Officer Lenka Wright, presents an annual update on the City of Mountain View’s Multicultural Engagement Program (MEP), which now operates within the Communications and Outreach Division. They explain that MEP’s core mission is to improve resident access to city information and decision‑making, particularly for communities with limited English proficiency. The current state reflects a significantly expanded team and portfolio compared to several years ago, with MEP now embedded across many major city initiatives.
- Nancy Ducos explains that the Multicultural Engagement Program supports public access to information that improves resident involvement in local public decision making by providing translation, interpretation, and multilingual outreach.
- Nancy Ducos notes that MEP moved under the Communications and Outreach Division about two years ago, with Lenka Wright as Chief Communications Officer, which she says injected new energy and tools into the program.
- Nancy Ducos states that the MEP team has grown to seven staff members, compared to a former structure of one part‑time coordinator and several very part‑time employees focused on Chinese and Russian outreach.
- Nancy Ducos emphasizes that MEP’s work goes beyond translation to relationship‑building with Latino, Chinese, Russian, and other communities, including outreach at events and via WhatsApp and WeChat.
- Nancy Ducos invites commissioners and the public to contact the program via the MEP@mountainview.gov email address with questions or needs.
#### Language services and housing‑related support
Nancy details how MEP supports housing, eviction, rent stabilization, zoning, and other policy initiatives through translation, interpretation, and community outreach. She underscores that housing issues generate a large share of language access requests and require continuous engagement with grassroots organizations. The program also provides ongoing interpretation at council and committee meetings and translates major city communications to ensure non‑English speakers can follow critical policy changes.
- Nancy Ducos reports that in the last year MEP translated approximately 288 documents and supported about 89 virtual outreach activities, including work done through WhatsApp and WeChat.
- Nancy Ducos states that MEP provides ongoing translation and review of written materials, FAQs, and webinars for the Housing and Eviction Help Center and the Rent Stabilization Program, and also supports outreach and interpretation as needed.
- Nancy Ducos explains that MEP supported outreach and interpretation for the city’s R3 rezoning outreach (referred to as ‘artery zoning’ in the transcript) when language access was needed.
- Nancy Ducos shares that MEP supported the HRC’s “Recovering Connection and History of Housing” event by translating flyers and descriptions, assisting with evaluations, and providing simultaneous interpretation during the event.
- Nancy Ducos says MEP played a major role in housing element outreach by connecting the city with grassroots groups such as tenant advocates, solidarity funds, and community action teams while offering simultaneous interpretation at community meetings.
- Nancy Ducos notes that MEP translates city news releases primarily into Spanish and Chinese, and into Russian as needed, and also provides interpretation for council meetings and translates the economic development and sustainability newsletters.
#### Civic Leadership Academies and ambassador pipeline
Nancy outlines three leadership development initiatives: the Spanish Language Civic Leadership Academy, the Chinese Language Civic Leadership Academy, and the Ambassador Program. These programs aim to develop future community leaders and connect them to city processes, and they are showing strong participation and retention. Graduates are already serving on commissions, city committees, and in community organizations, demonstrating a tangible pipeline from language‑specific leadership programs into civic engagement.
- Nancy Ducos recalls that the Spanish Language Civic Leadership Academy launched in November 2021 with 18 Latino community participants and was conducted largely via Zoom due to the pandemic, culminating in an in‑person graduation to foster community connection.
- Nancy Ducos reports that the first Chinese Language Civic Leadership Academy, held in spring 2022, had 17 participants with 100 percent retention and evolved from a planned virtual format to in‑person sessions at participants’ request.
- Nancy Ducos describes the Ambassador Program as a network of graduates from the Spanish academy who help with outreach by sharing city information and going door to door, including delivering 648 flyers about housing issues.
- Nancy Ducos states that for the current and upcoming fiscal years the city plans to continue and improve the existing Spanish and Chinese academies rather than add new language academies, with Spanish typically offered in fall and Chinese in spring.
- Nancy Ducos notes that academy graduates have moved into leadership roles, including service on the HRC, on the Environmental Planning Commission, on a performing arts committee, and in grassroots groups such as the Community Action Team and the Solidarity Fund.
#### Device access and digital literacy support
Nancy describes a pandemic‑driven realization that digital access is a prerequisite for participation in city programs and leadership academies. In response, MEP partnered with the Los Altos Mountain View Community Foundation and the public library to provide laptops and digital literacy training to academy participants and ambassadors who lacked functional devices. This effort aims to remove technology barriers and empower residents to engage with city information and online meetings.
- Nancy Ducos explains that during the pandemic MEP recognized that access to devices was critical for community members to participate in leadership programs and city engagement.
- Nancy Ducos reports that, with a grant from the Los Altos Mountain View Community Foundation, the city provided brand‑new laptops to Spanish Language Civic Leadership Academy participants and ambassadors who lacked working devices.
- Nancy Ducos states that, after distributing devices, MEP partnered with the library to offer basic technology classes so participants could learn how to use email and manage documents.
- Nancy Ducos highlights that the program now routinely uses digital tools, including QR codes, and notes that some participants actively request QR codes instead of paper flyers to access city information.
#### Language access policy and demand trends
Commissioners ask whether language demand is changing and how MEP plans for future language needs, including American Sign Language. Lenka responds with high‑level data from the American Community Survey showing substantial non‑English use and a focus on residents who speak English less than very well. She indicates that the city is developing a language access policy, which will formalize which languages receive translation and interpretation, informed by detailed 2020 and 2021 ACS data.
- Vice Chair IdaRose Sylvester asks whether the demand for Spanish, Chinese, and Russian services has changed over time and whether MEP is seeing emerging demand for additional languages.
- Nancy Ducos replies that as awareness of MEP grows, requests for Spanish and Chinese translation and interpretation have increased, and that for other languages like Hindi or modern Indo‑European languages, the city uses a contracted phone interpretation service, particularly for housing matters.
- Lenka Wright tells commissioners that the city is actively exploring a formal language access policy to guide which languages receive translation and interpretation support going forward.
- Lenka Wright cites 2021 American Community Survey data showing that about 53 percent of Mountain View residents speak only English, while roughly 47 percent speak a language other than English at home.
- Lenka Wright reports that approximately 13.5 percent of residents speak Spanish, with about 3,600 residents speaking English less than very well, and that roughly 19 percent speak Asian or Pacific Island languages with about 5,700 residents in that group speaking English less than very well.
- Lenka Wright notes that about 12.8 percent of residents speak other Indo‑European languages, with around 2,200 speaking English less than very well, but the city lacks a precise breakdown for specific languages like Russian in the high‑level data she cited.
- Commissioner Julie Solomon points out that U.S. Census instruments do not track American Sign Language use separately and suggests that the city explore other ways to assess local ASL needs.
#### Community engagement events and Elevate MV support
Nancy highlights several community‑facing efforts where MEP provides language and technology support, including surveys on language access, aid for Elevate Mountain View guaranteed income applications, and the Día de Muertos celebration aligned with but differentiated from Monster Bash. She also notes ongoing language assistance at the Eviction Help Center. These initiatives aim to meet residents where they are and build culturally respectful bridges between city programs and community needs.
- Nancy Ducos says MEP helped administer a survey to learn how residents access city services and language support, feeding into the language access policy development.
- Nancy Ducos reports that MEP provided on‑site technology support for Elevate Mountain View guaranteed income applications across two weekends, with staff assisting applicants in Chinese, Russian, Spanish, and English.
- Nancy Ducos explains that the program is organizing a Día de Muertos celebration on October 29 to coincide with Monster Bash while intentionally honoring the distinct cultural roots and meanings of each event.
- Nancy Ducos notes that MEP is crafting a plan to be more intentional in outreach by going to specific community locations and acting as a bridge between city services and residents’ needs.
- Nancy Ducos states that since January, multicultural engagement staff have regularly provided language assistance at the Eviction Help Center for residents who need support understanding processes and forms.
#### Commission questions on leadership pipeline and recruitment
Commissioners explore how leadership academy graduates transition into formal civic roles and how MEP supports advisory body recruitment, especially for non‑English‑dominant residents. Nancy and Lenka describe active follow‑up with alumni, translation of HRC descriptions, and direct promotion of commission openings in alumni networks. The discussion surfaces both successes and opportunities to strengthen the pipeline, such as prominently showcasing alumni stories online.
- Chair Annette Lin asks how graduates of the Spanish and Chinese leadership academies move into civic leadership roles in nonprofits and city advisory bodies, and how MEP supports recruitment for commissions like the HRC.
- Nancy Ducos responds that graduates from the Spanish academy now serve on the HRC, the Environmental Planning Commission, and a performing arts committee, and participate in grassroots efforts such as the Community Action Team and the Solidarity Fund.
- Nancy Ducos explains that MEP invests time in one‑on‑one conversations to demystify city structures and gradually build participants’ confidence to serve on commissions, which she says some residents initially find intimidating.
- Nancy Ducos notes that MEP has translated the HRC commission description into Spanish and Chinese to support recruitment and outreach to non‑English‑speaking residents.
- Lenka Wright adds that MEP stays in contact with academy graduates as community connectors and proactively shares translated information about commission and advisory board openings into those networks.
- Chair Annette Lin suggests more prominently listing academy alumni and their post‑academy community roles on the program’s web page to showcase outcomes and inspire new applicants, and Nancy responds that the current web page already features photos, names, and multilingual testimonials and is continually updated.

#### Form 700 (Statement of Economic Interests) requirement
Laurel informs commissioners that City Council has designated the HRC as a body whose members must file annual Form 700 Statements of Economic Interests with the Fair Political Practices Commission, due to the HRC’s role in recommending CDBG and HOME funding allocations. She explains the new requirement, the electronic filing process, and a forthcoming city policy tying non‑filing to unexcused absences from meetings. Commissioners raise timing and enforcement questions, and Laurel commits to clarifying details with the City Clerk and City Attorney offices.
- Laurel James reports that Council voted on September 27 to add HRC commissioners to the city’s list of ‘designated officials’ required to file Form 700 Statements of Economic Interests with the California Fair Political Practices Commission.
- Laurel James explains that this designation is based on the HRC’s annual recommendations to Council regarding CDBG and HOME funds, which she notes are rarely amended by Council and therefore qualify as substantive under FPPC guidelines.
- Laurel James states that commissioners will receive login information for Netfile, the city’s electronic Form 700 platform, which guides filers through data entry and e‑files the form with the City Clerk.
- Laurel James notes that Council will update city policy so that any advisory body member who fails to file Form 700 by the deadline will be barred from participating in meetings after the due date, with those absences counted as unexcused.
- Commissioner Nirvana Nwokidu asks how often the form must be completed, and Laurel responds that Form 700 is an annual requirement, typically due in the spring, plus a special first‑year filing covering the 12 months prior to the new designation date.
- Laurel James clarifies that the immediate filing will cover the period from September 27, 2021 to September 27, 2022 and is due in October, with the next annual filing expected in April only for the period not already covered.
- Vice Chair IdaRose Sylvester seeks clarification on whether commissioners must file both the October catch‑up form and the April annual form; Laurel confirms that both will be required but that April’s filing will cover only the remaining partial period.
- Commissioner Julie Solomon notes that exiting commissioners generally also file a leaving‑office Form 700 and asks how that applies to members leaving at year’s end.
- Chair Annette Lin and Commissioners Kevin Ball and Julie Solomon question what happens if departing commissioners do not file and whether there are state‑level penalties beyond the city’s planned unexcused‑absence rule, and Laurel agrees to consult the City Clerk and City Attorney to clarify requirements and consequences for departing members.
