### Overview
  National Climate Advisor Gina McCarthy and Special Presidential Envoy John Kerry lead an Earth Day briefing to announce the U.S. NDC committing to a 50–52% reduction in net greenhouse gas emissions below 2005 levels by 2030 and to frame climate action as an economic and job-creation opportunity tied to the American Jobs Plan, a message McCarthy emphasizes to unions and industry. Kerry highlights global summit progress, significant financial pledges and private-sector momentum that he says make emissions reductions more durable, and stresses intensive diplomacy ahead of Glasgow to secure broader ambition; both officials point to multiple policy pathways—executive actions, state/local measures, and private investment—to meet targets even if full legislation is delayed. During Q&A, officials defend the pledge’s credibility against political reversals by citing market entrenchment and corporate commitments, discuss workforce impacts and EV transitions, and address international engagement with Russia, Brazil, China, and transparency tools for accountability. Press Secretary Jen Psaki then shifts to domestic topics, briefing on improving unemployment indicators, previewing the American Families Plan and its pay-fors while reiterating no tax increases under $400,000 for individuals, welcoming bipartisan infrastructure talks as a starting point, and answering questions on COVID guidance, international climate finance appropriations, Russia/Ukraine tensions and Navalny, Nord Stream 2, D.C. statehood, White House vaccinations and masking policy, and other personnel and scheduling items raised by reporters.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### NDC announcement details
  National Climate Advisor Gina McCarthy presents the U.S. nationally determined contribution (NDC), committing to reduce net economy-wide greenhouse gas emissions 50–52% below 2005 levels by 2030. She frames the target as a whole-of-government product built from sector-by-sector analysis and broad stakeholder engagement. McCarthy emphasizes that the target links climate action to job creation, public health, environmental justice, and U.S. industrial competitiveness.
- Administrator Gina McCarthy says the United States will reduce net economy-wide greenhouse gas emissions 50 to 52 percent below 2005 levels by 2030.
- Administrator McCarthy attributes the target to a whole-of-government Climate Task Force process that analyzed electricity, transportation, buildings, industry, lands, and oceans.
- Administrator McCarthy frames the NDC as an economic opportunity that will create good-paying union jobs and advance environmental justice.
- Administrator McCarthy says the plan relies on existing cost-effective clean technologies, market innovation trends, and partnerships across federal, state, and local governments.
  #### Climate policy rationale
  McCarthy explains why the administration links climate action to economic recovery and jobs, arguing that large investments in clean infrastructure and innovation will boost manufacturing and exports. She highlights specific workforce opportunities in transmission, methane mitigation, EV production and charging, carbon capture, and agricultural carbon innovation. The administration positions the American Jobs Plan as the funding mechanism to capture these benefits.
- Administrator McCarthy argues that tackling climate change and creating jobs go hand in hand and that the American Jobs Plan helps unlock economic opportunities.
- Administrator McCarthy identifies workforce needs in transmission buildout, capping abandoned wells, EV manufacturing, and carbon-capture-related industries.
- Administrator McCarthy says the administration has engaged cities, states, businesses, workers, scientists, and youth in developing the plan.
  #### Summit outcomes and finance
  Special Presidential Envoy John Kerry summarizes global responses at the Climate Summit, citing financial commitments and arrangements that signal market momentum. Kerry highlights formation of financial alliances and bank commitments totaling trillions of dollars, and he stresses that private capital and market forces make climate progress durable. He also underscores the global participation of major emitters and the need for follow-up diplomacy ahead of Glasgow.
- Special Envoy John Kerry notes announcements including the Net-Zero Bank Alliance and commitments from financial institutions directing trillions toward climate-related investments.
- Special Envoy Kerry says roughly 55 percent of global GDP now commits to reductions consistent with keeping warming near 1.5°C.
- Special Envoy Kerry attributes renewed U.S. credibility to President Biden’s reengagement and the administration’s all-of-government approach.
  #### International pledges review
  Kerry reviews peer-country updates and pledges announced at the summit, calling out strengthened targets and policy shifts in Japan, Canada, Korea, India, Argentina, the UK, and the EU. He frames these national moves as evidence of rising global ambition and of private-sector and public finance unlocking deployment of renewables and other low-carbon technologies. He warns that the next six months of diplomacy are critical to secure improvements before Glasgow.
- Special Envoy Kerry reports Japan raised its 2030 ambition to about 46–50 percent and Canada strengthened its target to 40–45 percent.
- Special Envoy Kerry highlights India’s partnership to deploy 450 GW of renewable power as key to its 1.5°C alignment.
- Special Envoy Kerry warns that the next six months of diplomacy are critical to make Glasgow successful.
  #### Durability of U.S. pledge
  Reporters ask whether the U.S. pledge can withstand future political shifts; Kerry and McCarthy reply that many measures are executable by executive action and that market and private-sector commitments make policy durable. Kerry emphasizes that private capital, corporate commitments, and market dynamics will entrench low-carbon transitions even if political leadership changes. McCarthy highlights endorsements from labor and industry groups as indicators of cross-sector backing.
- Secretary John Kerry says much of the plan can be executed through executive authority and that market forces will make the transition durable.
- Administrator McCarthy cites endorsements from AFL-CIO, the Chamber of Commerce, the Edison Electric Institute, and IBEW as evidence of broad support.
- Secretary Kerry asserts that private-sector momentum and consumer demand reduce the likelihood that a future administration could reverse progress quickly.
  #### Jobs and workforce impacts
  Kerry and McCarthy describe specific labor market impacts, noting rapid growth in clean-energy occupations and the potential to create millions of jobs. Kerry cites Bureau of Labor Statistics projections showing large percentage growth for wind technician and solar installer jobs, while McCarthy points to manufacturing and infrastructure work that can benefit unionized labor. Both present job creation as the primary rationale for public investment in the transition.
- Secretary Kerry cites BLS data showing high projected growth for wind turbine technicians and solar panel installers.
- Administrator McCarthy says the American Jobs Plan will create opportunities for electricians, plumbers, pipefitters, steelworkers, and heavy-equipment operators.
- Administrator McCarthy frames job creation as central to the administration’s climate and economic strategy.
  #### Electric vehicle transition
  Speakers discuss the EV transition as market-driven and as a policy focus, with automakers voluntarily committing to higher EV production. McCarthy and Kerry emphasize public and private roles in building charging infrastructure and scaling EV adoption, with McCarthy visiting a Union Station charging site after the briefing. The administration argues for government incentives and partnerships to accelerate deployment and capture domestic manufacturing benefits.
- Administrator McCarthy notes that major automakers have announced commitments toward 100 percent EV production by mid-2030s.
- Secretary Kerry says Tesla’s market valuation and automaker commitments signal that the EV transition is irreversible.
- Press Secretary Jen Psaki and Administrator McCarthy indicate a government role in incentivizing charging infrastructure alongside private investment.
  #### Quantifying targets question
  Reporters press for concrete sector metrics (e.g., renewable share or EVs on roads) and whether the U.S. can meet targets without major legislative action. Kerry replies that the plan emphasizes investment and market dynamics rather than announcing exact sector splits today, and McCarthy points to multiple pathways including executive action, state/local measures, and private-sector change. Psaki reiterates that the administration has options to advance progress with and without new legislation.
- Secretary Kerry says the NDC reflects investments that make energy delivery more efficient and lower-cost without offering specific sector percentages today.
- Administrator McCarthy says multiple pathways exist across sectors and that many measures are already underway at state and local levels.
- Press Secretary Psaki stresses that the administration can pursue emissions reductions through executive actions, private-sector moves, and legislation.
  #### Reversibility concerns
  Reporters ask how the administration prevents rollback by a future president; Kerry reiterates market entrenchment and multi-year private investment as barriers to quick reversal. He cites automaker and financial-sector commitments as examples that will create economic inertia. McCarthy and Kerry both say continued technological progress could exceed current targets.
- Secretary Kerry argues that market investment and consumer demand will create entrenchment that future political actions cannot easily reverse.
- Administrator McCarthy emphasizes that continued technology and market shifts make the transition increasingly irreversible.
- Secretary Kerry expresses confidence that the U.S. will likely exceed the 50–52 percent target as technologies improve.
  #### Diplomacy with Russia, Brazil
  Kerry addresses comments by President Putin and President Bolsonaro, calling for follow-up diplomacy and noting ongoing conversations with their teams. He characterizes Putin’s remarks as relatively constructive and signals potential future travel or engagement if concrete cooperation opportunities arise. Kerry also highlights improved transparency tools like satellite monitoring for emissions and corporate footprint tracking to bolster accountability.
- Secretary Kerry says he has been in conversations with teams from Russia and Brazil and that further diplomacy may be warranted.
- Secretary Kerry finds some of President Putin’s remarks constructive and highlights potential areas for cooperation.
- Secretary Kerry highlights emerging satellite-based monitoring programs as tools that will increase emissions transparency and accountability.
  #### Adequacy of U.S. target
  Reporters and activists criticize the 50–52% goal as insufficient; Kerry acknowledges the frustration and anger of youth activists while arguing the pledge represents the strongest feasible step today. He frames the NDC as a starting point that can be improved through diplomatic work, technological breakthroughs, and accelerated investment. Kerry reiterates confidence that innovation will help the U.S. exceed current projections.
- Secretary Kerry acknowledges activists’ anger and calls their engagement important while saying the NDC is the best, achievable step at present.
- Secretary Kerry says diplomatic work and technological innovation can push the U.S. beyond its stated target.
- Administrator McCarthy and Secretary Kerry frame the NDC as a foundation for accountability and further ambition.
  #### Transition to briefing: economic data
  Press Secretary Jen Psaki transitions to a domestic briefing and highlights recent unemployment insurance claims as a sign of slow economic recovery and as validation of pandemic relief efforts and vaccinations. She frames the data as consistent with the administration’s aim to create jobs while addressing the pandemic. Psaki links economic progress to the administration’s broader investment agenda.
- Press Secretary Jen Psaki says recent unemployment insurance claims and a declining four-week average show the economy is slowly healing.
- Press Secretary Psaki attributes improving labor market signals in part to the American Rescue Plan and vaccine distribution.
  #### American Families Plan & taxes
  Reporters ask about tax increases reportedly tied to the American Families Plan; Psaki reiterates the President’s pledge not to raise taxes on those earning under $400,000 per year and says plan details will be unveiled in his Joint Address. She frames tax proposals as pay-fors for investments in childcare, education, and other priorities and says the administration will finalize details in coming days. Psaki stresses the plan’s intent to modernize the economy and invest in middle-class priorities.
- Press Secretary Psaki reiterates President Biden’s commitment not to raise taxes on Americans earning under $400,000 annually.
- Press Secretary Psaki says the American Families Plan will be outlined in the President’s Joint Address on April 28 and will include proposed pay-fors.
- Press Secretary Psaki frames tax changes as targeted to wealthier individuals and corporations to fund investments in childcare and education.
  #### Republican infrastructure overtures
  Psaki responds to a slimmer Republican infrastructure proposal by calling it a welcome good-faith starting point while noting many details remain to be negotiated. She says the administration welcomes bipartisan discussion, expects staff-level briefings, and anticipates the President inviting members to the White House after his Joint Address. The White House frames openness to negotiation while emphasizing the need for sufficient scale and investment.
- Press Secretary Psaki says the administration welcomes good-faith Republican infrastructure proposals as a starting point for negotiation.
- Press Secretary Psaki expects staff-level briefings and subsequent White House engagement with members after the President’s Joint Address.
  #### COVID guidance and CDC
  Reporters ask whether the President will extend mask guidance beyond his first 100 days and whether the CDC should alter outdoor mask recommendations for vaccinated people. Psaki defers to health experts and the CDC, saying the administration follows their guidance and will await their determinations. She emphasizes ongoing discussions with medical advisers about public-health strategy.
- Press Secretary Psaki says decisions about mask guidance are left to CDC and health experts and that the administration will follow their advice.
- Press Secretary Psaki says the President remains in discussions with medical advisors about pandemic controls and public messaging.
  #### Armenian genocide recognition
  A reporter asks whether the President will recognize the Armenian genocide imminently; Psaki declines to preview any decision and says she will not get ahead of the President. She acknowledges public interest but offers no new details. The question remains open.
- Press Secretary Psaki says she will not preannounce any presidential decision about formal recognition of the Armenian genocide from the podium.
  #### Reaching targets without legislation
  Reporters press whether the U.S. can reach the 2030 target absent passage of the American Jobs Plan; Psaki and McCarthy say multiple pathways exist, including executive actions, state and local measures, and private-sector moves. Psaki stresses that while legislation would accelerate and institutionalize change, the administration can pursue significant emissions reductions through a mix of authorities and partnerships. McCarthy reiterates sectoral pathways and existing policy tools.
- Press Secretary Psaki says multiple pathways—executive measures, state/local action, and private-sector shifts—can contribute to meeting the 2030 goal.
- Administrator McCarthy highlights ongoing sector-by-sector measures in electricity, transportation, buildings, industry, and lands that can be advanced without a single legislative package.
  #### EV charging public funding debate
  Reporters question why taxpayers should fund 500,000 EV charging stations if market forces will build them; Psaki argues government incentives are appropriate to accelerate equitable deployment and create domestic jobs while acknowledging private-sector role. The administration positions public investment as complementary to private capital to ensure U.S. leadership and nationwide access. Psaki frames infrastructure spending as a public good that catalyzes private activity.
- Press Secretary Psaki says the government has a role to incentivize and accelerate EV charging deployment alongside private-sector investment.
- Press Secretary Psaki frames public funding as a means to create jobs and ensure broad access to EV infrastructure nationwide.
  #### Capital gains and markets
  Reporters ask whether proposed tax changes, including potential capital gains increases, risk discouraging long-term investment amid an equity market decline. Psaki reiterates that tax specifics remain under finalization but that the administration believes taxing wealthier individuals and corporations to fund investments will not harm the economy. She reiterates the commitment not to raise taxes on households earning less than $400,000.
- Press Secretary Psaki says tax proposals are still being finalized and that the administration believes pay-fors will not harm long-term investment.
- Press Secretary Psaki reiterates the administration’s pledge not to raise taxes on individuals earning under $400,000.
  #### Pentagon energy attack reports

She points to ongoing reporting and says the administration will follow the military’s assessments. No new White House action is announced from the briefing.

Psaki acknowledges the need to work with Congress to appropriate funds and says administration officials will make a diplomatic and policy case for such investments. She frames climate finance as part of U.S. responsibility and global leadership on emissions.
- Press Secretary Psaki says securing funding for international climate finance will require working with Congress and that the administration will make its case to members.
- Press Secretary Psaki frames climate finance as part of U.S. obligations and global cooperation to address emissions.

She emphasizes U.S. concern and the desire to deescalate tensions, while indicating diplomacy at the National Security Advisor level and others will continue. No new bilateral call is announced immediately.

  #### Nord Stream 2 stance
  Psaki reiterates the administration’s continued opposition to Nord Stream 2 and ongoing dialogue with European partners, but she declines to preview additional sanctions or designations. The White House remains engaged with transatlantic partners and keeps options open. No immediate policy change is announced at the briefing.
- Press Secretary Psaki reiterates that the administration views Nord Stream 2 as problematic and continues to coordinate with European partners.

  #### Joint Address priorities
  Psaki previews the President’s upcoming Joint Address as the forum to outline the American Families Plan, including childcare and education investments, and other priorities such as police reform and healthcare. She notes the speech will not exhaust the administration’s full agenda but will set key priorities and payment proposals. Psaki says details on tax and pay-for proposals will be provided with the plan.
- Press Secretary Psaki says the President will use his Joint Address to outline the American Families Plan and related pay-for proposals.

She frames the issue as one of representation and the founding principles of no taxation without representation. The White House plans to advocate for the measure’s passage.
- Press Secretary Psaki says President Biden strongly supports D.C. statehood and the administration issued a statement backing H.R. 51.

She offers to provide updated vaccination numbers and says decisions on mask-wearing will follow medical guidance. The White House continues to enforce layered precautions in its operations.
