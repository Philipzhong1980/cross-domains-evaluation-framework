### Overview
The meeting centers on the Biden administration’s new U.S. climate pledge and its integration with economic and domestic policy, led by National Climate Advisor Gina McCarthy, Special Presidential Envoy for Climate John Kerry, and Press Secretary Jen Psaki. McCarthy and Kerry detail the newly announced nationally determined contribution to cut U.S. greenhouse gas emissions 50–52 percent below 2005 levels by 2030, emphasizing that it emerges from an unprecedented all‑of‑government process and is framed as a jobs and competitiveness strategy anchored in clean energy, infrastructure, and environmental justice. Kerry uses the ongoing Leaders Summit on Climate to argue that global markets and finance—via major bank alliances and trillions in climate‑aligned investment—are locking in an irreversible transition, while he recounts new pledges from Japan, Canada, Korea, India, the UK, the EU, and others as evidence of rising ambition ahead of the critical Glasgow COP, even as he concedes the world is still short of what’s needed. In an extended Q&A, Kerry and McCarthy address questions about the durability and realism of U.S. commitments, insisting that a combination of executive authority, state and local action, and private‑sector momentum—especially in fast‑growing fields like renewables and electric vehicles—will make the transition economically beneficial and politically hard to reverse, and Kerry engages youth activists’ criticisms by acknowledging their justified anger while casting today’s steps as foundational building blocks for stronger future action. Psaki then broadens the briefing to domestic issues, linking improving unemployment data and the American Rescue Plan to a recovering economy, previewing the American Families Plan and Biden’s joint session address—including tax changes for higher earners, childcare and education investments, and a firm $400,000 individual threshold for any tax increases—while also responding to questions on Republican infrastructure counterproposals, mask and vaccine protocols, D.C. statehood, Russia and Ukraine, Navalny, international climate finance, border and military issues, and even Biden’s personal downtime, all while reinforcing the administration’s view that economic recovery, social investment, and climate leadership are mutually reinforcing pillars of its agenda.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### U.S. 2030 climate target (NDC)
Administrator Gina McCarthy outlines President Biden’s new U.S. nationally determined contribution (NDC) under the Paris Agreement: a 50–52 percent reduction in net economy‑wide greenhouse gas emissions below 2005 levels by 2030. She explains that this target is the product of a whole‑of‑government process through the White House Climate Task Force, using sector‑by‑sector analysis of existing technologies and policies. The administration frames the target as both an emissions commitment to the UN and an economic strategy to position American workers and industry for a clean energy future. McCarthy emphasizes that achieving the NDC is feasible through multiple pathways spanning federal, state, local, and private‑sector actions.
- Administrator McCarthy states that the new U.S. NDC commits to reducing net economy‑wide greenhouse gas emissions by 50 to 52 percent below 2005 levels by 2030.
- Administrator McCarthy explains that the NDC was developed through a whole‑of‑government process organized by the President’s Climate Task Force and formally submitted under the U.N. climate convention.
- Administrator McCarthy notes that extensive engagement with cities, states, businesses, workers, scientists, and citizens revealed multiple pathways across all sectors to cut emissions while growing the economy.
- Administrator McCarthy stresses that the target builds on existing U.S. climate leadership and is designed to leverage current cost‑effective clean technologies and innovation trends.
#### Climate policy as jobs and economic strategy
McCarthy repeatedly links climate action to job creation, arguing that President Biden views climate policy as an engine for economic growth and middle‑class opportunity. She highlights specific job categories tied to clean energy and infrastructure, such as line workers building transmission lines, workers capping abandoned wells, autoworkers producing electric vehicles, engineers advancing carbon capture, and farmers deploying climate‑smart practices. The American Jobs Plan is positioned as a necessary investment to capture these opportunities, improve public health, and advance environmental justice. McCarthy frames the overarching vision as “every day is Earth Day and Jobs Day,” centering people, air quality, community strength, and the dignity of good work.
- Administrator McCarthy says that when President Biden hears “climate,” he thinks “jobs,” and describes the climate agenda as fundamentally an economic opportunity strategy.
- Administrator McCarthy cites job opportunities for line workers building thousands of miles of transmission lines, workers capping abandoned wells and reclaiming mines, autoworkers building electric vehicles, and engineers expanding carbon capture and green hydrogen.
- Administrator McCarthy argues that the American Jobs Plan is essential to fully capture the economic upside of climate action by investing in infrastructure, innovation, workers, and communities.
- Administrator McCarthy ties the President’s climate vision to environmental justice, public health, and the dignity of good jobs, stressing that post‑pandemic recovery should be about “renewed hope and opportunity.”
#### All‑of‑government climate governance and NDC credibility
Special Presidential Envoy John Kerry underscores the administrative and diplomatic significance of the new NDC and the climate summit. He credits McCarthy for successfully organizing an unprecedented all‑of‑government climate task force and notes that every Cabinet and agency leader is now required to factor climate consequences into decisions. Internationally, he acknowledges concerns about U.S. credibility after leaving the Paris Agreement, but argues that systemic market shifts toward clean energy and large‑scale financial commitments make the transition irreversible. Kerry positions the new NDC and summit as critical steps in restoring U.S. leadership and demonstrating seriousness in multilateral climate efforts.
- Secretary Kerry praises Administrator McCarthy for creating and running the first‑ever all‑of‑government climate task force, calling the NDC’s development a “gigantic administrative, organizational task.”
- Secretary Kerry explains that President Biden’s executive order requires every Cabinet officer and agency head to factor climate consequences into all decisions, institutionalizing climate across government.
- Secretary Kerry recounts that foreign leaders questioned U.S. credibility after withdrawing from Paris, asking what America’s NDC would be and whether it could be trusted going forward.
- Secretary Kerry asserts that global market forces and massive private‑sector investments in clean technologies now make it extremely difficult for any future politician to reverse the climate transition.
#### Global finance and private‑sector climate alignment
Kerry highlights the emerging role of global finance in enabling decarbonization, emphasizing initiatives announced around the summit. He points to the Net‑Zero Banking Alliance and the Glasgow Financial Alliance for Net Zero, which collectively represent tens of trillions of dollars in managed assets. He notes that six major banks have pledged $4.16 trillion over 10 years to climate‑related investments, framing this as a previously missing piece in climate action. Kerry argues that such financial commitments reinforce the momentum of the clean‑energy market and will underpin long‑term emissions reductions regardless of political changes.
- Secretary Kerry reports that the Net‑Zero Bank Alliance and Glasgow Financial Alliance for Net Zero now include around 43 financial institutions managing tens of trillions of dollars in assets.
- Secretary Kerry states that six banks have committed to invest $4.16 trillion into climate‑related efforts over the next decade, calling finance a “really missing element” that is now coming into place.
- Secretary Kerry argues that large‑scale private capital commitments to green investments will anchor the climate transition beyond the reach of political reversals.
- Secretary Kerry links the financial sector’s net‑zero moves directly to the credibility of national climate pledges and long‑term decarbonization pathways.
#### Restoring U.S. climate leadership and raising global ambition
Kerry reflects on the U.S. withdrawal from the Paris Agreement and stresses the diplomatic effort required to rebuild trust. He characterizes the previous administration’s exit as isolated and unsupported by science, leaving the Biden administration with a “big step” to regain credibility. The summit is presented as a milestone where major emitters, including China, India, Russia, and the EU, acknowledge the climate crisis and announce stronger actions. Kerry reports that roughly 55 percent of global GDP is now covered by reduction pledges consistent with limiting warming to 1.5°C, but he cautions that the next six months leading to the Glasgow COP are critical and that ambition must continue to rise.
- Secretary Kerry criticizes the former President as the only world leader to withdraw from the Paris Agreement without scientific justification, saying it damaged U.S. credibility.
- Secretary Kerry says the United States now has about 55 percent of global GDP committed to emission‑reduction levels consistent with keeping temperature rise to 1.5 degrees Celsius.
- Secretary Kerry lists new or strengthened commitments by Japan, Canada, the Republic of Korea, India, Argentina, the UK, and the EU as evidence that the world is raising climate ambition.
- Secretary Kerry calls the Glasgow climate conference the “last best hope” to coalesce the world’s top 20 emitters, who together account for 81 percent of emissions, behind sufficient action.
#### International partners’ specific climate pledges
Kerry details several concrete pledges announced or recently updated by key countries, tying them to the 1.5°C objective. Japan increases its 2030 reduction target to 46–50 percent; Canada to 40–45 percent; and the Republic of Korea commits to end overseas coal finance and align its NDC with net zero by 2050. India agrees with the U.S. to deploy 450 GW of renewable power, which he notes would keep India on a 1.5°C‑compatible path if properly financed. He also cites the UK’s 78 percent reduction goal by 2035 and the EU’s new law embedding a 55 percent reduction by 2030. These moves are presented as “progress with a heavy lift ahead,” underscoring both momentum and remaining gaps.
- Secretary Kerry specifies that Japan now targets a 46 to 50 percent emissions cut by 2030, up from a 26 percent goal.
- Secretary Kerry notes that Canada has strengthened its target from a 30 percent to a 40 to 45 percent reduction by 2030.
- Secretary Kerry reports that the Republic of Korea will terminate overseas coal finance and update its NDC to align with a 2050 net‑zero goal.
- Secretary Kerry describes a U.S.–India partnership to deploy 450 gigawatts of renewable power, saying this would help keep India on track with the 1.5‑degree target.
- Secretary Kerry points out that the UK adopted a 78 percent reduction target by 2035 and that the EU embedded a 55 percent by 2030 reduction and net‑zero goal into law.
#### Durability of U.S. climate commitments and role of markets
Reporters question how the U.S. can credibly promise deep cuts given domestic political divisions and the possibility of future policy reversals. Kerry and McCarthy respond that a significant share of the plan can proceed via executive action and that the private sector is already driving an irreversible transition toward clean energy. Kerry cites labor‑market data showing rapid growth in clean‑energy occupations and the decisions by major automakers to pivot to electric vehicles. McCarthy emphasizes broad endorsements of the jobs plan by labor, business, and utility groups, arguing that the trajectory is forward‑moving and that the key question is whether the U.S. chooses to lead.
- Secretary Kerry tells reporters that much of the U.S. climate plan is executable via executive action rather than legislation, reducing reliance on congressional consensus.
- Secretary Kerry argues that no future politician will be able to reverse the climate transition because trillions in global capital are already flowing into clean‑energy markets.
- Administrator McCarthy says the NDC and jobs plan are grounded in existing federal authorities, state and local actions, technological readiness, and job‑creation potential, making the targets deliverable.
- Administrator McCarthy highlights endorsements of the President’s plan from the AFL‑CIO, U.S. Chamber of Commerce, Edison Electric Institute, and IBEW as evidence of cross‑sector buy‑in.
- Secretary Kerry cites Bureau of Labor Statistics data showing wind turbine technicians and solar installers among the fastest‑growing occupations, reinforcing the economic case for climate action.
- Administrator McCarthy notes that wind and solar enjoyed their best year ever in 2020 and credits tax credits passed by a Republican‑controlled Congress, suggesting bipartisan recognition of clean‑energy growth.
#### Economic impacts of decarbonization and everyday implications
Kerry addresses concerns that achieving the 50–52 percent reduction might “turn the economy on its ear” by explaining that the transition is fundamentally an investment in more efficient, lower‑cost energy systems and new employment opportunities. He notes that coal plants are already unfinanceable in many regions and that markets and technology revolutions historically reshape economies, as seen in past industrial shifts. He predicts limited dislocation and significant opportunity, particularly in skilled trades needed for grid modernization and infrastructure. Psaki adds that many shifts, especially toward electric vehicles and efficiency, are already underway in the private sector, and that government’s role is to ensure the U.S. captures the associated jobs and industrial leadership.
- Secretary Kerry states that the climate plan represents an investment in new, more efficient, lower‑cost energy systems rather than a drag on the economy.
- Secretary Kerry says coal‑fired power plants are effectively unfinanceable in the U.S. and Europe, indicating that the market has already begun the transition away from coal.
- Secretary Kerry likens the coming clean‑energy transition to previous economic shifts such as the Industrial Revolution and the 1990s technology boom, arguing that new opportunities will replace old industries.
- Secretary Kerry lists electricians, plumbers, pipefitters, steelworkers, and heavy‑equipment operators as trades that will benefit from building out the grid and new infrastructure.
- Press Secretary Psaki reiterates that industries are already moving toward electric vehicles and that the administration’s focus is on ensuring U.S. workers gain long‑term opportunities from that shift.
#### Electric vehicle transition and charging infrastructure
McCarthy and Kerry use electric vehicles (EVs) as a central example of market‑driven climate change mitigation, pointing to automaker commitments and consumer demand. McCarthy notes that major car companies have voluntarily announced 100 percent EV sales by 2035, and Kerry references Tesla’s market valuation and GM and Ford’s EV strategies as signals of an irreversible shift. When asked why government should fund 500,000 charging stations if the market is already moving, Psaki argues that public investment accelerates deployment, supports job creation, and helps shape the domestic industrial base. The administration portrays EV infrastructure as a joint public‑private effort that ensures the U.S. leads rather than follows in the global auto transition.
- Administrator McCarthy notes that several major automakers told her unprompted that they are targeting 100 percent electric vehicle sales by 2035, calling EVs the clear future.
- Secretary Kerry cites Tesla’s status as the world’s most valuable auto company, producing only EVs, as a market signal that electrification is inevitable.
- Secretary Kerry reminds reporters that GM has announced it will produce only electric vehicles from 2035 onward, and he says Ford’s leadership describes EVs as a superior product.
- Press Secretary Psaki responds to concerns about subsidizing EV charging by insisting there is a legitimate public role in incentivizing infrastructure that creates jobs and secures U.S. competitiveness.
- Press Secretary Psaki emphasizes that public EV investments are designed to ensure the U.S. builds future industries domestically rather than ceding leadership to other countries.
#### Engagement with Russia and Brazil on climate and accountability
Asked about critical comments from President Putin and President Bolsonaro regarding historical U.S. emissions, Kerry adopts a pragmatic tone, noting ongoing diplomatic contacts with both governments. He says Putin’s remarks included rational and potentially cooperative ideas, and he was “surprised” by some constructive elements in Bolsonaro’s comments but stresses that implementation and enforcement will be the true tests. Kerry highlights emerging satellite‑based monitoring, such as the Trace program, that will enable real‑time tracking of emissions and land‑use impacts, limiting countries’ ability to “hide” noncompliance. He suggests that as broader global progress accelerates, Russia and Brazil will feel increasing pressure to align.
- Secretary Kerry confirms ongoing discussions with Russian and Brazilian teams but says travel will await clearer prospects for concrete outcomes.
- Secretary Kerry describes President Putin’s summit remarks as “pretty rational” and containing possible areas for U.S.–Russia cooperation on climate.
- Secretary Kerry says he was surprised that some of President Bolsonaro’s climate statements “work if you do those things,” but questions whether Brazil will follow through and enforce them.
- Secretary Kerry highlights the Trace satellite program as a tool to measure national and corporate emissions footprints in real time, asserting that there will be “no hiding anymore” on climate performance.
#### Adequacy of the 50–52 percent target and future technology
Some stakeholders view the U.S. pledge and climate finance as insufficient relative to the scale of the crisis, especially youth activists. Kerry responds by expressing empathy with their anger, drawing parallels to his own youth activism, and acknowledging that current actions are not yet enough. However, he argues that today’s commitments are a “huge step” that create accountability structures and that future technological breakthroughs are likely to enable even deeper reductions. He predicts that countries, including the U.S., may ultimately exceed their NDCs, citing ongoing R&D in batteries, green hydrogen, and carbon capture, and positioning Biden’s approach as the best available path to move from the current state toward adequate action.
- Secretary Kerry answers that the 50 to 52 percent U.S. target is “doable” but challenging and says he suspects the U.S. will ultimately exceed it as technologies advance.
- Secretary Kerry points to potential breakthroughs in battery storage, green hydrogen, and direct air carbon capture as reasons to expect stronger reductions over time than current models project.
- Secretary Kerry uses historical U.S. achievements such as going to the Moon, inventing the Internet, and rapidly developing COVID‑19 vaccines to argue for American innovation capacity.
- Secretary Kerry acknowledges that current efforts are “not enough” but describes the summit’s new and strengthened targets as the best available step to create momentum and hold leaders accountable.
#### Youth climate activism and political accountability
Kerry reflects extensively on youth climate activism after a young speaker criticized older generations for insufficient action. He recalls his own involvement in the first Earth Day, anti‑war protests, and environmental legislation in the 1970s, arguing that youth movements historically have driven major policy change. He lists landmark U.S. environmental laws that followed grassroots mobilization and describes young people today as rightly angry that adults have ignored overwhelming evidence. Kerry frames Biden’s climate agenda and the summit as tools for activists to hold leaders accountable, arguing that public commitments create leverage for continued pressure and democratic change.
- Secretary Kerry praises youth climate activist Xiye Bastida’s passion and says he understands her frustration even if some demands are physically impossible to meet immediately.
- Secretary Kerry recounts his 1971 testimony against the Vietnam War and involvement in early environmental movements, including the first Earth Day and the “Dirty Dozen” electoral campaign.
- Secretary Kerry notes that youth‑driven activism led to the Clean Air Act, Safe Drinking Water Act, Marine Mammal Protection Act, Coastal Zone Management Act, Endangered Species Act, and the creation of the EPA.
- Secretary Kerry urges that when facts show climate harms with near‑certainty, public officials must exercise a “precautionary principle” and act aggressively, likening climate action to buying insurance for guaranteed risks.
#### U.S. climate finance and support for developing countries
Questions arise about whether U.S. climate finance for developing countries—described as several billion dollars under $10 billion—is commensurate with both U.S. responsibility and budget capacity. Kerry acknowledges concerns but emphasizes that current commitments reflect what is realistically achievable now and that the priority is to demonstrate movement and build on it. Later, Psaki explains that part of the administration’s climate finance plan is a request to Congress for $1.25 billion for the Green Climate Fund and other international support. She notes the U.S. cannot solve climate change alone and argues that enabling developing countries to meet their obligations is essential to global success, although she concedes appropriations will require congressional persuasion.
- Secretary Kerry concedes that some groups view the U.S. NDC and climate finance as insufficient but argues that the administration is putting forward the most ambitious package it can deliver today.
- Secretary Kerry frames new climate finance pledges as essential building blocks that can scale as technologies become cheaper and the political space for funding grows.
- Press Secretary Psaki says the administration will ask Congress to appropriate $1.25 billion, including for the Green Climate Fund, as part of its international climate finance plan.
- Press Secretary Psaki argues that because the U.S. is a major emitter but not the only one, it must help developing countries access resources to meet their climate obligations.
#### Multiple pathways to U.S. climate goals and role of legislation
Reporters press the administration on whether the U.S. can meet its NDC without passing the American Jobs Plan. Psaki responds that there are multiple pathways across sectors and policy tools, including executive actions, regulatory standards, state and local initiatives, and private‑sector changes. She cites the goal of 100 percent carbon‑pollution‑free electricity by 2035, vehicle emissions standards, building efficiency upgrades, and transportation investments as examples of ongoing or planned measures. While she describes the Jobs Plan as a major accelerator and job creator, she maintains that progress toward the 2030 target will not hinge on a single bill, though the White House continues to push for its enactment this summer, ahead of the Glasgow conference.
- Press Secretary Psaki says there are “multiple pathways” to achieve sectoral emissions reductions, including electricity, transportation, buildings, industry, and lands, through a mix of executive actions, legislation, and private initiatives.
- Press Secretary Psaki reiterates the U.S. goal of reaching 100 percent carbon pollution‑free electricity by 2035 via multiple cost‑effective pathways that yield substantial reductions this decade.
- Press Secretary Psaki notes that tightening tailpipe emissions standards and improving vehicle efficiency are planned steps to reduce transportation emissions independent of infrastructure legislation.
- Press Secretary Psaki confirms that President Biden wants the American Jobs Plan enacted in the summer, leaving time to show concrete action before the Glasgow climate summit.
#### Tax policy, markets, and climate‑aligned investment
Switching to domestic economic policy, Psaki addresses reports that the forthcoming American Families Plan will include tax increases on higher‑income individuals and capital gains changes to pay for “human infrastructure” investments like education and childcare. She reiterates Biden’s pledge not to raise taxes on anyone earning under $400,000 and stresses that the administration’s economic team believes proposed tax changes will not harm long‑term investment. When asked about a stock market drop attributed to tax rumors, she maintains that modernizing infrastructure and investing in families justify asking wealthier Americans and corporations to contribute more. Psaki frames these revenue measures as part of a broader strategy to create jobs and maintain economic growth while tackling climate and social priorities.
- Press Secretary Psaki confirms that President Biden remains committed to not raising taxes on individuals earning under $400,000 per year and says this will be reflected in the American Families Plan.
- Press Secretary Psaki says the President will outline the American Families Plan, including both investments and “payfors,” in his April 28 joint session address.
- Press Secretary Psaki responds to questions about possible capital gains rate increases for high‑income investors by saying the administration’s view is that such changes will not have a negative impact on economic growth.
- Press Secretary Psaki contrasts Biden’s expected record of creating roughly 1 million jobs in his first 100 days with the previous administration’s job losses, arguing that critics of the current economic strategy supported less successful approaches.
#### Republican infrastructure counterproposal and bipartisan prospects
Psaki confirms that the White House views a Republican “slimmer” infrastructure proposal, focused on areas like roads, water, and broadband, as a good‑faith opening bid, despite its much smaller topline compared to the American Jobs Plan. She explains that staff‑level briefings and detailed exchanges will be the next steps, with Biden planning to invite lawmakers to the White House after his joint session address. Later, she contrasts this process with the COVID relief negotiations, noting that the American Rescue Plan was treated as an emergency requiring swift action, while infrastructure and climate investments have more time for bipartisan engagement and possibly multiple legislative vehicles. The administration signals openness to different mechanisms and smaller pieces while insisting that inaction is unacceptable.
- Press Secretary Psaki says the administration considers the new Republican infrastructure outline a “legitimate starting point” for conversation and a good‑faith effort.
- Press Secretary Psaki describes upcoming steps as detailed staff‑level briefings, continued exchanges of ideas, and eventual White House meetings with legislators after the President’s address to Congress.
- Press Secretary Psaki explains that the American Rescue Plan was an emergency measure requiring rapid passage, whereas infrastructure and climate investments allow for more time to pursue bipartisan solutions.
- Press Secretary Psaki signals flexibility, suggesting that infrastructure components could move as smaller packages or through various mechanisms, but stresses that failing to act is the only unacceptable outcome.
#### Mask guidance, vaccines, and White House COVID protocols
Psaki fields several questions about mask‑wearing, vaccination status within the White House, and potential changes to CDC guidance for vaccinated individuals. She reiterates that Biden will rely on health and medical experts for timing any updates to mask recommendations, including outdoors, and that the administration will follow CDC guidance. On internal protocols, she notes that vaccinating staff—especially those who interact closely with the President—is a priority, but she does not yet have exact figures and promises to provide them. She emphasizes that the White House continues to enforce strict measures such as N95 masks in meetings, a limited on‑site footprint, and telework for many staff.
- Press Secretary Psaki declines to preempt CDC decisions on mask guidance for vaccinated people outdoors, saying the administration will follow expert recommendations.
- Press Secretary Psaki answers that Biden’s decision on whether to extend his “100 days of masking” call is under discussion with health experts, with no announcement yet.
- Press Secretary Psaki says vaccinating White House staff, particularly those around the President, is a priority but acknowledges that not every staffer has been vaccinated yet.
- Press Secretary Psaki describes current White House safety measures, including N95 mask use in meetings, limited in‑person staffing, and extensive remote work.

The question reflects ongoing speculation ahead of Armenian Genocide Remembrance Day and broader foreign‑policy considerations with Turkey.

The administration’s stated goal is to reduce tensions both along the border and in the broader U.S.–Russia relationship.

#### D.C. statehood and voting representation

She calls the denial of congressional voting representation and full self‑government to 712,000 District residents a violation of core American principles like “no taxation without representation” and consent of the governed. The administration issued a formal Statement of Administration Policy backing H.R. 51 and pledges to work with Congress toward passage, despite anticipated Senate obstacles. Psaki also notes that many D.C. residents are active or retired military personnel who currently lack the full rights enjoyed by other Americans.
- Press Secretary Psaki states that President Biden strongly supports D.C. statehood and that the administration issued a Statement of Administration Policy endorsing H.R. 51.

#### Border issues, Vanessa Guillén case, and Hispanic Caucus discussion

#### Biden’s personal time and lifestyle
In a lighter exchange, a reporter asks how Biden spends his evenings and weekends. Psaki explains that he prioritizes time with his family, including Dr. Jill Biden, his children, and grandchildren, and that he enjoys movies and time with his two dogs. She notes that, like many Americans, he uses limited free time to “take a breath” amid the demands of the presidency. The brief discussion humanizes the President without touching on policy.
- Press Secretary Psaki says Biden spends much of his limited free time with his family, including his grandchildren and children.
- Press Secretary Psaki mentions that Biden enjoys watching movies and spending time with his two dogs.
- Press Secretary Psaki characterizes Biden’s off‑hours as similar to other Americans who use weekends and evenings to relax and reconnect with loved ones.
