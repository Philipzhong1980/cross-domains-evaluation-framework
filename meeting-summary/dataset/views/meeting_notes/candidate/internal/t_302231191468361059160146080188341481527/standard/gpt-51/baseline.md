### Overview
The Visual Arts Committee (VAC) meeting focuses on approving prior minutes, hearing public input, and refining both future agenda priorities and core processes for selecting and managing public art. After unanimously approving the October 12 minutes, the committee listens to public oral communications, including a local artist, Naya, who introduces a conceptually driven project responding to El Camino Real mission bells and is directed by staff to follow up via email for guidance on Mountain View’s public art process, and community member Bruce England, who raises usability issues with agenda PDFs and meeting links that John Lang confirms he is already working on with document processing. In reviewing upcoming agenda topics, John outlines major downtown-related CIP projects, public art strategy updates, K5 policy augmentation, and a potential joint session with the Downtown Committee, while members such as Susi Merhar and Jesse Cupp emphasize early VAC involvement in the pedestrian mall plan, landscape integration, and emerging opportunities like art in protected bike lanes. A scheduled AB 361 action is rendered moot when John reports new direction from the City Clerk requiring all advisory bodies to return to in-person meetings as of February 1, 2023, prompting questions from members about health, hybrid meeting logistics, and fairness, which John advises they route to the Clerk’s office for further consideration. The core of the meeting is an extended discussion of lessons learned on public art scale and placement in parks and other CIP projects, where John relays Public Works suggestions such as leveraging 3D models when available, improving scale communication, organizing Brown Act-compliant site visits, and sharing playground design details, while committee members propose engaging park neighbors more directly, adopting a standard checklist for artist coordination, defining budget thresholds for more iterative, stipend-supported selection processes, and building contractual progress checkpoints to reduce unpleasant surprises between concept approval and installation. Staff then provides brief status updates on the Aquatic Center, Fayette Park, and Rengstorff Park maintenance/tennis building projects, noting that construction is progressing and that some art contracts await insurance resolution before final mural concepts return to the VAC, and updates the committee on the Center for the Performing Arts gallery call for artists, which has 29 current submissions on CaFÉ and will require jurying before the December 13 meeting to select roughly five to eight exhibitors for 2023. The session closes with committee comments, including a request from Vice Chair Toni Hsu for clear protocol on how members should respond to direct public inquiries—a question John has already forwarded to the City Clerk’s office—before Chair Jesse Cupp adjourns the meeting.

#### Public art scale, placement, and process improvements (parks and CIP)
The committee and staff conduct an in-depth lessons-learned discussion on public art scale, placement, and process, prompted by recent experiences at Pyramid and Mora Parks. John Lang relays feedback from Public Works and explores tools such as 3D modeling, clearer scale comparisons, site visits, and better coordination with playground design to help the VAC understand context. Committee members propose engaging park neighbors more directly, creating checklists for artist coordination, defining thresholds for more iterative selection processes with stipends, and adding contractual checkpoints to align expectations between approved concepts and final work. The group seeks to clarify how to balance artist autonomy with the city’s need for predictable, contextually appropriate public art outcomes.
- John Lang frames the discussion as a response to concerns about art scale, proximity, and fit raised after Pyramid Park’s art installation and describes it as an opportunity to refine K5 CIP art processes going forward.
- John Lang reports that larger CIP projects often have 3D models that could be shared with the VAC to visualize art in context, while smaller projects likely cannot justify formal 3D modeling.
- John Lang commits to providing clearer scale references in future staff reports, noting that simple dimensions like “six feet” can be interpreted very differently without comparative visuals.
- John Lang suggests that the VAC could conduct group site visits with Public Works for future projects to better understand context and potential art locations, acknowledging these would need Brown Act-compliant noticing.
- Public Works staff, via John Lang, recommend sharing playground equipment design, color palettes, and look-and-feel with the VAC, because these elements often are already selected or procured by the time art is considered.
- John Lang notes that artists sometimes make on-site adjustments to elements such as the height or placement of smaller components (e.g., a butterfly or bee on a sculpture), but that the committee should explicitly discuss orientation and visibility during selection so expectations are clear.
- Clifford Bryant stresses that the VAC should see playground equipment plans and colors when selecting art for parks so artists can design pieces that complement, rather than conflict with, the overall park environment.
- Clifford Bryant asks whether the neighborhood residents engaged by Parks in play equipment selection can also be engaged in the art selection process, and John Lang agrees to explore how VAC might tap into that existing outreach without duplicating effort.
- Don Whitebread cautions against overcomplicating public involvement and suggests that the VAC continue to represent neighborhood interests while potentially gathering targeted input from already-engaged community members.
- Chair Jesse Cupp supports group site visits and notes that even viewing an undeveloped site can help members understand adjacent roads, trees, and housing and make better art choices.
- John Lang confirms that group site visits are feasible if properly noticed under the Brown Act and proposes wording such as a noticed meeting on-site to discuss public art placement.
- Toot Too Thomson recommends developing a standard checklist for art approvals covering items such as color, size, material, orientation, and height, with flexibility to leave some choices to the artist when the committee does not have strong preferences.
- Susi Merhar suggests slightly enlarging the pool of finalists for some projects so the committee can apply additional contextual information and negotiate details such as dimensions and placement with a manageable subset of artists.
- Vice Chair Toni Hsu points out that more iterative processes (such as the RFQ-style approach used at Rengstorff Park Aquatic Center) require additional work from artists and argues that the city must provide stipends when asking for more detailed proposals or revisions.
- Chair Jesse Cupp agrees that finalists must know up front whether they will be expected to produce additional concept work, receive a stipend, or simply submit a single proposal that will stand as their final design if selected.
- John Lang proposes analyzing past projects to determine the dollar thresholds at which the VAC has historically used more involved, stipend-supported selection methods and returning with that data so the committee can formalize process “path A” and “path B” based on per-piece art budgets.
- Chair Jesse Cupp favors setting thresholds based on the budget for each individual art piece rather than the total CIP budget, since large projects often fund multiple, differently scaled artworks.
- Toot Too Thomson argues for maintaining flexibility to decide case-by-case which aspects (e.g., orientation or height) matter most for each piece rather than relying solely on dollar thresholds, while others emphasize the benefits of predictable process for artists.
- An attending artist, Alette, suggests that 3D visualization tools would help the VAC envision how 3D art will look in its environment and shares her own practice of building simple virtual “rooms” before installing exhibitions; John Lang responds that some CIP projects already produce 3D models and these can be leveraged where available.
- Clifford Bryant recalls that the colors he expected for the Pyramid Park mobile did not fully match the installed work and says he wants checkpoints to avoid being “thoroughly surprised” in negative ways, while also noting pleasantly surprising alignment at Mora Park.
- John Lang explains that currently the artist’s concept materials approved by the VAC and Council are attached to the contract and there is no built-in iterative review between Council approval and final installation.
- In response to concerns, John Lang suggests adding contractual requirements for artists to provide progress documentation at milestones (e.g., 25, 50, and 75 percent of fabrication) so staff can brief the VAC and verify alignment with approved concepts.
- Chair Jesse Cupp expresses appreciation for receiving work-in-progress photos but cautions against micromanaging small details or expecting photorealistic matches between renderings and final works.
- Susi Merhar clarifies that she wants a “happy middle” that allows the committee to catch significant deviations without unduly interfering with artistic process, and she emphasizes the importance of the VAC seeing finalized design concepts before contracts are executed in multi-step projects like the maintenance building murals.
- John Lang confirms that once insurance issues are resolved for the Rengstorff Park maintenance and tennis building murals, staff will bring back design concepts informed by Youth and Senior Advisory Committees for VAC review and attachment to the final contracts.
