### Overview
The briefing, led by NSC spokesman John Kirby with questions from multiple reporters, focuses on U.S. policy responses to the Gaza conflict, Ukraine war funding, and related security and diplomatic issues. Kirby opens by announcing Israel’s decision, after U.S. urging, to open the Kerem Shalom crossing for direct humanitarian aid into Gaza while stressing that Washington continues pressing Israel, including through Secretary Austin’s current visit, to minimize civilian casualties and improve deconfliction mechanisms following incidents such as the deadly church shooting and the mistaken killing of three hostages. He describes intense but non-imminent efforts by U.S. officials including Jake Sullivan, Brett McGurk, and David Satterfield to secure another hostage deal and humanitarian pause, underscores U.S. support for a revitalized Palestinian Authority role in post-conflict Gaza alongside a longer-term two-state solution, and rejects comparisons between Israeli conduct and Russian or Hamas war aims, arguing that Israel is not deliberately targeting civilians even as the U.S. repeatedly raises concerns over civilian harm. On Ukraine, Kirby explains that the Pentagon has allocated remaining replenishment authority, that only one more sizable aid package is possible under current law, and that congressional action on new funding is urgent given ongoing Russian attacks and attempted offensives, while also highlighting the U.S. defense industry’s central role in sustaining Ukraine and strengthening U.S. capabilities. He confirms active negotiations on a border security compromise linked to Ukraine and Israel aid, outlines efforts to bolster the Combined Maritime Forces against Houthi attacks in the Red Sea, and warns against escalation on Israel’s northern border with Hezbollah and attacks on the Lebanese Armed Forces. Kirby declines to discuss CIA Director Bill Burns’s travel, private-sector transactions like Nippon Steel’s bid for U.S. Steel, or campaign rhetoric about immigrants, and he offers no specifics yet on the timing or content of an NDAA signing statement, framing the administration throughout as engaged, cautious about sensitive negotiations, and focused on balancing security objectives with humanitarian and legal concerns.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Opening of Kerem Shalom crossing
Kirby announces that Israel has opened the Kerem Shalom border crossing for direct delivery of humanitarian assistance into Gaza, a step the U.S. has been pressing Israel to take. He notes that roughly 80 trucks transited Kerem Shalom yesterday and, combined with Rafah, nearly 200 trucks entered Gaza in a single day. This matters because it modestly increases humanitarian aid flows to civilians amid ongoing conflict. The U.S. frames this as part of its broader push to alleviate Palestinian suffering while continuing to support Israel’s security operations.
- Mr. Kirby states that Israel informed Jake Sullivan on Friday of its decision to open the Kerem Shalom crossing for direct humanitarian assistance into Gaza.
- Mr. Kirby reports that the Kerem Shalom crossing opened Sunday and that about 80 trucks passed there yesterday, contributing to nearly 200 total aid trucks entering Gaza via Kerem Shalom and Rafah combined.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that the United States has been working since the beginning of the conflict to get humanitarian aid into Gaza and will continue these efforts.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that the administration has long urged Israel to open Kerem Shalom and views this step as significant for improving aid access.
#### Civilian protection and U.S. messages to Israel
Kirby reiterates that the U.S. continues pressing Israel to minimize civilian casualties and conduct operations as surgically as possible. He notes Secretary Austin is in Israel delivering the same message directly to Israeli leaders. This focus on operational conduct is portrayed as central to U.S. engagement with Israel during the conflict. The administration seeks to balance support for Israel’s right to self-defense with pressure to reduce harm to civilians and protect humanitarian operations.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. continues to urge Israel to do everything possible to prevent civilian casualties and act with surgical precision in Gaza.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Secretary Austin is in Tel Aviv delivering the same civilian-protection message to Defense Minister Gallant and other Israeli officials.
- Mr. Kirby says these concerns about civilian harm and operational conduct are a recurring theme in U.S. engagements with Israel.
#### Ukraine aid funding and replenishment authority
Kirby outlines that the Pentagon has allocated the remaining replenishment authority used to restock U.S. supplies and replace weapons sent to Ukraine. He explains that this authority has enabled expanded production lines in U.S. states, supporting American jobs and strengthening the defense industrial base. The administration expects to announce one final Ukraine aid package this month, after which replenishment authority will be exhausted. Kirby stresses that Congress must act quickly on new funding because continued U.S. support is framed as critical to Ukraine’s defense and to U.S. national security interests.
- Mr. Kirby cites Comptroller Mike McCord’s notification to Congress that the remaining funding to restock U.S. supplies for Ukraine has been allocated.
- Mr. Kirby explains that replenishment authority allows the Defense Department to purchase new weapons and equipment from American manufacturers, supporting U.S. jobs and production lines.
- Mr. Kirby says the administration still plans one more Ukraine aid package this month but will have no replenishment authority left after that package.
- Mr. Kirby quotes Mike McCord’s message that continued assistance for Ukraine is in the U.S. national interest and vitally needed for Ukraine’s fight for freedom.
- Mr. Kirby urges Congress to act without delay to approve additional Ukraine funding once current authorities are exhausted.
#### Russian offensive and need for continued Ukraine support
Kirby situates the funding issue within ongoing Russian attacks and attempted offensives in eastern Ukraine. He describes nightly barrages of drones and missiles against Ukrainian cities and notes that Russia’s ground offensive has been blunted in part because Ukraine is well-armed. The administration argues that stopping or delaying U.S. support would undermine Ukraine’s ability to hold the line. Kirby frames congressional inaction as a direct risk to both Ukrainian democracy and U.S. national security interests.
- Mr. Kirby reports that in the past 24 hours Russia has launched dozens of drones and at least one cruise missile at Ukrainian cities in near-nightly barrages.
- Mr. Kirby says Russian forces are trying to mount a ground offensive in eastern Ukraine but have not been very successful, partly because Ukrainians are well-armed and well-resourced.
- Mr. Kirby insists Ukraine still needs U.S. help and argues it is well past time for Congress to act to support freedom and democracy and protect U.S. security interests.
#### Hostage negotiations and humanitarian pause prospects
Responding to questions, Kirby describes intensive U.S. efforts to secure a new hostage deal in Gaza, which would be tied to another humanitarian pause and more aid. He declines to discuss CIA Director Bill Burns’s travel but says Jake Sullivan raised hostage issues heavily on his recent regional trip, and U.S. envoys McGurk and Satterfield work on this nearly hourly. While negotiators have held some recent conversations, Kirby stresses that no new deal is imminent, and he refuses to discuss parameters publicly to avoid harming talks. He also acknowledges acute concern about the passage of time for hostages held in harsh conditions and says the U.S. is urgently trying to get both sides back to the table.
- Mr. Kirby refuses to comment on CIA Director Bill Burns’s travel or meetings related to hostage talks, referring questions to the CIA.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. continues to work very hard to secure another hostage deal in Gaza that would include a humanitarian pause and more humanitarian assistance.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Jake Sullivan recently discussed hostage negotiations with regional partners and tested how close the parties might be to another agreement.
- Mr. Kirby indicates that negotiators have had conversations in recent days but says he cannot claim that a new deal is imminent.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that U.S. officials, including Brett McGurk and David Satterfield, are working the hostage issue almost by the hour.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges concern about the “tyranny of the clock,” noting that every minute hostages remain in captivity is a minute they risk torture, rape, beatings, or denial of medical care.
- Mr. Kirby refuses to outline negotiating parameters in public, arguing that doing so could torpedo chances for another pause and hostage release.
#### Israeli killing of three hostages and rules of engagement
Kirby addresses Israeli forces’ killing of three hostages in Gaza and questions about Israeli rules of engagement (ROE). He declines to discuss Israeli ROE directly but notes the IDF quickly admitted a mistake and is expected to conduct forensic analysis to understand what happened. He says such incidents sometimes lead to changes in ROE, but sometimes reflect enforcement or interpretation issues at the unit level rather than systemic rules. From Washington, he urges caution about prematurely blaming the rules themselves without full details.
- Mr. Kirby says he will not discuss Israeli rules of engagement, just as he would not publicly discuss U.S. rules of engagement.
- Mr. Kirby notes that the IDF quickly admitted it made a mistake in killing three hostages and says he expects Israel to conduct forensics to prevent a recurrence.
- Mr. Kirby calls the incident tragic and traumatic, including for the soldiers involved, and stresses the need for a thorough investigation.
- Mr. Kirby explains that tactical events like this sometimes require review of rules of engagement, but sometimes the issue is enforcement or interpretation by units or individual soldiers.
- Mr. Kirby urges caution about blaming Israeli rules of engagement from Washington at this early stage without full details of the incident.
#### Border security negotiations tied to Ukraine and Israel aid
Kirby confirms that active negotiations are underway in Washington on a border security deal linked to Ukraine and Israel funding. He says the White House participates in weekend talks and that the President is willing to negotiate in good faith and make compromises on immigration policy and border security. However, he avoids specific details to prevent undercutting the negotiations publicly. The administration’s message is that progress is being pursued quietly and that the border and supplemental aid issues are now intertwined.
- Mr. Kirby confirms there were active discussions over the weekend about a border deal that would unlock Ukraine and Israel aid, with the White House directly involved.
- Mr. Kirby says President Biden is willing to negotiate in good faith and make compromises on both immigration policy and border security measures.
- Mr. Kirby declines to provide specifics on the border negotiations, saying he does not want to negotiate in public or put talks at risk.
#### Red Sea maritime coalition against Houthi attacks
Kirby discusses efforts to bolster an existing multinational naval framework, the Combined Maritime Forces under U.S. Fifth Fleet in Bahrain, in response to Houthi attacks in the Red Sea. He says the U.S. is working to strengthen, bolster, and newly operationalize this structure for the current threat environment. Secretary Austin is scheduled to hold regional meetings focused on this maritime effort and may announce further details afterward. The goal is to protect shipping from Houthi attacks through a more active coalition presence.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. is working to strengthen and operationalize the Combined Maritime Forces under Fifth Fleet to counter Houthi attacks in the Red Sea.
- Mr. Kirby explains that the effort involves expanding how the existing maritime force operates in ways it had not before the Houthi campaign.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Secretary Austin will hold regional meetings on this topic and is expected to provide more specifics afterward, though Kirby will not preempt him.
#### Tensions on Israel–Lebanon border and escalation risks
In response to questions about northern Israel and Lebanon, Kirby says the U.S. is concerned about continued cross-border attacks and wants to avoid opening a northern front. He emphasizes that the conflict should remain focused on Hamas after October 7 and that the U.S. is in active discussions with Israel about activity along the border. While affirming Israel’s right to self-defense, he warns against a full-scale conflict with Hezbollah and expresses concern over attacks on the Lebanese Armed Forces, which the U.S. sees as outside the conflict. The U.S. is closely monitoring developments and engaging diplomatically to prevent wider war, without disclosing specific proposals for a long-term arrangement.
- Mr. Kirby says continued attacks across the Israel–Lebanon border are of concern and that the U.S. does not want the conflict to widen or a northern front to open.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that the U.S. wants the conflict to remain focused on Hamas in the wake of October 7th.
- Mr. Kirby confirms active U.S. discussions with Israeli counterparts about northern border activities.
- Mr. Kirby affirms Israel’s right to self-defense but says the U.S. does not want to see a full-on conflict between Hezbollah and Israel.
- Mr. Kirby calls attacks on the Lebanese Armed Forces deeply concerning because the LAF is not a party to the conflict and does not seek to be.
#### “Day after” governance of Gaza and Palestinian Authority role
Kirby addresses questions about post-conflict governance in Gaza and Israeli resistance to a Palestinian Authority (PA) role. He reiterates that the U.S. believes Palestinians deserve transparent, competent governance and that the ultimate solution is a two-state outcome, though not imminent. In the interim, the administration maintains that a revamped and revitalized PA should have a significant role in governing Gaza and shaping the future political framework. Kirby notes that Jake Sullivan met Mahmoud Abbas in Ramallah specifically to explore these possibilities, and he underscores that this remains President Biden’s position regardless of Israeli public statements.
- Mr. Kirby states that Palestinians deserve a vote and a voice in their future, with competent, accessible, transparent governance that meets their needs and aspirations for peace and security.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that the U.S. sees a two-state solution as the ultimate answer, even if it will not be implemented soon.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. believes a revamped and revitalized Palestinian Authority should and could play a significant role in post-conflict Gaza governance.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Jake Sullivan met with Mahmoud Abbas in Ramallah to discuss potential roles for the Palestinian Authority in Gaza’s future governance.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that the administration has not changed its view that this approach is best for both Palestinians and Israelis, even as he declines to speak for Prime Minister Netanyahu’s position.
#### Church shooting incident in Gaza and deconfliction concerns
Kirby responds to questions about reports that an Israeli sniper killed a mother and daughter in a church compound, which has drawn international condemnation. He calls every civilian death a tragedy, expresses condolences, and confirms the U.S. has raised specific concerns with Israel about this incident and ensuring wounded can be safely evacuated. He uses the event to highlight the importance of reliable deconfliction mechanisms to protect civilians and humanitarian workers during Israeli operations. Kirby acknowledges that the U.S. believes more can be done by Israel to improve deconfliction and reduce miscalculation and mistakes.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. is closely following alarming reports from a church compound where a mother and daughter were reportedly killed and calls every civilian death a tragedy.
- Mr. Kirby states that the United States has raised concerns about this specific incident with the Israeli government, including the need to safely evacuate the wounded for medical care.
- Mr. Kirby underscores the need for effective deconfliction channels so Israeli operations against Hamas do not endanger sheltering civilians or humanitarian workers.
- Mr. Kirby notes that the need for better deconfliction has been raised in multiple channels, including directly between President Biden and Prime Minister Netanyahu, and by Jake Sullivan and Secretary Austin.
- Mr. Kirby tells NBC’s Gabe that the U.S. believes Israel can do more on deconfliction and will continue urging improvements to reduce miscalculation and mistakes.
#### Clarifying remaining Ukraine funding and final aid package
Kirby clarifies that while the U.S. is nearly out of Ukraine funding, there is enough replenishment authority left for one final “good” aid package before the end of the month. He refers reporters to the Pentagon for precise figures and composition of that package, saying he has not seen a draft and that DOD is unlikely to preview contents. His comments reinforce urgency around congressional action while signaling that one more tranche of support is still forthcoming under existing authorities.
- Mr. Kirby says there is sufficient replenishment authority for one more substantial Ukraine aid package before the end of the month.
- Mr. Kirby directs questions about the exact remaining amount and package composition to the Defense Department, noting he has not seen a draft.
- Mr. Kirby suggests the Pentagon is unlikely to provide an advance, detailed preview of the aid package’s content.
#### Standards on alleged Israeli violations of international law
Vivian presses Kirby on why the U.S. has not publicly cited Israeli violations of international law despite incidents like the church shooting and deaths of journalists, contrasting this with early, forceful U.S. language on Russian war crimes in Ukraine. Kirby responds that the administration will not adjudicate each tactical incident or act as judge and jury on every event. He distinguishes Israel from Russia and Hamas by arguing that, unlike Putin’s forces and Hamas, Israel does not make the torture, rape, and slaughter of civilians a war aim or operational objective, even though civilian casualties are occurring and deeply concerning. He stresses that the U.S. consistently raises concerns about civilian harm with Israel, including specific cases, and urges more caution, but says the administration has not seen evidence that deliberately targeting civilians is part of Israel’s war aims.
- Mr. Kirby clarifies that the administration will not adjudicate each tactical event in Gaza or act as judge and jury over every incident.
- Mr. Kirby recalls that in a prior question about journalists’ deaths he said the U.S. had no indication they were deliberately targeted by the Israeli military.
- Mr. Kirby draws a contrast with Russia’s war in Ukraine, saying torture, rape, slaughter of civilians, and attacks on civilian infrastructure are baked into Putin’s war plan and are being documented as war crimes.
- Mr. Kirby asserts that Israel, unlike Russia and Hamas, is not making the slaughter, torture, and rape of civilians a war aim or operational necessity.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that the U.S. believes the right number of civilian casualties is zero and continues to urge Israel to be as careful, deliberate, and surgical as possible to avoid a tactical victory turning into a strategic defeat.
- Mr. Kirby confirms that the U.S. raised the church incident specifically with Israel and says such concerns are regularly conveyed, even as the administration has not concluded that deliberate targeting of civilians is an Israeli war objective.
#### Hamas hostage video and propaganda tactics

He notes Hamas’s reputation for brutality toward captives and says that if the video is authentic, it would exemplify the group’s depravity and use of psychological terror against innocent life. He uses the question to contrast Israel’s obligations under the law of armed conflict with Hamas’s complete disregard for those norms. Kirby underscores that Hamas is both a terrorist and military organization that does not even pretend to follow international humanitarian law.
- Mr. Kirby says he has not seen the newly released hostage video and cannot validate its authenticity, so he will not comment on specifics.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Hamas has a reputation for brutal treatment of captives and hostages.

- Mr. Kirby contrasts Israel’s status as a democracy that the U.S. urges to comply with the law of armed conflict with Hamas as a terrorist and military organization that does not pretend to follow those laws.

No policy position or national security assessment is provided in this forum. The topic is acknowledged but not substantively discussed.

He instead focuses on the administration’s policy posture, noting that Biden proposed comprehensive immigration reform on day one that Congress has not acted on. He emphasizes ongoing good-faith negotiations with Congress on border security and immigration policies, tied to funding for Ukraine and Israel. The White House presents itself as willing to compromise on both policy and security measures while avoiding commentary on politically charged language.
- Mr. Kirby declines to weigh in on campaign rhetoric such as “poisoning the blood of our country,” saying it would be inappropriate for the National Security Council to do so.

Kirby answers a question about a potential “Plan B” for Ukraine and the role of the private sector. He reiterates that the administration has only one more aid package available under current authority and insists new supplemental funding is essential to maintain support. He then highlights how the U.S. defense industry is already a key partner, with the administration working to strengthen production lines, develop new systems, and build resilience in both the U.S. and Ukrainian defense industrial bases. This partnership is portrayed as a force multiplier that advances both Ukraine’s defense and U.S. national security, provided Congress supplies the necessary funding.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that the U.S. has one more Ukraine aid package available under current replenishment authority and needs supplemental funding to continue support beyond this month.
