### Overview
The meeting centers on the Biden administration’s gender equity, pandemic recovery, and governance agenda, featuring remarks from White House Press Secretary Jen Psaki and newly appointed Gender Policy Council co‑chairs Julissa Reynoso and Jennifer Klein. Reynoso and Klein explain that President Biden has signed two executive orders: one formally establishing a White House Gender Policy Council with a broad domestic and foreign policy mandate to advance gender equity—including for women of color and LGBTQ+ people—and another directing the Department of Education to review and potentially revise Title IX–related rules to ensure all students can learn free from sexual violence while maintaining fair procedures for all parties. They describe a whole‑of‑government strategy, specialized staffing on gender‑based violence and women and girls of color, and active coordination with other White House councils, Cabinet agencies, and external partners, while also tying the Council’s work to immediate challenges such as women’s disproportionate economic losses during COVID‑19 and longer‑term structural reforms on caregiving, paid leave, and workplace discrimination. Psaki then broadens the briefing to highlight the nearly completed American Rescue Plan as a historic, progressive recovery package delivering direct checks, expanded child tax credits, unemployment extensions, rental and childcare support, and lower health premiums, while previewing President Biden’s upcoming primetime address marking one year of the pandemic. Throughout extensive questioning, she defends the administration’s commitment to bipartisanship despite unanimous Republican opposition to the ARP, reiterates Biden’s preference not to change Senate filibuster rules, and reaffirms priorities on voting rights, a $15 minimum wage, and future infrastructure and caregiving investments. Reporters press her and Reynoso on conditions for unaccompanied migrant children at the border and the limited transparency around a recent delegation visit; Psaki confirms visits to a CBP facility and the Carrizo Springs shelter, stresses efforts to expand safe capacity and move children quickly into appropriate placements, acknowledges the need to better communicate that most migrants are turned away, and promises future media access to facilities consistent with privacy and COVID safeguards. Additional exchanges cover the administration’s inclusive approach to gender (including openness to examining a third gender marker on federal IDs), its response to state transgender sports bans, its stance on Russian anti‑vaccine disinformation, Houthi attacks on Saudi Arabia, U.S. commitments to Taiwan, potential vaccine exports, and the Merck–Johnson & Johnson manufacturing partnership. Across all topics, Psaki underscores that COVID‑19 response and economic relief remain the organizing focus of Biden’s agenda, that the White House will pursue both immediate and structural gender and racial equity goals, and that the President will increase direct engagement with the press through a formal press conference before month’s end.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Creation of Gender Policy Council
Julissa Reynoso outlines President Biden’s executive order establishing the White House Gender Policy Council with authority over both domestic and foreign policy. She emphasizes that full participation of women and girls is essential to economic well‑being, health, security, and democratic stability. The Council’s mandate explicitly includes addressing intersecting forms of discrimination affecting women of color and LGBTQ+ people. The Council is positioned as a core mechanism in the administration’s effort to embed gender equity across all federal decision‑making.
- Ms. Reynoso states that President Biden signed an executive order formally establishing the Gender Policy Council within the Executive Office of the President with an explicit role in domestic and foreign policy development.
- Ms. Reynoso emphasizes that women’s full participation in society is essential to reducing poverty, promoting economic growth, increasing access to education, improving health outcomes, advancing political stability, and fostering democracy.
- Ms. Reynoso notes that the Council will prioritize the unique needs of those facing multiple forms of discrimination, including people of color and LGBTQ+ individuals.
- Ms. Reynoso explains that the administration views gender equality as a matter of human rights, justice, and fairness.
#### Gender equity priorities amid COVID-19
Reynoso links the creation of the Council to the disproportionate impact of COVID‑19 on women, particularly in health, economic, and caregiving dimensions. She argues the pandemic has intensified pre‑existing barriers facing women and girls. Against this backdrop, the administration frames gender equity as central to recovery and to confronting systemic racism and discrimination. The Council’s work is therefore tied directly to pandemic response and long‑term structural reform.
- Ms. Reynoso says a year into COVID‑19 it is clear that women have been disproportionately impacted by the public health, economic, and caregiving crises.
- Ms. Reynoso explains that the pandemic has exacerbated long‑standing barriers that historically impacted women and girls.
- Ms. Reynoso notes that as the country grapples with COVID‑19 and systemic racism, President Biden believes a government‑wide focus on uplifting the rights of all women and girls is needed.
#### Council policy agenda and scope
Reynoso details the Council’s substantive agenda, spanning economic security, labor force participation, caregiving, health, gender‑based violence, education, leadership, and global gender equality. She mentions plans for a National Action Plan on gender‑based violence and coordination across diplomacy, development, trade, and defense. The agenda aims to address both immediate needs and long‑term structural inequities. This establishes a broad, cross‑sectoral remit for the Council.
- Ms. Reynoso says the Council will combat systemic bias and discrimination, including sexual harassment.
- Ms. Reynoso explains the Council will work to increase women’s economic security by addressing structural barriers to labor force participation, reducing wage and wealth gaps, and supporting caregiving needs and care workers.
- Ms. Reynoso states the Council will ensure access to comprehensive healthcare and strengthen efforts to prevent and respond to gender‑based violence, including through a National Action Plan with a government‑wide approach.
- Ms. Reynoso notes the Council will promote equity and opportunity in education and leadership and advance gender equality globally through diplomacy, development, trade, and defense.
#### Council operations, staffing, and integration
Jennifer Klein elaborates on how the Gender Policy Council will operate, emphasizing a whole‑of‑government strategy and measurement framework. The executive order requires a national gender equity strategy and annual progress reports, including recommendations on policies, programs, budgets, and data collection. Staffing includes a Senior Advisor on Gender‑Based Violence and multiple Special Assistants, including one focused on women and girls of color. The Council will align with racial equity work, coordinate with Cabinet agencies and White House policy councils, and engage non‑governmental and international partners.
- Ms. Klein explains that the executive order requires the Council to submit a government‑wide strategy for advancing gender equity and equality and an annual report measuring progress.
- Ms. Klein says the strategy will evaluate and recommend improvements to policies, programs, budgets, and data collection across agencies using a gender lens.
- Ms. Klein notes that the Council staff will include a Special Assistant to the President and Senior Advisor on Gender‑Based Violence and at least two additional Special Assistants for Gender Policy.
- Ms. Klein states that one Special Assistant will focus on advancing equity for Black, Indigenous, Latina, and women and girls of color.
- Ms. Klein emphasizes that the Council’s work will be fully integrated with racial equity efforts led by the Domestic Policy Council and coordinated with Cabinet members, White House policy councils, the Office of Public Engagement, and external partners including NGOs, state and local and tribal entities, the private sector, and foreign and multilateral organizations.
#### Title IX review and education free from sexual violence
Klein describes the second executive order directing the Department of Education to review all Title IX‑related regulations, guidance, and policies for consistency with the administration’s position that all students deserve education free from sexual violence and discrimination. The Secretary is empowered to suspend, revise, or rescind Trump‑era actions that conflict with this policy. The order affirms protections against discrimination based on sex, sexual orientation, gender identity, and recognizes compounded discrimination based on race, disability, and national origin. The administration also stresses the need for fair and equitable procedures for all parties in campus sexual misconduct cases.
- Ms. Klein explains that the Title IX executive order directs the Education Department to review its regulations, orders, guidance, and policies to ensure consistency with the policy that all students be guaranteed education free from sexual violence.
- Ms. Klein says the Secretary of Education will consider suspending, revising, rescinding, or proposing new rules to alter regulations that are inconsistent with administration policy.
- Ms. Klein states that the administration’s policy is that all students are entitled to an educational environment free from sex‑based discrimination, including sexual harassment, sexual violence, and discrimination based on sexual orientation or gender identity.
- Ms. Klein notes the order recognizes that students may also face discrimination based on race, disability, and national origin and that LGBTQ+ students experience significant discrimination.
- Ms. Klein affirms that the administration takes seriously the responsibility to ensure fair and equitable procedures for everyone involved in sexual misconduct processes.

#### Due process and Title IX regulations
A reporter asks whether there is anything wrong with Trump‑era Title IX regulations that require due process for accused students. Klein responds that the administration supports fair and full process for all parties and that the Education Secretary will review the regulations in that spirit. She avoids prejudging changes to specific provisions, reiterating that the core policy is to guarantee education free from sexual violence with fair procedures for complainants and respondents alike. This frames the review as balancing survivor protections and due process rather than choosing one over the other.
- An unidentified reporter asks what is wrong with a Title IX regulation requiring due process for those accused of sexual misconduct.
- Ms. Klein says there is nothing wrong with the idea that both accused and accuser should have a fair and full process, and that this is the administration’s policy.
- Ms. Klein explains that President Biden has asked the Education Secretary to review each part of the regulations and recommend what should be revoked or rescinded, while ensuring every student has access to fair processes and education free of sexual violence.
#### Women’s workforce losses and economic response
Geoff raises concerns about declining female labor force participation, especially among Black and Latina women hit hard by the pandemic. Reynoso and Klein describe a two‑track response: immediate relief via the American Rescue Plan (ARP) and longer‑term structural reforms. They highlight ARP measures such as direct payments, expanded child tax credit, unemployment insurance, childcare stabilization, and school reopening as near‑term supports for women. Longer‑term, they point to forthcoming legislation and policy work on paid leave, affordable childcare, long‑term care, and improving conditions for paid caregivers, all aimed at correcting systemic inequities in pay, job access, and caregiving burdens.
- Geoff asks how the administration plans to boost the declining number of women in the workforce, particularly Black and Latino women disproportionately affected by the pandemic.
- Ms. Reynoso says the American Rescue Plan will significantly advance the economic interests of all Americans while acknowledging that women of color, including Latinas, Black, Asian American, and Indigenous women, have been disproportionately impacted.
- Ms. Reynoso notes that the administration is addressing accumulated factors such as sectoral job losses in female‑dominated industries, historical pay inequity, limited job access, and discrimination, with the ARP as a starting point and additional legislation expected in coming months.
- Ms. Reynoso says she and Ms. Klein will focus on long‑term structural changes to support full integration of women, particularly women of color, into the labor force and to tackle systemic discrimination and pay inequity.
- Ms. Klein adds that the ARP includes immediate relief for women such as direct payments, expanded child tax credit, unemployment insurance, safe school reopening, childcare industry stabilization, and affordability support for parents.
- Ms. Klein outlines longer‑term priorities including paid family and medical leave, more affordable childcare, better support for long‑term care of sick or aging relatives, and improved wages and dignity for paid caregivers.

She notes that specific staff within the Council are tasked with short‑term policy integration as well as long‑term planning. Klein reinforces that current crises in health, economy, and caregiving require both immediate relief and structural reforms, and she maps ARP provisions directly to women’s short‑term needs.

- Ms. Klein underscores that women are experiencing layered public health, economic, and caregiving crises and that the ARP contains multiple provisions that will bring immediate relief and help women return to work.

#### American Rescue Plan benefits and passage
Psaki shifts to a detailed briefing on the American Rescue Plan (ARP) as it nears final passage. She highlights its scale and progressive nature, citing quotes from Senator Sanders and enumerating key provisions: $1,400 checks, child tax credit expansion, unemployment insurance extension, rental and homeowner assistance, childcare investment, earned income tax credit expansion, and health insurance premium reductions. She underscores the bill’s role in cutting child poverty, supporting essential workers, and providing tools to crush the virus and reopen schools. She also notes Biden’s upcoming primetime address marking the pandemic’s one‑year anniversary and framing the ARP within the broader COVID response.
- Ms. Psaki states that the Senate‑passed American Rescue Plan moves the country a huge step closer to enacting one of the most consequential and progressive pieces of legislation in American history.
- Ms. Psaki cites Senator Sanders describing the Rescue Plan as the most significant legislation to benefit working families in modern U.S. history.
- Ms. Psaki details that $1,400 checks will go to about 158.5 million Americans, with a typical family of four making $100,000 receiving $5,600.
- Ms. Psaki notes the ARP is projected to lift 11 million people out of poverty and cut child poverty in half in one year, aided by a historic expansion of the Child Tax Credit benefiting 66 million children.
- Ms. Psaki explains the bill extends unemployment insurance for around 11 million Americans, adds tens of billions in rental and homeowner assistance focused on lower‑income and disproportionately Black and brown communities, and represents the most significant childcare investment since World War II.
- Ms. Psaki highlights that the ARP will expand the Earned Income Tax Credit to 17 million low‑income workers and significantly reduce health insurance premiums, with a family of four making $90,000 potentially saving $200 per month on premiums.
- Ms. Psaki announces that President Biden will deliver his first primetime address on Thursday to mark the one‑year anniversary of COVID‑related shutdowns, honoring sacrifices, acknowledging losses, and outlining the path back to normal.

#### Minimum wage and intra‑party tensions
A reporter raises concerns that the challenges of securing Senator Manchin’s support on ARP foreshadow difficulties on progressive priorities like a $15 minimum wage. Psaki responds by emphasizing that Democrats across the ideological spectrum just united behind a large, progressive relief bill. She reiterates Biden’s commitment to a $15 federal minimum wage and says there are no current negotiations about lowering the target. She indicates that the White House will work with Democratic leaders and others to find the best vehicle for the policy, suggesting it will be pursued separately from ARP.
- A reporter asks whether last week’s negotiations with Senator Manchin highlight a broader numbers problem for Biden’s agenda, particularly on raising the minimum wage to $15.
- Ms. Psaki counters that Democrats from Senator Manchin to Senator Sanders just supported a $1.9 trillion, highly progressive package that maintained its core elements, including $1,400 checks and school funding.
- Ms. Psaki reaffirms that President Biden remains committed to a $15 minimum wage and that there are no negotiations underway to lower that number.
- Ms. Psaki says Biden will work with Democratic leaders and anyone interested on the best legislative vehicle to advance the minimum wage increase, but notes that current focus is on the ARP.

She does not specify frequency beyond that but indicates she can raise further questions about media availability with him. This is meant to address press concerns about transparency and accessibility.

- Ms. Psaki notes that Biden has already conducted around 40 Q&A sessions since taking office but acknowledges the distinct importance of a formal press conference.
- Ms. Psaki says she will ask Biden about the future cadence of such events but offers no specific commitment beyond the upcoming session.
#### Unemployment benefits continuity and IRS backlogs
Yamiche asks whether there will be a lapse in enhanced unemployment benefits as the March 14 deadline approaches and whether IRS backlogs might delay ARP payments. Psaki does not give a firm guarantee from the podium but emphasizes Treasury Secretary Yellen’s focus on quick implementation and notes the administration’s awareness of prior gaps in delivery. She reiterates that Treasury will own the mechanics and communications around both unemployment continuity and check distribution, with the White House emphasizing urgency.
- Yamiche asks if the administration can ensure there will be no lapse in enhanced unemployment benefits around the March 14 deadline and whether IRS backlogs remain a concern.
- Ms. Psaki says Treasury Secretary Yellen is intensely focused on setting up clear processes to get assistance out as quickly as possible but defers to Treasury on specifics.
- Ms. Psaki notes that the administration is aware of gaps in previous relief rounds and is factoring those lessons into planning for ARP implementation.
- Ms. Psaki reiterates that Treasury will provide detail on mechanics, while the White House continues pressing for speed.
#### Border policy questions and public expectations
Yamiche and another reporter press for more detail on children’s experiences in border facilities and on whether migrants are hearing the administration’s “do not come now” message. Psaki acknowledges strong public interest and admits the message has not yet fully penetrated in the region. She underscores that most adults and families are being turned back while unaccompanied children are handled under more humane policies. She frames the delegation’s visit as part of an internal policy review focused on operational questions like staffing, timelines, and process bottlenecks, and reiterates that sanctuary conditions, educational access, legal support, and medical care are central considerations.
- Yamiche asks what the border delegation saw, what most concerns them, and whether migrants are receiving the message that now is not the time to come.
- Ms. Psaki says Biden wants to hear directly from the delegation before more public detail is given and frames the trip as part of a normal internal policy process aimed at understanding operational challenges, such as staffing and processing timelines.
- Ms. Psaki acknowledges that Americans are highly interested in conditions for unaccompanied minors and says the administration is focused on ensuring safe facilities with educational, legal, and health resources and on expediting placement with family members or sponsors.
- In response to follow‑up questions, Ms. Psaki concedes that the administration needs to do more to communicate that most people are turned away at the border and that now is not the time to migrate.
#### Houthi attacks on Saudi Arabia and Iran context
A reporter asks about increased Houthi attacks on Saudi Arabia, including a strike allegedly launched from Iraq or Iran. Psaki condemns the attacks as unacceptable and dangerous, stressing they are not the actions of a group serious about peace and that they threaten civilians, including Americans. She acknowledges Saudi Arabia faces genuine security threats from Yemen and others and says the U.S. will look for ways to strengthen Saudi defensive capabilities through an interagency process. On negotiations with Iran, she notes that discussions around a possible EU‑facilitated meeting on the nuclear deal are ongoing, with no date set yet, framing such diplomacy as part of a broader process.
- A reporter cites the Saudi claim that a recent Houthi attack originated from Iraq or Iran and asks whether this is a game changer and whether Iran will be held accountable.
- Ms. Psaki says the U.S. is alarmed by the frequency of Houthi attacks, which are unacceptable, dangerous, and inconsistent with seeking peace, and notes they threaten civilians, including Americans.
- Ms. Psaki acknowledges that Saudi Arabia faces genuine security threats from Yemen and the region and says the U.S. will use an interagency process to explore ways to bolster Saudi defensive capabilities.
- When asked about nuclear negotiations with Iran, Ms. Psaki says discussions about the Iranians’ response to an EU invitation for talks are ongoing but that there is no date or timeline yet for a meeting.
#### COVID-focused week and public communication
A reporter notes that Biden’s schedule is dominated by COVID‑related events and asks why this week is distinctive. Psaki explains that the one‑year mark since pandemic shutdowns provides an opportunity to highlight progress and remaining challenges. She says the administration wants to reflect public concerns about the virus’s impact on health, the economy, and school closures, and to communicate clearly about the path forward. The schedule is designed to underscore that COVID response is Biden’s top priority and to contextualize the ARP and vaccine rollout within that overarching mission.
- A reporter asks why this week’s presidential schedule is so heavily focused on COVID‑19 events.
- Ms. Psaki says no issue is more important to Americans than controlling the pandemic, which underlies the recession, food insecurity, and school closures.
- Ms. Psaki notes that this week marks roughly one year since nationwide shutdowns began, making it an appropriate moment to highlight progress and the road ahead.
- Ms. Psaki emphasizes that the COVID response is the number one priority for President Biden, Vice President Harris, and the entire team, and the schedule reflects that.
