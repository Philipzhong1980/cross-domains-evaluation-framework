### Overview
The City of Mountain View’s Council Finance Committee and Investment Review Committee convenes virtually to review the city’s investment portfolio performance, broader economic conditions, and the draft FY 2021–22 Investment Review Committee report. Investment advisor Carlos Oblites of Chandler Asset Management outlines how pandemic stimulus, supply chain disruptions, and the Russia–Ukraine war have driven inflation to multi‑decade highs, prompting the Federal Reserve to raise rates aggressively, which in turn has reduced bond values and produced a roughly –3.3% to –3.4% total return for the year despite stable credit quality and strong long‑term average returns around 3.3%. He emphasizes that the city’s conservative, high‑grade, short‑duration portfolio—shaped by state law and local policy—has outperformed benchmarks and limited losses, a point reinforced by Committee Member Steven Permut as he credits the post–Orange County risk posture for avoiding deeper drawdowns. The committee discusses trade‑offs between active bond management and the city’s hold‑to‑maturity policy, reviews new but small 144A private placement holdings now permitted under SEC QIB rules and considers future policy caps on such securities, and hears Finance Director Jesse Takahashi’s update on an in‑process private placement refinancing of 2011 Shoreline bonds that will call bonds the city itself holds. Investment Officer Tyler Cook then presents the draft annual report, confirming full compliance with duration, diversification, and quality limits, a portfolio size near $842 million with the general fund at about 20%, and no recommended policy changes, which the committee supports without amendment while expressing appreciation for ongoing monthly reporting. The group also notes a recent city letter urging LAIF to divest from carbon‑related investments, revisits and unanimously approves previously questioned minutes after clarifying attendance and vote counts, and closes with acknowledgements of staff and members, including Takahashi’s service and an upcoming in‑person city event.

#### Economic environment and inflation impacts
Investment advisor Carlos Oblites provides an extensive economic briefing to explain the macro forces affecting the city’s bond portfolio. He describes elevated inflation levels not seen since the early 1980s, driven by pandemic stimulus, pent‑up consumer demand, supply chain disruptions, and the Russia–Ukraine war’s impact on commodities. He emphasizes that the Federal Reserve is aggressively raising interest rates to curb inflation, which softens economic growth and raises the risk of recession but currently occurs alongside a historically strong labor market.
- Advisor Oblites reports that headline CPI inflation peaked at 9.1% in June and remains above 8% as of August, the highest since the early Reagan era.
- Advisor Oblites explains that COVID‑era fiscal stimulus totaling about $5.5 trillion and near‑zero interest rates boosted savings and pent‑up demand, which later drove prices higher when the economy reopened.
- Advisor Oblites notes that global supply chain issues, including chip shortages and shipping delays, plus the Russia–Ukraine war’s effects on oil, gas, grain, and fertilizer, have pushed commodity prices and inflation up.
- Advisor Oblites states that the Federal Reserve has raised the federal funds rate five times this year, moving from a 0–0.25% range to about 3.25%, including multiple 75‑basis‑point hikes.
- Advisor Oblites highlights that the U.S. labor market remains very strong with unemployment at 3.7% and recent monthly job gains averaging around 381,000, making it difficult to label the current period a standard recession despite two negative GDP quarters.
- Advisor Oblites points out that housing and manufacturing indicators are softening as higher mortgage rates near 7% cool demand and broader economic data slows in response to Fed tightening.
- Advisor Oblites notes that the Fed’s preferred core PCE inflation measure has recently ticked up again, and Fed governors are signaling a likely federal funds rate path up toward roughly 4.5%.

#### Interest rate spike and bond valuation effects
Oblites explains how the sharp rise in interest rates has reduced the market value of fixed‑income securities, producing negative total returns for the city’s portfolio on paper. He shows that yields on short‑ and intermediate‑term U.S. Treasuries have climbed from well below 1% to above 4%, creating significant unrealized losses. He stresses that because the city largely holds bonds to maturity, these losses are not expected to be realized, and higher current yields will improve interest income going forward.
- Advisor Oblites shows that the 2‑year U.S. Treasury yield has risen from about 0.7% at the start of the year to peaks around 4.3–4.4%, driving bond prices down.
- Advisor Oblites states that when interest rates rise, bond prices fall, and this rate move in a short period has significantly reduced the fair value of the city’s holdings.
- Advisor Oblites underscores that the city’s practice is to hold bonds to maturity, so the decline in fair value is unrealized and principal is expected to be repaid at par.
- Advisor Oblites explains that as existing bonds mature, their proceeds are now reinvested at much higher yields, causing the portfolio’s yield to maturity and future interest income to rise.
- Advisor Oblites clarifies that reported total returns blend interest income with the change in fair value, and the negative returns for the year are driven by valuation changes rather than credit problems.
- Advisor Oblites notes that despite negative absolute returns, the city’s portfolio has outperformed its market benchmarks, meaning it lost less than the broader market under the same conditions.

#### Portfolio structure, policy constraints, and risk posture
The advisor reviews the city’s investment policy framework and portfolio structure, emphasizing the primacy of safety and liquidity as dictated by California Government Code and local policy. The city invests only in high‑grade fixed‑income securities, with stricter internal limits (AA or better) than state law requires. He describes the portfolio’s duration profile, sector allocation, and large allocation to Treasuries and government securities, noting that the city’s conservative approach limits risk and volatility, even at the cost of some yield in benign markets.
- Advisor Oblites reiterates that California Government Code requires public portfolios to prioritize safety, liquidity, and then return, limiting investments to high‑quality fixed income with maximum maturities of five years.
- Advisor Oblites explains that while state law permits single‑A credits, Mountain View’s policy further restricts corporate holdings to AA or higher, materially shrinking the eligible corporate universe.
- Advisor Oblites reports that the consolidated portfolio’s weighted average maturity is under five years and its duration is around 1.89 years, aligning it with a 0–5‑year benchmark and controlling interest rate risk.
- Advisor Oblites notes that the portfolio’s yield to maturity as of June 30 is about 1.52%, up from 1.30% three months earlier, showing that reinvestment at higher market yields is already boosting earnings potential.
- Advisor Oblites describes that roughly three‑quarters of the portfolio is invested in government‑related securities, including U.S. Treasuries, federal agencies, LAIF, and supranationals, with a smaller slice in high‑grade corporates managed by Chandler.
- Advisor Oblites highlights that corporate allocations remain well below the policy cap of 15% and far below the 30% permitted by state code, reflecting the city’s conservative bias.
- Advisor Oblites emphasizes that issuer concentrations in non‑government securities are kept low, with many positions around a quarter of a percent, to avoid overexposure to any single corporate name.
- Advisor Oblites characterizes the city’s overall approach as risk management first, focusing on preserving capital and maintaining liquidity rather than maximizing return.

#### Portfolio performance and historical returns
The presentation reviews total return results for the past year, showing modest but negative performance due to rising rates while noting solid long‑term outcomes. For the latest fiscal year, the overall portfolio return is approximately –3.4%, worse than any year in the last decade but still better than benchmarks. Over the long term since 1995, the city’s investment program averages roughly 3.3% annually despite a decade of near‑zero rates, demonstrating strong cumulative performance under a conservative mandate.
- Advisor Oblites reports that the consolidated portfolio’s one‑year total return is about –3.41%, driven by fair value losses that more than offset roughly 1.4% in interest income.
- Advisor Oblites notes that corporate holdings experienced more negative returns than Treasuries over the year because credit spreads widened, further depressing credit prices relative to risk‑free bonds.
- Advisor Oblites emphasizes that the city’s portfolio outperformed its benchmarks on both the government and corporate segments, meaning it declined less than comparable indices during the downturn.
- Advisor Oblites states that over the long run, the city‑managed portfolio has earned an average annual return of approximately 3.29–3.31% since 1995, despite extended periods when market rates were below 1%.
- Advisor Oblites confirms there is no evidence of deteriorating credit quality, and that negative performance is attributable to interest rate movements, not issuer defaults or downgrades in core holdings.

#### Value of conservative strategy in volatile markets
Committee Member Steven Permut offers a perspective on the benefits of the city’s long‑standing conservative investment approach in the current environment. He notes that while the portfolio shows an unrealized loss of roughly $27 million and a –3% total return, these figures would be substantially worse if the city had taken more duration or credit risk. He links the city’s cautious posture back to lessons from the Orange County investment debacle, arguing that the current stress period validates the policy choice to favor safety and moderate returns over aggressive yield enhancement.
- Member Permut observes that the current unrealized loss of about $27 million and –3% total return are among the largest negatives the committee has seen, but would likely be far larger under a longer‑duration or lower‑quality strategy.
- Member Permut points out that credit spreads have widened over the past year, meaning lower‑quality bonds would have suffered more severe price declines than the city’s high‑grade holdings.
- Member Permut reminds the committee that the investment review structure was originally created in the wake of the Orange County bankruptcy, and that Mountain View has deliberately kept a conservative portfolio ever since.
- Member Permut argues that, while this approach may have sacrificed some yield in prior years, it has allowed the city to weather this period of extreme rate volatility with smaller losses and greater peace of mind.
- Advisor Oblites affirms that corporate spread widening has indeed caused corporate bonds to underperform Treasuries, reinforcing the benefit of the city’s cautious positioning.

#### Active management versus hold‑to‑maturity policy
Committee Member Doug Radtke explores whether active bond management could add value by selling depressed bonds and reinvesting proceeds into higher‑yielding alternatives. He and Oblites discuss the use of modified Dietz methodology for performance measurement and the potential for “break‑even” analyses on swapping holdings. Oblites confirms that while Chandler routinely applies such techniques for other clients, Mountain View’s policy is to hold securities to maturity, and Chandler respects that constraint in this mandate.
- Member Radtke asks how returns are calculated and whether they reflect fair value changes, prompting a discussion of the modified Dietz method that incorporates both income and valuation movements.
- Advisor Oblites explains that performance figures use an industry‑standard modified Dietz calculation, blending realized interest with realized and unrealized changes in market value while accounting for cash flows.
- Member Radtke inquires whether it can make economic sense to sell bonds at a loss and reinvest in higher‑yielding instruments, effectively conducting a break‑even analysis on swaps.
- Advisor Oblites confirms that active bond managers regularly execute such swap strategies to improve yield or risk positioning, but notes that Mountain View’s explicit policy is to hold investments to maturity.
- Advisor Oblites states that if the city chose to revisit its policy and allow more active trading, Chandler has a suite of tools and approaches to implement those strategies, but he does not advocate that change unprompted given the city’s risk‑averse culture.

#### Use of 144A/QIB private placements
The committee examines the city’s recent move into Rule 144A securities that are restricted to Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs), specifically MetLife and New York Life holdings. Oblites explains that SEC rule changes now treat large local governments as QIBs, enabling access to certain high‑quality private placements that were previously unavailable. Member Permut raises concerns about liquidity and concentration limits in 144A securities, and suggests the committee may want to set policy caps for these instruments in the future.
- Advisor Oblites reports that the SEC has expanded the definition of Qualified Institutional Buyer to include local governments with at least $100 million in investable assets, which now allows Mountain View to buy certain 144A securities.
- Advisor Oblites notes that Chandler added private placements from MetLife and New York Life, both rated AA or better, to diversify the portfolio and capture modest yield advantages.
- Member Permut asks whether these 144A issues are private placements and whether they are less liquid than public bonds, raising a potential concern about marketability in a stress scenario.
- Advisor Oblites confirms the securities are 144A private placements but asserts that they are widely held, high‑quality, and sufficiently liquid for the city’s needs given their small size.
- Advisor Oblites indicates that New York Life and MetLife positions each represent about 0.3% of the total investment program, far from any concentration level that would pose systemic risk.
- Member Permut suggests that the committee may want to consider adding an explicit aggregate percentage cap on 144A holdings in the investment policy, to avoid a scenario where private placements become a dominant share of the portfolio.
- Advisor Oblites acknowledges that current policy does not impose specific 144A limits and notes that the committee is free to adopt such constraints if it aligns with the city’s culture.

#### Potential future policy adjustments and culture
Councilmember Matichak asks Chandler whether there are changes to the investment policy or strategy the city should begin considering for the future. Oblites responds that Chandler could recommend various adjustments if the city wished to align more closely with what state law permits, such as broader corporate usage or different structures. However, in light of the city’s conservative risk culture and successful track record, he does not proactively push for major changes and suggests any adjustments be driven by the council’s comfort and objectives.
- Councilmember Matichak asks how long Chandler has worked with the city and whether the advisor recommends any policy changes the committee should start discussing.
- Advisor Oblites reports that Chandler has supported the city with reporting services since the mid‑2000s and has served as an investment advisor on the corporate segment since 2019, with the mandate renewed in 2021.
- Advisor Oblites states that Chandler has “a host of ideas” that could be implemented if the city wished to move closer to the flexibility allowed under California Government Code.
- Advisor Oblites clarifies that, given Mountain View’s historically conservative posture and success with that approach, he is not recommending major policy shifts unless the city explicitly seeks them.
- Councilmember Matichak acknowledges the answer and indicates she has no immediate changes to propose to the draft annual report.

#### Shoreline bond refinancing and self‑held bonds
Councilmember Matichak raises a question about the city purchasing its own Shoreline bonds within the investment portfolio while concurrently pursuing a refinancing of that debt. Finance Director Takahashi explains that the city is actively working to refund the 2011 Shoreline bonds, likely via a private placement, and that interest rate increases have reduced but not eliminated the potential savings. He notes that the city’s holdings of its own bonds will be called as part of the refinancing, creating both a benefit and a drawback depending on whether one views the situation from the investor or issuer perspective.
- Councilmember Matichak expresses concern that the city both issues and invests in Shoreline bonds, which creates a dynamic where refinancing benefits the issuer while potentially harming the investor side of the city’s portfolio.
- Finance Director Takahashi shares that staff have circulated a request for proposals and are actively pursuing a refunding of the 2011 Shoreline bonds, targeting council approval and closing in November.
- Finance Director Takahashi notes that the refinancing is still expected to generate savings despite rising interest rates, though earlier estimates of around $7 million in savings have been reduced by rate hikes.
- Finance Director Takahashi confirms that all outstanding callable Shoreline bonds, including those held in the city’s own investment portfolio, will be called as part of the refunding, illustrating the “two‑edged sword” of being both issuer and investor.
- Member Permut clarifies that the maturities extend out much longer than 2031, and Takahashi confirms that the 2011 bonds reach out to approximately 2040.

#### Annual Investment Review Committee draft report
Finance staff present the draft fiscal year 2021–22 Investment Review Committee report to the council and confirm that the portfolio complied with all policy and code requirements. Tyler Cook summarizes key metrics including the –3.3% total return, 1.3% average earnings rate, portfolio growth to about $842 million, and ongoing diversification within policy limits. No policy violations occurred, and staff recommend no changes to the investment policy, while inviting committee feedback before forwarding the report to the full council.
- Finance Director Takahashi introduces Investment Officer Tyler Cook as the primary portfolio manager to present the draft Investment Review Committee report for FY 2021–22.
- Cook reports that the portfolio’s total rate of return for the fiscal year is –3.3%, matching the negative figures discussed in Chandler’s presentation and representing the largest annual decline in the last decade.
- Cook states that the portfolio’s average duration for the year is 2.08, closely aligned with the 1.97 benchmark and within the policy requirement to remain within 15% of the benchmark and rebalanced within 3% quarterly.
- Cook calculates an average earnings rate of 1.3% for the fiscal year by comparing interest earnings to the average portfolio size, noting that low COVID‑era purchase rates depressed this figure despite recent rate increases.
- Cook shows that the total portfolio has grown from roughly $307 million in 2013 to about $841.9 million as of June 30, 2022, with the general fund representing approximately $192.7 million or 19.8% of the total.
- Cook provides a diversification compliance table showing all asset classes are within policy limits, with corporate allocations rising modestly from 6.2% to 7.3% over the year.
- Cook confirms there were no violations of the investment policy during the fiscal year in terms of duration, diversification, safety, or credit quality.
- Councilmember Matichak states she has no suggested changes to the draft and expresses appreciation for the monthly investment reports that keep councilmembers informed about the city’s financial position.
