### Overview
  The committee received a detailed briefing on the city's investment portfolio and policy, led by Carlos Oblites of Chandler and Finance staff manager Tyler Cook. Carlos framed the program around California Government Code constraints and the city's more conservative AA-or-better policy, then explained how multi-decade-high inflation, pandemic stimulus, supply-chain disruptions and the Ukraine war prompted the Federal Reserve to raise policy rates sharply, driving market yields higher and pressuring bond valuations. As a result, the portfolio recorded a materially negative total return for the fiscal year (about -3.3% as reported) driven by unrealized fair-value declines, even as interest income and prospective earnings rose because maturing cash can be reinvested at higher rates. Staff reported the portfolio remains short in duration, highly liquid, and credit-quality focused, with corporate allocations modestly increased (to roughly 7%) and recent selective purchases of 144A private-placement securities (e.g., New York Life and MetLife) after an SEC QIB rule change; committee members discussed liquidity and suggested considering an aggregate cap for 144A exposure. Tyler presented the draft FY21/22 Investment Review Committee report confirming policy compliance, no recommended changes, and portfolio growth over the past decade. The committee resolved an earlier minutes-count discrepancy (attributed to a vacancy and an omitted roll call), approved the minutes unanimously, and closed with staff and member acknowledgements and follow-up availability from Chandler and city finance staff.
  
  ### Detailed Summary

#### Investment policy constraints
  Carlos Oblites reviewed the city's investment objectives and legal constraints under California Government Code, emphasizing safety and liquidity ahead of return. He explained the city's policy is more restrictive than state code, requiring higher credit quality (AA or better) and limiting maturities and eligible sectors. These constraints define the permissible opportunity set and shape portfolio positioning and risk management.
- Carlos Oblites explained the portfolio follows California Government Code priorities: safety, then liquidity, then return.
- Carlos Oblites noted state code allows investments to single-A or higher while the city policy restricts to AA or higher.
- Carlos Oblites said eligible securities are limited to short-term, high-quality fixed-income instruments and defined sectors under the policy.
#### Inflation drivers overview
  Carlos summarized the recent inflation surge, citing high CPI readings driven by pandemic-related stimulus, pent-up demand, supply-chain disruptions and commodity pressures from the war in Eastern Europe. He emphasized that these forces pushed inflation to multi-decade highs and prompted central bank response. Understanding these drivers explains why interest rates and yields rose sharply and why the investment program is being positioned conservatively.
- Carlos Oblites attributed elevated inflation to pandemic stimulus, pent-up consumer demand, and supply-chain bottlenecks.
- Carlos Oblites noted the Ukraine conflict added pressure to energy and agricultural commodity prices.
- Carlos Oblites said the CPI reached levels not seen since the early Reagan era and that those readings demand central-bank action.
#### Federal Reserve actions
  Carlos described the Federal Reserve's rapid tightening cycle in response to inflation, explaining multiple rate hikes that pushed the federal funds effective range materially higher over the year. He indicated Fed officials signaled further increases could be required, which keeps rate expectations and market volatility elevated. The Fed's path matters directly to the portfolio because it drives short- and intermediate-term yields and market valuations.
- Carlos Oblites detailed several Fed increases—from near zero to a federal-funds range around 3.25% at the time of presentation.
- Carlos Oblites reported that some Fed officials expect policy rates to move higher, potentially toward a roughly 4.5% terminal rate.
- Carlos Oblites said ongoing Fed tightening is maintaining volatility in fixed-income markets.
#### Economic indicators and outlook
  Carlos walked through key economic data: jobs remain strong with low unemployment, GDP readings were mixed with recent negative quarters, and housing and manufacturing are softening due to higher rates. He observed that the combination of strong employment and decelerating activity creates an uncertain outlook and complicates the Fed's trade-off between inflation and labor markets. That ambiguity increases the likelihood of volatile market moves and the possibility of a soft landing or a mild recession.
- Carlos Oblites said monthly job creation remained robust and unemployment was low at about 3.7%, supporting the labor market.
- Carlos Oblites noted two sequential quarters of negative GDP growth, which commonly signals recession though the NBER had not declared one.
- Carlos Oblites explained housing activity and manufacturing indicators have slowed as mortgage and borrowing costs rose.
#### Interest-rate impact on portfolio
  Carlos explained that rising yields drove meaningful declines in bond market valuations, producing negative total returns even as cash income increased. He emphasized that unrealized fair-value losses overwhelm interest income in short windows, but those losses are not realized if securities are held to maturity and maturing proceeds can be reinvested at higher yields. The near-term result is depressed reported returns but improving prospective earnings as reinvestment occurs.
- Carlos Oblites said higher market interest rates reduced the market value of fixed-income holdings, producing negative reported returns.
- Carlos Oblites explained that holding securities to maturity would eliminate realized principal loss because principal returns at maturity.
- Carlos Oblites said maturing securities and new money are being reinvested at materially higher yields, increasing future interest income.
#### Portfolio composition and metrics
  Carlos and staff presented portfolio metrics showing a short average maturity/duration profile and rising yield-to-maturity. They reported high credit quality across holdings, a significant governmental securities allocation, and a modest but growing corporate credit sleeve that Chandler manages. These structural choices preserve liquidity and limit sensitivity to rate moves while positioning the portfolio to earn more as cash rolls and reinvests.
- Carlos Oblites reported the consolidated portfolio averaged roughly 1.89 years in maturity and a modest duration consistent with the zero-to-five-year benchmark.
- Carlos Oblites said the portfolio earned a yield-to-maturity around 1.52% as of June 30th and that market yields rose further into October.
- Carlos Oblites noted the investment program comprised mostly government securities with a corporate allocation the city caps at 15% and currently keeps well below that limit.
#### Corporate allocations and 144A/QIB securities
  Carlos described adding select 144A (private placement) issues that became available after the SEC revised the Qualified Institutional Buyer definition to include large local governments. He explained the city purchased AAA/AA-quality private placement securities (New York Life Global Funding and MetLife) in small allocations to diversify and pick up incremental yield. Committee members asked about liquidity and policy limits, and one member suggested considering an aggregate cap on 144A exposure.
- Carlos Oblites said the SEC change allowing local governments with $100M+ investable assets to buy certain 144A securities enabled purchases of New York Life and MetLife.
- Carlos Oblites reported New York Life and MetLife each represent roughly 0.3% of the portfolio and are high credit quality.
- Steven Permut proposed the committee consider setting an aggregate percentage cap on 144A securities to limit private-placement concentration.
#### Active management versus hold-to-maturity
  Doug Radtke asked whether selling securities at a loss to reinvest in higher-yielding instruments could be beneficial; Carlos affirmed that active trading and break-even analyses can add value. Carlos clarified that while Chandler executes active management for other clients, the city's policy is to generally hold securities to maturity, and staff follows that hold-to-maturity approach. The committee recognized that active management would be possible but would require a policy change.
- Doug Radtke asked whether the firm performs break-even analysis to decide if selling a security and reinvesting at higher yields makes sense.
- Carlos Oblites said active management and tactical sales are performed for other clients and can add value, but the city's policy customarily holds securities to maturity.
- Carlos Oblites indicated any move toward active sales would require a change to the city's investment policy.
#### Conservatism benefits highlighted
  Committee members emphasized that the city's conservative policy and short duration mitigated losses during a period of steep rate increases. Steven Permut noted the unrealized loss (~$27M) and a roughly -3.3% total return were sizable but would have been materially worse with longer duration or lower-quality holdings. The group agreed conservatism has protected the portfolio during recent market volatility.
- Steven Permut observed an unrealized loss of about $27 million and a roughly -3% total return for the year but argued results would be worse with longer duration or lower credit quality.
- Carlos Oblites agreed that conservative positioning and high credit standards limited downside relative to benchmarks.
- Steven Permut referenced the historical rationale for conservatism, noting the committee's creation after Orange County's default.
