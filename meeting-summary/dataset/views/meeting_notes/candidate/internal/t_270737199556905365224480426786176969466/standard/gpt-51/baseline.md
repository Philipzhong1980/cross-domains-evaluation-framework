### Overview
The Visual Arts Committee (VAC), led by Chair Jesse Cupp with Vice Chair Susi Merhar and members including Cliff Grant and Regina Cycles, conducts a working session that both handles routine business and advances a long-term public art strategy. After unanimously approving prior minutes and formally adopting their annual work plan—with a key adjustment reframing the update of the 1996 public art book and online map as part of “promoting the public art strategy”—the committee receives capital project updates from staff liaison John Lang on Aquatic Center artworks, completed Fayette Park functional sculptures awaiting installation, Rengstorff Park mural concept development, and transit grade separation art proposals headed for a September review. The ad hoc subcommittee reports significant progress on a comprehensive public art inventory and discusses vanished works, hospital interiors, small community installations, and how this data will feed an enhanced GIS-based public map. Chair Cupp presents economic impact research from SV Creates and Americans for the Arts to support the public art strategy’s case that arts investment drives local spending and jobs, and Lang emphasizes using this data to elevate arts in Council deliberations. The core of the meeting is a detailed review of staff’s draft initiatives under three strategies—policy refinement, expanded opportunities, and collaborations—where members broadly endorse the direction but recommend clarifications around private percent-for-art funding, broadened collaboration beyond arts groups, explicit support for temporary and interactive art and events, stronger diversity and participation goals, more precise mapping and wayfinding language, and mechanisms to relocate or pool art funds for use in under-served areas. The VAC opts for an open, unthemed 2024 call for CPA exhibits, notes that the second floor will expand display capacity, and acknowledges a very full September agenda that will include the revised strategy, major project reviews, and exhibit selections. Public participants, including Naya and online attendee Jasmin Aber, ask about future access to the inventory and the role of public input, and Lang clarifies both that inventory data will be surfaced via the public map and that meeting chat remains disabled under city rules, offering direct follow-up to support ongoing engagement.

#### Art and economic impact research for the strategy
Chair Jesse Cupp presents research on the economic value of arts and culture, drawing on recent studies from SV Creates and Americans for the Arts. These data will be used to support the forthcoming public art strategy and to demonstrate to City Council and the community that arts investment drives local spending, jobs, and tax revenues. Staff underscores that positioning arts and culture as an economic and community development asset is critical to securing support for new policies, funding mechanisms, and partnerships.
- Chair Jesse Cupp introduces a 2021 SV Creates report, “The Business of Arts and Culture,” and points members to key narrative sections on how arts improve community health, well-being, and civic vitality.
- John Lang describes SV Creates as both an arts advocacy and organizing group for Silicon Valley, noting their collaborations with Content Magazine and their event-based model of releasing arts publications as a way to build cultural community.
- Jesse Cupp notes that SV Creates’ long-form 2019 “Creative Community Builders” report includes detailed economic analysis, and that the shorter 2021 publication serves as an accessible summary of those findings.
- Jesse Cupp outlines Americans for the Arts’ “Arts & Economic Prosperity 5” (2017) study and explains that although an updated “AEP6” report is delayed due to COVID, the 2017 numbers remain the best available benchmark.
- Jesse Cupp highlights a two-page Americans for the Arts summary specific to Santa Clara County, which shows that attendees at nonprofit arts events spend an average of $26.27 per person beyond admission, with non-residents spending even more, and points out that these figures exceed national averages due to local costs.
- Jesse Cupp demonstrates an online economic impact calculator from Americans for the Arts, showing how assumed spending by an example set of events and attendees translates into estimates of local jobs, household income, and tax revenues.
- John Lang explains that staff has obtained attendance numbers for city events such as the Art & Wine Festival and will apply the per-attendee spending figures to estimate Mountain View’s arts-driven economic impact for use in the strategy and presentations to Council.
- John Lang emphasizes that the purpose of this analysis is to elevate arts and culture onto the same decision-making level as other city priorities, showing that investment in arts not only enriches community life but also generates tangible economic value.

#### Draft public art strategy initiatives discussion
The committee conducts an extended working session on a draft set of initiatives organized under three previously defined strategies: (1) refine city policies and procedures; (2) create more public art opportunities; and (3) pursue collaborations. Members generally endorse staff’s synthesis, noting it reflects prior VAC discussions, but propose numerous refinements. Key themes include clarifying private “percent for art” funding, broadening collaborations beyond arts organizations, explicitly supporting temporary and interactive art and events, strengthening diversity and participation language, better specifying mapping and wayfinding, and creating mechanisms to move or pool funds across locations. Staff commits to revising the draft, exploring legal feasibility on funding flexibilities, and revisiting goals and vision alignment with the ad hoc subcommittee before returning to the full VAC in September.
- John Lang reviews the existing framework: a vision statement previously adopted by the VAC, four higher-level goals developed with a consultant (community, placemaking, heritage, economic development), and three strategies the committee had already endorsed (policy refinement, more opportunities, collaborations).
- John Lang presents a detailed list of proposed initiatives under Strategy 1, including establishing a public art fund, improving jury processes and public engagement, building a comprehensive inventory, aligning and updating Policy K-5, creating selection/maintenance/deaccession policies, reviewing donation policies, encouraging early architect–artist collaboration, using art in open spaces and wayfinding, supporting the creative class, and exploring ways to shift percent-for-art dollars to underserved areas.
- He then outlines initiatives under Strategy 2, covering increased work/live spaces for artists, art-based wayfinding, stimulating private arts and culture initiatives, context-appropriate artworks throughout the community, expanding temporary exhibit programs to more sites, emphasizing functional and environmental art in public facilities, reviving the utility box program, and identifying youth-centered art and maker spaces.
- Under Strategy 3, he proposes initiatives to strengthen relationships with arts organizations, collaborate with the Performing Arts Committee, partner on public art education, work with the Historical Society to provide historical context for artists, and pursue joint grant opportunities.
- Cliff Grant comments that the draft reflects past VAC discussions well but flags the need to explicitly mention private “percent for art” in the initiative about creating a public art fund, noting that ultimate implementation depends on funding mechanisms.
- John Lang agrees and updates his notes to clarify that Initiative 1.1 addresses a private-sector percent-for-art model and associated in-lieu contributions to a public art fund.
- Vice Chair Susi Merhar observes that the four high-level goals omit explicit references to diversity, reflection, inspiration, and participation, and she suggests broadening them to incorporate representation, cultural enrichment, and community engagement with art.
- Merhar also questions why Strategy 3 is worded as collaboration solely with “artist organizations,” proposing that the strategy should encompass partnerships with businesses, schools, nonprofits, other city boards and commissions, and residents, with additional initiatives to describe those relationships.
- Merhar recommends that Initiative 1.3 on inventory explicitly mention improving visibility of public art via guided tours and the online map, not just internal cataloging.
- On Initiative 1.8 about open space, Jesse Cupp proposes expanding the focus beyond parks and open spaces to explicitly include pedestrian and bike trails and easements, so that art can enhance active transportation routes as well as formal parkland.
- Cupp suggests modifying Initiative 1.10 on reallocating percent-for-art monies so it contemplates not only shifting project-based funds geographically but also, where legally permissible, contributing to a broader art fund that can later support art in under-served locations such as older parks or trail segments.
- John Lang explains that he intentionally framed 1.10 as a feasibility study because legal and funding constraints may limit the city’s ability to move K-5 project funds away from the original capital project, while in-lieu private contributions are more flexible.
- Cupp and Merhar both argue that past experience—such as a Shoreline-area fire facility project where earmarked art funds effectively evaporated—shows the value of creating mechanisms to park unused CIP art money for future use rather than losing it if a specific location proves unsuitable.
- On Initiative 2.7 about utility boxes, Jesse Cupp asks to broaden the siting focus to include public transit routes in addition to bike and pedestrian corridors, linking art to the city’s active transportation goals.
- Responding to Cliff Grant’s concerns about long-term box quality, Cupp and John Lang agree that a future maintenance plan should include periodic evaluation and potential repainting or replacement of weaker designs once the program has saturated available boxes.
- Merhar argues for explicitly exploring alternatives to traditional permanent sculpture, such as temporary installations, interactive art, light-based works, and events or happenings, suggesting that this be added under Strategy 2 to broaden the definition of public art experiences.
- The committee discusses where to place this new emphasis and leans toward incorporating temporary and experiential art into Initiative 2.4, which already addresses context-appropriate artworks across the community.
- Merhar further recommends that Strategy 2 include a separate initiative around public participation and programming—e.g., art walks, plein-air events, or community-engaged art projects—so residents can actively participate rather than only observe finished works.
- Cliff Grant cites examples such as Annapolis’s plein-air painting events as models for engaging both artists and the public in time-limited art experiences that showcase local landscapes like Shoreline.
- Merhar points out that Initiative 2.1 on work/live spaces for artists should also mention places where artists can exhibit, suggesting revised language to “increase availability of spaces for artists to work, live, and exhibit their work,” possibly including hybrid studio-gallery spaces.
- John Lang acknowledges that he attempted to capture maker spaces and youth spaces with Initiative 2.8 but agrees that the language may be overly narrow, and he notes Merhar’s suggestion to generalize it to “identify locations for artist and maker spaces and adopt programs encouraging participation by residents, including youth.”
- Merhar raises the issue of diversity and representation in artist selection, noting that the older inventory is dominated by metal sculptures by white male artists, and she advocates for explicitly stating in the strategy that the VAC will seek diversity in artists and themes going forward.
- Jesse Cupp counters that recent commissions may already have better gender balance but agrees that articulating a diversity and representation goal is valuable as a guiding principle for future juries.
- Public attendee Jasmin Aber asks how public input informs the strategy and what role artists and the broader community play in shaping policy versus city staff and VAC members.
- In response, John Lang explains that the VAC’s ongoing discussions, public comments at meetings, and later Council deliberations all form part of the input process, and that specific policies or partnerships emerging from the strategy will go back to Council with explicit references to the adopted framework.
- After extensive discussion, the committee agrees that the ad hoc subcommittee will work with staff over the summer to revise the detailed initiatives and revisit the higher-level goals and potentially the vision statement for better alignment, with a revised draft to be reviewed by the full VAC at the September meeting.
#### CPA exhibit program timing and call-for-artists parameters
The committee briefly reviews the Center for Performing Arts (CPA) exhibit program schedule and decides how to frame the upcoming 2024 call for artists. Staff explains that COVID disruptions knocked the timeline off its normal cycle, and that the goal is to reset by issuing the call earlier to align with CPA bookings. The VAC chooses not to impose a thematic constraint, preferring to give artists maximum freedom to submit current work, and notes that the second floor will be available, allowing for more pieces per exhibit.
- John Lang explains that the CPA 2023 call for artists was conducted late due to COVID-era disruptions and that staff now wants to shift the 2024 call to the summer timeframe to align with CPA’s event booking cycle.
- John Lang reports that in 2024 the CPA’s second floor will also be available for visual art, which will allow exhibiting artists to show additional works.
- He asks whether the committee wishes to set a specific theme for the 2024 exhibit season or issue an open call.
- Vice Chair Susi Merhar argues against imposing a theme, saying that themed calls can force artists to shoehorn unrelated work and risk excluding strong new work that does not fit the chosen topic.
- Chair Jesse Cupp concurs and observes that when themes are used, submissions often loosely stretch to fit, whereas an open call better respects artists’ existing practice and recent work.
- The committee reaches consensus to run an unthemed, open call for the CPA exhibits and to finalize selections in time to fit the CPA’s 2024 schedule, with a more detailed action item expected at the September or October VAC meeting.
