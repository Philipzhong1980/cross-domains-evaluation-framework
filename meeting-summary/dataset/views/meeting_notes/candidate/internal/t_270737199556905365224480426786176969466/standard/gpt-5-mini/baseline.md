### Overview
  The Visual Arts Committee meeting covers approval of minutes, updates on active public-art projects, progress on a comprehensive public-art inventory, and detailed development of a public art strategy and annual work plan. Staff (John Lang) and committee leadership (Chair Jesse Cupp and Vice Chair Susi Merhar) flag substantial September agenda items including interim Castro Street treatment, an Evelyn Mini Park call for artists, transit grade separation artist evaluations, and mural concepts for park buildings, while artists and project deliverables are scheduled for review. The inventory ad hoc reported a sizable expansion of the city’s art spreadsheet with missing and vanished works noted, and staff proposes migrating the inventory into an ArcGIS story map with an intake workflow; the committee discussed deaccessioning and maintenance policies for artworks. The group reviewed economic-impact research to support the strategy, amended and unanimously approved the committee work plan, and held a focused session on strategy content where members asked for stronger language on diversity, participation, alternative/temporary public-art forms, broader partnership goals, artist live/work/exhibit spaces, and clearer percent-for-art funding mechanics. Actions: staff will pursue GIS mapping and publication of the inventory, resolve funding/policy feasibility for reallocating percent-for-art or in-lieu funds, finalize artist deliverables and September schedules, and the ad hoc will draft revised strategy language for full-committee review in the fall; public participants are invited to follow up with staff for engagement.

#### Inventory subcommittee & GIS
  The ad hoc inventory subcommittee presents progress on a comprehensive public-art spreadsheet and photo inventory that expands and updates the city's 2016 map. Members discuss missing or vanished works, private-property pieces (including Google/Shoreline and hospital collections), access and inclusion criteria for what counts as public art, and next steps to migrate the spreadsheet into an institutionalized ArcGIS story map so the inventory can be published and maintained. Staff and committee identify the need for an intake/update process, unique IDs for artworks, and further outreach to property managers and departments to resolve provenance gaps.
- Susi Merhar explains the spreadsheet inventory is now ordered by year and highlights gaps where artist or date information is missing.
- Cliff Grant reports discovering two pieces from the original inventory that appear to have disappeared in the late 1990s and that he contacted one artist who confirmed loss of the work.
- John Lang emphasizes the value of institutionalizing the inventory in the city's GIS/ArcGIS system to create a living map and story map for public consumption.
- Susi Merhar raises the question of whether interior hospital artworks should be included and flags access and definition issues for publicly accessible art.
- Cliff Grant and John Lang note a need for a deaccession policy and formal processes for tracking, storing, or reclaiming artworks when sites redevelop or private owners move pieces.
- John Lang proposes IT/GIS support to convert the spreadsheet to a mapped database with photos and to explore an intake form for future updates.

#### Utility box program & maintenance
  The committee discusses reinstating the utility/painted box program with an emphasis on selecting locations along bike/pedestrian corridors, public transit routes, and establishing maintenance and refresh cycles. Members note variability in box quality and lifespan and request a maintenance plan to extend artwork life and a process to evaluate replacement or repainting choices. The committee also raises the broader maintenance and deaccessioning questions for permanent public art pieces and the need for policy guidance.
- Jesse Cupp advocates for returning the utility box art program and prioritizing installations along bike and pedestrian thoroughfares as well as public transit routes.
- John Lang asks whether painted utility boxes are intended as temporary or long-term installations and notes maintenance implications.
- Cliff Grant requests a formal evaluation and process for refreshing or redoing boxes that do not meet quality or durability expectations.
- John Lang and the committee agree a maintenance plan and clearer policy on deaccessioning and refresh cycles are needed for both utility boxes and permanent artwork.
