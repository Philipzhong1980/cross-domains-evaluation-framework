### Overview
The Mountain View Council Finance Committee convened to review the fiscal year 2021-2022 annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR) and audit results presented by external auditor Benjamin Lau of MGO. Lau reported a clean audit opinion, highlighting the significant impact of implementing the new lease accounting standard GASB 87, which resulted in a large lease receivable balance primarily related to a major tech firm. No internal control issues were found, and the single audit on federal compliance is pending. Mayor Lucas Ramirez raised concerns about the $26 million balance in the equipment maintenance and replacement fund, prompting staff to explain the pooled cash system and recent fire fleet replacement expenditures; staff agreed to review fee charges to better align fund balances with actual needs. The committee also discussed challenges in tracking community benefit revenues from development projects, noting these funds are tracked internally but not separately reported, complicating council oversight. Council Member Lisa Matichak inquired about top employers and public safety spending, leading to clarifications about employee count thresholds and pension investment income effects, and suggested updates to report terminology and formatting for electronic use. The committee unanimously recommended City Council accept the ACFR and audit reports, expressed appreciation for Finance Director Jesse Takahashi's service as he prepares to depart, and requested future updates on state budget impacts affecting city programs. The meeting concluded with thanks to all finance staff and the external auditor.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Annual Financial Audit Results
The committee reviewed the fiscal year 2021-2022 annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR) presented by external auditor Benjamin Lau from MGO. The audit provided a clean opinion on the city's financial statements, highlighting the significant implementation of the new lease accounting standard GASB 87, which resulted in a large lease receivable balance. No internal control findings were identified, and the single audit on federal compliance is pending but expected by March 31, 2023.
- Benjamin Lau, auditor, confirms a clean opinion on the financial statements for FY 2022.
- Lau explains the large lease receivable balance of approximately $800 million due to GASB 87 implementation, mainly related to leases with a major tech firm.
- No findings on internal control over financial reporting were identified this year, as stated by Lau.
- Single audit on federal awards compliance is not yet complete but expected by March 31, 2023, according to Lau.
- Jesse Takahashi, Finance Director, notes the general operating fund ended FY 2022 with a $17.5 million operating balance.
#### Equipment Maintenance Fund Balance
Mayor Ramirez questioned the large balance of $26 million in the equipment maintenance and replacement fund, noting it exceeds historical expenditure patterns. Staff explained that the fund operates within a pooled cash and investment system and that the large balance partly reflects a recent fire fleet replacement. The committee discussed the possibility of reviewing fee charges to better align with actual replacement needs.
- Mayor Ramirez expresses concern that the equipment maintenance fund balance is disproportionately high compared to historical spending.
- Jesse Takahashi explains the fund's cash and investments are part of a citywide pooled system, not segregated accounts.
- Benjamin Lau clarifies the pooled cash system allows liquidity and that the $26 million balance is available for fund needs.
- Takahashi confirms no major one-time fleet expenditures are anticipated soon beyond the recent fire apparatus replacement.
- Takahashi agrees to review the fee structure to potentially adjust charges and reduce the fund balance to a more reasonable level.
#### Community Benefit Revenue Tracking
The committee discussed challenges in tracking community benefit revenues from development projects, which are more flexible than restricted developer fees. Staff explained that while these funds are tracked in separate ledgers within the city's financial system, they are not separately reported in the ACFR, making it difficult for council members to monitor over time.
- Mayor Ramirez notes community benefit revenues are flexible and not restricted like other developer fees, complicating tracking.
- Jesse Takahashi confirms community benefit funds are tracked in sub-ledgers within the city's financial system but not separately reported in the ACFR.
- Lisa Matichak points out some community benefits, such as subsidized rent or infrastructure, do not flow through the city and thus are not recorded in city ledgers.
- Takahashi acknowledges that some community benefits are direct developer expenditures and not reflected in city financials, leading to an incomplete picture.
- The committee recognizes the need for improved reporting or accounting methods to better track these funds for council oversight.
#### Top Employers and Public Safety Spending
Council Member Matichak inquired about the top employers list, noting LinkedIn's absence likely due to employee count thresholds. She also questioned a decrease in public safety spending, which staff explained was influenced by pension investment income reducing net expenditures. Suggestions were made to update terminology in reports and improve formatting for electronic readability.
- Benjamin Lau explains LinkedIn's absence from the top employers list is likely because their Mountain View employee count is below the 500 threshold.
- Grace Zheng clarifies that the decrease in public safety spending is due to significant pension investment income offsetting expenses.
- Lisa Matichak requests updating report terminology from 'Mountain View citizens' to 'residents' and adding the Public Safety Advisory Board.
- Jesse Takahashi and Grace Zheng agree to update the report to reflect these changes.
- Matichak suggests improving the ACFR's formatting to better suit electronic review rather than print layout.
#### Committee Actions and Staff Appreciation
Mayor Ramirez moved to recommend the City Council accept the ACFR and related audit reports, which was seconded and unanimously approved by the committee. The committee expressed appreciation for the finance staff's work, especially Jesse Takahashi's service as this was his last meeting. Chair Lieber requested future information on state budget impacts for council consideration.
- Mayor Ramirez moves to recommend City Council acceptance of the ACFR and auditor's reports, seconded by Lisa Matichak.
- The motion passes unanimously by roll call vote.
- Committee members thank Jesse Takahashi for his years of service and contributions to the city's finance operations.
- Chair Sally Lieber requests future briefings on how state budget challenges may impact city programs and funding.
- The meeting adjourns with thanks to all finance staff and the external auditor.
