### Overview
The Finance Committee of the Mountain View City Council meets via video conference to review and recommend acceptance of the fiscal year 2021–2022 Annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR) and related audit documents, with Finance Director Jesse Takahashi and auditor Benjamin Lau of MGO presenting. Lau reports that MGO issues an unmodified (clean) opinion on the City’s financial statements, highlights the substantial impact of implementing GASB 87 on leases—including roughly $800 million in lease receivables tied largely to a major technology tenant—and confirms there are no internal control findings, no unrecorded misstatements, and that the single audit covering federal funds such as ARPA will be completed by the March 31, 2023 deadline. Committee members, led by Mayor Lucas Ramirez and Councilmember Lisa Matichak, probe the large balance in the Equipment Maintenance and Replacement Fund, the mechanics of the City’s pooled cash and investments, and the tracking of community benefit revenues, prompting staff commitments to review internal service fund rates and to clarify how flexible community benefit funds are monitored outside the ACFR. Matichak also raises several presentation issues—including employer listing interpretation, pension-driven changes in reported public safety costs, terminology updates from “citizens” to “residents,” inclusion of the Public Safety Advisory Board, and reformatting the ACFR for digital use—which staff agree to address in future editions. The committee ultimately votes unanimously to recommend that the City Council accept the ACFR and auditor’s reports, offers farewell appreciation to Takahashi at his last Finance Committee meeting, and, at Chair Sally Lieber’s request, asks staff to later brief the Council on how the state’s emerging budget deficit could affect City-funded programs before adjourning.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Annual audit purpose and process
Finance Director Jesse Takahashi introduces the City’s annual financial audit and explains that independent auditors from MGO have completed their review of Mountain View’s books for fiscal year ending June 30, 2022. He notes that the audit is required by the City Charter and that the resulting Annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR) and related auditor reports are scheduled for City Council acceptance on December 13, pending the Finance Committee’s recommendation. Takahashi briefly characterizes the General Operating Fund’s performance and frames the committee’s role as reviewing results and recommending acceptance. This sets the stage for the auditor’s detailed report on financial statements, standards applied, and key findings.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi explains that MGO serves as the City’s independent auditor pursuant to City Charter Section 1106, which requires an external CPA to perform the annual audit.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi states that the Annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR) and related auditor reports will go to the full Council as a consent item on December 13, contingent on the Finance Committee’s recommendation.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi reports that the General Operating Fund closed fiscal year 2022 with a $17.5 million operating balance.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi clarifies that staff will seek a formal recommendation from the Finance Committee to accept the ACFR and the auditor’s reports.
#### Audit results and lease accounting implementation
Auditor Benjamin (Benny) Lau from MGO presents the core audit results for the year ended June 30, 2022. He reports that MGO issues an unmodified, or “clean,” opinion on the City’s financial statements, prepared under U.S. GAAP, generally accepted auditing standards, and government auditing standards. Lau highlights the implementation of GASB Statement No. 87 on leases, which significantly increases reported lease receivables due to large tech-related leases in the City, and he notes key footnotes and the scale of the new balances. He also confirms there are no findings on internal control over financial reporting, no unrecorded misstatements, and that the single audit of federal awards (including ARPA funds) is still pending but expected by the March 31, 2023 deadline.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau states that MGO has completed the audit of the City’s ACFR and issued an unmodified (clean) opinion on the financial statements.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau explains that the audit follows U.S. GAAP, generally accepted auditing standards, and government auditing standards to provide reasonable assurance the statements are free of material misstatement.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau reports that the opinion, including an emphasis of matter on lease accounting, appears on page 1 of the opinion document (PDF page 25 of the 258-page ACFR).
- Auditor Benjamin Lau highlights that the City implemented GASB Statement No. 87 on leases, resulting in approximately $800 million of lease receivables with offsetting deferred inflows recorded on the balance sheet.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau notes that the large lease receivable balance primarily relates to a few major leases with a large technology firm in the city, making Mountain View’s lease balance unusually high relative to most municipalities.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau confirms that MGO performed detailed testing of the lease balances due to their magnitude and considers them reasonably stated.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau identifies key disclosure notes as Note 8 and Note 9 (open items), Note 11 (leases, around page 114), and Note 13 (commitments).
- Auditor Benjamin Lau reports that MGO identified no findings related to internal control over financial reporting for fiscal year 2022.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau states that the City’s accounting estimates and practices are reasonable and that there are no unrecorded misstatements to report.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau informs the committee that the single audit on federal awards, including compliance testing, has not yet been completed but is expected to meet the March 31, 2023 statutory deadline.
#### Single audit and ARPA funds treatment
Mayor Lucas Ramirez seeks clarification on how American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) funds will appear in the City’s audits. He confirms with the auditor that ARPA funds, as federal money, will be covered in the single audit rather than fully reflected in the current general ACFR analysis. This distinction matters for tracking federal compliance separately from overall financial performance. The committee gains assurance that ARPA-related activity will be examined under the single audit timeline.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez asks whether the single audit will include American Rescue Plan Act funds because they are federal funds, and the auditor confirms that ARPA will be part of the single audit.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez clarifies that ARPA appropriations would be more fully addressed through the single audit process rather than directly in the ACFR narrative.
#### Equipment maintenance and replacement fund balance
Mayor Ramirez raises concerns about the high and growing balance in the Equipment Maintenance and Replacement Fund, currently about $26 million, relative to historical spending patterns. He notes that, apart from a one-time fire fleet replacement, expenditures have not approached the accumulated balance over the past 15 years, suggesting potential overcharging of departmental fees. Takahashi explains how charges are set to amortize fleet replacement with inflation and that extended vehicle life can slow spending, but he acknowledges the balance may be higher than necessary at any given time. Staff commit to reviewing the funding level and internal charges to determine if rate adjustments are warranted and will report back to the committee.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez observes that the Equipment Maintenance and Replacement Fund balance has grown to approximately $26 million, which appears large relative to the fund’s historical expenditure levels.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez notes that the only recent significant draw on the fund was the one-time replacement of the fire apparatus fleet and that similar large expenditures are not anticipated soon.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi explains that the fire fleet replacement was a unique, policy-driven decision, while other fleet assets are replaced individually as needed.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi confirms there is no comparable large, one-time fleet replacement currently planned beyond the staged payments for the fire apparatus, which will be made over one to two years as vehicles are delivered.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez questions whether departmental charges to the fund may be too high, given that balances continue to accumulate beyond what usage history seems to justify.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi explains that internal charges are based on amortizing expected replacement costs over asset lives, including inflation, with a goal of funding at 100% rather than overfunding.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi notes that vehicles sometimes remain in service longer than their assumed useful lives, delaying replacement spending and contributing to higher interim fund balances.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau clarifies that the $26 million is reported as “cash and investments” because it represents this fund’s share of the City’s pooled cash and investment portfolio rather than a separate, dedicated bank account.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi acknowledges that the likelihood of needing to replace the entire fleet at once is low and agrees that staff can examine whether the fund’s target balance and internal rates can be reduced to maintain a more reasonable level.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi commits that staff will review the Equipment Maintenance and Replacement Fund’s charges and balance levels and will report back to the Finance Committee with potential rate adjustments or updated targets.
#### Cash and investment pooling structure
In the context of the equipment fund, Mayor Ramirez asks what portion of the $26 million balance is cash versus investments. Auditor Lau explains the City’s pooled cash and investment structure, where individual funds hold proportional shares of a central pool rather than separate accounts. He notes that the “cash and investments” label reflects this shared pool, which is managed with a focus on liquidity and includes some separately held bond proceeds. This clarification helps the committee understand the nature and accessibility of reported balances in internal service funds.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez asks how much of the Equipment Maintenance and Replacement Fund’s $26 million balance is held as cash versus investments.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau explains that the City operates a single pooled cash and investment system, and the reported amount represents the internal service fund’s share of that pool rather than a separate account.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau states that the term “cash and investments” is a consolidated description because the pooled balance includes both liquid cash and investment instruments governed by the City’s investment policy.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau notes that while some funds tied to bond issuances may be held in distinct trustee accounts, internal service funds such as the equipment fund generally participate in the central cash and investment pool.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau emphasizes that the pool is managed to maintain sufficient liquidity so that each fund, including the equipment fund, can draw cash as needed for operations and capital purchases.
#### Tracking unrestricted community benefit revenues
Mayor Ramirez shifts to the issue of tracking community benefit revenues from development, distinguishing them from restricted developer fees. He notes that community benefit contributions are often more flexible and wants a way for Council to see how much is received and how it is used over time, separate from other developer fees. Takahashi explains that the ACFR aggregates these revenues with other developer-related income and that detailed tracking occurs in internal subledgers rather than in published financial statements. Ramirez suggests that over time it may become harder for policymakers to distinguish unrestricted community benefits from restricted fees and asks staff to consider ways to make this reporting clearer, even if not in the ACFR itself.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez notes that community benefit revenues from development are generally more flexible than restricted developer fees and asks how they can be tracked over time for policy oversight.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi explains that community benefit funds are tracked in internal subledgers within the City’s financial system rather than as a separate line item in the ACFR.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi confirms that community benefit revenues are aggregated within broader categories such as “other developer fees” in the ACFR, making them hard to isolate in that document.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez points out that many developer fees are restricted for specific purposes (such as staffing or particular programs) while community benefit contributions are not, and he expresses concern that this distinction is not visible in current high-level reporting.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi acknowledges the reporting limitations in the ACFR and reiterates that staff can access detailed community benefit data from internal ledgers when needed for Council reporting.
#### Community benefits that bypass city ledgers
Councilmember Lisa Matichak extends the community benefits discussion by pointing out that not all such benefits take the form of cash paid to the City. She cites the recently approved East Whisman master plan, where community benefits include rent subsidies, parking spaces, and infrastructure investments provided directly by the developer. Takahashi confirms that when benefits do not involve payments to the City, they do not appear in the City’s financial ledgers or the ACFR. As a result, the City’s accounting only captures a partial picture of community benefit value, with in-kind or third-party-directed benefits tracked outside formal financial statements.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak observes that many community benefits, such as subsidized rents or developer-built infrastructure in the East Whisman master plan, never flow through the City’s accounts.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak asks whether community benefits provided directly by developers to tenants or for on-site improvements are recorded in the City’s financial statements.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi confirms that community benefits that do not result in cash payments to the City are not recorded on City ledgers and thus do not appear in the ACFR.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi notes that this means the City’s financial reports provide an incomplete picture of total community benefit value, capturing only the cash-based components.
#### ACFR content clarifications and terminology
Councilmember Matichak shares several detailed observations about the ACFR, focusing on how information is presented and labeled. She questions a schedule showing top employers where LinkedIn appears to have zero employees in 2021–22 and learns from Lau that the schedule reflects only the current top ten employers with at least 500 employees; prior-period comparatives show a different top ten from a decade earlier. She also seeks clarification on why public safety expenditures appear to have declined, prompting an explanation from Assistant Director Grace Zheng that favorable CalPERS investment returns under GASB 68 reduced reported entity-wide public safety expense. These clarifications help committee members interpret the ACFR’s comparative tables more accurately.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak notes that the ACFR schedule of principal employers appears to show LinkedIn with zero Mountain View employees in 2021–22 and asks if that reflects reality.
- Auditor Benjamin Lau explains that the principal employer schedule lists only the top ten current employers with at least 500 employees and that LinkedIn does not appear because its local headcount likely falls below that threshold, not because it is necessarily zero.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi adds that the schedule’s right-hand column shows the top ten employers from ten years ago, which is why some entities like Symantec remain listed historically but not in the current top ten.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak questions why public safety expenditures appear to decline year-over-year, given staffing and service expectations.
- Assistant Finance Director Grace Zheng explains that the public safety expenditure figure reflects citywide public safety costs including impacts from GASB 68 pension accounting, and that strong CalPERS investment returns significantly reduced net pension expense for the public safety plan in fiscal year 2022.
- Assistant Finance Director Grace Zheng clarifies that the favorable pension accounting impact applies specifically to the public safety plan, which is why it shows as a decrease in the public safety expenditure category.
#### ACFR governance terminology and boards listing
Matichak requests specific editorial changes to make the ACFR more inclusive and up to date in its governance descriptions. She asks that references to “Mountain View citizens” be updated to “Mountain View residents,” noting that “citizens” can be interpreted as U.S. citizenship rather than all people who live in the city. She also points out that the Public Safety Advisory Board is missing from the listed boards and commissions. Takahashi and Zheng acknowledge these points, explain that some content is as of January 2022, and commit to correcting terminology and adding the advisory board in future iterations or where appropriate.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak requests that the ACFR’s Exhibit 3 be updated to replace the term “Mountain View citizens” with “Mountain View residents” to more accurately encompass all who live in the city.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi notes that a similar terminology change has already been made in the City’s budget documents and agrees it should also be applied in the ACFR.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak points out that the Public Safety Advisory Board is missing from the ACFR’s listing of boards and commissions and asks that it be added.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi and Assistant Finance Director Grace Zheng acknowledge that the ACFR content is as of January 2022 but agree that the Public Safety Advisory Board should be reflected and commit to correcting the document moving forward.
#### Improving ACFR format for digital use
Matichak comments on the ACFR’s layout, noting that it is optimized for print rather than digital reading. She explains that some tables span across pages in ways that require opening two copies of the PDF side-by-side to read horizontally, which complicates review given the City’s shift away from printed materials. She urges staff to modernize the formatting for electronic consumption. Takahashi thanks her for the feedback and agrees that staff will explore reformatting options to better support digital access.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak reports that the ACFR’s current print-oriented layout makes it cumbersome to read electronically, particularly for wide tables that span across pages.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak explains that she had to open two instances of the PDF side-by-side to read some schedules, which she finds inefficient given the City’s transition to mainly electronic document delivery.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak recommends that the ACFR be reformatted to better support digital review, reflecting how Council and the public now typically access documents.
- Finance Director Jesse Takahashi thanks her for the comments and commits that staff will work on reformatting the ACFR for the “electronic age.”
#### Committee recommendation to accept ACFR and auditor reports
After discussion, Mayor Ramirez asks whether a formal motion is required to recommend City Council acceptance of the ACFR and the audit reports. Upon confirmation, he moves staff’s recommendation, expressing strong appreciation for the thoroughness of the document and staff’s work. Matichak seconds the motion, and the committee votes unanimously in favor via roll call. This action forwards a recommendation that Council accept the fiscal year 2021–2022 ACFR and associated auditor reports at the upcoming December 13 meeting.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez asks if the Finance Committee must formally recommend that the City Council accept the ACFR and related auditor reports, and staff confirms that a motion is needed.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez moves to approve the staff recommendation that the Council accept the Annual Comprehensive Financial Report and the auditor’s reports.
- Councilmember Lisa Matichak seconds the motion to recommend Council acceptance of the ACFR and audit reports.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez publicly thanks Auditor Benjamin Lau, Finance Director Jesse Takahashi, City Clerk and staff including Victoria Labrador and Grace Zheng, and the broader finance team for producing a comprehensive and informative ACFR.
- City Clerk Victoria Labrador conducts a roll call vote, and the motion to recommend Council acceptance of the ACFR and related reports passes unanimously.

#### Request for analysis of state budget impacts
Chair Lieber requests that staff prepare information for the Council on how the State of California’s deteriorating fiscal outlook could affect Mountain View’s programs. She references the Legislative Analyst’s Office report projecting a $25 billion state budget shortfall that could grow to $50 billion, noting that the City frequently relies on temporary state funding streams. Lieber asks for an assessment of how changes at the state level might impact programs to which the City has made commitments. This request establishes a future work item for staff outside the ACFR review itself.
- Chair Sally Lieber asks for an analysis of how the State of California’s changing financial picture, including a projected $25 billion budget shortfall potentially growing to $50 billion, might impact City programs.
- Chair Sally Lieber notes that many City initiatives rely on ongoing or renewed state funding and expresses concern about the sustainability of such support given the state’s projected deficits.
- Chair Sally Lieber requests that finance staff prepare information for the full Council outlining potential impacts on programs for which the City has made commitments if state funding is reduced or eliminated.
