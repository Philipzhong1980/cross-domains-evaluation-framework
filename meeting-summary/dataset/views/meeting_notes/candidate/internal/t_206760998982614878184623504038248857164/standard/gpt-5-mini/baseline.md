### Overview
  The Finance Committee convenes to review and recommend the city's FY2021-22 Annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR) following an external audit by Macias Gini & O'Connell (MGO). Auditor Benjamin Lau presents an unmodified (clean) opinion while emphasizing the impact of the new GASB 87 lease standard—an approximately $800 million lease receivable and offsetting deferred inflow driven largely by a major local tech firm—which MGO reviewed in detail and deems reasonably stated; MGO also reports no internal control findings. Committee discussion covers single-audit timing (federal awards including ARPA funds are expected to be audited by March 31, 2023), concerns about a $26 million equipment replacement fund balance (staff will review funding levels and charge methodology), the tracking of community benefit revenues (staff track these in sub-ledgers though some developer benefits do not flow through city accounts), data presentation items (top-employers cutoff, pension investment impacts on public safety net expense), and editorial/formatting corrections to the ACFR (for example changing “citizens” to “residents” and adding the Public Safety Advisory Board). Staff commits to follow up on the equipment fund review and ACFR corrections, and Chair Lieber requests analysis of potential state budget impacts on city commitments. The committee unanimously recommends that Council accept the ACFR and related auditor reports and thanks the audit and finance teams for their work.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Minutes approval and roll call
  The committee reviews and approves the minutes from February 28, 2022, by motion and roll call. The vote is unanimous and the clerk records the results. This completes the procedural business before moving to new items.
- Chair Sally Lieber calls for a motion to approve the February 28, 2022 minutes.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez moves to approve the minutes and Council Member Lisa Matichak seconds the motion, as reported by Jesse Takahashi.
- City Clerk Victoria Labrador conducts the virtual roll call and records a unanimous approval.
  #### Annual financial audit presentation
  Auditor Benjamin Lau of MGO presents the city's fiscal year 2021-22 audit results and the Annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR). He summarizes scope, standards applied, and the overall outcome while staff confirms the general fund ended the year with a $17.5 million operating balance. The presentation frames the ACFR acceptance as a consent item pending the committee's recommendation to full Council.
- Jesse Takahashi introduces Benjamin Lau of Macias Gini & O'Connell (MGO) as the city's external auditor.
- Benjamin Lau states that MGO completed the audit of the ACFR for the year ended June 30, 2022.
- Jesse Takahashi notes the general operating fund closed fiscal year 2022 with a $17.5 million operating balance.
- Benjamin Lau explains the ACFR is extensive (about 258 pages) and will be presented to Council as a consent item on December 13th pending this committee's recommendation.
  #### GASB 87 leases impact
  The audit highlights the city's implementation of the new lease accounting standard (GASB 87), which creates a material presentation effect on the balance sheet. The city records an approximately $800 million lease receivable with an offsetting deferred inflow, largely driven by significant leases from a major local tech firm. The auditor reports that, despite the unusual magnitude, he reviewed the balances in detail and deems them reasonably stated.
- Benjamin Lau reports an approximately $800 million lease receivable recorded under the city's implementation of GASB 87.
- Benjamin Lau attributes the large lease amounts primarily to leases associated with a major technology firm operating in the city.
- Benjamin Lau indicates the receivable and offsetting deferred inflow amortize differently and that MGO reviewed the amounts in detail and finds them reasonable.
  #### Internal control and single audit
  MGO reports no findings on internal control over financial reporting for FY2021-22 and deems accounting estimates reasonable. The single audit of federal awards is not yet complete but is expected to meet the statutory March 31, 2023 deadline. Committee members confirm that American Rescue Plan (ARPA) federal funds will be included in the single audit.
- Benjamin Lau reports that MGO identified no internal control findings in the financial statement audit for FY2021-22.
- Benjamin Lau states the city's accounting estimates and practices are reasonable and no unrecorded adjustments were identified.
- Benjamin Lau explains the single audit for federal awards is pending and expected to be completed by March 31, 2023.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez and staff confirm that ARPA (American Rescue Plan) funds will be included in the single audit of federal awards.
  #### Equipment fund balance review
  Mayor Ramirez raises concerns about the Equipment Maintenance and Replacement Fund holding approximately $26 million, questioning whether charges and accumulated balances are higher than needed given historical spending. Staff explains that some of the balance represents committed but not yet paid amounts (for example, staggered payments for recently ordered fire apparatus) and that internal service funds participate in a pooled city investment account. Jesse Takahashi commits to reviewing funding levels and the charge methodology and to report back with potential adjustments.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez questions why the Equipment Maintenance and Replacement Fund holds about $26 million when historical spending is much lower.
- Benjamin Lau explains that the 'cash and investments' line reflects each fund's share of the city's pooled cash and investments rather than discrete separate bank accounts.
- Jesse Takahashi explains some funds are committed but not yet spent (for example, staged payments for fire apparatus) and that balances can reflect those commitments.
- Jesse Takahashi commits that staff will review the equipment replacement fund charging levels and consider adjustments and will report back to the committee.
  #### Community benefits tracking
  Mayor Ramirez asks how community benefit revenues from development are tracked over time and whether they should be reported distinctly in the ACFR. Jesse Takahashi explains community benefits are tracked in the city's financial system sub-ledgers and are not separately presented within the ACFR, and that some developer-provided community benefits do not flow through city accounts. The discussion highlights difficulty for council members in tracking unrestricted community benefit balances from the ACFR alone.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez asks how the city tracks developer-paid community benefits over time and whether they should be separately visible in the ACFR.
- Jesse Takahashi explains those contributions are tracked in sub-ledgers in the city's financial system and are not separately broken out in the ACFR document.
- Jesse Takahashi notes that some community benefits are mixed in the developer fee aggregates and are therefore not readily visible in the ACFR.
- Lisa Matichak observes that many developer-provided benefits (such as subsidized rent or built infrastructure) never flow through city accounts and therefore do not appear in city financial statements.
  #### Top employers listing question
  Council Member Matichak notes the ACFR schedule showing top employers lists LinkedIn with zero employees, prompting a question about data accuracy. Auditor Benjamin Lau clarifies that the schedule only lists top employers above the report's cutoff (about 500 employees), so LinkedIn may be present in the city but fall below the cutoff for listing. The committee recognizes the schedule reflects a top-ten cutoff rather than an absolute zero.
- Lisa Matichak asks why LinkedIn appears with zero employees on the top employers schedule.
- Benjamin Lau explains the schedule lists only the top ten employers using a cutoff (around 500 employees), so entities below that threshold will not appear on the list.
  #### Public safety expenditure change
  Council Member Matichak notes a decrease in public safety net expenditures and asks for clarification. Assistant Director Grace Zheng explains that pension plan investment earnings under GASB 68 materially reduced the net public safety expense in FY2021-22, which is reflected in the ACFR's public safety category. The committee accepts that actuarial/pension accounting movements drive the observed change rather than direct reductions in operational spending.
- Lisa Matichak observes public safety expenditures declined compared to prior years and asks whether overtime or staffing changes explain it.
- Grace Zheng explains that pension plan investment earnings recorded under GASB 68 reduce the entity-wide net expense and specifically lower the public safety category in the ACFR.
  #### ACFR content and formatting corrections
  Council members recommend editorial and formatting updates to the ACFR for clarity and electronic readability, including changing labels from 'citizens' to 'residents' and adding the Public Safety Advisory Board to the list of advisory bodies. Staff agrees to make the requested textual corrections and to explore improved electronic formatting for easier review. This is presented as a follow-up action for staff rather than an issue in the audit opinion.
- Lisa Matichak requests changing the terminology from 'citizens' to 'residents' in the ACFR and adding the Public Safety Advisory Board to the advisory list.
- Lisa Matichak requests that the ACFR be reformatted for easier electronic review rather than a print-oriented layout.
- Jesse Takahashi and Grace Zheng agree to correct the exhibit and pursue improved electronic formatting for future reports.
  #### Recommendation to accept ACFR
  Mayor Ramirez moves that the committee recommend Council accept the ACFR and related auditor reports, and Council Member Matichak seconds the motion. The committee conducts a roll call and unanimously approves the recommendation to place the ACFR on the December 13 consent calendar. The recommendation completes the committee's action on the FY2021-22 financial reporting cycle.
- Mayor Lucas Ramirez moves to recommend that the City Council accept the FY2021-22 ACFR and related auditor reports.
- Council Member Lisa Matichak seconds the motion and Assistant Clerk Victoria Labrador records the unanimous approval by roll call.
- Jesse Takahashi confirms the ACFR will be a consent item on the December 13 Council agenda following the committee recommendation.

Chair Sally Lieber requests that staff analyze potential state budget changes and their likely impacts on city commitments given recent state fiscal uncertainty.
