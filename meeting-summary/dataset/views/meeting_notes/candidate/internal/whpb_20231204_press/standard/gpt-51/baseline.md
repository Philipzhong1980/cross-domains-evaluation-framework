### Overview
The briefing features Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan detailing the administration’s domestic and foreign policy priorities, with an emphasis on security, Ukraine, and the Middle East. Jean-Pierre opens by touting enforcement gains under the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act and new EPA methane rules announced at COP28, while framing gun violence prevention and climate policy as core Biden priorities. Sullivan then describes intensive diplomacy with Israel, Qatar, Egypt, and others to free hostages, including Americans, and to keep humanitarian aid flowing into Gaza as Israel resumes operations against Hamas; he stresses Israel’s right to self-defense alongside a U.S. insistence on civilian protection and adherence to the laws of war. He also outlines growing Iran-enabled threats across the region, including Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping and militia strikes on U.S. forces, and previews potential multinational maritime security efforts. Turning to Ukraine, Sullivan and Jean-Pierre underscore that U.S. weapons funding will soon be exhausted absent congressional action, argue that past aid has enabled major Ukrainian gains, and contend that voting against new funding would objectively strengthen Vladimir Putin, even as border-policy negotiations complicate passage of the broader supplemental for Ukraine, Israel, and Taiwan. Throughout, Jean-Pierre deflects detailed questions on sensitive issues—ranging from FISA surveillance and social media moderation to border concessions, state election practices, and Hunter Biden financial allegations—while reiterating high-level positions on voting access, opposition to antisemitism and hate, and unequivocal condemnation of Hamas’s October 7 atrocities, including sexual violence, positioning the administration as focused on security, legality, and humanitarian principles at home and abroad.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Impact of Bipartisan Safer Communities Act
Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre highlights the administration’s progress on gun violence prevention, emphasizing enforcement of the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act. She notes that DOJ has prosecuted over 250 people for illegal gun purchases and trafficking, strengthening deterrence and reducing the flow of weapons to criminals. The law also expands background checks and funds state red flag laws, which 21 states have now implemented. The administration frames these actions as significant but insufficient absent further congressional legislation.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that the Department of Justice has prosecuted over 250 people for illegal gun purchases and firearms trafficking under the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre explains that stronger penalty provisions in the law are meant to signal to potential offenders that they should reconsider illegal gun purchases and trafficking.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that the Act strengthens background checks and provides hundreds of millions of dollars for states to implement red flag laws.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says 21 states, including Minnesota and Michigan, have implemented their own red flag laws using federal funding.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre contrasts President Biden’s support for police funding with what she describes as Republican efforts to cut such funding via the Default on America Act.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre urges Congress to pass universal background checks, a national red flag law, an assault weapons ban, and further investments in proven anti-violence solutions.
#### EPA methane standards and COP28
Jean-Pierre announces EPA’s final standards to sharply reduce methane emissions from oil and gas operations, unveiled during COP28. She underscores that the rule advances President Biden’s climate agenda, aiming to prevent 1.5 billion metric tons of greenhouse gas emissions and generate major health and economic benefits. She links this to broader domestic and international efforts to cut super-pollutants like methane. The administration positions climate change as an existential threat and stresses continued U.S. global leadership.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre announces that EPA has finalized standards to sharply reduce methane emissions from oil and gas operations.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the methane rule will prevent 1.5 billion metric tons of greenhouse gas emissions and deliver billions of dollars in health and economic benefits.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre asserts that the United States is turbocharging the speed and scale of climate action at home and abroad, especially on super pollutants like methane.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reiterates that President Biden treats climate change as an existential threat and pursues the most ambitious U.S. climate agenda ever.
#### Hostage negotiations and Gaza humanitarian aid
National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan reports on intensive diplomatic efforts with Israel, Egypt, Qatar, and others since Hamas’s October 7 attacks. He describes ongoing negotiations to secure hostage releases, including Americans, and sustain humanitarian aid into Gaza, while Israel continues operations against Hamas. Sullivan says Hamas’s refusal to release remaining civilian women ended the temporary hostage agreement and pause in hostilities. He also details U.S. humanitarian support, including $100 million in aid and a first U.S. airlift of 54,000 pounds of supplies, and stresses continued work to increase aid flows via Rafah.
- Mr. Sullivan says he has held several rounds of intensive calls with partners in Israel, Egypt, Qatar, and other nations to address hostages, humanitarian assistance, and Israel’s military operations.
- Mr. Sullivan reports that he met with families of American hostages for a second time and describes their experience as gut‑wrenching and unimaginable.
- Mr. Sullivan states that Hamas’s refusal to release civilian women who should have been part of the agreement caused the end of the hostage deal and the pause in fighting.
- Mr. Sullivan pledges that President Biden, Secretary Blinken, and CIA Director Burns will keep pressing Israeli, Qatari, and Egyptian counterparts to secure hostage releases, especially of Americans.
- Mr. Sullivan notes the U.S. has announced $100 million in humanitarian support for Palestinians and calls on the international community to fulfill the U.N. flash appeal.
- Mr. Sullivan highlights that a U.S. airlift delivered over 54,000 pounds of medical items and food aid into Gaza as the first of several planned deliveries.
- Mr. Sullivan later confirms that aid trucks are moving through the Rafah crossing after the pause and that Washington is pressing Israel to increase throughput.
#### Israeli operations in Gaza and civilian protection
Sullivan addresses questions about Israel’s renewed operations in Gaza after the pause, focusing on efforts to protect civilians. He notes that Israel has delineated specific ground maneuver areas in the south and urged civilians to move, and he says the U.S. is pushing Israel to ensure civilians receive and can act on those warnings. He acknowledges high Palestinian civilian casualties and reiterates U.S. expectations that Israel comply with the laws of war and safeguard civilians, including in designated no‑strike zones and allegedly safer areas. He avoids detailed qualitative judgments on Israel’s conduct pending more data, but frames civilian protection as a central topic in U.S.-Israel discussions.
- Mr. Sullivan says it is too soon, after only 48–72 hours since the pause ended, to offer a comprehensive public judgment on Israel’s current operations in southern Gaza.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that Israel has taken the unusual step of identifying specific areas for ground maneuver and asking civilians in those areas to move out.
- Mr. Sullivan emphasizes that the U.S. is working to ensure a sustained flow of food, water, medicine, and shelter into Gaza so displaced civilians have resources when they move.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates that Israel has every right to target Hamas terrorists responsible for the October 7 massacre and ongoing rocket fire but also has a responsibility to protect civilians.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Israel has designated no‑strike zones in the south and says the U.S. expects Israel to abide by those commitments.
- Mr. Sullivan states that too many innocent civilians have died in this conflict and that U.S. officials consistently stress operations must be consistent with the laws of war.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. urges Israel to give civilians time, capacity, and real opportunity to leave areas before ground operations begin, viewing this as both morally right and operationally effective.
#### Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping
Sullivan outlines a series of Houthi attacks on commercial vessels in the southern Red Sea and the response by the USS Carney. He notes that three ships, linked to 14 countries, came under attack and that the Carney downed three incoming UAVs. Sullivan stresses that many targeted ships may have no ties to Israel, underscoring the attacks’ illegality and global economic implications. He attributes enabling responsibility to Iran, says attacks on commercial shipping are unacceptable, and describes ongoing consultations to form a multinational maritime task force and broader response, while refusing to detail potential consequences for Iran until consultations conclude.
- Mr. Sullivan reports that four attacks were carried out against three commercial vessels operating in international waters in the southern Red Sea, involving ships connected to 14 different nations.
- Mr. Sullivan says the USS Carney responded to distress calls and took down three UAVs approaching at different times, though it is not yet clear if the destroyer itself was the target.
- Mr. Sullivan asserts that attacks on commercial shipping in international waters are totally unacceptable under international law and must stop.
- Mr. Sullivan states that the Houthis are conducting the attacks but that the weapons are supplied by Iran, making Iran the ultimate responsible party in Washington’s view.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that the U.S. is in talks with partner nations about forming a maritime task force to ensure safe passage of ships in the Red Sea.
- Mr. Sullivan says the administration is consulting allies and partners to craft a response with broad international buy‑in before announcing next steps.
#### Iran-backed regional threats and U.S. deterrence
Sullivan places the Houthi attacks in a wider pattern of Iran-enabled actions across the region. He notes repeated attacks by Shia militia groups in Iraq and Syria against U.S. forces, and cites a recent CENTCOM strike that killed five militants preparing a UAV attack. He argues that U.S. force posture, including carrier deployments, has helped prevent a broader regional war since October 7. While acknowledging continued alarming behavior, he insists Washington will respond in coordination with partners at a time and manner of its choosing to deter further escalation.
- Mr. Sullivan says U.S. deployment of carrier strike groups and other assets has helped prevent Israel–Hamas fighting from expanding into a full regional conflagration.
- Mr. Sullivan highlights ongoing attacks against U.S. forces by Iranian-enabled Shia militias in Iraq and Syria and notes the U.S. is both protecting personnel and striking back.
- Mr. Sullivan references a recent CENTCOM strike that killed five militants attempting to attack U.S. forces with UAVs.
- Mr. Sullivan underscores that dealing with Houthi threats to shipping must be a global effort, not just a U.S. responsibility, and that Washington will act in concert with others at a time and place of its choosing.
#### Ukraine funding urgency and congressional dynamics
Sullivan and Jean-Pierre stress that U.S. funding for Ukraine is nearly exhausted and that congressional approval of the supplemental request is critical. Sullivan cites a letter from OMB Director Shalanda Young warning resources to procure and backfill weapons will run out by year-end, implying U.S. support will halt absent new funds. He argues current and past aid helped Ukraine halt Russian advances, regain over 50% of occupied territory, and strengthen the U.S. defense industrial base. He frames a vote against Ukraine funding as objectively aiding Putin, while Jean-Pierre echoes the national security stakes and points to ongoing outreach to Congress amid stalled border-linked negotiations.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that without new congressional action, the administration will soon lack resources to procure additional weapons for Ukraine or to replenish U.S. stocks without harming readiness.
- Mr. Sullivan credits U.S. and allied support with halting Russia’s advances, enabling Ukraine to retake more than 50 percent of previously occupied territory, and revitalizing the U.S. defense industrial base.
- Mr. Sullivan states that any member of Congress who votes against funding for Ukraine is voting for an outcome that improves Putin’s strategic position, even if that is not their motive.
- Mr. Sullivan insists there is no “magical pot of funding” to draw from and that Congress must act to sustain Ukraine support.
- Mr. Sullivan says failure by the U.S. to provide aid would weaken the broad international coalition backing Ukraine and undermine appeals for others to contribute.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that a strong majority in both parties supports Ukraine aid and argues there is no reason it should not receive an up-or-down vote in both chambers.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reinforces that the supplemental is essential to U.S. national security and says President Biden and aides have been in regular contact with congressional leaders.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre declines to detail specific border policy concessions under discussion, saying she will not negotiate from the podium and that staff are providing technical assistance to Hill talks.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre frames continued support to Ukraine as the right and necessary response to Russian aggression, rejecting the notion that seeking funding is an act of desperation.
#### Linkage to Taiwan, Israel, and border policy
Sullivan responds to questions about the broader security supplemental, which also includes funding for Taiwan and Israel, and potential immigration policy tradeoffs. He avoids framing opposition to the package as direct support for adversaries like Xi or Hamas but asserts that every dollar in the package advances U.S. national security and stability in key regions. Jean-Pierre, addressing reports that Republicans see the White House as eager enough on Ukraine aid to accept restrictive border policies, rejects the characterization of desperation. She reiterates Biden’s long-standing push for comprehensive immigration reform and insists any border measures must remain consistent with a humane and protective approach, while refusing to publicly specify negotiating red lines.
- Mr. Sullivan says each component of the national security supplemental is designed to enhance U.S. security and stability in vital regions, including funding for Taiwan and Israel.
- Mr. Sullivan argues that failing to pass the supplemental in its entirety is a vote against what the administration considers necessary to secure U.S. national security objectives.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre rejects the claim that the White House is so desperate for Ukraine funding that it will accept otherwise unacceptable asylum or immigration changes, calling support for Ukraine a principled stance, not desperation.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre underscores that President Biden proposed comprehensive immigration reform on day one and faults Congress for failing to act over three years.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reiterates that the President seeks border policies that protect Americans and treat migrants humanely, and she refuses to discuss specific asylum concessions being negotiated in the Senate.
#### Post-conflict Gaza, refugees, and Arab engagement
Sullivan discusses preliminary conversations about Gaza’s future and Arab roles while keeping details largely confidential. He confirms ongoing U.S. discussions with Israel and regional partners on post-conflict arrangements and notes that Vice President Harris met leaders at COP28 and then briefed President Biden extensively. He clarifies that the U.S. is not currently engaged in active talks to resettle Palestinians from Gaza into other countries. He also previews upcoming visits by Arab foreign ministers to Washington, where long-term political horizons for Palestinians and differences over a ceasefire will be central topics.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that the administration is talking with Israel about the duration and trajectory of current military operations but declines to share specifics publicly.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Vice President Harris met regional leaders at COP28 and later gave President Biden a detailed private readout of those discussions, including future-of-Gaza issues.
- Mr. Sullivan states that the U.S. is not in active talks with other countries about resettling Palestinians from Gaza into their territories at this time.
- Mr. Sullivan says there is broad agreement with Arab partners on a long-term vision that includes greater regional peace and a political horizon for Palestinians rooted in a two‑state solution.
- Mr. Sullivan acknowledges a clear disagreement with visiting Arab foreign ministers over calls for an immediate ceasefire, reiterating the U.S. position that Israel has the right to continue military operations against Hamas while hostages remain in captivity.
#### American and other hostages held by Hamas
Sullivan details the stalled hostage negotiations and U.S. priorities concerning Americans still held by Hamas. He explains that the initial agreement focused on women and children, but Hamas’s refusal to release remaining civilian women halted the deal. Israel, he says, refuses to close the book on those women and is also prepared to discuss other categories of hostages once Hamas complies. The U.S. continues intense talks with Israel, Qatar, and Egypt and is considering all options, including approaches focused on American dual nationals, though Sullivan declines to describe specific strategies publicly.
- Mr. Sullivan recounts that the first phase of hostage negotiations centered on releasing women and children, but Hamas is still holding civilian women who should have been freed under the deal.
- Mr. Sullivan says Hamas’s failure to release those women is the core reason the hostage agreement and the pause in fighting collapsed.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that Israel insists Hamas follow through on releasing remaining civilian women and is open to then discussing releases of civilian men, the wounded, and ultimately all hostages, including IDF soldiers.
- Mr. Sullivan calls the release of American hostages a paramount U.S. priority and says President Biden is considering all available options to secure their freedom.
- Mr. Sullivan declines to speculate publicly on Hamas’s motives for withholding the women or to detail U.S. leverage and strategies, citing the sensitivity of ongoing diplomacy.
#### U.S. arms to Israel and monitoring of use
Sullivan faces questions on the volume and type of U.S. weapons being supplied to Israel and whether Washington monitors their use given reports of heavy bombing and extensive destruction in Gaza. He defers specific quantities and types of munitions to the Defense Department but confirms continuous high-level communication between the Pentagon and the Israel Defense Forces about the scope and nature of operations. He implies that those channels encompass discussions about how U.S.-supplied assistance is used in the conflict, while reiterating standard U.S. expectations regarding the laws of war and civilian protection.
- Mr. Sullivan refers questions about reports of 15,000 bombs and specific weapons transfers to the Department of Defense as the best source for detailed data.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. Defense Department and the Israeli Defense Forces are in constant communication about the scale and nature of Israel’s military operations.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that those ongoing conversations include how U.S. military assistance is deployed in the conflict.
#### Future of Gaza and Palestinian displacement concerns
A reporter asks whether the U.S. envisions refugee resettlement for Palestinians from Gaza in post-conflict planning. Sullivan responds that Washington is not currently engaged in active discussions with countries about resettling Palestinians out of Gaza, implicitly distancing the administration from proposals that might suggest permanent displacement. This stance aligns with the broader U.S. messaging that the focus should be on Gaza’s reconstruction and governance within a framework leading toward a two‑state solution, not on depopulation.
- Mr. Sullivan clarifies that the U.S. is not in active talks with any countries about resettling Palestinians from Gaza outside the territory.
- Mr. Sullivan’s answer implicitly affirms that post-conflict planning is oriented around Gaza’s future rather than large-scale external resettlement.
#### FISA Section 702 and domestic surveillance
In response to a question about Senator Wyden’s claims of a domestic call-record dragnet and the status of Section 702 of FISA, Jean-Pierre says she has no specific information to share. She indicates she would need to consult with internal teams and does not provide any substantive comment on the alleged AT&T program or the pause-and-resume pattern identified in reporting. The administration leaves unresolved, in this setting, how it views the program and the broader surveillance reauthorization debate.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says she has nothing specific to share regarding questions about a reported domestic call record program tied to Section 702 of FISA.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre indicates she must check with internal teams and offers no comment on the reasons such a program might have been paused and resumed.
#### Content moderation, subpoenas, and online speech
Asked about a House Judiciary Committee subpoena to former White House official Rob Flaherty over alleged efforts to influence social media moderation, Jean-Pierre declines to comment. She notes that the White House Counsel’s Office has already addressed the issue publicly and refers reporters to that office. The administration therefore avoids re-litigating past communications with platforms or expressing regret or defense over content-flagging practices in this briefing.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre declines to comment on the subpoena of Rob Flaherty regarding past White House contacts with social media platforms about content moderation.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre refers reporters to the White House Counsel’s Office, saying that office has already addressed the issue.
#### Journalist safety and war reporting in Gaza
Jean-Pierre responds to concerns over high journalist casualties in the Israel–Hamas war, where at least 60 media workers, mostly Palestinians, have reportedly been killed. Although she has not seen the specific interview cited, she affirms the administration’s view that journalists play a critical role in documenting events and must be protected. She says the U.S. monitors the situation and emphasizes that reporters should be able to gather and report facts without being deliberately put in danger, even recognizing that war zones are inherently risky.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says protecting journalists on the ground is critical because their reporting is essential to understanding what is happening in conflict zones.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre emphasizes that journalists should be able to gather and report facts without facing threats or deliberate endangerment, although she acknowledges they operate in dangerous settings.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that the U.S. is monitoring the situation and discussing concerns around journalist safety, even though she has not seen the specific interview referenced about casualties.
#### Election integrity, monitoring, and voting access
Jean-Pierre fields several questions about domestic election issues, including whether more election monitors are needed in swing states and whether specific state-level practices constitute voter suppression. She declines to opine on 2024-specific mechanisms, citing legal constraints and deference to campaigns and state authorities. However, she reiterates that the administration believes voting should be easier and more accessible, points to a Biden executive order expanding federal support for voter access, and maintains prior concerns about obstacles to voting, even as she refuses to re-litigate Georgia’s 2021 law or comment on Florida’s Democratic primary decisions.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the administration’s core position is that voting should be made easier, not harder, and that Americans should not face obstacles to exercising their rights.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that states manage their own election processes and declines to recommend additional election monitors or judge specific swing-state infrastructure.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre cites President Biden’s early executive action on voting access as evidence of federal efforts to improve voter education and accessibility.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre refuses to add to previous comments about Georgia’s 2021 election law or to characterize it again as voter suppression.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre declines to comment on the Florida Democratic Party’s decision effectively canceling its presidential primary, referring such questions to the campaign or the DNC and citing restrictions on commenting about 2024.
#### Hunter Biden business payments allegation
In closing, Jean-Pierre is asked about House Oversight Committee claims that a Hunter Biden company (Owasco PC) made direct payments to President Biden, seemingly contradicting prior statements that father and son were never in business together. She says she has not seen the report and refers the question to the White House Counsel’s Office. The administration thus provides no substantive response or explanation on the alleged financial arrangement during this briefing.
- A reporter cites bank records obtained by the House Oversight Committee showing direct payments from Hunter Biden’s company Owasco PC to President Biden and asks why such an arrangement existed if they were never in business together.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says she has not seen the report and directs questions about it to the White House Counsel’s Office, offering no further comment.
#### Border policy, Ukraine/Israel aid, and immigration advocates
Jean-Pierre addresses repeated questions about how far the administration is willing to go on border and asylum changes to unlock Ukraine and Israel funding. She refuses to lay out specific positions or red lines, insisting she will not negotiate from the podium. She stresses that Biden has approached immigration enforcement with a mix of enforcement, deterrence, and diplomacy and aims to handle border policy humanely while protecting Americans. She also rejects the idea that seeking a deal reflects desperation, and when asked about concerns from immigration advocates, she declines to address hypothetical outcomes but reiterates the President’s commitment to humane, secure policy.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre repeatedly declines to discuss specific border or asylum policy concessions linked to the supplemental funding negotiations, saying she will not negotiate publicly.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre emphasizes that President Biden has taken border security seriously since day one, combining enforcement, deterrence, and diplomacy while seeking humane treatment of migrants.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that Biden sent a comprehensive immigration reform bill to Congress on his first day in office and argues that lawmakers have failed to act.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre rejects the framing that the administration is “desperate” for Ukraine funding, characterizing support for Ukraine as a principled stance rather than a bargaining weakness.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre tells immigration advocates, indirectly, that she will not speculate on the specifics of any deal but insists that Biden’s approach has consistently balanced security with humanity.
#### Antisemitism, Muslim hate, and Hanukkah security
Jean-Pierre responds to questions about increased antisemitism and the safety of public Hanukkah events as Jewish communities prepare to celebrate amid the Israel–Hamas war. She acknowledges a clear uptick in antisemitic incidents and broader hate against multiple communities, including Muslims. She says the administration understands Jewish Americans’ fear and is taking actions to help these communities feel safe, although she does not detail specific measures or changes to planned White House Hanukkah observances.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre affirms that there has been a significant increase in antisemitism since the start of the Israel–Hamas war and says the administration understands why Jewish communities are fearful.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that hate incidents have also increased against other communities, including Muslims.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre pledges that the administration will do everything it can so affected communities, including Jewish Americans, feel safe, especially around holidays such as Hanukkah.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre does not provide specific details on security measures or changes to Hanukkah plans at the White House but indicates the issue is being taken seriously.
#### Sexual violence on October 7 and public commentary
Jean-Pierre is asked to respond to Representative Pramila Jayapal’s comment that sexual violence should be condemned but that condemnation must be balanced. She declines to critique Jayapal explicitly but reiterates the administration’s view in unequivocal terms. She says Hamas’s actions on October 7 are reprehensible and that the use of rape as a weapon of war is also reprehensible, “full stop,” reflecting President Biden’s stance. She frames her comments as speaking for the President rather than adjudicating intra-party rhetoric.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that Hamas’s actions on October 7 are absolutely reprehensible, full stop.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the use of rape as a weapon is also reprehensible, full stop, and must be condemned without equivocation.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre indicates she is speaking for President Biden in underscoring these positions, effectively distancing the White House from any suggestion of “balanced” condemnation of such acts.
#### Vice President Harris’s COP28 climate diplomacy
Sullivan highlights Vice President Harris’s trip to Dubai for COP28, where she delivered the U.S. National Statement and joined a leaders’ session on renewable energy. He says she made clear that the Biden–Harris administration is delivering on an ambitious climate agenda, addressing emissions reductions, adaptation, and resilience. Her engagements are portrayed as reinforcing U.S. leadership on climate and connecting diplomatic outreach in the Middle East with environmental priorities.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Vice President Harris traveled to Dubai for COP28 to deliver the U.S. National Statement and participate in a leaders’ session on renewable energy.
- Mr. Sullivan says Harris emphasized that the Biden–Harris administration is delivering on the most ambitious climate agenda in history, at home and abroad.
- Mr. Sullivan argues that, under President Biden’s leadership, the U.S. is again leading the world in responding to the climate crisis on an urgent and sustained basis.
#### Domestic political accountability and Speaker Johnson’s critique
Responding to Speaker Mike Johnson’s claim that the administration lacks a clear Ukraine strategy and accountability plan, Jean-Pierre defends the administration’s record. She points to battlefield results and territorial gains as evidence that the strategy is working and that U.S. support has been effective. She underscores that legislative affairs staff and senior officials are in regular communication with Congress and reiterates that Shalanda Young’s letter outlines the stakes and urgency. She does not address specific oversight mechanisms but reiterates broader strategic objectives and achievements as rebuttal.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre responds to Speaker Johnson’s critique by arguing that Ukraine’s battlefield successes over nearly two years, including retaining Kyiv and reclaiming more than half of occupied territory, show the U.S. strategy is effective.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the administration has consistently engaged with Congress through legislative affairs staff and senior officials, including President Biden, to explain the supplemental and its accountability mechanisms.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre cites Shalanda Young’s letter as clearly laying out why Ukraine funding is vital to U.S. national security and should be approved promptly.
