### Overview
  The briefing covers a mix of domestic policy updates and intense national security issues. Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre highlights administration efforts to reduce gun trafficking under the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act and announces final EPA methane standards tied to COP28 climate engagements. National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan details sustained diplomacy on Israel-Hamas, emphasizing efforts to free hostages, expand humanitarian aid to Gaza (including a $100 million announcement and a U.S. airlift), and press Israel to protect civilians while supporting its right to counter Hamas; he also describes weekend attacks on commercial vessels in the Southern Red Sea, the USS Carney’s defense against UAVs, and U.S. assessments that Iran is enabling Houthi actions while consultations on multinational maritime security responses continue. Sullivan and Jean-Pierre stress urgency on Ukraine supplemental funding after OMB Director Shalanda Young’s letter warning of depleted resources, insist Congress must act to sustain weapons assistance, and reiterate U.S. climate leadership at COP28; on domestic matters the White House urges Congress to resolve border-linked negotiations but declines to negotiate from the podium, refers legal and oversight questions to the White House Counsel, and says it is monitoring journalist safety and rising antisemitism while working to protect communities ahead of upcoming holidays.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Gun violence enforcement
  The briefing opened with an administration update on reducing illegal gun trafficking and broader gun violence. Ms. Jean-Pierre highlights prosecutions under the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act and encourages further Congressional action on background checks, red flag laws, and assault weapons bans. The administration frames its policing and funding priorities as measures to make communities safer while contrasting them with Republican proposals it says would cut police funding.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the Department of Justice has prosecuted over 250 people for illegal gun purchases and trafficking under the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act strengthened penalties, background checks, and provided funds for state red flag laws.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes 21 states have implemented red flag laws and encourages Congress to enact universal background checks, a national red flag law, and an assault weapons ban.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre asserts President Biden has funded police through his budgets and the American Rescue Plan and opposed proposals to cut police funding.
  #### Methane rule/COP28 announcement
  Ms. Jean-Pierre announced the EPA's final standards to sharply reduce methane emissions from oil and gas operations, tying the action to the administration's climate agenda at COP28. She emphasizes the rule's projected climate and economic benefits and frames methane reductions as a critical near-term action to slow climate change. The administration presents this as part of broader U.S. leadership on climate.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the EPA’s final standards will prevent 1.5 billion metric tons of greenhouse gas emissions and deliver billions in health and economic benefits.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says sharp methane cuts are among the most critical short-term actions to slow the rate of climate change.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre connects the rule to President Biden’s broader climate agenda and U.S. engagement at COP28.
  #### Israel-Hamas overview
  National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan summarizes intensive diplomatic engagement since the October 7 attacks, focusing on hostages, humanitarian access to Gaza, and Israel’s military operations. He says he and senior officials are in near-constant contact with Israeli, Qatari, and Egyptian counterparts and that President Biden is regularly briefed. Sullivan frames U.S. efforts as simultaneously pressing for hostage releases, protecting civilians, and supporting Israel’s right to defend itself against Hamas.
- Mr. Sullivan says he held multiple phone calls with partners in Israel, Egypt, Qatar, and other nations about developments since October 7th.
- Mr. Sullivan says he regularly briefs President Biden and that the U.S. is engaged on hostages, humanitarian assistance, and the status of Israel’s military operation.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. will continue to press Hamas and stay in close contact with Israeli, Qatari, and Egyptian counterparts.
  #### Hostage negotiations breakdown
  Sullivan reports that a hostage agreement paused hostilities but ended because Hamas refused to release civilian women who were part of the deal. He describes meetings with families of Americans held hostage and says the U.S. will continue all efforts to secure returns. The administration emphasizes it will press partners and use available diplomatic tools to try to bring Americans and other hostages home.
- Mr. Sullivan says Hamas’s refusal to release civilian women caused the end of the hostage agreement and the pause in hostilities.
- Mr. Sullivan says he met with families of Americans being held hostage and describes their situation as gut-wrenching.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. will continue doing everything under the President’s leadership to bring American hostages and all hostages home.
  #### Humanitarian assistance to Gaza
  Sullivan outlines the U.S. humanitarian response, including a $100 million announcement and a U.S. airlift delivering over 54,000 pounds of medical items and food. He says the U.S. is working to overcome obstacles to sustain and expand aid flows, with Secretary Blinken prioritizing humanitarian access in recent regional travel. Sullivan emphasizes efforts to increase throughput at the Rafah crossing to ensure civilians have food, water, medicine, and shelter.
- Mr. Sullivan says the administration announced $100 million for the Palestinian people through UNRWA, U.N. agencies, and other humanitarian actors.
- Mr. Sullivan reports a successful U.S. airlift of over 54,000 pounds of medical items and food for delivery to civilians in Gaza.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. is working to overcome obstacles and sustain increased humanitarian assistance through crossings such as Rafah.
  #### Red Sea vessel attacks
  Sullivan describes four weekend attacks against three commercial vessels in the Southern Red Sea that involved ships connected to 14 nations, stressing the global implications. He notes the USS Carney responded to distress calls, detected and shot down three UAVs, and that CENTCOM continues to assess whether the destroyer was itself a target. The administration signals ongoing consultations with allies to determine appropriate responses.
- Mr. Sullivan says four attacks hit three commercial vessels in the Southern Red Sea over the weekend, involving ships linked to 14 nations.
- Mr. Sullivan says the Arleigh Burke-class destroyer USS Carney responded and detected and took action against three UAVs.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. is consulting closely with allies and partners to determine appropriate next steps in response to these attacks.
  #### Iran enabling Houthis
  Sullivan repeatedly attributes the attacks in the region to Houthis in Yemen acting with enabling support from Iran, calling Iran ultimately responsible. He frames the Houthi actions as reckless and stresses that attacks on commercial shipping in international waters are unacceptable under international law. The U.S. says it will coordinate international responses and build broad buy-in from partners before acting.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. believes the Houthi attacks were launched by the Houthis and were fully enabled by Iran.
- Mr. Sullivan says attacks on commercial shipping in international waters are totally unacceptable and that the U.S. will consult with partners on responses.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. intends to build a response with as much international buy-in as possible before acting.
  #### Maritime security consultations
  In response to questions about escorts, Sullivan and others note ongoing talks about maritime task forces involving partner navies to protect shipping in the Red Sea, modeled on existing task forces in other regions. He says discussions are active but offers no formal announcement, indicating such coordination would be a natural part of the response. The administration emphasizes multinational burden-sharing as a likely component of maritime security.
- An unidentified speaker says talks are underway with partner nations about creating a maritime task force to ensure safe passage in the Red Sea.
- Mr. Sullivan says the idea of partnering with other countries’ naval vessels to provide greater security through the Red Sea is being actively discussed.
- Mr. Sullivan says he has nothing formal to announce and that consultations remain ongoing.
  #### U.S. responses to regional attacks
  Sullivan describes U.S. measures to prevent the Israel-Hamas war from expanding regionally, including carrier movements and strikes against militants attacking U.S. forces. He notes CENTCOM announced a strike that took out five militants attempting UAV attacks and that the U.S. will take appropriate action in consultation with partners at a time and place of its choosing. The administration emphasizes protecting U.S. personnel while seeking coordinated international responses.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. moved carriers and air wings to prevent the conflict from spilling into a broader regional war.
- Mr. Sullivan says CENTCOM announced a strike that eliminated five militants who were attempting to attack U.S. forces with UAVs.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. will take appropriate action in consultation with allies and at a time and place of its choosing.
  #### Ukraine supplemental urgency
  Sullivan stresses urgency after OMB Director Shalanda Young warned Congress that administration resources to procure weapons for Ukraine will run out by year-end without action. He argues prior U.S. assistance halted Russian advances, enabled Ukrainian territorial gains, and revitalized the defense industrial base, framing the current vote as a stark choice that will shape Putin’s prospects. Sullivan insists Congress must pass the President’s supplemental request to sustain U.S. national security objectives and Ukraine’s defense.
- Mr. Sullivan cites OMB Director Shalanda Young’s letter warning that the U.S. will run out of resources to provide weapons to Ukraine by the end of the year without congressional action.
- Mr. Sullivan says past U.S. assistance helped halt Russian advances and enabled Ukraine to regain over 50 percent of occupied territory.
- Mr. Sullivan says Congress faces a simple choice: support the supplemental to help Ukraine and U.S. security or risk improving Putin’s strategic position.
  #### Climate leadership by VP Harris
  Sullivan reiterates Vice President Harris’s participation in COP28, where she delivered the U.S. national statement and engaged on renewable energy and climate resilience. He frames her engagements as evidence the administration is implementing the most ambitious climate agenda in U.S. history. The administration connects diplomatic work at COP28 to domestic rules like the EPA methane standards previously discussed.
- Mr. Sullivan says Vice President Harris delivered the U.S. National Statement at COP28 and participated in a leaders’ session on renewable energy.
- Mr. Sullivan says the administration is delivering on an ambitious climate agenda and advancing efforts to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and boost resilience.
- Mr. Sullivan links the diplomatic engagement at COP28 to domestic climate measures such as the EPA’s methane rule.
  #### Ships' ties to Israel questioned
  In Q&A, reporters ask whether the attacked commercial vessels had ties to Israel; Sullivan says the U.S. does not believe all ships did. He underscores that any ship attacked in international waters is an unjustifiable target regardless of past ties to Israel and that investigators continue to determine connections. The answer is used to emphasize the recklessness of the Houthi campaign.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. does not think all three targeted ships had ties to Israel but cannot answer definitively yet.
- Mr. Sullivan says attacks on commercial ships in international waters are unacceptable regardless of any past ties to Israel.
- Mr. Sullivan says U.S. assessments continue as officials run the connections to ground.
  #### Israel’s civilian-protection measures
  Sullivan addresses questions about Israeli warnings, no-strike zones, and whether civilians can receive and act on evacuation messages, saying Israel identified areas for ground maneuver and asked people to move out. He stresses the U.S. expectation that Israel protect civilians in zones it designates and facilitate humanitarian assistance, while noting it is too soon to pass a comprehensive judgment on recent operations. The administration says it is pressing Israel to use every tool to ensure civilians receive safe passage and needed aid.
- Mr. Sullivan says Israel identified specific areas for ground maneuver and asked civilians to leave those areas.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. expects Israel to follow through on no-strike zones and to protect civilians in areas it designates.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. is working to ensure humanitarian assistance flows through Rafah to support civilians who move to safer areas.
  #### Hostage leverage and prioritization
  On stalled formal talks in Doha, Sullivan says intensive discussions continue among the U.S., Israel, Qatar, and Egypt about strategies to free hostages and that securing American hostages is the paramount U.S. priority. He declines to disclose sensitive diplomatic options but says the U.S. is giving thought to tools to secure Americans’ release. Sullivan reiterates that Hamas’s failure to deliver on prior agreements complicates returning to pauses and negotiated exchanges.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S., Israel, Qatar, and Egypt are continuing intensive discussions about how to advance hostage releases.
- Mr. Sullivan says securing American hostages is the paramount U.S. priority and that the President is reviewing options.
- Mr. Sullivan says he will refrain from publicly detailing sensitive diplomatic strategies for securing hostage releases.
  #### Aid flow and Rafah crossing status
  When asked whether aid has entered Gaza since the pause ended, Sullivan confirms trucks are flowing through Rafah and that the U.S. is working to increase throughput. He says the administration will provide updated precise numbers when available and continues phone consultations with Israeli counterparts to raise aid levels. The state of aid flows is presented as dynamic and a primary focus of U.S. diplomacy.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that trucks have been flowing into Gaza via the Rafah Crossing since Friday.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. is working to sustain and increase the throughput of humanitarian assistance through Rafah.
- Mr. Sullivan says he has been on the phone with Israeli colleagues about ways to raise aid delivery above current levels.
  #### Ukraine funding contingency
  Reporters press Sullivan on a plan B if supplemental funding for Ukraine fails; he stresses there is no substitute and reiterates Director Young’s warning that U.S. drawdown authority depends on Congress-provided funds to replenish stocks. He says the U.S. cannot continue providing weapons from stocks without impacting readiness unless Congress supplies requested funding, and that bipartisan support should be achievable if votes are taken. Sullivan emphasizes the negative implications for international coalition-building if the U.S. does not remain a reliable provider.
- Mr. Sullivan says there is no plan B and that without Congress providing funds the U.S. cannot continue to supply weapons without impacting readiness.
- Mr. Sullivan says drawdowns are replaced using Congress-provided funds and that the arithmetic requires congressional action.
- Mr. Sullivan says failing to fund Ukraine would undermine the U.S. case to rally other coalition partners.
  #### Post-conflict resettlement talks
  When asked about refugee resettlement for Palestinians after conflict, Sullivan says the U.S. is not in active talks with countries about resettling Palestinians out of Gaza. He acknowledges long-term discussions about the future of Gaza have occurred privately but refrains from public detail. The administration frames post-conflict planning as a sensitive, evolving diplomatic matter.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. is not in active talks with countries about resettling Palestinians out of Gaza at this time.
- Mr. Sullivan says discussions about the future of Gaza have taken place privately but he will not characterize private diplomatic exchanges publicly.
- Mr. Sullivan says long-term planning and regional cooperation are topics the administration continues to discuss.
  #### Congressional outreach and border talks
  Back at the podium, Ms. Jean-Pierre addresses the OMB letter and the collapse of congressional border talks tied to supplemental funding, declining to discuss negotiation specifics while urging Congress to act on Ukraine funding. She describes the administration’s offer of technical assistance to Congress and reiterates that the President is engaged on the issue. The White House stresses urgency but avoids public negotiation positions on immigration-related provisions.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the White House offers technical assistance to Congress but will not get into hypotheticals or negotiation specifics from the podium.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reiterates the administration’s view of the OMB Director’s letter and urges Congress to act on supplemental funding for Ukraine.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the President has been engaged with leaders on the Hill and the White House’s Office of Legislative Affairs remains in regular contact.
  #### Journalist safety and antisemitism concerns
  Reporters raise concerns about journalist casualties and rising antisemitism; Ms. Jean-Pierre emphasizes the administration’s interest in protecting journalists and vulnerable communities. She acknowledges an uptick in antisemitic incidents and says the administration is monitoring and taking steps to help communities feel protected, particularly ahead of Hanukkah. The White House frames protection of journalists and community safety as priorities but provides no new operational details.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the administration is monitoring reports of journalists killed and emphasizes the importance of protecting journalists on the ground.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the White House is concerned about an uptick in antisemitism and will do everything it can to help communities feel protected.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says she has no specific readouts on holiday security planning to announce but stresses the administration’s focus on community safety.
  #### Legal and oversight referrals
  Several domestic oversight topics arise late in the briefing—questions about Section 702 surveillance funding, social media moderation subpoenas, and Hunter Biden-related bank records. Ms. Jean-Pierre refers reporters to the White House Counsel’s Office for legal or classified matters and declines to comment on ongoing investigations or subpoena strategy. The administration repeatedly defers to counsel or other agencies for details it deems outside the press office’s purview.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre refers questions about Section 702 and alleged call-record programs to other teams and says she does not have details to share.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the White House Counsel’s Office has addressed inquiries about social media moderation and refers reporters to that office.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says she has not seen the House Oversight-reported bank record material referenced in questions and refers those inquiries to the White House Counsel.
