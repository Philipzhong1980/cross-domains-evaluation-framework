### Overview
The Goal Setting Subcommittee—comprising Chair (Mayor) Lucas Ramirez, Vice Mayor Alison Hicks, and former Mayor Ellen Kamei—with support from City Manager Kimbre McCarthy, Assistant City Manager Audrey Ramberg, and Principal Management Analyst Laurel James, meets to shape the process for developing Mountain View’s FY 2023–24 and 2024–25 Council work plan. After approving prior minutes and hearing public comments from Bruce England on Zoom accessibility, hybrid meetings, and specific work plan items, the subcommittee reviews staff’s proposed timeline that runs from a January 24 Council adoption of the process through February and April work plan study sessions to a June 2023 adoption aligned with the budget and semiannual implementation check‑ins. Members endorse a feedback-based role for advisory bodies, with standardized questions and consistent facilitation by the City Manager’s Office, and they stress the need for an equitable and realistic process that recognizes the large volume of ongoing and non‑discretionary work alongside new Council initiatives. They agree to add explicit analysis criteria for urgency/timeliness, alignment with the seven strategic priorities, and a more robust equity lens linked to the REI action plan; adopt staff’s ABC priority buckets with staff-prepared initial classifications; improve documentation to show status, projected completion quarter/year, and item origin; and build in mechanisms to remove stale projects, such as those superseded by state law. The subcommittee ultimately directs staff to bring the refined process forward as a January consent item, understanding it may be pulled for discussion, and confirms next steps for a midyear work plan and budget update in February before adjourning at 5:35 PM.

#### Overview of 2023–2025 work plan development process
Principal Management Analyst Laurel James presents the proposed process and timeline for developing the City Council’s fiscal years 2023–24 and 2024–25 work plan. The process spans from a January Council item adopting the process through study sessions in February and April, culminating in work plan adoption in June 2023 alongside the FY 2023–24 budget. Staff also proposes regular six‑month check‑ins on work plan implementation, emphasizing the need to balance Council initiatives with core services, non‑discretionary obligations, and staff capacity. Subcommittee members seek clarity on the nature of attachments, how ongoing housing element work fits in, and how prior processes involving CivicMakers and advisory bodies inform this cycle.
- Principal Management Analyst James explains that the purpose of the item is to present the proposed work plan development process and seek feedback on analysis criteria, advisory body input, and project prioritization.
- Laurel James notes that the work plan will be framed by the seven existing strategic priorities adopted in 2021: community for all, intentional development and housing options, mobility and connectivity, sustainability and climate resiliency, livability and quality of life, economic vitality, and organizational strength and good governance.
- Laurel James outlines a timeline of study sessions and Council business items starting in January and culminating in adoption of the FY 2023–25 work plan in June 2023 together with the FY 2023–24 budget.
- Laurel James states that staff will provide regular six‑month check‑ins to Council on work plan implementation throughout the two‑year cycle.
- Committee Member Hicks asks whether the 2021–23 project list in the attachment represents the full set of projects for future consideration, noting that some known items such as sustainable leading or the Moffett Precise Plan do not appear.
- Laurel James clarifies that the tabled attachment is the latest status update on the existing 2021–23 work plan, included for context, and not a definitive list of all projects to be considered for the new work plan.
- Committee Member Hicks asks how housing element implementation projects are handled and whether they are treated as non‑discretionary.
- City staff confirm that housing element implementation projects scheduled within the upcoming work plan window are listed as non‑discretionary items, with later‑cycle items appearing in future work plans.
- City Manager McCarthy explains that all housing element actions must be completed within the eight‑year cycle, but Council can prioritize which housing element items are addressed in the upcoming two‑year work plan depending on staff capacity.
- Assistant City Manager Ramberg recalls that in 2021 the City engaged CivicMakers and used a similar feedback-oriented process with advisory bodies, and that prior cycles also grappled with large work plans and capacity constraints.
#### Proposed analysis criteria for potential projects
Staff proposes analysis criteria to evaluate potential work plan projects: impact on advancing Council’s strategic priorities, equity considerations, impact on future capacity and dependencies, staff capacity, and financial cost and revenues. The subcommittee supports these but requests refinements to better capture urgency, explicit alignment with strategic priorities, and integration with the Race, Equity, and Inclusion (REI) Action Plan. Members want criteria that more realistically constrain the work plan to what staff can accomplish and that surface tradeoffs between new initiatives and existing obligations.
- Laurel James states that the primary analysis criterion will be each project’s impact on achieving or advancing the Council’s adopted strategic priorities.
- Laurel James proposes that staff identify whether projects benefit or burden historically disadvantaged or underserved communities as part of an equity-focused criterion.
- Laurel James notes that staff will analyze whether projects create efficiencies, free up staff time, or are foundational to future projects, citing a precise plan as an example of a dependency project.
- Laurel James emphasizes that staff capacity—considering positions, vacancies, leaves, emergencies, workload, morale, and engagement—will be a critical component of project analysis and phasing.
- Laurel James adds that staff will estimate project costs and any anticipated revenues or resource needs when analyzing potential work plan items.
- Committee Member Kamei recommends tying the equity analysis to the City’s existing equity lens for budgeting and to the Race, Equity, and Inclusion action plan, so that equity is operationalized rather than treated generically.
- Committee Member Kamei suggests explicitly indicating which strategic priority or priorities each proposed project advances, so Council and the public can see alignment or misalignment with the adopted priorities.
- Committee Member Hicks observes that the proposed criteria do not clearly capture urgency or timeliness and cites the Moffett Precise Plan as an example where delayed action could result in loss of neighborhood-serving retail and reduced walkability.
- Laurel James acknowledges that urgency is not explicitly called out as a separate criterion, though some aspects might fall under project dependencies, and agrees it is useful to treat timeliness as a distinct analytical factor.
- Chair Ramirez supports adding an urgency/timeliness criterion and states that projects not aligned with the strategic priorities should generally be lower priority unless Council revises the priorities themselves.
- Chair Ramirez agrees that the equity criterion should be strengthened with more objective metrics informed by the REI action plan, rather than remaining vague and subjective.
#### Advisory body participation and equity in input
Staff proposes that the City Manager’s Office and advisory body staff liaisons attend regular advisory body meetings to facilitate structured discussions on the work plan. Advisory bodies would give high-level feedback on potential projects, identify critical issues within their purview, and suggest what they consider top priorities. The subcommittee endorses a standardized and equitable approach across all advisory bodies, with shared questions and clear expectations about capacity, and wants staff to be the consistent messenger. There is consensus that advisory bodies should provide feedback on a defined set of potential projects rather than generate extensive new lists from scratch, and staff notes that timing makes it difficult to seek advisory input before Council’s initial project-identification session.
- Laurel James outlines a proposed process where City Manager’s Office staff and Council advisory body staff liaisons attend advisory body meetings to facilitate structured discussions about the Council work plan and potential projects.
- Laurel James explains that advisory bodies would be asked for high-level input on identified potential projects, to highlight critical issues for the work plan, and to share their views on the highest priorities.
- City Manager McCarthy notes that prior cycles used a feedback process where advisory bodies reacted to staff- and Council-identified items, partly to avoid receiving a list of 50 new ideas that staff and Council could not realistically accommodate.
- Assistant City Manager Ramberg confirms that in both the 2021 CivicMakers process and earlier cycles, advisory bodies primarily provided feedback on existing items rather than building the work plan from scratch.
- Member of the public Bruce England states that advisory body input is essential and urges the City to ensure that commissions can meaningfully participate in shaping the work plan.
- Chair Ramirez expresses concern that advisory bodies have been treated inconsistently in the past, with some told to comment only within their scope and others allowed to comment on anything, and calls for a consistent and equitable approach.
- Chair Ramirez suggests that ideally advisory body feedback would come before Council’s own project brainstorming so that Council could see advisory input early, but staff later explains the timeline makes this infeasible for this cycle.
- Committee Member Kamei recommends that staff develop one or two common, broad questions to pose to every advisory body, enabling more comparable feedback and helping commissioners feel directly engaged in the process.
- Committee Member Hicks supports providing advisory bodies with the same realistic explanation of capacity constraints that Council receives so they do not expend effort on expansive wish lists that cannot be implemented.
- Committee Member Hicks agrees that instructions should be consistent across advisory bodies and leans toward having them focus on items within their scope, while Chair Ramirez voices a preference for allowing all bodies to comment on all work plan topics to avoid excluding bodies like the Performing Arts Committee.
#### Staff capacity, holistic workload, and iceberg metaphor
Chair Ramirez raises a broader concern that Council goal setting often treats the work plan as a blank slate of Council-initiated projects, ignoring the large volume of ongoing and mandated work below the “surface.” He cites the Community Workforce Agreement as an example of a 2019 priority that persists over multiple cycles because staff must also deliver housing element actions, general plan and ESAP items, and other strategies. Staff acknowledges this issue and describes efforts to better quantify capacity, though they note limits to how precisely they can model citywide workload. The subcommittee encourages staff to bring forward clearer, more holistic context so Council enters the goal setting sessions with realistic expectations about what can be done in two years.
- Chair Ramirez argues that Council-initiated projects should not be considered apart from the substantial workload generated by non-discretionary mandates, the general plan, the environmental sustainability action plan, the downtown parking strategy, and economic vitality programs.
- Chair Ramirez uses the iceberg metaphor to describe how Council only sees the small portion of work above the surface while most staff workload remains opaque, leading to unrealistic lists of Council projects that cannot be completed within a two-year cycle.
- Chair Ramirez cites the Community Workforce Agreement as a long-standing Council priority first elevated in June 2019 that still has not returned to Council, illustrating the consequences of overloading the work plan and deferring items across multiple cycles.
- Assistant City Manager Ramberg explains that staff has moved toward providing more context on ongoing obligations and estimates of what percentage of staff time is available for “special projects,” but notes it remains difficult to fully quantify and prioritize all responsibilities.
- Assistant City Manager Ramberg states that staff will use the analysis criteria and capacity assessment to recommend realistic ABC groupings of projects that the City can commit to, rather than presenting Council with an unconstrained list.
- Committee Member Hicks asks whether the solution is to provide a longer list capturing both Council projects and planned non-Council initiatives so that Council sees the full workload, and Chair Ramirez responds that he is open to whatever format best conveys the totality of work.
- Committee Member Kamei suggests presenting current work plan items and new potential projects in consistent, table-based formats, possibly side-by-side, to help Council see the combined load and how items enter the work plan (e.g., via item 8, business items, or prior work plans).
- City Manager McCarthy indicates staff can refine the matrices and visuals to show core work, non-discretionary projects, ongoing work plan items, and potential new projects together, while clearly signaling where capacity exists for additional initiatives.

#### Project prioritization categories (A, B, C)
Staff proposes three priority categories—A, B, and C—to structure the Council work plan, with Category A as top priorities for staff allocation, Category B as next-level high priorities, and Category C as important projects pursued as time and resources allow. The subcommittee supports this bucket approach and appreciates that staff will pre‑populate recommended categories based on analysis, while Council retains the ability to move items. Members request additional table fields to make status and projected completion clearer, discuss the treatment of in‑progress projects, and emphasize the need to constrain the total number of projects to what staff can deliver. There is an understanding that many ongoing items will likely fall in Category A due to sunk effort, but the subcommittee encourages staff to avoid automatically crowding out all new initiatives.
- Laurel James proposes three project priority categories: A for highest priorities that are considered first in staff allocation and phasing; B for high priorities considered after A; and C for high-priority Council projects that proceed as time and resources allow.
- Laurel James explains that the categorization is designed to impose a hierarchy on work plan projects in light of limited capacity and the significant volume of core services and non-discretionary work.
- Assistant City Manager Ramberg says staff will apply the agreed analysis criteria and capacity assessments to recommend initial A/B/C classifications for Council to review and adjust.
- City Manager McCarthy explains that the ABC buckets are intended to avoid an unstructured list of 50 projects and to help both Council and staff focus on a realistic set of top priorities, noting that past work plans were too large to complete.
- City Manager McCarthy notes that Category C could include complex, multi‑year efforts such as the broadband initiative, where progress is desired but multiple constraints mean completion will take longer than a single cycle.
- Committee Member Kamei suggests that the work plan tables should explicitly show status (with a projected quarter/year of completion) and an ABC priority label, rather than relying on vague “in progress” notes buried in update text.
- Committee Member Kamei proposes that most in‑progress projects should be treated as Category A, since staff is already investing resources in them, with only a small number potentially rolling into the next cycle, and recommends that staff clarify expected completion timing in the January or February updates.
- City Manager McCarthy agrees to improve the tables by replacing generic status labels with estimated completion time frames and by clearly indicating which items will be finished in this cycle versus moved forward.
- Committee Member Hicks cautions that automatically placing all ongoing projects in Category A could push all new projects into lower categories, and she urges staff to consider urgency and timeliness when classifying items.
- Chair Ramirez supports the bucket model but stresses the need for a clearly communicated prioritization method so Council knows in advance how items move between categories and how votes or consensus will translate into final priorities.
- City Manager McCarthy reminds the subcommittee that the total work plan should likely be around 40 items, given existing ongoing work and capacity, and suggests limiting the number of new items per Council member to avoid an unmanageable list.
#### Method for generating and scoping new project ideas
The subcommittee and staff discuss how Council should introduce new project ideas into the work plan process. Past methods have included dot‑voting on a whiteboard, Council memos submitted in advance, and CivicMakers‑facilitated thematic synthesis. Members express different preferences but agree that the method should discourage large numbers of loosely defined ideas. Staff proposes that Council first review the existing work plan and workload in February, then bring forward candidate projects with basic scoping information, after which staff would analyze and assign preliminary ABC categories for Council review in April. The group does not settle on a single technique (dots vs memos vs brainstorming) but asks staff to return with a clearer, standardized approach and possibly a template for describing project proposals.
- Chair Ramirez states that brainstorming sessions often produce very high‑level ideas without clear scope, making it difficult for staff to assess workload and for Council to understand feasibility, and cites a past idea for a comprehensive transportation master plan as an example that took years to define.
- Assistant City Manager Ramberg describes a two‑step process in which Council first reviews status of the current work plan at a February meeting and then identifies projects it is interested in considering for the new work plan, without yet prioritizing them.
- Assistant City Manager Ramberg says staff will analyze these identified projects between February and April using the agreed criteria, including capacity and dependencies, and then come back with recommendations and preliminary ABC buckets at an April meeting for Council to adjust.
- City Manager McCarthy recounts that previous cycles used different approaches—dot exercises, pre‑written memos, and third‑party facilitation via CivicMakers—and notes that feedback has been mixed, with no single method pleasing everyone.
- City Manager McCarthy asks the subcommittee for guidance on whether to return to dot‑voting, rely on pre‑submitted memos, limit the number of new ideas per Council member, or adopt another structured method, emphasizing the need to avoid an unwieldy work plan with 80 or 90 items.
- Committee Member Hicks expresses hope that, because the current Council is experienced and the work plan was disrupted by COVID‑19, members will bring fewer new ideas and will focus on pruning stale items and reaffirming or modestly updating priorities.
- Committee Member Hicks notes that she is not strongly wedded to any one process (dots vs memos vs grids) and is comfortable with whichever structured approach staff proposes for the current context.
- Chair Ramirez suggests staff design a standardized format or template for project proposals so each idea arrives with consistent, basic scoping information that supports staff analysis and Council’s understanding.
- The subcommittee does not choose a specific technique for generating new ideas but agrees staff should outline a clear, predictable method in the January staff report, including expectations about scope and the volume of new proposals.
#### Standardizing and clarifying the work plan documentation
Throughout the discussion, the subcommittee identifies several improvements to the way work plan information is presented to Council and the public. They call for consistent table formats across attachments showing status, estimated completion quarter/year, priority category, and genesis of each item. They also ask that staff clarify which items are non‑discretionary, which arise from prior Council direction, and which come from other policy plans. These changes aim to reduce confusion, as noted by both Bruce England and members, and to provide a more transparent picture of the work plan “iceberg.”
- Chair Ramirez notes that Attachment 2, listing items mentioned by Council members since the last work plan, may confuse the public into thinking it is a formal proposed list, and he requests clearer contextualization of such lists.
- Member of the public Bruce England points out several inconsistencies and ambiguities in the current work plan tables, such as outdated status updates and unclear descriptions of items like intranet redesign.
- Committee Member Kamei proposes using consistent, table-based work plan formats across attachments and including columns for department lead, status, projected completion, and the origin of each item (e.g., item 8, Council business, prior work plan).
- Committee Member Kamei suggests adding explicit status categories that tie to estimated completion (e.g., Q3 2022) rather than generic labels like “in progress,” which require reading lengthy update text to interpret.
- Committee Member Kamei also calls for indicating the ABC priority category directly in the tables once assigned, to make relative priority transparent.
- City Manager McCarthy agrees to refine the presentations so they more clearly show what will be completed in the current cycle, what will roll to the next, and how items are prioritized.
- Chair Ramirez underscores the importance of distinguishing non‑discretionary items and externally driven mandates from discretionary Council projects so that everyone can better understand capacity constraints.
- The subcommittee requests that staff explicitly identify projects that should be considered for removal because they have become stale, superseded by state legislation, or otherwise redundant, such as the gas leaf blower item rendered unnecessary by new state law, as cited by Committee Member Kamei.
#### Process for removing or sunsetting stale projects
Subcommittee members stress the importance of being able to remove or sunset projects from the work plan, both to keep it manageable and to reflect changing conditions. They note examples where state legislation or external actions have effectively completed or superseded local initiatives, as well as projects that have lingered since 2019. Staff acknowledges that prior cycles included opportunities to drop items and agrees this should continue. A more explicit removal step will help prevent the accumulation of stale items and free space for new, higher‑priority work.
- Committee Member Kamei recalls that in past cycles Council could remove work plan projects that had become unnecessary, and she cites the gas leaf blower regulation item as an example where state law ultimately addressed the issue.
- Committee Member Kamei recommends that staff highlight opportunities where items can be removed due to state or county action, joint powers agency work, or other external developments that make local initiatives redundant.
- Committee Member Hicks supports reviewing the list for projects that have gone stale over several years and either eliminating them or downsizing them to reflect current priorities.
- Chair Ramirez notes that some projects prioritized as early as June 2019, such as the Community Workforce Agreement, are still outstanding and suggests that an explicit removal or re‑scoping process is necessary to avoid endless carryover without meaningful progress.
- The subcommittee asks staff to build an explicit mechanism into the work plan process for identifying items that should be sunset, including clear documentation in the work plan tables when a project is removed and why.
- Staff agree to incorporate this removal/sunsetting step into the process description and to support Council discussions about which items to drop during the February and April meetings.
#### Subcommittee’s consolidated direction and next steps
By the end of the meeting, the subcommittee reaches consensus on several key process refinements and confirms next steps for staff. They support using a January consent item to adopt the work plan development process, adding timeliness and strategic-priority alignment to the analysis criteria, standardizing advisory body input, and implementing ABC priority buckets with clearer status reporting. Staff will revise the documentation and visuals accordingly and bring the updated process to Council on January 24, followed by a February midyear update and project discussion. The subcommittee then adjourns, with Chair Ramirez noting this is his first and last time chairing this particular committee.
- Chair Ramirez summarizes majority direction that the January Council item adopting the work plan development process should be placed on the consent calendar, with the caveat it can be pulled for discussion if desired.
- Chair Ramirez notes consensus to enhance analysis criteria by adding an urgency/timeliness factor, explicitly indicating alignment with the seven strategic priorities, and strengthening the equity criterion using the Race, Equity, and Inclusion action plan.
- Chair Ramirez records agreement that advisory bodies should receive a consistent process with shared questions posed by a City Manager’s Office representative, and that their feedback will be structured and capacity-aware.
- Chair Ramirez confirms subcommittee support for the ABC prioritization buckets, with staff preparing initial classifications based on analysis and Council retaining the ability to adjust categories and remove projects.
- Chair Ramirez notes that the subcommittee supports clearer status reporting in work plan tables, including projected completion quarter/year, ABC category, and better documentation of which projects will be finished this cycle versus rolled forward.
- Assistant City Manager Ramberg reiterates that staff will incorporate the subcommittee’s input into the process description and bring it to Council on January 24 for adoption, followed by a February 28 work plan update and discussion tied to the midyear budget.
