### Overview
  The briefing centered on the administration’s announcement to provide armor to Ukraine and related policy, logistical, and diplomatic implications, led by National Security Council spokesperson Admiral John Kirby and followed by White House Press Secretary Karine Jean‑Pierre on domestic matters. Kirby explains that U.S. budgetary assistance to Ukraine is routed through the World Bank and reports no evidence of misuse while underscoring stepped-up accountability for security aid; he frames the decision to supply 31 M1 Abrams as driven by evolving battlefield needs, sustainment and training considerations, and close coordination with allies—including Germany’s Leopard commitments—and cautions that Abrams deliveries and associated training will take many months because of complex maintenance and fuel requirements. Reporters pressed escalation risks and nuclear concerns, and Kirby said the administration sees no evidence Moscow intends to strike NATO or employ weapons of mass destruction while emphasizing strengthened NATO posture and troop presence in Europe. Jean‑Pierre opened with a domestic update that ACA enrollment hit a record 16.3 million and previewed the President’s economic messaging and upcoming events, repeatedly stressing the administration’s readiness to work with Congress while deferring to legal or congressional processes on ongoing investigations, personnel moves, and specific oversight decisions; she also addressed questions on the debt‑limit posture, marijuana banking as a legislative issue, and preparations for the State of the Union. Overall, the session conveys a coordinated U.S. and allied effort to support Ukraine’s longer‑term battlefield needs while balancing logistical realities and continued attention to domestic political and policy priorities.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Ukraine budgetary assistance
  Reporters asked about U.S. direct budgetary assistance to Ukraine after President Zelenskyy fired senior leaders over corruption concerns. Admiral John Kirby explains that U.S. budgetary aid to Ukraine is routed through the World Bank and that the administration is confident in that mechanism. The administration stresses it takes corruption concerns seriously while allowing Ukrainian leaders to address them.
- MR. KIRBY says U.S. budgetary assistance to Ukraine is directed through the World Bank and the administration is confident in that process.
- MR. KIRBY says the U.S. shares President Zelenskyy’s concerns about corruption and will let Ukrainian authorities work through those allegations.
- MR. KIRBY states that the U.S. has not seen signs that U.S. budgetary assistance has been misused in Ukraine.
  #### Security assistance accountability
  Reporters pressed whether U.S. security assistance and equipment have been misused or diverted. Admiral Kirby says the administration has increased accountability measures and has not seen indications of U.S.-provided weapons being diverted to the wrong hands. The National Security Council emphasizes continued close work with Ukrainian partners on tracking and sustainment.
- MR. KIRBY says the U.S. is working in lockstep with Ukrainians on accountability measures for security assistance.
- MR. KIRBY reports no indications that U.S.-provided weapons or systems have ended up in the wrong hands.
- MR. KIRBY says the defense attaché’s office in Kyiv has increased efforts to improve visibility over supplied equipment.
  #### Abrams tanks decision rationale
  The White House announced a decision to provide M1 Abrams tanks to Ukraine and reporters asked why the change after prior caution about complexity. Admiral Kirby says the decision reflects evolving battlefield conditions and Ukraine’s need for combined-arms capabilities on open terrain, while acknowledging the Abrams’ sophisticated training and maintenance requirements. He underscores that tanks were never ruled out but required assessment of conditions, sustainment, and coalition coordination.
- MR. KIRBY says the Abrams is the most capable tank in the world and requires extensive training, maintenance, and a supply chain.
- MR. KIRBY explains the decision to provide Abrams follows evolving battlefield conditions and anticipated fighting in 2023.
- MR. KIRBY says the administration never formally ruled out sending tanks but needed to match capabilities to operational requirements.
  #### Allied coordination on armor
  Reporters asked whether the U.S. decision was intended to give cover to European allies and how it fits with Germany’s Leopard tanks. Admiral Kirby rejects the notion of providing 'cover' and frames the move as coordinated action demonstrating allied unity. He details that Germany announced a company of Leopards immediately and that the U.S. announcement is part of a broader, synchronized allied effort.
- MR. KIRBY says the decision demonstrates unity with allies and partners rather than providing 'cover' for them.
- MR. KIRBY notes Chancellor Scholz announced immediate provision of a Leopard company and will work with partners to get more Leopard companies to Ukraine.
- MR. KIRBY says the U.S., Germany, Britain, and France are coordinating armored contributions to meet Ukraine’s needs.
  #### Tank quantities and organization
  Reporters sought specifics about how many tanks Ukraine needs and why the U.S. is sending 31 Abrams. Admiral Kirby explains the U.S. contribution matches Ukrainian battalion organization and that a U.S.-provided battalion for Ukraine equates to roughly 30–31 tanks. He notes allied contributions discussed during the day could add roughly 60 Leopards from Germany and other tanks from Britain and France.
- MR. KIRBY says the 31 Abrams correspond to the size of a Ukrainian tank battalion configured at roughly 30–31 tanks.
- MR. KIRBY says Chancellor Scholz’s announcement will provide about one company of Leopards immediately and Germany plans to assemble additional companies.
- MR. KIRBY says Ukrainians want as many tanks as possible quickly, but he will not speak for President Zelenskyy on exact requirements.
  #### Delivery timeline and training
  Reporters pressed when tanks will arrive and how training will be handled given statements that delivery will take many months. Admiral Kirby and others explain there is no date certain and that Abrams in particular will take many months to arrive, necessitating parallel training, sustainment planning, and supply-chain setup. Training will occur outside Ukraine and U.S. officials intend to accelerate and prioritize programs similar to prior efforts to meet operational needs.
- MR. KIRBY says there is no calendar date certain and that Abrams will likely take many months before they arrive in Ukraine.
- MR. KIRBY says training for Ukrainian tank crews will occur outside Ukraine and the Pentagon will soon begin the training effort.
- MR. KIRBY says the administration will accelerate training and sustainment planning to prepare Ukrainians before tanks arrive.
  #### Sustainment and fuel concerns
  Reporters asked about sustainment issues, especially fuel for the Abrams’ gas turbine engine and the broader logistics pipeline. Admiral Kirby acknowledges the Abrams requires jet fuel and a unique supply chain and says establishing that logistical pipeline is part of the months-long readiness work. The administration emphasizes that sustainment planning is integral to the decision to send heavy armor.
- MR. KIRBY explains the Abrams uses a gas turbine engine that requires jet fuel and a dedicated logistical pipeline.
- MR. KIRBY says fuel and spare parts are central to sustainment planning and are being addressed as part of the procurement and training process.
- MR. KIRBY says sustainment concerns were a key reason for careful planning before announcing Abrams deliveries.
  #### Escalation and Russian threats
  Reporters asked whether Russian rhetoric about escalation, including threats to target Western capitals or use nuclear weapons, influences U.S. decisions. Admiral Kirby says the administration does not dismiss Russian threats but sees no indication Putin intends to strike NATO or use weapons of mass destruction, while acknowledging Russia remains capable and lethal on the battlefield. The U.S. monitors for escalation risks and emphasizes deterrence and readiness.
- MR. KIRBY says the administration does not take Russian threats for granted but has no indication Vladimir Putin intends to strike NATO territory.
- MR. KIRBY says the U.S. sees no indication that Russia plans to employ weapons of mass destruction, including nuclear weapons.
- MR. KIRBY warns that Russia remains lethal and that the U.S. and allies must prepare for continued fighting as weather improves.
  #### NATO posture and deterrence
  Reporters asked if NATO has sufficient forces to defend Allies and whether Russia is too weakened to respond. Admiral Kirby emphasizes the United States takes Article 5 commitments seriously and has maintained a net of about 100,000 U.S. troops on the European continent, including 20,000 additional troops ordered earlier. He says Russia’s military is weaker than a year ago but retains significant lethality, so NATO deterrence and readiness remain essential.
- MR. KIRBY says President Biden ordered an additional 20,000 U.S. troops to Europe and the net U.S. presence of about 100,000 on the continent remains in place.
- MR. KIRBY says the United States is confident it has the capability and resources to meet its Article 5 commitments to NATO Allies.
- MR. KIRBY says Russia’s military is weaker than 11 months ago but remains capable and lethal on the battlefield.
  #### China communications and COVID
  Reporters asked whether the U.S. has communicated to China about the implications of supporting Russia and about China’s COVID reporting. Admiral Kirby says private and public messaging urge nations not to support Russia and that China must decide its posture, noting China continues to buy Russian oil and its compliance with caps is uncertain. On COVID, Kirby says the U.S. encourages Chinese transparency and cannot verify China’s reported death figures.
- MR. KIRBY says the U.S. has been clear in private and public communications that now is not the time for business as usual with Russia.
- MR. KIRBY notes China is making choices about its relationship with Russia and continues to buy Russian oil, with unclear adherence to price caps.
- MR. KIRBY urges China to be fully transparent about COVID data and says the U.S. cannot speak to the veracity of reported Chinese numbers.
  #### Classified documents and handling
  Reporters raised the broader issue of classified documents found in former officials’ possession and whether the system is broken. Admiral Kirby emphasizes that the President and NSC staff take classified material seriously, follow established rules, and that procedures evolve with technology, while the White House counsel and legal processes handle specific matters. Karine Jean-Pierre reiterated the administration’s cooperation with DOJ and Special Counsel reviews and declined to preview personnel or procedural changes.
- MR. KIRBY says the President and National Security Council treat classified material seriously and follow established procedures.
- MR. KIRBY says the handling guidelines evolve with technology and the administration continually reviews those procedures.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the White House is cooperating with the Department of Justice and the Special Counsel on classified document matters.
  #### Training program specifics
  Reporters pressed for details about training length, locations, and pacing for tank crews and other systems. Admiral Kirby says training will not be conducted inside Ukraine, that locations are still being determined, and that the Pentagon plans an ambitious program modeled after prior accelerated training efforts like Patriot battery training at Fort Sill. He suggests training program details will be developed in weeks rather than months.
- MR. KIRBY says Abrams training will occur outside Ukraine and the Pentagon has not yet finalized training locations.
- MR. KIRBY says the Pentagon will run an ambitious and accelerated training program similar to past efforts such as Patriot training at Fort Sill.
- MR. KIRBY estimates military planners will nail down training details in weeks, not months.
  #### Decision process and diplomacy
  Reporters asked what internal and allied discussions led to the tank announcement and whether Germany conditioned Leopard deliveries on U.S. action. Admiral Kirby says tanks were never off the table, that the decision results from weeks of robust, multilevel conversations with allies and the Ukrainians, and that Ramstein and senior defense-to-defense talks factored into the coordinated outcome. He declines to detail internal deliberations but traces the announcement to intensive allied coordination.
- MR. KIRBY says the decision to provide Abrams and the allied announcements resulted from many weeks of discussions with Germany, other allies, and Ukraine.
- MR. KIRBY says tanks were never off the table and the administration was transparent about the Abrams’ unique sustainment challenges.
- MR. KIRBY cites Ramstein and senior military-to-military engagements as venues where the issue of tanks was discussed.
  #### ACA enrollment and economic messaging
  Karine Jean-Pierre opened the White House press segment by announcing record ACA marketplace enrollment and previewing the President’s upcoming remarks on the economy. She reported 16.3 million Americans signed up during the 2023 Open Enrollment Period and that most consumers could find plans for $10 or less per month because of policies enacted in the American Rescue Plan and Inflation Reduction Act. She also previewed the President’s Springfield, Virginia remarks contrasting his economic plan with Republican proposals.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE announces that a record 16.3 million Americans signed up for ACA coverage during the 2023 Open Enrollment Period.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says four out of five consumers could find ACA plans for $10 or less per month due to policy changes in the American Rescue Plan and Inflation Reduction Act.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE previews the President’s upcoming remarks in Springfield, Virginia highlighting job creation and contrasting his economic agenda with Republican proposals.
  #### Debt limit and Senator Manchin
  Reporters asked whether Senator Joe Manchin conveyed a willingness to negotiate spending cuts in exchange for debt limit relief and about Manchin delaying EV tax credits. Karine Jean-Pierre said she had no conversations to share from the White House with Manchin and reiterated the administration’s position that avoiding default should be done without conditions. She said the President is open to policy ideas to reduce debt but sees default as a distinct issue that Congress should resolve by paying existing obligations.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says she has no White House conversations to share with Senator Manchin and refers debt-limit negotiation details to Congress.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE reiterates the White House view that default should be avoided without conditions and that Congress should pay the debt it incurred.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the President is willing to hear ideas to lower the debt but treats the default question as separate and urgent.
  #### Congressional relations and oversight
  Reporters asked about Speaker McCarthy visiting the White House, House oversight plans, and Republican committee moves such as limiting members from intelligence access. Jean-Pierre stressed the White House’s willingness to work in good faith while criticizing some Republican priorities, and she deferred many specific procedural or personnel committee matters to Congress. She also said the White House prefers that intelligence committees not be politicized and noted ongoing contact with congressional members.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the President looks forward to meeting with Speaker McCarthy but has no date to share yet and that such meetings are traditional.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the White House is willing to work in good faith with congressional committees but expects oversight to be conducted appropriately.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says decisions about committee access and membership are matters for Congress and the relevant party conferences to decide.
  #### Personnel and staffing questions
  Reporters sought updates on potential staffing changes, including Jeff Zients as Chief of Staff and Ron Klain’s departure. Jean-Pierre declined to preview personnel announcements and said no personnel decisions were being announced from the podium. She praised Ron Klain’s leadership and described Jeff Zients as a known colleague with operations experience while committing to announce personnel developments when appropriate.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says there are no personnel announcements to make about the Chief of Staff transition at this time.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE praises Ron Klain’s leadership and says the White House will share personnel decisions when they are finalized.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says she knows Jeff Zients and describes him as an experienced implementer but offers no confirmation of an appointment.
  #### Domestic issues and inquiries
  Reporters asked about a federal civil-rights investigation in Memphis, potential economic spillovers from tech and banking layoffs, marijuana banking complications, and Hunter Biden’s art sales. Jean-Pierre declined to comment on ongoing investigations, pointed to Congressional responsibility for marijuana-banking legislation, and referenced economic data showing continued growth while acknowledging the administration monitors economic risks. She deferred questions about Hunter Biden to his representatives and said the White House would follow up on a question about the Architect of the Capitol.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the White House is cautious about commenting on the ongoing federal investigation in Memphis and expresses sympathy for the family involved.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the White House monitors economic indicators and highlighted job and inflation data while declining to comment on private-sector layoffs.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says marijuana banking is a Congressional issue and refers questions on Hunter Biden’s art sales to his representatives.
  #### State of the Union and other events
  Reporters asked about State of the Union preparations, the President’s annual physical, and a joint President-Vice President event in Philadelphia. Jean-Pierre said the President is taking the State of the Union seriously and will outline his economic record and agenda, that his physical remains on track to be completed by month’s end, and that the President and Vice President will travel to Philadelphia on February 3 to highlight economic implementation. She did not preview specific speech content.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the President is preparing for the State of the Union and will focus on economic progress and plans for 2023.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE confirms the President’s physical is still expected to be completed by the end of the month.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the President and Vice President will travel to Philadelphia on February 3 to discuss implementation of the Biden‑Harris economic agenda.
