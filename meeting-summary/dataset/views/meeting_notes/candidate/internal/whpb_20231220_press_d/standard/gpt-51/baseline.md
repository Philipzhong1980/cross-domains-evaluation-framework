### Overview
Senior administration officials brief reporters under embargo on a fragile but advanced agreement in which Venezuela is expected to release 10 Americans, including six designated as wrongfully detained, while the United States grants clemency to money-laundering defendant Alex Saab and secures the extradition of fugitive defense contractor Leonard “Fat Leonard” Francis, whose return will allow full accountability for a major U.S. Navy bribery scandal. Officials explain that the deal, shaped over months by President Biden, National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan, Principal Deputy NSA Jon Finer, and Secretary Blinken, also includes Venezuela’s release of 20 political prisoners, opposition figure Roberto Abdul, and the suspension of three arrest warrants, steps that restore compliance with the October Barbados agreement and support the goal of competitive, inclusive elections in 2024. They emphasize that sanctions relief remains tied to concrete democratic progress, not to detainee negotiations, confirm that Nicolás Maduro still faces U.S. charges, and highlight Qatar’s role in facilitating talks. Throughout, officials decline to identify the freed Americans until family notifications are complete, stress that they have warned Caracas against any new detentions of U.S. citizens, and frame the outcome as both a humanitarian achievement that reunites dozens of families and a strategic move within a broader U.S. effort to promote a more democratic, stable Venezuela.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Agreement to release Americans
Officials announce an agreement with Venezuela to secure the release of six wrongfully detained Americans and four other Americans, along with the return of U.S. fugitive Leonard “Fat Leonard” Francis. This outcome reflects months of negotiations and direct involvement by President Biden, National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan, and Principal Deputy NSA Jon Finer. The deal aims to reunite American families while ensuring Francis faces accountability in U.S. custody. Officials stress that events are still unfolding and the situation remains fluid at the time of the call.
- A senior administration official says the U.S. has reached an agreement to secure the release of six wrongfully detained Americans in Venezuela plus four other Americans, meaning all wrongfully detained Americans in Venezuela will be freed.
- A senior administration official notes that the agreement results from many months of negotiations and regular consultations with President Biden.
- A senior administration official explains that as part of the arrangement Leonard “Fat Leonard” Francis will be extradited from Venezuela to a U.S. federal detention facility.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that this effort builds on the return of nine wrongfully detained Americans last year and reflects President Biden’s prioritization of bringing home hostages and wrongful detainees.
#### Background on Leonard "Fat Leonard" Francis
Officials provide detailed context on Leonard Francis’s criminal case to underscore the significance of his return. Francis orchestrated a massive bribery and fraud scheme involving U.S. Navy officials from 2004 to 2013 and later fled U.S. custody while awaiting sentencing. His recapture and extradition are presented as a major accountability milestone tied to the broader prisoner exchange. The narrative connects his return to the integrity of U.S. military operations and rule of law.
- A senior administration official states that Leonard Francis oversaw one of the most brazen bribery conspiracies in U.S. Navy history from 2004 until his 2013 arrest.
- A senior administration official explains that Francis admitted to steering Navy contracts and ship schedules to benefit his company and to bribing scores of Navy and other federal officials with tens of millions of dollars in fraud proceeds and lavish gifts.
- A senior administration official recounts that while on house arrest awaiting sentencing, Francis cut off his ankle monitor and fled to Venezuela, where he was later arrested as he tried to board a plane to Florida.
- A senior administration official asserts that Francis’s return to the United States will ensure he is fully held accountable both for his original crimes and his attempt to escape justice.
#### President’s clemency decision for Alex Saab
The administration outlines President Biden’s decision to grant clemency to Alex Saab, a Venezuelan figure pending trial in the U.S. for money laundering. Officials characterize this as a difficult but necessary trade to secure the release of 10 Americans and the return of Francis. They stress that this clemency is an exercise of presidential authority, not interference in Department of Justice investigations. The decision is framed as a humanitarian outcome that reunites families and advances accountability for a major corruption case.
- A senior administration official explains that to secure the exchange, President Biden granted clemency to Alex Saab, who was pending trial for money laundering, allowing Saab’s return to Venezuela.
- A senior administration official describes the deal as an exchange of 10 Americans and a fugitive from justice for one person returned to Venezuela.
- Responding to a question, a senior administration official stresses that the White House does not interfere with Department of Justice investigations and that clemency for Saab is a lawful exercise of presidential authority.
- A senior administration official says the President made a difficult but right choice to grant clemency in order to free 10 Americans, including six wrongfully detained, and to secure the return of Fat Leonard for prosecution.
#### Humanitarian and policy significance of releases
Officials highlight the humanitarian impact of reuniting Americans and Venezuelans with their families and situate the action within a broader global effort to free hostages and wrongful detainees. They note the administration’s track record in countries such as Afghanistan, Burma, Haiti, Russia, Venezuela, and West Africa. The releases are also tied to ongoing U.S. efforts to support democratic outcomes and stability in Venezuela and the wider region. The moment is portrayed as both emotionally significant and strategically important, even as officials caution that it remains fragile.
- A senior administration official states that the consequences of the decision include reuniting parents and children and ensuring that Fat Leonard is held to account for his crimes.
- A senior administration official notes that this case adds to the administration’s record of bringing home wrongfully detained Americans from Afghanistan, Burma, Haiti, Russia, Venezuela, West Africa, and other regions.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that there is still complex, fragile work underway and that events on the ground could face unanticipated hurdles.
- Another senior administration official says they are very proud that months of work are coming to fruition to reunite American and Venezuelan prisoners with their families.
#### Release of Venezuelan prisoners and political figures
The agreement also covers the release of 20 Venezuelan prisoners, the release of opposition figure Roberto Abdul, and the suspension of three arrest warrants for Venezuelans. Officials explain that these steps are part of commitments linked to the October Barbados agreement between the Maduro representatives and the opposition Unitary Platform. National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan insists on these Venezuelan releases to restore alignment with Barbados and advance conditions for competitive elections. The U.S. underscores that these releases are actions by Venezuela, not prisoner releases by the United States.
- A senior administration official reports that Maduro representatives have agreed to release 20 Venezuelan prisoners, to release Roberto Abdul of Súmate, and to suspend three arrest warrants for Venezuelan individuals.
- A senior administration official clarifies that these Venezuelan releases will occur the same morning as the call, bringing about 24 Venezuelans into freedom or out from under arrest warrants.
- A senior administration official underscores that Jake Sullivan insisted on including the release of 20 prisoners, Abdul, and the suspension of warrants to bring Venezuela back into alignment with the Barbados agreement.
- A senior administration official stresses that the only person the United States is releasing is Alex Saab and that all Venezuelans being freed are released by Venezuelan authorities from Venezuelan custody.
- A senior administration official later confirms that the 20 Venezuelan prisoners are part of commitments made under the Barbados agreement and that this step brings implementation back on track.
#### Role of Jake Sullivan, Jon Finer, and broader diplomacy
Officials describe high-level U.S. engagement on Venezuela, including regular strategy meetings and direct regional diplomacy to prevent conflict and support negotiations. National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan calibrates sanctions policy based on concrete outcomes, engages partners globally, and helps avert escalation around the Essequibo referendum. Principal Deputy NSA Jon Finer’s work in Bogotá and ongoing leadership of deputy-level discussions help set conditions for direct negotiations with Maduro’s representatives. These efforts underpin both the prisoner releases and the political dialogue over Venezuelan elections.
- A senior administration official notes that since May, Jake Sullivan and the Secretary of State have held regular strategy meetings on Venezuela and engaged partners in the region, Europe, and the Middle East to support elections and liberation of Americans.
- A senior administration official credits Jon Finer’s April visit to Bogotá and his role in an international meeting on Venezuela with laying the groundwork for direct U.S.–Maduro negotiations.
- A senior administration official explains that since 2021 Jon Finer has chaired active deputy-level discussions on Venezuela and provided real-time instructions to the U.S. negotiating team.
- A senior administration official recounts that ahead of and after the Essequibo referendum, Jake Sullivan engaged President Irfaan Ali of Guyana and Brazilian National Security Advisor Celso Amorim to discourage military confrontation and promote diplomacy.
#### U.S. policy on Venezuela and elections
The administration situates the prisoner exchange within a broader strategy of engaging both Maduro’s representatives and the Venezuelan opposition, including the Unitary Platform and presidential candidate María Corina Machado. Officials argue that dialogue and calibrated sanctions tied to concrete outcomes are producing progress, such as the October Barbados agreement and continuing political prisoner releases. The goal is to create conditions for competitive, inclusive elections in 2024 that allow Venezuelans to determine their country’s future. The exchange is portrayed as reinforcing this policy trajectory rather than substituting for political reforms.
- A senior administration official explains that U.S. policy combines direct engagement with Maduro’s representatives, close consultation with the Unitary Platform and María Corina Machado, and active engagement with Congress.
- A senior administration official states that this approach has enabled breakthroughs including the signing of the Barbados agreement, the release of Americans, and ongoing releases of Venezuelan prisoners.
- A senior administration official says that these developments demonstrate the results of dialogue and help open the door to competitive and inclusive elections next year in Venezuela.
- Another senior administration official notes that achieving a more democratic and stable Venezuela is important for regional wellbeing and remains a central focus of U.S. policy.
- A senior administration official reiterates that U.S. sanctions policy is calibrated to concrete outcomes in negotiations and is aimed at supporting a negotiated path to competitive, inclusive elections.
#### International facilitation and Qatar’s role
Officials describe how international partners, especially Qatar, help facilitate negotiations between the U.S. and Venezuelan authorities. Qatar hosts and supports conversations focused on both electoral conditions and the release of Americans. This facilitation contributes to the progress announced on the call. The U.S. expresses gratitude for Qatar’s ongoing good offices while emphasizing that core decisions result from U.S.–Venezuelan negotiations and presidential-level choices.
- A senior administration official states that Secretary Blinken has leveraged relationships worldwide and coordination with democratic forces in Venezuela to advance both the release of detainees and prospects for democracy.
- A senior administration official says Qatar has been an important partner across multiple issues and has for months facilitated conversations between Maduro authorities and U.S. officials.
- A senior administration official specifies that Qatar’s facilitation focuses on clearing a path toward competitive 2024 elections and the return of wrongfully detained Americans.
- A senior administration official notes that Qatar continues to offer its good offices when requested, and the U.S. is grateful for that support.
#### Numbers and identities of released individuals
During the Q&A, reporters seek clarity on how many Americans and Venezuelans are being freed and who they are. Officials confirm that, assuming events proceed as planned, 10 Americans plus Leonard Francis will come home and 24 Venezuelans will benefit from releases or suspended warrants. However, they decline to name the six wrongfully detained Americans or the other four until families are notified. This cautious approach reflects both operational uncertainty and a desire to protect families from learning news through the press.
- Responding to a question, a senior administration official confirms that, if all goes as hoped, 10 Americans and Fat Leonard will come home, while Venezuela will release 20 political prisoners plus Roberto Abdul.
- A senior administration official clarifies that three Venezuelans with outstanding arrest warrants will see those warrants suspended, bringing the Venezuelan total to 24 people affected.
- A senior administration official tells Louise Radnofsky that the administration will not yet release the names of the six wrongfully detained Americans or the other four until family notifications are complete.
- A senior administration official states that they will follow up later in the morning with details after ensuring families hear the news directly from the government rather than from reporters.
#### Sanctions, charges, and non-linkage to detainee deal
Officials address questions about sanctions relief and legal status of Venezuelan leaders, clarifying that detainee negotiations are distinct from sanctions policy. They stress that sanctions adjustments announced earlier in response to the Barbados agreement remain in place and will continue to be calibrated to progress toward competitive elections. They also confirm that Nicolás Maduro still faces pending U.S. charges. The administration underscores that sanctions relief is not traded for the release of wrongfully detained Americans, preserving the integrity of both policy tracks.
- A senior administration official states explicitly that sanctions relief, including oil and gas measures, is not offered in exchange for releasing wrongfully detained Americans.
- A senior administration official explains that sanctions decisions are tied to ongoing negotiations between the Unitary Platform and Maduro’s representatives and will be revisited after the current detainee actions.
- A senior administration official confirms to Margaret Brennan that Nicolás Maduro still faces pending criminal charges in the United States.
- A senior administration official notes that sanctions relief provided after the Barbados agreement remains in place and will be maintained only as long as there is continued progress toward competitive elections.
- A senior administration official says the United States will keep calibrating sanctions based on concrete outcomes among Venezuelan parties and remain focused on achieving competitive and inclusive elections.
#### Future risk of detentions and deterrence
Reporters raise concerns that prisoner exchanges may incentivize future detentions of Americans. Officials respond that the U.S. has clearly communicated expectations to Venezuelan authorities that no additional Americans be detained and secured commitments to that effect. They reiterate strong travel warnings against going to Venezuela and signal readiness to respond if commitments are violated. The administration acknowledges uncertainty but frames its approach as balancing urgent humanitarian imperatives with efforts to deter future hostage-taking.
- In response to a question about future detentions, a senior administration official says the U.S. has made abundantly clear its expectation that Venezuela not detain additional Americans and has secured commitments on that point.
- A senior administration official notes that the U.S. continues to warn Americans not to travel to Venezuela due to the risk of detention.
- A senior administration official says the United States will take appropriate actions if Venezuela fails to meet its commitments and remains committed to protecting Americans from arbitrary detention.
- A senior administration official links ongoing conversations about Venezuelan political prisoners to the broader goal of ensuring that competitive, inclusive elections are not undermined by fear of arbitrary detention.
