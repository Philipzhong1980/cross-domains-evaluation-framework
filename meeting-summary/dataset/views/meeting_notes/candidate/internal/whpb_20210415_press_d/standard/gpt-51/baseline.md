### Overview
Senior administration officials brief on a coordinated U.S. response to harmful Russian activities, explaining that the Biden administration seeks a stable, predictable relationship with Moscow while defending U.S. sovereignty through resolute but proportionate measures. They formally attribute the SolarWinds cyber intrusion to Russia’s SVR with high confidence and announce a new sanctions executive order that expands authorities to target malign behavior, including prohibitions on U.S. participation in new Russian sovereign debt issuances, designations of six Russian cyber-enabler firms, and sanctions on 32 entities and individuals involved in 2020 election interference, as well as Crimea-related sanctions and the expulsion of 10 Russian diplomats. Officials update reporters on low- to moderate-confidence intelligence that Russian officers encouraged Taliban attacks on U.S. and coalition forces, clarify that current punitive actions focus on SolarWinds and election interference, and underline that any targeting of U.S. personnel is unacceptable. They detail a broader cyber strategy that includes modernizing federal cybersecurity, considering supply chain protections under existing authorities, warning companies about ICT risks tied to Russia, and launching international initiatives such as a cyber attribution course and expanded CYBER FLAG exercises with European allies. Throughout, they justify SolarWinds as crossing a line due to its scale, disruptive potential, and burden on private victims, and emphasize that while the United States retains options to escalate, it prefers de-escalation and has proposed a leader-level summit with President Putin in Europe to manage the bilateral relationship and prevent further deterioration.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### U.S.-Russia policy context
Senior administration officials outline the Biden administration’s approach to Russia as seeking a stable, predictable relationship while being prepared to defend U.S. national interests. They explain that recent actions respond to Russian behavior that targets U.S. sovereignty, including cyber intrusions and election interference. Officials emphasize that the response includes both seen and unseen measures, with the public steps calibrated to be resolute yet proportionate. The President has directly communicated this approach to President Putin and linked it to earlier commitments to respond to malign activities.
- A senior administration official states that the United States seeks a relationship with Russia that is stable and predictable and wants to avoid a downward spiral.
- A senior administration official explains that President Biden warned President Putin in their first call that the United States would respond to harmful Russian foreign activities, including the poisoning of Aleksey Navalny, the SolarWinds cyber intrusion, and interference in the 2020 election.
- A senior administration official notes that the measures announced are the public component of a broader response, some elements of which will remain unseen, and are intended to be resolute but proportionate in imposing costs on Russia.
#### Attribution of SolarWinds cyber intrusion
Officials formally attribute the SolarWinds cyber espionage campaign to Russia’s Foreign Intelligence Service (SVR), specifically the APT29/Cozy Bear/Dukes unit. They stress that the U.S. intelligence community has high confidence in this assessment, upgrading a prior judgment that the incident was only “likely of Russian origin.” This formal naming underpins the broader response—including sanctions and cybersecurity measures—and signals that supply chain cyber operations of this scale are unacceptable. It also aligns U.S. findings with statements of support from allies like Australia and the EU.
- A senior administration official announces that the U.S. is formally naming the Russian Foreign Intelligence Service (SVR) as the perpetrator of the broad-scope SolarWinds cyber espionage campaign.
- A senior administration official specifies that the SVR unit known as APT29, Cozy Bear, or the Dukes is attributed as conducting the SolarWinds intrusion, with high confidence from the U.S. intelligence community.
- A senior administration official notes that this high-confidence attribution updates the January 5, 2021 assessment by the previous administration that SolarWinds was only likely of Russian origin.
- A senior administration official later reiterates that the U.S. intelligence community has high confidence in its attribution of SolarWinds to the SVR and links this to the broader U.S. cyber response.
#### New Russia sanctions executive order
President Biden signs a new executive order that expands U.S. authorities to respond to Russia’s harmful foreign activities. Officials explain that the EO enables impactful economic measures, including sovereign debt restrictions and designations of entities supporting Russian intelligence cyber programs. While only some authorities are activated immediately, the breadth of the EO is designed to deter future election interference and malicious cyber activity by signaling the potential for substantial, lasting costs. The approach is framed as proportionate and flexible, allowing escalation if Russian behavior continues or worsens.
- A senior administration official states that President Biden has signed a new sanctions executive order providing strengthened authorities to respond to and deter Russia’s harmful foreign activities.
- A senior administration official explains that the new EO signals the United States will not accept destabilizing Russian behavior and will impose economically impactful costs if such activities continue or escalate.
- A senior administration official highlights that the EO includes additional authorities not exercised today, which are intended to send a clear signal that further election interference or malicious cyber activities are unacceptable and may trigger substantial and lasting costs.
#### Sovereign debt sanctions on Russia
The administration details new sovereign debt restrictions as a core element of the sanctions package. Under the EO, Treasury prohibits U.S. financial institutions from participating in the primary market for ruble and non-ruble bonds issued after June 14, 2021, by key Russian state financial entities, expanding prior limits that only covered non-ruble issuances. Officials describe this as targeted at the main market funding the Russian government, designed to raise borrowing costs and pressure the economy while limiting global financial spillovers. They emphasize the measure’s flexibility and link its future expansion to Russia’s choices and behavior.
- A senior administration official explains that Treasury has issued a directive prohibiting U.S. financial institutions from participating in the primary market for ruble or non-ruble bonds issued after June 14, 2021, by Russia’s Central Bank, National Wealth Fund, or Ministry of Finance.
- A senior administration official notes that prior to this action, U.S. sanctions only restricted purchases of non-ruble denominated Russian sovereign debt at issuance, leaving over 80 percent of ruble-denominated debt untouched.
- A senior administration official 2 describes this move as a matter of principle, arguing there is no credible reason for Americans to directly fund the Russian government while it undermines U.S. sovereignty.
- A senior administration official 2 clarifies that the new prohibition applies only to newly issued ruble sovereign debt in the primary market and does not cover secondary market trading or existing holdings.
- A senior administration official 2 states that the June 14 effective date is chosen to allow financial institutions time to understand and adapt to the measures and to limit spillovers to U.S. and global financial markets.
- A senior administration official 2 explains that removing U.S. investors from Russia’s approximately $185 billion ruble-denominated sovereign debt market, a quarter of which is foreign-owned, can trigger higher borrowing costs, capital flight, and a weaker currency, impacting Russia’s growth and inflation outcomes.
- A senior administration official 2 emphasizes that the speed and magnitude of these economic effects depend on Russia’s subsequent choices and reiterates that the United States is aiming for a tailored, proportionate response and a stable, predictable relationship.
#### Sanctions on cyber enablers and election interference actors
Beyond sovereign debt, the U.S. imposes targeted sanctions on entities tied to Russian cyber operations and election interference. Six Russian companies that support SVR and other intelligence cyber programs are designated for operating in Russia’s technology sector, and 32 entities and individuals are sanctioned for Russian government-directed efforts to influence the 2020 U.S. election and spread disinformation. Officials frame these actions as disrupting operational support networks and messaging operations that seek to delegitimize democratic processes in the U.S. and among allies. These measures complement SolarWinds-related responses by imposing costs directly on operational enablers.
- A senior administration official announces that Treasury has designated six Russian companies that support the SVR’s cyber program and other Russian intelligence agencies’ cyber activities, including by providing expertise and developing tools and infrastructure.
- A senior administration official states that these companies are being designated for operating in the technology sector of the Russian Federation’s economy and affirms that the United States will continue to hold them accountable.
- A senior administration official reports that Treasury has sanctioned 32 entities and individuals for Russian government-directed attempts to influence the 2020 U.S. presidential election and to conduct disinformation and influence campaigns against U.S. partners and allies.
- A senior administration official explains that this action aims to disrupt coordinated efforts by Russian officials, proxies, and intelligence agencies to delegitimize democratic electoral processes.
#### Sanctions related to Crimea and diplomatic expulsions
The administration coordinates with allies to respond to Russia’s continued occupation and repression in Crimea and simultaneously takes diplomatic action in Washington. Treasury, in concert with the EU, UK, Australia, and Canada, sanctions eight individuals and entities associated with Russia’s activities in Crimea. Additionally, the U.S. expels 10 Russian officials from Russia’s diplomatic mission in Washington, citing conduct inconsistent with their diplomatic status. These steps reinforce a multilateral front on regional aggression and signal consequences for misuse of diplomatic presence.
- A senior administration official announces that Treasury, together with the EU, UK, Australia, and Canada, has sanctioned eight individuals and entities linked to Russia’s ongoing occupation and repression in Crimea.
- A senior administration official states that the United States is expelling 10 Russian officials from Russia’s Washington, D.C., diplomatic mission.
- In response to a question, a senior administration official explains that the 10 Russian individuals are being expelled because they were determined to be acting in a manner inconsistent with their status in the United States.
#### Intelligence on reported Russian bounties
Officials provide an update on intelligence regarding alleged Russian bounties to incentivize Taliban attacks on U.S. and coalition troops in Afghanistan. They explain that the intelligence community has low to moderate confidence that Russian intelligence officers sought to encourage such attacks through financial incentives, due in part to reliance on detainee reporting and Afghanistan’s challenging environment. While this assessment burdens Russia to explain its activities, the officials stress that the day’s concrete actions are not directly tied to this issue but instead to SolarWinds and election interference. The U.S. uses diplomatic, intelligence, and military channels to convey strong concern and signals that targeting U.S. personnel is unacceptable.
- A senior administration official reports that the U.S. intelligence community assesses with low to moderate confidence that Russian intelligence officers sought to encourage Taliban attacks against U.S. and coalition personnel in Afghanistan in 2019 and earlier, including through financial incentives.
- A senior administration official notes that the low to moderate confidence level stems partly from reliance on detainee reporting and the difficult operating environment in Afghanistan.
- A senior administration official states that information showing connections between criminal agents in Afghanistan and elements of the Russian government places a burden on Moscow to explain its actions and address this pattern of behavior.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that the safety of U.S. and allied military personnel is a top national security interest and that the United States will not accept targeting of its forces.
- In response to a question, a senior administration official clarifies that the actions announced are in response to cyber intrusions and election interference, not specifically tied to the bounty reports, though the U.S. has delivered strong messages to Russia on that issue.
- A senior administration official explains that, given the low- to moderate-confidence assessment, the priority is to send a clear message about the steps the United States would take if such behavior were to continue.
#### Cybersecurity policy, SolarWinds response, and supply chain security
Officials articulate a two-part response to SolarWinds: imposing costs on the perpetrator and reinforcing norms for an open, interoperable, secure, and reliable Internet. They highlight the risks of supply chain exploitation and warn about using ICT services tied to Russia, while encouraging U.S. companies to review their infrastructure and prepare for potential future U.S. actions. The government is considering using Executive Order 13873 to further protect the communications and technology supply chain and is already moving to rapidly modernize federal cybersecurity—especially at nine compromised agencies—through encryption, multi-factor authentication, cloud security, logging, endpoint detection, and mature security operations centers. An upcoming executive order will further set procurement standards to leverage federal buying power to improve software and hardware security broadly.
- A senior administration official explains that the U.S. response to SolarWinds has two aspects: naming and imposing costs on the perpetrator and strongly affirming the importance of an open, interoperable, secure, and reliable Internet.
- A senior administration official warns that the SVR’s compromise of SolarWinds and other companies underscores the risks of supply chain exploitation by Russia targeting companies worldwide.
- A senior administration official cautions that using ICT services supplied by firms that operate or store data in Russia or rely on Russian-based development or support poses risks and should prompt U.S. companies to reassess their infrastructure security.
- A senior administration official notes that the U.S. government is evaluating whether to use Executive Order 13873 to better protect the communications and technology supply chain from further Russian exploitation.
- A senior administration official 2 says SolarWinds highlighted the need to rapidly modernize federal cybersecurity and describes efforts focused on nine compromised federal agencies to deploy encryption, multi-factor authentication, cloud security, logging, and endpoint detection, and to establish effective security operations centers.
- A senior administration official 2 announces that an upcoming executive order will set standards for software the U.S. government procures, using federal IT spending power to encourage more secure software and hardware that benefit governments and companies globally.
#### International cyber cooperation and CYBER FLAG exercise
The administration emphasizes strengthening international norms and collective defense in cyberspace as a central element of its response. Officials note public support from allies such as Australia and the EU for the SolarWinds attribution and outline new initiatives to promote responsible state behavior online. These include a first-of-its-kind course at the George C. Marshall Center on attributing cyber incidents and enhanced collaboration through the Department of Defense’s CYBER FLAG exercise, with the UK, France, Denmark, and Estonia joining in planning. The aim is to build a community of defensive operators and improve joint ability to detect, synchronize, and respond to malicious cyber activity targeting critical infrastructure.
- A senior administration official states that malicious Russian cyber activity is a shared challenge and notes supportive statements from Australia and the European Union regarding U.S. attribution of SolarWinds to Russia.
- A senior administration official announces that the United States is strengthening efforts to promote a framework of responsible state behavior in cyberspace and to cooperate with allies and partners against malign activities like those by Moscow.
- A senior administration official reveals that the U.S. will offer a first-of-its-kind course at the George C. Marshall Center in Germany for policymakers worldwide on policy and technical aspects of publicly attributing cyber incidents.
- A senior administration official announces that the U.S. will invite the UK, France, Denmark, and Estonia to join in planning this year’s CYBER FLAG exercise, and that these countries have agreed to participate.
- A senior administration official explains that CYBER FLAG will build a community of defensive cyber operators and improve the capability of the U.S. and allies to jointly identify and respond to malicious cyber activities targeting critical infrastructure and key resources.
- A senior administration official notes that SolarWinds demonstrates the need for domestic and international partnerships and threat intelligence sharing to better defend against and mitigate malicious cyber activity from Russia and other adversaries.
#### Rationale for responding to SolarWinds
In response to a question about whether SolarWinds warranted such a strong response given that it was characterized as espionage, officials explain why they view it as reckless and unacceptable. They stress the extraordinary scope and scale of the compromise, which affected over 16,000 systems worldwide and created national security and public safety risks. They also highlight the ease with which a campaign can pivot from espionage to disruption, especially given Russia’s history of reckless cyber behavior, and the heavy mitigation costs placed on mostly private-sector victims. These factors drive the administration’s judgment that SolarWinds crossed a line in cyberspace behavior and required a clear, public response.
- In reply to a question, a senior administration official notes that the SolarWinds supply chain compromise gave the SVR the ability to spy on or potentially disrupt more than 16,000 computer systems worldwide.
- A senior administration official cites three main reasons for deeming SolarWinds unacceptable: the broad scope and scale of the compromise, the rapid potential to shift from espionage to disruption, and the undue burden placed on private-sector victims to mitigate the attack.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that given Russia’s history of malicious and reckless cyber activity, the speed with which such an operation can turn disruptive poses serious national security and public safety concerns.
- A senior administration official concludes that, based on these factors, the United States needed to make clear that activities like SolarWinds are reckless and unacceptable.
#### De-escalation strategy and proposed U.S.-Russia summit
Throughout the briefing, officials stress that the U.S. aims to avoid an escalatory cycle with Russia even as it imposes costs. They underscore that the measures are tailored to past actions and that Washington remains open to a stable and predictable relationship. President Biden has proposed a summit with President Putin in a third European country in the coming months to address the full range of bilateral issues and to manage risks of escalation. The U.S. signals that further actions remain available but frames de-escalation and dialogue as the preferred path, contingent on Russia’s responses.
- A senior administration official reiterates that the United States does not seek an escalatory cycle with Russia and intends its responses to be proportionate and tailored to Russia’s past activities.
- A senior administration official reports that President Biden told President Putin in a recent call that the United States seeks a stable and predictable relationship and, in that spirit, proposed a summit in a third European country in the coming months.
- A senior administration official notes that Russia has not yet responded to the summit proposal but argues it is vital for the two leaders to meet to manage the relationship and prevent escalation from spinning out of control.
- In response to a question about potential Russian escalation, a senior administration official says the U.S. has made clear there is more it could do but prefers to get off the escalation ladder and find a stable way forward, reserving the right to act further if necessary.
