### Overview
The gaggle, led by National Security Council spokesman John Kirby and driven by questions from multiple outlets, focuses on U.S. diplomacy and policy around the Gaza conflict, regional security, and related humanitarian and political issues. Kirby credits Jake Sullivan’s meetings in Israel with helping secure Israel’s decision to open the Kerem Shalom crossing for direct humanitarian aid into Gaza, and he describes ongoing but unresolved discussions with Israel, the Palestinian Authority, and regional partners over post-conflict governance and security, including the potential role of a revitalized Palestinian Authority. He explains that the United States is pressing Israel to transition from high-intensity to more targeted, lower-intensity operations while not dictating timelines, and he responds to the mistaken killing of three Israeli hostages by calling it tragic, expecting an Israeli investigation, and noting that President Biden has been briefed. Kirby addresses wider regional security by condemning Houthi attacks that have disrupted Red Sea shipping and outlining U.S. efforts to bolster a maritime task force, and he confronts press safety concerns by offering condolences for the killing of Al Jazeera journalist Samer Abu Daka while maintaining that the U.S. has no evidence Israel is deliberately targeting journalists, even as it urges greater precision to protect civilians. He reiterates that all U.S. military aid must comply with international humanitarian law but does not announce new conditions on assistance to Israel, and he defends the U.S. record on West Bank settler violence—highlighting public statements, private pressure, and visa bans on violent settlers—even though Washington did not join a new EU-led statement on the issue.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Opening of Kerem Shalom crossing
John Kirby announces that Israel decides to open the Kerem Shalom crossing for the direct delivery of humanitarian assistance into Gaza, following U.S. urging conveyed by Jake Sullivan. He frames this as a significant step that should improve access to food, water, medicine, and fuel for Palestinians. The decision aligns with U.S. requests to expand humanitarian access and is presented as a concrete outcome of Sullivan’s meetings in Israel. The current state is that inspections are underway and direct truck entry is now approved in principle.
- Mr. Kirby explains that Jake Sullivan welcomed Prime Minister Netanyahu’s initial decision to open the Kerem Shalom crossing for inspections and urged Israel to allow direct truck entry into Gaza.
- Mr. Kirby states that before leaving Israel, Jake Sullivan was informed that Israel had decided to open Kerem Shalom for direct delivery of humanitarian assistance to Palestinians in Gaza.
- Mr. Kirby describes the opening of Kerem Shalom as a significant step forward that the United States requested and for which the administration is very grateful.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that the decision is expected to make a meaningful difference for people in Gaza who still urgently need food, water, medicine, and fuel.
#### Post-conflict governance and security in Gaza
In response to questions about future security in Gaza, Kirby says the U.S. is actively exploring post-conflict governance arrangements but does not yet have firm answers. He notes that Sullivan discussed governance possibilities with Palestinian Authority President Mahmoud Abbas and that Washington is considering roles for a revitalized Palestinian Authority. The U.S. position is that Gaza cannot return to the pre-October 7 status quo with Hamas in control and must better reflect Palestinian aspirations for peace, stability, and security. The current state is exploratory, with ongoing consultations and no final model or security structure decided.
- Mr. Kirby tells Aamer that questions about reintroducing Palestinian security forces into Gaza are valid but that the United States does not yet know what post-conflict security will look like.
- Mr. Kirby says Jake Sullivan discussed governance possibilities for Gaza directly with Mahmoud Abbas, indicating active U.S. engagement with the Palestinian Authority on future arrangements.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that whatever post-conflict Gaza looks like, it cannot resemble October 6 with Hamas in control and must better represent the aspirations of the Palestinian people for peace and security.
- Mr. Kirby confirms that the administration is working with a range of partners, including Israel and the Palestinian Authority, to define post-conflict governance and security, but stresses that there are no final answers yet.
#### Role and reform of the Palestinian Authority
Kirby addresses how the Palestinian Authority (PA) might factor into Gaza’s future, without divulging detailed private exchanges between Sullivan and Abbas. He explains that the U.S. discusses a "revamped and revitalized" PA that would bear responsibility and accountability for Palestinians’ future. A key focus is increasing the PA’s credibility, authenticity, and accountability to the Palestinian people. The discussion remains conceptual, with ongoing deliberations about how to strengthen the PA for a potential expanded role.
- Mr. Kirby declines to detail whether Jake Sullivan asked Mahmoud Abbas directly if he would take over Gaza but confirms they discussed post-conflict Gaza and governance issues.
- Mr. Kirby notes that the U.S. envisions a revamped and revitalized Palestinian Authority that assumes responsibility and accountability for managing the future of the Palestinian people.
- Mr. Kirby says there is a broad discussion underway about what the Palestinian Authority’s role should and can be, as well as what reforms are needed to make it more credible and accountable.
#### Transition from high- to low-intensity Israeli operations
Kirby outlines ongoing U.S.–Israeli discussions about transitioning from high-intensity to more targeted operations in Gaza. He stresses that the U.S. is not dictating terms but is urging Israel to move toward more precise, surgical operations when conditions allow. He acknowledges both sides expect the conflict could last for months while emphasizing the importance of planning and asking hard questions about the next phase. Timelines and specific conditions remain in Israel’s hands, but there is a shared understanding that a lower-intensity phase is an important objective.
- Mr. Kirby tells Steve Holland that the United States discusses with Israel how it plans to transition from high-intensity to lower-intensity military operations, but does not dictate Israeli decisions or timelines.
- Mr. Kirby says both the United States and Israel agree the conflict could continue for months, making it important to think ahead about transitioning to more targeted and precise operations.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Jake Sullivan had constructive conversations in Israel about the importance of transitioning phases and that such phased operations are standard military practice.
- Mr. Kirby states there is general agreement that moving to lower-intensity operations is an important next step, but he declines to provide details on parameters, conditions, or timing, leaving that to Israel.
#### Mistaken killing of three Israeli hostages
Kirby reacts to news that the Israel Defense Forces mistakenly killed three hostages during operations in Gaza. He calls the incident heartbreaking and tragic, noting that the U.S. lacks full visibility into how it occurred and expects Israel to conduct a forensic review. He cautions against drawing broad conclusions from this single incident about Israel’s ability to conduct precise operations, while pointing to smaller-scale operations in north Gaza as evidence of some adaptation. President Biden has been briefed but has not spoken to Prime Minister Netanyahu specifically about this incident.
- Mr. Kirby responds to Gabe that the killing of three hostages by Israeli forces during a raid is heartbreaking and tragic and not an outcome anyone wanted.
- Mr. Kirby says the United States does not yet have perfect visibility into how the operation unfolded or how the mistake occurred and expects Israel to conduct a forensic review.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Israel has previously operated in north Gaza with a smaller footprint and smaller-unit operations than originally planned, suggesting efforts to be more targeted.
- Mr. Kirby declines to make a broad judgment about Israel’s precision based on this single incident and stresses that the immediate focus is mourning with the families of the hostages.
- Mr. Kirby confirms to Gabe that President Biden has not spoken directly with Prime Minister Netanyahu about the incident but has been briefed by his national security team.
#### Red Sea shipping threats and maritime task force
Kirby addresses concerns about major shipping companies, such as Maersk, halting transit through the Red Sea due to Houthi attacks. He acknowledges that commercial transit in the Red Sea is more dangerous now and reiterates U.S. condemnation of Houthi actions that threaten international commerce and freedom of navigation. He explains that the U.S. is working to strengthen existing combined maritime forces and is developing a maritime task force, with more details expected soon. The current state is one of active coalition-building to secure a vital trade corridor.
- Mr. Kirby tells Felicia that companies like Maersk must make their own risk calculations but acknowledges that transit in the Red Sea is more dangerous due to Houthi attacks on commercial shipping.
- Mr. Kirby says the United States is working hard to bolster and strengthen existing combined maritime forces in the region and expects to share more specifics about a maritime task force in coming days.
- Mr. Kirby condemns in the strongest terms Houthi threats and attacks that disrupt international commerce, restrict freedom of navigation, and endanger ships and crews.
- Mr. Kirby states that the United States will continue working with a range of partners to improve the safety and security of commerce through this vital international waterway.
#### Killing of Al Jazeera journalist and targeting concerns
Kirby responds to questions about the killing of Al Jazeera journalist Samer Abu Daka, reportedly in an Israeli drone strike. He offers condolences to the journalist’s family and colleagues and emphasizes the importance of press freedom and the principle that journalists must never be deliberately targeted. Kirby says the U.S. does not yet know the details of the strike and still has no indication that Israel is intentionally targeting journalists as a matter of policy or doctrine. He explains that evidence of deliberate targeting would require detailed knowledge of the purpose, origin, and targeting decisions behind specific strikes, and reiterates that the U.S. presses Israel to be precise and avoid killing civilians, including journalists.
- Mr. Kirby offers deepest sympathies and condolences to the family, colleagues, and network of Al Jazeera photographer Samer Abu Daka and to journalists worldwide mourning his loss.
- Mr. Kirby underscores that journalists must have the freedom to cover conflicts and that it is never acceptable to deliberately target them while they conduct vital and dangerous work.
- Mr. Kirby says the United States does not yet know all the details of the strike that killed Abu Daka and therefore cannot characterize the nature of the operation.
- Mr. Kirby states that the United States still has no indications that Israel is deliberately targeting journalists as a matter of policy or operational doctrine.
- Mr. Kirby explains that determining deliberate targeting would require specific knowledge of the strike’s purpose, origin, targeting process, and decision-making, which the U.S. does not have in this case.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that the U.S. will not act as judge and jury over every strike but will continue to urge Israel to be precise, careful, and mindful of civilian lives, explicitly including journalists.
#### Conditions on U.S. military aid to Israel
In response to questions about conditioning U.S. military assistance to Israel, Kirby reiterates longstanding policy expectations without announcing any new conditionality. He affirms that all U.S. foreign military assistance must be used in compliance with international humanitarian law and against appropriate targets, and that Israel is not an exception. Kirby notes that President Biden believes the current U.S. approach has produced results, including some Israeli steps to reduce civilian harm. He indicates the U.S. will keep pressing Israel for further measures but offers no change in policy or explicit conditions on aid.
- Mr. Kirby tells Selina that he has addressed the question of conditioning aid repeatedly and has no additional context to add at this time.

- Mr. Kirby notes that Israel has taken some steps to reduce civilian harm and that the United States will continue to urge additional steps, without announcing any new conditions on aid.

He highlights U.S. visa bans on violent settlers as a concrete measure and maintains that the U.S. has been clear and consistent in its opposition to such violence.
