### Overview
Senior administration officials brief reporters on a new U.S. executive order that President Biden will sign to strengthen sanctions against Russia by introducing, for the first time in this conflict, secondary sanctions authority aimed at financial institutions that facilitate Russia’s military‑industrial base. They situate the move within a two‑year strategy of using sanctions and export controls to deny Russia war revenues and critical inputs, citing significant military losses, production constraints, falling energy revenues under the G7 price cap, and broader economic strain as evidence that existing measures are working but are being challenged by Kremlin‑directed evasion networks using cutouts and front companies to procure semiconductors, machine tools, and other key items. Officials explain that the EO, coordinated with G7 partners and aligned with the EU’s 12th sanctions package and UK actions, will make the global financial system a chokepoint by forcing banks—especially in third‑country jurisdictions, via pressure from their U.S. and European correspondent banks—to either ensure their transactions do not support Russia’s war production or risk losing access to the U.S. financial system; the order also enables enforcement of bans on Russian‑origin products, such as processed diamonds, that are substantially transformed abroad. They stress that while they are not aware of U.S. or European banks currently violating these rules and hope the new authority will mainly deter risky behavior, this tool is one part of a broader effort that still depends on Congress passing supplemental aid for Ukraine, and they confirm that separate discussions continue within the G7 on how and whether to use frozen Russian assets to ensure Russia ultimately compensates Ukraine for war damages.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Context of Russia sanctions strategy
Senior administration officials outline that, since Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, the United States uses sanctions, export controls, and financial measures to impose costs on Russia while supporting Ukraine militarily, financially, and humanitarily. They stress that the core strategy is to deny Russia access to the international financial system, critical technologies, and materials needed to fuel its war. Officials emphasize that entities supplying goods or processing transactions that materially support Russia’s military‑industrial base are considered complicit in its aggression. This context frames the new executive order as part of a longer-term, evolving economic pressure campaign.
- A senior administration official states that since the invasion of Ukraine, the United States has taken strong and decisive actions to impose costs on Russia and support Ukraine with military, financial, and humanitarian assistance.
- A senior administration official explains that the core strategy is denying Russia access to the international financial system, increasing economic costs over time, and preventing access to equipment, materials, and technology for its war effort.
- A senior administration official notes that thousands of entities and individuals, including multinational procurement networks, have been sanctioned and that U.S. teams have engaged foreign governments, companies, and financial institutions to highlight sanctions risks.
- A senior administration official asserts that those supplying goods or processing transactions that materially support Russia’s military‑industrial base are complicit in Russia’s violation of Ukraine’s sovereignty and territorial integrity.
#### Announcement of new Russia sanctions EO
Officials announce that President Biden will sign a new executive order focused on strengthening U.S. sanctions authorities against Russia, particularly targeting financial facilitators of its war. They describe the EO as a key step to disrupt material support for Russia’s defense industrial base and to obstruct efforts to expand its military capacity. The action is coordinated with G7 partners and aligns with a December 6 G7 leaders’ statement on curtailing Russia’s use of the financial system and reducing its revenue from sectors such as non‑industrial diamonds. Officials also underline that sanctions are only one part of a broader strategy that depends on continued congressional funding for Ukraine.
- A senior administration official says President Biden, at the direction of the President and National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan, will sign a new executive order to step up efforts against Russia’s military‑industrial expansion.
- A senior administration official notes that the new executive order strengthens U.S. sanctions authority against Russia with a specific focus on financial facilitators for Russia’s war.
- A senior administration official characterizes the EO as an important step to disrupt material support for Russia’s defense industrial base and obstruct the expansion of its military capacity.
- A senior administration official explains that the step is coordinated with partners and cites the December 6 G7 leaders’ statement committing to further curtail Russia’s use of the international financial system and reduce revenues from areas including non‑industrial diamonds.
- A senior administration official stresses that sanctions are one prong of a broader strategy and reiterates that passage of supplemental funding for Ukraine by Congress remains essential.
#### Objectives and design of sanctions strategy
Treasury officials describe the overarching economic strategy against Russia as denying war revenues and degrading its war machine by disrupting supply chains. They recount that planning began before the invasion, with an emphasis on holding Russia accountable while limiting spillover to allies and partners. The new EO ties into the second objective by targeting the supply chain for weapons production through financial channels. Officials also emphasize ongoing support for Ukraine and the intent to keep using economic tools as long as the war continues.
- A senior administration official recalls that more than two years ago the President directed the Treasury Secretary to develop sanctions and export control options that would hold Russia accountable while limiting impacts on allies and partners.
- A senior administration official states that the strategy has two goals: denying Russia access to revenues needed to fight the war in Ukraine and putting sand in the gears of Russia’s war machine by taking apart its supply chain.
- A senior administration official explains that the executive order being described primarily supports the second objective of disrupting Russia’s supply chain and production capacity.
- A senior administration official affirms that the United States has stood with Ukrainians since the start of the war and will continue to do so unequivocally.
#### Impact of existing sanctions on Russia
Officials provide data to argue that sanctions and export controls already meaningfully strain Russia’s military and economy. They report large equipment losses, production constraints, labor shortages, and restricted access to foreign components, alongside a sharp rise in defense spending and a drop in energy revenues due in part to the G7 price cap. They add that Russia’s economy underperforms pre‑war projections and other oil exporters, faces historic emigration, negative foreign direct investment, and has effectively become a war economy at the expense of domestic needs. This context supports the case that sanctions are working but must be tightened further to address evasion.
- A senior administration official reports that since February 2022 Russia’s military has lost over 13,000 pieces of equipment, including tanks, UAVs, and missile systems, and is struggling to rebuild due to production constraints, labor shortages, and restricted access to foreign components.
- A senior administration official states that as Russia’s defense spending rose by almost 75 percent in the first half of 2023, its energy revenues declined by almost 40 percent due to the price cap implemented by the United States and partners.
- A senior administration official notes that Russia is increasingly isolated in global trade, faces a historic brain drain, and has seen foreign direct investment flip to negative.
- A senior administration official explains that Russia’s economy is about 5 percent smaller than predicted prior to the war and is significantly underperforming other oil‑exporting countries.
- A senior administration official says Russia has converted into a war economy, reorienting manufacturing capacity and labor to weapons production, which weakens the domestic economy and forces painful long‑term trade‑offs.
#### Russian sanctions evasion and critical goods
Treasury officials describe how the Kremlin devotes significant intelligence and financial resources to circumventing multilateral sanctions and export controls. They explain that Russia uses cutouts, front companies, and intermediaries—both witting and unwitting—to procure critical items like semiconductors, machine tools, chemical precursors, ball bearings, and optical systems. Over the past year, the U.S. and its coalition have exposed and sanctioned hundreds of such entities. The new EO is framed as the next phase, shifting pressure onto financial institutions that facilitate these transactions rather than just the front companies themselves.
- A senior administration official says the Kremlin has tasked its intelligence services with finding ways to evade multilateral sanctions and export controls, using cutouts, front companies, and intermediaries to source critical components.
- A senior administration official lists semiconductors, machine tools, chemical precursors, ball bearings, and optical systems as examples of items Russia seeks to obtain for its wartime economy.
- A senior administration official notes that over the past year the United States and its coalition partners have exposed and cut off evasion networks by sanctioning or listing hundreds of front companies, middlemen, and Russian firms moving these parts.
- A senior administration official argues that the new executive order will allow the United States to shift focus to financial institutions that enable these transactions instead of only targeting the trading entities.
#### Secondary sanctions authority on financial institutions
Officials explain that the executive order introduces, for the first time in this conflict, a secondary sanctions tool specifically aimed at financial institutions facilitating Russia’s military‑industrial activities. This creates a clear choice for banks: ensure that clients’ transactions do not support Russia’s war production or risk being sanctioned and potentially cut off from the U.S. financial system. They emphasize that the financial system acts as a chokepoint for Russia’s acquisition of key goods, and this authority is meant to disincentivize risky behavior globally. The officials expect the tool’s deterrent effect to be strong because global banks value access to the U.S. market and dollar system.
- A senior administration official describes the EO as giving Treasury a surgical tool to go after financial institutions conducting transactions that further Russia’s military‑industrial complex.
- A senior administration official explains that previous sanctions hit Russian access to goods and technology but the Kremlin responded by building cutouts and facilitators, making the financial system the chokepoint for enforcement.
- A senior administration official states that under the new tool, banks must either ensure that their transactions do not support Russia’s military‑industrial base or stop such business, or they will be subject to sanctions, including secondary sanctions for the first time in this conflict.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that financial institutions worldwide are now explicitly responsible for avoiding facilitation of transfers of inputs that Russia needs to expand its military production.
- A senior administration official argues that most banks will choose to retain access to the U.S. financial system rather than continue modest business tied to Russia’s military‑industrial complex, making the tool a powerful deterrent.
#### Clarifying what changes under new EO
In response to questions, officials clarify that the EO does not create entirely new categories of targets but enhances their ability to pressure financial intermediaries. They explain that the authority allows more direct action against banks in third‑country jurisdictions that continue to facilitate transactions for Russia’s military supply chain, even if those banks are not U.S. or European. They reiterate that the financial system is the practical chokepoint for Russia’s procurement networks. They also stress that the EO is intended to reinforce, not replace, extensive prior work with partners to identify and sanction entities involved in sanctions evasion.
- In response to Andrea Shalal, a senior administration official says the EO provides a specific, surgical tool to target financial institutions that conduct transactions furthering Russia’s military‑industrial complex.
- A senior administration official notes that, to date, the United States has sanctioned many cutout companies but that the true chokepoint is the financial system because such companies must transact financially to move goods into Russia.
- A senior administration official stresses that the new authority clarifies and reinforces the responsibility of financial institutions not to become facilitators of transfers of inputs that help Russia expand its military‑industrial base.
- A senior administration official says the EO builds on prior work with partners to address third‑country entities evading sanctions, but adds a more targeted focus on financial facilitation as a way to slow Russia’s war effort.
#### Timing, scope, and alignment with EU actions
Officials confirm that President Biden intends to sign the EO the day after the call, and its financial facilitator provisions take effect immediately upon signing. They stress that the EO is part of a broader, coordinated sanctions posture aligned with the EU’s 12th sanctions package and the UK, particularly on financial measures and diamond‑related restrictions. The EO also enables U.S. departments to enforce bans on Russian‑origin products that are substantially transformed abroad, such as diamonds cut or processed in third countries. Officials note that the United States has already designated over 250 targets this month in categories overlapping with the EU package, demonstrating prior and ongoing alignment.
- A senior administration official confirms that the President intends to sign the EO the day after the call, and that the authority to use the new tool against banks will be effective immediately.
- A senior administration official explains that the EO targets materials critical to Russia’s ability to build weapons of war and uses the financial system as a chokepoint to deny access to such goods.
- A senior administration official states that the sanctions package the United States has released is consistent with the EU’s 12th package and with UK actions, all driven by the G7 leaders’ statement.
- A senior administration official outlines that the EO authorizes relevant departments to implement bans on Russian‑origin products that are substantially transformed outside Russia, including processed diamonds, aligning with the EU’s 12th package.
- A senior administration official notes that the United States has already designated over 250 targets this month in categories similar to the EU’s 12th package and that, taken together, these steps amount to alignment with that package.
#### Expected bank behavior and outreach strategy
Officials explain that they will use the new authority in tandem with intensive outreach to U.S. and European banks that act as correspondent institutions for banks in higher‑risk jurisdictions. They plan to brief these institutions on the EO and urge them to press their foreign counterparts to improve due diligence and avoid facilitating Russian military procurement. The goal is to create cascading compliance pressure through the global banking network. Treasury expects that the threat of losing access to U.S. correspondent relationships will motivate banks in third countries to stop supporting Russia’s military‑industrial complex even before sanctions are applied.
- In answer to questions about enforcement, a senior administration official says Treasury will work with U.S. and European banks over the coming weeks to inform them about the EO and the need to warn correspondent banks in third countries.
- A senior administration official explains that American and European financial institutions generally no longer do much direct business with Russia but continue to serve as correspondent banks for institutions in third‑country jurisdictions that may facilitate sanctioned trade.
- A senior administration official states that banks whose clients ship goods to Russia must either ensure that these goods do not reach Russia’s military‑industrial complex or cease such business or risk being cut off from the U.S. financial system.
- A senior administration official reiterates that the overarching goal is to put sand in the gears of Russia’s supply chain, and that leveraging correspondent banking relationships is a key part of this strategy.
#### Status of U.S. and European banks’ compliance
In response to a question about whether American or European institutions are already in violation, officials say they are not aware of any such cases. They argue that Western firms have largely withdrawn from direct Russian business, as reflected in negative foreign direct investment data. The primary concern now lies with banks in third‑country jurisdictions that maintain ties to Russia and may help it circumvent sanctions. Treasury intends to use the new EO and cooperation with Western correspondent banks to pressure those third‑country banks to change behavior or face consequences.
- Responding to Alan Rappeport, a senior administration official says they are not aware of any American or European financial institutions that have facilitated the targeted transactions in a way that would violate the new regime.
- A senior administration official cites a Treasury economists’ report showing that Russia has experienced negative foreign direct investment since the war, as companies and financial institutions have been pulling out.
- A senior administration official explains that the main concern is with banks in third‑country jurisdictions that do business with Russia and maintain correspondent relationships with U.S. and European banks.
- A senior administration official reiterates that those third‑country banks risk losing correspondent ties if they fail to prevent transactions that further Russia’s military‑industrial complex.
#### Need for continued U.S. support to Ukraine
Officials repeatedly tie sanctions policy to the broader requirement to sustain Ukraine’s defense and economy. They stress that economic pressure on Russia is only one tool, and that Ukraine needs continued military and financial assistance, which depends on congressional action on supplemental funding. They present sanctions and export controls as slowing Russia down, while direct aid helps Ukraine “speed up” and maintain resilience. This linkage underscores that the EO complements but cannot replace appropriated support.
- A senior administration official underscores that sanctions and export controls are one tool of a broader strategy that must be paired with essential economic, military, and humanitarian support for Ukraine.
- A senior administration official states that for Ukrainians to speed up and go faster on the battlefield, they need continued U.S. support, which requires Congress to approve supplemental funding.
- A senior administration official links the new EO to the overall effort to hold Russia accountable while maintaining Ukraine’s ability to defend itself and sustain its economy.
#### Seizure of Russian assets and G7 discussions
In response to questions about seizing Russian bank assets, officials confirm that the U.S. is in active discussions with G7 partners about the most appropriate way to ensure Russia compensates Ukraine for war damage. They frame this as an international law obligation and a strategic objective: Russia should not control when or whether it pays. However, they state there is no change to the U.S. position on outright asset seizure at this time. The topic remains under consideration within broader diplomatic and legal deliberations.
- Answering Aamer, a senior administration official confirms ongoing G7 discussions about how to ensure Russia does not get to choose when it repays Ukraine for the damage it has caused.
- A senior administration official stresses that ensuring Russia compensates Ukraine is both an international law obligation and a strategic aim of U.S. policy.
- A senior administration official says there is no current change in the U.S. position on seizing Russian assets, though the issue remains under active discussion with partners and allies.
- A senior administration official reiterates that the United States seeks ways to engage Russia to cease its illegal aggression while also ensuring accountability for damages.
