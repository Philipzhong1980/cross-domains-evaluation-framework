### Overview
Senior administration officials detailed the United States' ongoing strategy to counter Russia's invasion of Ukraine through comprehensive sanctions, export controls, and military support. Central to the discussion was the announcement of a new executive order, set to be signed by President Biden, which introduces secondary sanctions targeting financial institutions that facilitate transactions supporting Russia's military industrial base. This measure aims to disrupt Russia's supply chains more effectively and is coordinated with G7 partners, aligning with recent EU sanctions. Officials highlighted the significant impact of existing sanctions on Russia's military capabilities and economy, noting challenges such as equipment losses and economic isolation. They also addressed efforts to combat sanctions evasion through front companies and financial intermediaries. Engagement with US and European financial institutions is a priority to ensure compliance and prevent facilitation of prohibited transactions. The officials underscored the need for supplemental congressional funding to support Ukraine and acknowledged ongoing international discussions regarding Russian asset seizures, without announcing policy changes. Overall, the briefing emphasized a multifaceted approach combining economic pressure, international coordination, and diplomatic efforts to support Ukraine and hold Russia accountable.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### US Strategy Against Russia
The United States has maintained a comprehensive strategy since Russia's invasion of Ukraine nearly two years ago, focusing on imposing costs on Russia through military aid to Ukraine, financial and humanitarian assistance, and economic sanctions. The strategy aims to deny Russia access to the international financial system and restrict its ability to acquire materials and technology necessary for its military aggression. This approach is critical to holding Russia accountable and supporting Ukraine's defense efforts.
- Senior administration official states the US strategy centers on denying Russia access to international finance and critical military materials.
- The US has imposed sanctions and export controls on thousands of entities aiding Russia's military.
- Efforts include global engagement with governments and financial institutions to enforce sanctions risks.
#### New Executive Order on Sanctions
President Biden plans to sign a new executive order to strengthen US sanctions authorities targeting financial facilitators of Russia's war efforts. This order introduces secondary sanctions against financial institutions that enable transactions supporting Russia's military industrial base, marking a significant escalation in the US's economic pressure on Russia. The measure aligns with commitments made by G7 leaders to curtail Russia's military expansion and revenue sources.
- Senior administration official announces the upcoming executive order focusing on financial facilitators for Russia's war.
- The order will disrupt material support to Russia's defense industrial base and obstruct military capacity expansion.
- The action is coordinated with G7 partners, reflecting a unified international stance.
#### Impact of Sanctions on Russia's Economy
Sanctions and export controls have significantly impacted Russia's military capabilities and economy. Russia has lost over 13,000 military equipment pieces and faces production constraints due to restricted access to foreign components and labor shortages. Economic pressures include a 40% drop in energy revenues and negative foreign direct investment, leading Russia to convert into a war economy focused on weapons production at the expense of its broader economic health.
- Treasury official notes Russia's military losses and struggles to replenish equipment.
- Russia's defense spending increased by 75% while energy revenues dropped by 40% in 2023.
- Russia faces a brain drain and economic isolation, with a 5% smaller economy than predicted pre-war.
- The Kremlin has redirected manufacturing and labor towards wartime production, weakening the domestic economy.
#### Sanctions Evasion and Financial Facilitation
The Kremlin has actively sought to evade sanctions by creating front companies and using financial intermediaries to procure critical military components. The new executive order empowers the US to target financial institutions that facilitate these transactions, providing a strong deterrent against enabling Russia's military industrial complex. This tool introduces secondary sanctions to hold financial actors accountable and disrupt Russia's supply chains more effectively.
- Officials describe Kremlin efforts to circumvent sanctions via cutouts and front companies.
- The executive order allows targeting financial institutions that fail to prevent shipments to Russia's military complex.
- This is the first use of secondary sanctions against financial institutions in this conflict.
- The US has engaged with financial institutions globally to emphasize compliance and accountability.
#### Alignment with International Partners
The US sanctions and export control measures are closely aligned with actions taken by the European Union, the United Kingdom, and other G7 partners. The executive order complements the EU's 12th sanctions package, including measures on Russian-origin products like non-industrial diamonds. This coordinated approach strengthens the global effort to isolate Russia economically and limit its war-fighting capabilities.
- Officials confirm the executive order aligns with the EU's 12th sanctions package and UK actions.
- The order includes bans on products substantially transformed outside Russia, such as diamonds.
- Over 250 individual targets have been sanctioned this month consistent with allied measures.
- Coordination with international partners is central to the sanctions strategy.
#### Engagement with Financial Institutions
US and European financial institutions largely do not conduct direct business with Russia but serve as correspondent banks for other institutions that may facilitate transactions benefiting Russia's military. The US plans to work closely with these banks to ensure they enforce due diligence and prevent their networks from supporting Russia's military industrial base. The executive order serves as a clear warning that failure to comply risks losing access to the US financial system.
- Officials state no known US or European banks are currently violating sanctions by facilitating transactions.
- Many banks act as correspondent banks for institutions in third countries that may be involved in sanction evasion.
- The US will inform and collaborate with banks to prevent facilitation of Russia's military supply chain.
- Banks face a stark choice: enforce compliance or risk exclusion from the US financial system.
#### Supplemental Funding for Ukraine and Asset Discussions
Officials emphasize the necessity of supplemental funding from Congress to support Ukraine's military and economic needs. Discussions continue among G7 partners regarding the appropriate handling of Russian assets to ensure compensation for Ukraine, but no changes in US policy on asset seizure have been announced. The broader strategy includes diplomatic efforts to end Russia's illegal aggression alongside economic measures.
- Officials stress the importance of congressional approval for supplemental Ukraine funding.
- Active discussions are ongoing about seizing Russian assets to compensate Ukraine.
- No new US position on asset seizure has been announced at this time.
- Diplomatic engagement remains a key component of the overall strategy.
