### Overview
  Senior administration officials briefed reporters on a new executive order President Biden plans to sign tomorrow that strengthens U.S. sanctions authorities to target financial facilitators of Russia’s military industrial base. Officials (including Treasury representatives) explain the EO introduces secondary sanctions for the first time in this conflict and takes effect immediately upon signing, with the aim of forcing banks to perform due diligence or cease transactions that enable Russia’s weapons production. They frame the measure as part of a broader, allied approach—aligned with recent G7 and EU actions and complemented by prior U.S. packages—that builds on evidence the coalition’s economic tools have already degraded Russian military capacity and revenues. Officials say the administration will engage U.S. and European banks to warn correspondent partners, report no known U.S./European firms currently violating these standards, and note ongoing multilateral discussions (including on the treatment of seized assets) but announce no change in U.S. policy on asset seizure at this time.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### U.S. sanctions strategy
  Senior administration officials outline a sustained U.S. strategy of using sanctions, export controls, military aid, and diplomatic engagement to hold Russia accountable for its invasion of Ukraine. They emphasize denying Russia access to the international financial system and preventing it from acquiring materials and technology needed for its military industrial base. The officials frame sanctions as one prong of a broader effort that also requires congressional funding for continued U.S. support to Ukraine.
- Senior administration official says the United States has used military, financial, humanitarian, and economic tools since Russia’s invasion to impose costs and support Ukraine.
- Senior administration official states the core of U.S. strategy is denying Russia access to the international financial system and blocking equipment and technology needed for its war.
- Senior administration official notes sanctions and export controls are one element of a broader strategy that also depends on congressional supplemental funding for Ukraine.
  #### New executive order announced
  Officials announce that President Biden intends to sign a new executive order aimed at strengthening U.S. sanctions authorities against Russia with a specific focus on financial facilitators supporting Russia’s war. They describe the EO as a further step to disrupt material support for Russia’s defense industrial base and to obstruct its efforts to increase military capacity. The signing is scheduled for the following day and officials present the EO as part of coordinated action with allies.
- Senior administration official announces that President Biden intends to sign a new executive order tomorrow to strengthen sanctions authorities against Russia.
- Senior administration official says the EO focuses on financial facilitators who materially support Russia’s defense industrial base.
- Senior administration official frames the EO as coordinated with partners and aligned with recent G7 commitments.
  #### Objective: choke financial access
  Officials emphasize the tactical objective of choking off revenue and inputs to Russia’s war effort by targeting the financial chokepoints that facilitate transfers of goods to the Russian military industrial base. They explain the EO provides a tool to go after financial institutions that knowingly or negligently enable such transactions. This measure is intended to disincentivize banks and intermediaries from facilitating sanctioned trade into Russia.
- Senior administration official explains the EO is designed to target the financial chokepoint that enables transfers of goods to Russia’s military industrial complex.
- Senior administration official says the tool will force financial institutions to choose either to conduct proper due diligence or stop supporting transactions that further Russia’s war efforts.
- Senior administration official describes the EO as a new ability to hold financial institutions accountable for facilitating material support to Russia’s defense sector.
  #### Economic impact on Russia
  A Treasury official summarizes the cumulative economic effects of sanctions and export controls, highlighting measurable impacts on Russia’s military and broader economy. They cite losses of military equipment, declines in energy revenue tied to price caps, negative foreign direct investment, immigration-driven brain drain, and a reorientation of Russia into a wartime economy with long-term costs. Officials portray these pressures as evidence the U.S. and coalition measures are degrading Russia’s capacity to sustain and replenish its forces.
- Treasury official reports that since February 2022 Russia’s military has lost over 13,000 pieces of equipment, including tanks, UAVs, and missile systems.
- Treasury official says Kremlin defense spending rose nearly 75 percent in the first half of 2023 while energy revenues dropped about 40 percent this year due to the allied price cap.
- Treasury official states Russia’s economy is roughly 5 percent smaller than projected prior to the war and is underperforming other oil-exporting countries.
- Treasury official warns that Russia has reoriented toward a war economy, which imposes painful long-term trade-offs from underinvestment and labor shortages.
  #### Sanctions evasion and supply chains
  Officials describe how the Kremlin has attempted to evade sanctions by using front companies, cutouts, and financial intermediaries to procure critical components. They identify targeted items such as semiconductors, machine tools, chemical precursors, ball bearings, and optical systems that are essential to weapons production. The coalition has exposed and sanctioned hundreds of middlemen and front companies over the past year, and the EO is presented as the next tool to address the financial side of those evasion networks.
- Senior administration official says the Kremlin directed intelligence services to build cutouts and front companies to evade multilateral sanctions and export controls.
- Senior administration official lists semiconductors, machine tools, chemical precursors, ball bearings, and optical systems as key items Russia seeks to import.
- Senior administration official reports that the global coalition has sanctioned or listed hundreds of front companies, middlemen, and Russian companies involved in evasion.
- Senior administration official states the EO enables the U.S. to target financial institutions that facilitate those sanction-evading transactions.
  #### Secondary sanctions authority
  Officials explain that the EO introduces a secondary sanctions mechanism for the first time in this conflict, allowing the U.S. to sanction financial institutions that facilitate Russia’s military procurement. They characterize the authority as a 'surgical' tool that takes effect immediately upon the President’s signing and is intended to compel banks to cease risky business or face sanctions. Officials emphasize that the goal is deterrence and that they expect many banks to change behavior to preserve access to the U.S. financial system.
- Senior administration official says the EO introduces secondary sanctions to go after financial institutions for the first time during this conflict.
- Senior administration official confirms the financial facilitators provisions take effect immediately when the President signs the EO.
- Senior administration official calls the authority a 'surgical tool' meant to compel banks to stop enabling transfers that support Russia’s defense industrial base.
- Senior administration official says officials expect many banks will proactively comply to avoid jeopardizing access to the U.S. financial system.
  #### Coordination with allies
  Officials stress coordination with the G7, EU, and U.K., noting alignment with the G7 leaders’ statement and the EU’s 12th sanctions package. They say the United States has already adopted many parallel measures and that the EO contains components—such as banning imports of Russian-origin products substantially transformed abroad—that mirror recent EU steps on items like non-industrial diamonds. The administration frames the EO as complementary to allied measures and part of a joint effort to close loopholes.
- Senior administration official states the EO aligns with the G7 leaders’ statement and the EU’s 12th package.
- Senior administration official notes the U.S. has already issued a sanctions package this month covering many of the same categories as the EU.
- Senior administration official explains the EO enables bans on Russian-origin items that are substantially transformed abroad, consistent with recent EU diamond measures.
  #### Engagement with banks
  Officials describe planned engagement with U.S. and European banks to warn correspondent partners and encourage due diligence to prevent facilitation of sanctioned trade. They highlight correspondent banking relationships as an enforcement leverage point because many third-country banks rely on U.S. and European connections for access to global markets. The administration intends to use outreach and the threat of sanction exposure to induce banks to stop enabling supply chains that feed Russia’s military.
- Senior administration official says Treasury will work with U.S. and European banks to inform them about the EO and warn correspondent partners.
- Senior administration official asserts that most banks will choose to preserve access to the U.S. financial system rather than risk sanctions for limited business with Russia.
- Senior administration official describes correspondent relationships as a chokepoint that the U.S. plans to leverage to deter sanction evasion.
  #### Implications and asset-seizure discussions
  In response to questions, officials say they are not aware of American or European firms currently violating these standards, but acknowledge third-country banks with concerning activity often have relationships with U.S./European banks that could be at risk. On the question of seizing Russian assets to compensate Ukraine, officials say discussions are ongoing with G7 partners but they have no change in U.S. policy to announce. They reiterate that multilateral coordination remains central to enforcing and expanding these measures.
- Senior administration official says they are not aware of U.S. or European financial firms currently facilitating transactions that would trigger the new sanctions.
- Senior administration official cautions that third-country banks engaged in risky activity may be exposed through their relationships with U.S. and European correspondent banks.
- Senior administration official confirms that discussions on seizing Russian assets are active among G7 partners but that there is no new U.S. announcement on changing policy.
