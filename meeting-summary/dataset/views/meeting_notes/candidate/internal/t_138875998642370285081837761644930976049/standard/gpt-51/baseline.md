### Overview
The Mountain View City Council’s January 10, 2023 joint meeting with the Shoreline Regional Park Community serves primarily as an in-person and hybrid-format reorganization session featuring extensive recognitions and leadership transitions. After briefly convening and later reporting out from a closed session where City Attorney Jennifer Logue announces the unanimous denial of two liability claims, Mayor Lucas Ramirez outlines new hybrid participation procedures and then presents a detailed proclamation honoring former Council Member Sally Lieber’s two-year term and broader public service, with council colleagues and community members—including Supervisor Joe Simitian and multiple local advocates—praising her environmental leadership, housing and equity work, and deep compassion as she departs for the California Board of Equalization. The Council then pivots to recognize outgoing Mayor Ramirez, with Vice Mayor Alison Hicks reading a proclamation highlighting his 2022 leadership on housing approvals, affordable housing master plans, social safety net investments, small business recovery, and downtown activation, and colleagues commending his analytical rigor, collaborative style, humor, and meeting management; Ramirez responds with remarks that largely credit staff across all departments and share his key lesson that a mayor’s true power lies in convening stakeholders. After City Clerk Heather Glaser administers the oath of office to re-elected Council Members Hicks, Ellen Kamei, and Ramirez, each offers brief remarks on their priorities and gratitude to supporters, and visiting officials such as Congresswoman Anna Eshoo, Foothill-De Anza Board President Patrick Ahrens, and high school trustee Thida Cornes extend congratulations while underscoring ongoing partnerships on affordable housing, student supports, safe routes to school, and infrastructure funding. The Council then unanimously elects Hicks as Mayor and Pat Showalter as Vice Mayor, with Kamei and Margaret Abe-Koga citing their planning and environmental credentials, decades-long community involvement, and commitment to climate action and housing, followed by Showalter’s pledge to uphold the Council’s tradition of civility and collaborative, regionally engaged decision-making and Hicks’s inaugural remarks framing the first in-person hybrid meeting as an opportunity to reengage residents on priorities ranging from downtown pedestrianization, climate acceleration, safe parking, biodiversity, and tree and park planning to active transportation and community-controlled affordable housing. Routine business, including a unanimously approved consent calendar authorizing continued virtual meetings under AB 361 and amending bus-only lanes on Charleston Road, and a brief oral communication request from community leader Maria Marroquin for a moratorium on 72-hour parking enforcement against vehicle dwellers during storms, rounds out the agenda before Mayor Hicks adjourns at 8:03 PM and invites attendees to a reception, signaling both continuity and renewal in Mountain View’s civic leadership and engagement.

The meeting opens with Mayor Lucas Ramirez announcing two closed session items related to liability claims by Paul Mansourian and Vijay Kumar under Government Code Section 54956.9. The City Attorney describes the claims and invites public comment on these items. No members of the public speak, either in person or virtually. The Council then recesses to closed session before returning later for regular business.

#### Hybrid meeting logistics and public participation
Upon reconvening, Mayor Ramirez opens the joint meeting of the City Council and Shoreline Regional Park Community and notes it is the first in-person meeting in almost three years and the first hybrid-format meeting. He outlines new procedures for sharing audio/video content, using blue speaker cards for in-person speakers, and accessing virtual participation instructions on the agenda. The focus is on setting expectations and ensuring the public understands how to participate effectively in the new hybrid environment.
- Mayor Ramirez explains that this is the first in-person meeting in the council chambers in almost three years and the first time meetings are held in a hybrid format.
- Mayor Ramirez clarifies that audio and video presentations can no longer be shared from the lectern and must be submitted in advance to city.clerk@mountainview.gov by 4:30 PM on the meeting date.
- Mayor Ramirez announces that in-person speakers must use individual blue speaker cards for each item instead of lining up at the lectern.
- Mayor Ramirez notes that virtual participation instructions are available on the posted agenda so remote attendees can address the Council.
#### Proclamation honoring former Council Member Sally Lieber
The Council presents a formal proclamation recognizing former Council Member Sally J. Lieber’s two years on the current Council and her broader record of public service as she joins the California Board of Equalization. Mayor Ramirez reads a detailed proclamation highlighting her committee work, regional representation, and policy leadership on gun safety, climate action, reproductive rights, housing, equity, and protections for vulnerable communities. The proclamation underscores her impact on housing approvals, climate adaptation, and social justice programs, and formally expresses the City’s appreciation for her dedicated service.
- Mayor Ramirez notes that former Council Member Lieber was elected to the California Board of Equalization in November 2022 and ended her City Council service on December 30, 2022.
- Mayor Ramirez highlights that Lieber chaired the Finance Committee and several other council committees, including the Interim Castro Street Pedestrian Mall Ad Hoc Committee to support downtown economic recovery.
- Mayor Ramirez states that Lieber represented Mountain View regionally on bodies such as the San Francisco Bay Restoration Authority, Silicon Valley Animal Control Authority, and the Moffett Field Restoration Authority Board.
- Mayor Ramirez credits Lieber with supporting local gun safety ordinances and legislative actions to further restrict assault weapons, regulate non-serialized firearms, promote safe firearm storage, and limit firearm marketing to children.
- Mayor Ramirez describes Lieber’s support for climate action, including her role in helping create the San Francisco Bay Restoration Authority to advance regional climate adaptation and support Mountain View’s carbon neutrality goals.
- Mayor Ramirez emphasizes Lieber’s advocacy for reproductive freedom, including her support of California Proposition 1 to enshrine reproductive rights in the state constitution.
- Mayor Ramirez reports that during Lieber’s service, 2,479 new housing units were approved (654 affordable) and 4,978 units were built (559 affordable), alongside major master plan approvals in North Bayshore and Middlefield Park and approval of a city lot lease for affordable housing.
- Mayor Ramirez notes that Lieber supported policies for affordable housing, mobile home rent stabilization, wage theft prevention, responsible construction, disability-focused budget equity, the Elevate MV guaranteed basic income pilot, and COVID-19 relief and ARPA funding for vulnerable residents.
- The proclamation concludes by formally expressing the City Council’s great appreciation to Sally J. Lieber for her two years of dedicated service on the Mountain View City Council.

- Vice Mayor Hicks reports that community members asked her to do anything possible to keep Lieber on the Council, even though this was not feasible.

Lieber notes the important role of local activists in pushing the Council on issues like bicycling and the environment and commits to making every meeting count at the State Board of Equalization while remaining an active presence in Mountain View due to her proximity and deep affection for the community.

- Lieber says she felt both impressed and exhausted listening to the long list of accomplishments, but stresses that it reflects a team effort among council and staff rather than individual achievement.
- Lieber praises Mountain View staff as wonderful and incredibly supportive, highlighting their policy advice and technical knowledge for both the full council and committee work.
- Lieber notes that each council member has done ‘top notch’ things for the community and states that Mountain View deserves the best.
- Lieber describes Mountain View residents as highly engaged and ‘wonkish,’ citing strong participation and support for public radio and public television, and emphasizes the value of activists who ask tough questions on topics like bicycling and the environment.
- Lieber explains that she now represents 2.2 million Californians on the State Board of Equalization and pledges to make every meeting count as she did on the City Council.
- Lieber reminds attendees that she lives only a block away from City Hall and promises not to be a stranger to the chambers or the community.

#### Oral communications: vehicle dwellers and parking enforcement
During oral communications on non-agenda items, community leader Maria Marroquin raises concerns about the impact of heavy storms and the 72-hour parking rule on residents living in vehicles. She asks the Council to consider a moratorium on enforcing the requirement to move vehicles every 72 hours, arguing that the rule exacerbates stress and hardship for people already in precarious situations. Although the Council cannot act immediately due to Brown Act constraints on non-agendized items, her comments signal ongoing community concern about enforcement policies affecting vehicle dwellers during emergencies.
- Maria Marroquin thanks the Council and expresses excitement at seeing them in person again before turning to the challenges faced by people living in cars during storms and heavy rain.
- Marroquin requests a moratorium on enforcement of the 72-hour parking rule for vehicle dwellers, citing the current emergency conditions and associated stress.
- Marroquin argues that requiring vehicle residents to move 1,000 feet every 72 hours increases pain and suffering for people already struggling in Mountain View.
- Marroquin asks the Council to keep this issue in mind and do all that is within their power, even if not immediately.

#### Incoming Mayor Hicks’s vision and priorities
Mayor Alison Hicks concludes the reorganization with her inaugural remarks, framing the moment as a major milestone: the first in-person meeting in almost three years and the City’s first hybrid meeting. She emphasizes the benefits of hybrid participation for residents, invites feedback on the new format, and articulates a vision of city life rooted in livability and everyday experiences like walking to visit friends or access amenities. She connects this to substantive policy goals, including accelerating climate action, expanding housing and anti-displacement measures, enhancing downtown as a pedestrian-friendly space, updating parks and tree planning, and advancing active transportation and community-controlled affordable housing. Her comments set an agenda focused on both serious policy challenges and making Mountain View a city that people genuinely enjoy living in.
- Mayor Hicks notes that this is the City’s first in-person meeting in nearly three years and the first hybrid meeting, observing that all appointees and many staff are new to the public in a physical setting.
- She thanks members of the public for attending despite storm-related flooding and acknowledges that prior attempts to resume in-person meetings were derailed by pandemic surges and major weather events.
- Hicks highlights hybrid and virtual meetings as one of the few positive outcomes of COVID-19, saying residents have told her the format will help them participate more easily in city governance.
- She welcomes residents from Mountain View, the Bay Area, and beyond, including her 94-year-old mother in Petaluma and 93-year-old mother-in-law in Turkey, illustrating the broader reach of hybrid access.
- Hicks invites ongoing feedback on what works and does not work in the hybrid system, recognizing that the city will ‘stumble’ as it refines the new approach.
- She expresses excitement at being Mayor as the City resumes in-person civic life and is able to refocus on long-delayed priorities beyond immediate pandemic response.
- Hicks recounts asking friends and family in a small Sierra foothills town whether they prefer city or country life and notes that most, including a 12-year-old, said the city, citing the ease of seeing friends, accessing entertainment, and walking to amenities.
- She argues that while the Council tackles serious issues like housing affordability, homelessness, and climate change, its core mission is also to make the city a place where people love to live and can enjoy everyday urban life.
- Hicks lists priority topics she expects on upcoming agendas: accelerating climate action, creating a downtown pedestrian mall, nurturing small businesses via the Economic Vitality Plan, expanding safe parking, preserving downtown history, managing biodiversity and wildlife, building affordable housing and combating displacement, improving the Community Tree Master Plan, updating the parks and open space plan, advancing an active transportation plan, and pursuing community-controlled affordable housing models.
- Hicks closes by again thanking those who came through the rain and invites residents to join future meetings in person or virtually to offer input on these issues.
#### Closed session outcomes: liability claims decisions
At the end of the meeting, City Attorney Jennifer Logue reports out from closed session on two liability claims addressed earlier in the evening. The Council denies both claims: one from Paul Mansourian alleging unlawful seizure related to a DUI arrest and another from Vijay Kumar seeking damages tied to incorrect mail forwarding by the U.S. Post Office. The votes are unanimous in both cases, and the announcements satisfy legal reporting requirements for closed session actions.
- City Attorney Logue reports that in closed session the Council took final action on the liability claim filed by Paul Mansourian alleging violation of his Fourth Amendment rights when he was taken into custody for driving under the influence, with claimed damages of $450,000.
- Logue states that the Council voted with six ayes, zero noes, and zero abstentions to deny Mansourian’s claim.
- Logue also reports that the Council took final action on a liability claim filed by Vijay Kumar, who sought millions of dollars in damages related to the U.S. Post Office incorrectly forwarding his mail to someone with a similar name in Florida.
- She explains that the Council again voted six to zero with no abstentions to deny Kumar’s claim.
