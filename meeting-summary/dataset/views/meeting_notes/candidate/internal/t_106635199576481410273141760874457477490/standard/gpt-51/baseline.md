### Overview
The Rental Housing Committee meets via videoconference, approves routine consent items, and then holds a focused debate on whether to continue remote meetings under AB 361, with Committee Member Pardo Dazella unsuccessfully pushing for an immediate hybrid format and the majority voting 4–1 to extend virtual meetings due to ongoing state and local emergencies and City IT readiness. A member of the public, Steven, reinforces that decision by citing continued COVID‑19 risks and inadequate ventilation in the Council chamber. Staff then presents the September 2022 status report, highlighting high inquiry and mediation volumes, significant bilingual service use, stable but differentiated vacancy rates, rising rents above pre‑COVID levels, and an uptick in tenant petitions, with committee members requesting more longitudinal data and expressing enthusiasm for forthcoming mobile‑home and registry‑compliance metrics. The committee unanimously adopts new CSFRA and MHRSO hearing‑procedure amendments that create a structured compliance‑hearing process for disputes over implementation of final decisions, assign landlords the burden of proving compliance, preserve appeals, and standardize a 30‑day rent‑restoration notice. Housing staff member Andrea delivers an in‑depth briefing on the Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance and SB 330, explaining eligibility thresholds, benefits, and first‑right‑of‑return rules and walking the committee through several redevelopment and rehabilitation projects involving CSFRA units, including Charleston, Gamel Way, El Camino, Mariposa, Rengstorff, and Middlefield, with data on total payments and tenant relocation destinations. Finally, staff presents a draft 2023 work plan featuring RUBS and tenant master‑tenant issues as key regulatory topics; after feedback from public commenter Joshua Howard and several members about workload, stakeholder engagement, and transparency, the committee agrees to keep RUBS early in the year, move the tenant/sub‑tenant topic to the second half, and add a June work‑plan review while staff continues extensive outreach through the Housing and Eviction Help Center, workshops, and community events before adjourning to its next scheduled meeting on November 14, 2022.

#### Compliance procedures for hearing decisions
The committee considers and adopts regulatory amendments to CSFRA Chapter 5 and MHRSO Chapter 6 to establish a formal process for resolving disputes over compliance with final hearing decisions. Staff explains that while existing rules cover petitions, hearings, and appeals, they do not specify how to address disagreements about whether a landlord has actually implemented ordered remedies. The new process allows either party to request a compliance determination, clarifies burdens of proof, preserves appeal rights, and standardizes a 30‑day notice requirement before rent restoration, aligning with state law noticing rules.
- Staff member Patricia Henslow explains that current hearing procedures under CSFRA and MHRSO require written decisions and provide for appeals but lack a mechanism to resolve post‑decision disputes about compliance, such as whether repairs were completed or back rent was paid.
- Patricia Henslow outlines the new process, under which any party to a downward adjustment petition may request a compliance hearing on a designated form, with supporting documentation required, and staff will notify all affected parties within 10 days.
- Under the amendments, if no party objects, the hearing officer may decide compliance issues administratively on the written record; if any party objects, a full compliance hearing is scheduled and the officer takes evidence from all sides.
- Staff clarifies that the landlord bears the burden of proving compliance with the prior order, since the original decision directed the landlord to act.
- The hearing officer’s compliance decision may either confirm compliance and identify the date and extent of compliance—allowing rent to be restored—or find continuing noncompliance and order continued rent reductions or other equitable remedies.
- Patricia Henslow states that compliance decisions will be appealable to the Rental Housing Committee under existing CSFRA and MHRSO appeal procedures, preserving due process.
- Staff adds that hearing officers will now be required to include in all future downward adjustment decisions a requirement that landlords give tenants 30 days’ written notice before restoring rent and information about the new compliance‑hearing option.
- Chair Nicole Haynes questions whether the 30‑day notice requirement is consistent with earlier committee positions that hearing‑ordered rent reductions are distinct from concessions; staff and counsel respond that the noticing requirement derives from state rent‑increase law and has already been standard practice in hearing orders.
- Vice Chair Emily Ramos moves to adopt the amendments to CSFRA Regulations Chapter 5 and MHRSO Regulations Chapter 6 as presented, and Committee Member Almond seconds the motion.
- The committee approves the compliance‑procedures amendments on a 4–0–1 vote, with Committee Member Pardo Dazella abstaining.
#### Update on Tenant Relocation Assistance and SB 330
Staff delivers a comprehensive update on the City’s Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance (TRAIL) and the Housing Crisis Act of 2019 (SB 330), detailing eligibility rules, benefit structures, and how both frameworks apply to recent and ongoing redevelopment projects. The presentation explains that CSFRA and mobile‑home tenants displaced for specific just‑cause reasons may receive relocation payments, special‑circumstance supplements, advisory support, and first‑right‑of‑return, while SB 330 imposes additional obligations when protected units are demolished for new housing, including 42‑month rent differentials and affordable replacement units. Staff walks through concrete project examples (Charleston, Gamel Way, El Camino, Mariposa, Rankin, and Middlefield), highlighting total payments, where tenants moved, and the declining number of CSFRA units being demolished since SB 330 took effect.
- Staff member Andrea explains that the City’s Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance (TRAIL) applies to CSFRA‑covered and MHRSO mobile home units subject to certain just‑cause terminations, and in some cases to other rentals where three or more units on a parcel are vacated in a year for demolition, withdrawal, or major rehabilitation.
- Andrea notes that to qualify under TRAIL, a household’s income must not exceed 120% of Area Median Income (AMI) plus $5,000, and she displays a chart showing the resulting income caps by household size (for example, $207,200 for a household of four).
- Under TRAIL, eligible households receive a full security‑deposit refund (minus necessary repair costs), access to a rental‑listing subscription service, a cash relocation payment equal to three months of median rent for comparable units, an inflation‑adjusted special‑circumstance payment (about $8,503 in 2022) for seniors, persons with disabilities, or households with dependent minors, and a first right of return if the original unit returns to the rental market.
- Andrea outlines that SB 330 applies when existing “protected units” are demolished in order to build new housing, defining protected units as those under local rent stabilization, subject to affordability covenants, occupied by low‑income households (≤80% AMI), or withdrawn under the Ellis Act within the past 10 years.
- For SB 330, Andrea explains that eligible households must have incomes at or below 80% AMI and are entitled to 42 months of rent differential (based on the lower of 30% of income or prior rent versus comparable replacement rent), intensive relocation advisory services, moving‑expense assistance, and a right to rent or buy an affordable unit in the new development.
- Andrea presents a comparative chart showing that when both TRAIL and SB 330 apply, income‑eligible households (≤80% AMI) receive the stricter or cumulative benefits from both systems—for example, 42 months of rent differential under SB 330 plus TRAIL’s special‑circumstance and deposit‑refund provisions as applicable.
- Andrea notes that since SB 330 took effect in 2020, redevelopment projects that demolish CSFRA units have declined, as developers must replace protected units with affordable ones, which may reduce project profitability unless unit counts increase substantially.
- For 1851 Charleston Road, Andrea reports that four CSFRA units will be demolished to build a parking lot serving a Google office project; the site is subject only to TRAIL because no housing will be built, and the current tenant vacate date is June 1, 2023.
- Regarding 1919–1945 Gamel Way, Andrea explains that 29 CSFRA units are slated for demolition to build 121 for‑sale condominiums; the project predates SB 330, so only TRAIL applies, but the developer voluntarily agreed to benefits mirroring SB 330 as part of purchasing the public street, and the project is currently on economic hold with vacate dates being extended.
- For 870 E. El Camino Real, Andrea states that 42 of 180 CSFRA units will be demolished and replaced with 233 new rentals; both TRAIL and SB 330 apply, requiring affordable replacement units and providing returning rights for income‑eligible households, though no termination notices have yet been issued after City Council’s recent project approval.
- Andrea describes the 660 Mariposa rehabilitation project, noting that while it is not formally subject to TRAIL, the developer temporarily relocated tenants during unit upgrades with multiple options (on‑site renovated units, extended‑stay hotels, or direct payments), covered moving/storage costs, guaranteed first right of return, and froze rent increases until one year after the last tenant’s return, with future increases constrained to 4% annually.
- For 570 S. Rengstorff Avenue, Andrea reports that 70 CSFRA units were demolished for 92 row homes; 40 households qualified for TRAIL assistance under the 2018 rules, receiving a total of about $617,612, and most relocated within Mountain View or the broader Bay Area.
- Andrea notes that at 777 W. Middlefield Road, 208 CSFRA units were demolished for a 716‑unit apartment complex; the developer provided enhanced TRAIL‑based benefits totaling over $1.8 million, with tiered payments of at least $10,000 and higher amounts for lower‑income households, and post‑move data show that many displaced tenants remained in Mountain View or the Bay Area, with some moving out of state or abroad.
- Chair Nicole Haynes asks whether “affordable” homes offered for purchase under SB 330 carry deed restrictions, and Andrea confirms that SB 330’s affordable homeownership units are indeed deed‑restricted.
#### Draft 2023 Rental Housing Committee work plan
Staff presents a draft 2023 work plan outlining recurring items (reports, budget, elections, outreach) and two new regulatory priorities: ratio utility billing systems (RUBS) and tenant/master‑tenant issues. Committee members and a housing‑provider representative discuss workload balancing, stakeholder engagement, and transparency around how regulatory topics are selected. The committee signals support for tackling RUBS early in the year, defers the tenant–subtenant item to the second half, and agrees to formally revisit and, if needed, amend the work plan mid‑year to reflect emerging issues and actual capacity.
- Program Manager Anky van Deursen explains that the annual work plan is a flexible blueprint grouping agenda topics into categories such as as‑needed items (appeals, regulations), monthly reports, quarterly financial updates, and annual actions like budgets, fees, Aga determinations, outreach reviews, legislative updates, and the work plan itself.
- Staff highlights two notable potential 2023 regulatory items: ratio utility billing systems (RUBS) and tenant/master‑tenant lease issues, which have been raised by committee members and in the context of a recent petition case respectively.
- Public commenter Joshua Howard, on behalf of the California Apartment Association, welcomes the explicit listing of RUBS and tenant sub‑tenant regulations in the work plan, calls for structured stakeholder engagement similar to the concessions process, and urges updating the work plan whenever new regulatory topics are added to maintain transparency.
- Joshua Howard also suggests that the committee avoid front‑loading major regulatory items into the first five months of the year to reduce strain on staff, committee members, and the public.
- Chair Nicole Haynes notes that earlier in 2022, heavy agendas led to lengthy meetings and continuances, and she recommends adding more time for RUBS (extending its work across four meetings) and shifting the tenant sub‑tenant study and regulations into later months to avoid compression.
- Vice Chair Emily Ramos supports keeping RUBS early given longstanding committee interest and the prior vote to agendize the topic, but proposes adding a mid‑year work‑plan check‑in so the committee can adjust priorities based on actual experience and emergent issues such as concessions during COVID.
- Committee Member Almond expresses concern that January–March appear particularly dense and emphasizes the need to match agenda scope to realistic meeting duration so complex topics (like relocation and concessions) get adequate attention without routinely pushing items to later meetings.
- Committee Member Grunwald requests that staff look at the work plan from a capacity perspective—using historical data on how long recurring items and appeals take, and aiming for meetings of about three hours—while clarifying how new topics like RUBS and tenant master‑tenant issues rise to the top of the priority list.
- Staff explains that RUBS was formally requested and agendized by the chair and supported by prior committee votes, while the tenant master‑tenant issue stems from a specific petition where both master tenant and tenant were corporate entities and the CSFRA text did not clearly resolve the questions raised.
- Anky van Deursen agrees to revise the draft work plan by moving the tenant/master‑tenant topic to the second half of 2023 and adding a June work‑plan review, and will recirculate the updated plan to the committee.
