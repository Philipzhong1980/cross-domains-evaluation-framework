### Overview
  At this meeting the Human Relations Commission receives a detailed staff briefing on the City’s federal CDBG and HOME programs and reviews proposed policy principles, funding thresholds, and an implementation timeline for the upcoming NOFA cycle. Housing staff (Harsha Ramchandani, Michaela Helman‑Tincher, and Wayne Chen) explain HUD requirements, recent annual grant levels, program income, and eligibility constraints, and they recommend a $7,500 suggested minimum for public service requests (with a $5,000 hard minimum), a $30,000 set‑aside for minor home repairs with up to $5,000 per household, a typical capital‑project threshold of $300,000 (with case‑by‑case review for smaller requests), and directing HOME funds to shovel‑ready pipeline projects rather than issuing a HOME NOFA; staff will issue the NOFA in early December, return recommendations to HRC in March, and seek Council approval in April. Commissioners ask about retrofit eligibility, nonprofit capacity for minor repairs, the City’s affordable housing pipeline (including Crestview, La Avenida, Montecito, Lot 12, and Terra Bella), and how exceptions and discretion will be handled; staff agrees to research retrofit funding options, monitor demand for the repairs program, and present evaluation criteria and project recommendations in March. The meeting also includes subcommittee updates (bystander training and housing/security storytelling work), a recruitment update that Council will interview HRC candidates on November 21, Chair Annette Lin’s announcement of her upcoming resignation to allow timely appointment of a successor, and staff notices about United Against Hate Week and an extension of virtual meetings through December 1. No funding decisions are made at this meeting; staff requests HRC feedback on the proposed policy framework and will return with formal recommendations.

  #### CDBG/HOME program overview
  Staff from Housing and Neighborhood Services present an overview of the federal Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) and HOME Investment Partnership (HOME) programs, the City's consolidated plan requirements, and typical recent funding levels. Harsha Ramchandani explains that the City must submit a five-year consolidated plan and annual action plans to HUD and reports recent annual grants of roughly $620,000 for CDBG and $295,000 for HOME. Staff requests HRC feedback on proposed policy recommendations and previews the upcoming funding cycle.
- Harsha Ramchandani introduces the presentation and requests HRC feedback on proposed policy recommendations for CDBG and HOME.
- Harsha Ramchandani states the City must develop a five-year consolidated plan and annual action plans to receive HUD formula grants.
- Harsha Ramchandani reports recent annual grant amounts of approximately $620,000 for CDBG and $295,000 for HOME.
- Harsha Ramchandani notes that the City also receives program income from repayments of prior CDBG and HOME loans.
  #### Funding uses and NOFA cycles
  Staff outlines eligible uses and typical allocation rules for CDBG and HOME funds and describes NOFA cycles for public services and capital projects. Harsha explains percentage limits cited in the presentation and that the City issues public services NOFAs on a two-year cycle and capital NOFAs typically annually. Staff emphasizes that capital projects often require larger allocations and careful administration due to reporting and monitoring requirements.
- Harsha Ramchandani explains that CDBG allows a portion of funds for public services and that the City issues public services NOFAs on a two-year cycle.
- Harsha Ramchandani states that capital project NOFAs are typically issued annually and that capital projects tend to require larger funding and more intensive monitoring.
- Harsha Ramchandani emphasizes that CDBG and HOME funds have reporting and compliance requirements that influence administration.
  #### City general fund supplement
  Staff notes the City supplements federal funds with an annual General Fund allocation to support programs not eligible for federal funding. Michaela Helman-Tincher states the City budgets $171,000 annually to support human services, educational and cultural arts programs, and that this allocation is coordinated through the NOFA process. The General Fund amount is intended to enhance local services alongside federal grants.
- Michaela Helman-Tincher states the City budgets an annual General Fund appropriation of $171,000 to supplement federal funds.
- Michaela Helman-Tincher says the General Fund allocation is included in the CDBG and HOME NOFA process to support programs not eligible for federal funds.
  #### Policy principles framework
  Staff presents a set of guiding principles to improve CDBG and HOME program implementation, informed by prior HRC feedback, HUD consultants, grant recipients, and best practices. The principles emphasize alignment with the consolidated plan, effective and flexible capital fund administration, and establishing funding thresholds to reduce administrative burden. Staff frames these principles as a policy foundation for the upcoming NOFA cycle.
- Harsha Ramchandani states staff developed principles to guide CDBG and HOME policy recommendations based on prior HRC input and external consultation.
- Harsha Ramchandani says the principles prioritize alignment with the City's consolidated plan goals and effective, flexible administration of capital funds.
- Harsha Ramchandani explains one principle calls for establishing funding thresholds to balance administrative burden and program reach.
  #### Specific policy recommendations
  Staff proposes concrete policy changes for the upcoming NOFA cycle, including funding thresholds and capital allocations. Recommendations include a $7,500 recommended minimum request for public service grants (with a hard floor at $5,000), a $30,000 annual set-aside for minor home repairs providing grants up to $5,000 per household, a typical capital-project minimum threshold of $300,000 (with smaller requests evaluated case-by-case), and a recommendation to allocate HOME funds directly to pipeline affordable housing projects rather than issuing a HOME NOFA. Staff frames these recommendations to enhance administration and leverage other city resources for affordable housing development.
- Harsha Ramchandani recommends a $7,500 minimum grant request for CDBG Public Services grants with a hard minimum of $5,000.
- Harsha Ramchandani proposes setting aside $30,000 of CDBG Capital Projects funding for nonprofits to provide minor home repair grants of up to $5,000 per household.
- Harsha Ramchandani recommends a typical capital-project minimum funding threshold of $300,000 and states requests under that amount will be evaluated case-by-case.
- Harsha Ramchandani recommends not issuing a HOME NOFA and instead working directly with shovel-ready affordable housing projects in the City's pipeline to fill funding gaps.
  #### NOFA and funding timeline

Harsha Ramchandani states the CDBG and General Fund NOFA will be issued in early December 2022, closed in January 2023, evaluated by staff, returned to the HRC in March 2023 for consideration, forwarded to Council for approval in April 2023, submitted to HUD in May 2023, and the program year begins in January 2023. Staff requests HRC feedback in advance of the NOFA cycle.
- Harsha Ramchandani states the NOFA will be issued in early December 2022 and will close by January 2023.
- Harsha Ramchandani says staff will evaluate funding requests and bring public service and capital funding applications with recommendations to the HRC in March 2023.
- Harsha Ramchandani explains City Council will review funding recommendations in April 2023 and staff will submit the Annual Action Plan to HUD in May 2023.

  #### Minor repairs program demand discussion
  Commissioner Jill Rakestraw questions whether the proposed $30,000 minor home repair set-aside is sufficient given community need. Michaela Helman-Tincher explains staff consulted nonprofits that deliver similar services and used their demand estimates to recommend $30,000, and she notes staff can adjust funding if demand justifies it. Wayne Chen adds that nonprofit capacity and regional service patterns influenced the estimate and recommends reintroducing the program this year and monitoring demand for subsequent adjustments.
- Commissioner Jill Rakestraw expresses concern that a $30,000 set-aside at $5,000 per household would serve very few residents.
- Michaela Helman-Tincher explains staff consulted nonprofits about anticipated demand and used that feedback to recommend the $30,000 amount.
- Michaela Helman-Tincher says staff prefers not to allocate funds they cannot spend due to HUD penalties but is open to adjusting the amount in future cycles based on observed demand.
- Wayne Chen adds that nonprofit capacity and regional service delivery patterns were factors in arriving at the $30,000 recommendation.
