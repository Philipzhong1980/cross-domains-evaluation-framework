### Overview
The Human Relations Commission meeting begins with roll call and formal classification of absences under Council Policy K‑02, recording Vice Chair Sylvester’s absence as unexcused and Commissioner Ball’s as excused by unanimous consent. The Commission then approves the October 6, 2022 minutes via roll‑call vote and, with no public attendees, closes the general public comment period. Housing staff—Harsha Ramchandani, Michaela Helman‑Tincher, and Wayne Chen—deliver an in‑depth briefing on the City’s Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) and HOME programs, explaining funding levels, Consolidated Plan priorities, and the roles of public services, capital projects, and General Fund supplements, before outlining proposed policy principles on strategic alignment, flexible capital administration, and minimum grant thresholds. They recommend a $7,500 minimum request for public service grants, a $30,000 CDBG set‑aside for minor home repairs, a $300,000 target minimum for capital projects with case‑by‑case flexibility for smaller proposals, and a shift away from HOME NOFAs toward direct allocations to affordable housing pipeline projects, while commissioners seek assurances on seismic retrofit possibilities, equitable access for diverse developers, and retention of local discretion within HUD rules ahead of the December NOFA launch and March 2023 funding recommendations. In unfinished business, the Bystander Intervention Training Subcommittee reports early work to map existing trainings and develop plain‑language messaging, and the Housing Insecurity Subcommittee describes initial planning with community partner Reach Potential to pair potential events with durable storytelling content about residents experiencing housing instability, while the mental health subcommittee has not yet convened. Under commissioner and staff reports, members hear that there are applicants for all current and anticipated HRC vacancies, that one History of Housing transcript is still being finalized, and that City Council has proclaimed United Against Hate Week with related library programming and has extended virtual meetings through December before planning a return to in‑person sessions in January. The meeting closes with Chair Lin’s announcement that she will resign before the next recruitment deadline due to relocating to San Mateo—drawing appreciation from colleagues for her service and confirmation that Vice Chair Sylvester will chair the December 1 meeting, which will remain virtual.

#### Overview of CDBG and HOME programs and funding structure
Housing staff provide a detailed briefing on the City of Mountain View’s use of federal Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) and HOME Investment Partnerships (HOME) funds. They explain that the City receives formula‑based annual grants from HUD and must maintain a five‑year Consolidated Plan and annual action plans that align expenditures with six priority community goals. Staff outline recent annual funding levels, the role of program income from past loans, and the required allocation splits among public services, administration, and capital projects for both CDBG and HOME. They also note that the City supplements these federal resources with $171,000 in General Fund dollars each year, which is integrated into the same funding process.
- Housing Specialist Harsha Ramchandani states that Mountain View receives approximately $620,000 in CDBG and $295,000 in HOME funds annually, subject to federal budget changes.
- Harsha Ramchandani explains that HUD requires a five‑year Consolidated Plan and annual action plans detailing how CDBG and HOME funds will advance six priority goals.
- Harsha Ramchandani notes that up to 15 percent of CDBG funds may be used for public services, up to 20 percent for administration, and at least 65 percent must go to capital projects.
- Harsha Ramchandani explains that up to 10 percent of HOME funds can be used for administration, with the remainder going to eligible affordable housing activities including new construction and rehabilitation.
- Harsha Ramchandani states that the City also receives CDBG and HOME program income from prior loans to affordable housing developers, which can be reinvested.
- Harsha Ramchandani explains that the City budgets $171,000 annually from the General Fund to support human services, education, cultural arts, and other programs not eligible for federal funds, and that this allocation participates in the same NOFA process as CDBG and HOME.
#### Proposed principles for CDBG and HOME policy framework
Staff propose three overarching principles to guide future CDBG and HOME allocations and improve program implementation. First, all expenditures must directly advance the goals of the City’s Consolidated Plan, reinforcing strategic alignment. Second, the City should administer capital funds flexibly, either through competitive NOFAs or direct allocations to identified priority projects, to respond to low nonprofit demand and the relatively small size of the capital pool. Third, staff recommend establishing minimum funding thresholds due to the significant reporting and monitoring burden associated with federal grants, thereby ensuring that project sizes justify administrative costs.
- Harsha Ramchandani states that the first guiding principle is that all CDBG and HOME expenditures must further the goals of the City’s Consolidated Plan.
- Harsha Ramchandani explains that the second principle focuses on effective and flexible administration of capital project funds, including the option to directly allocate funds to priority affordable housing pipeline projects or to issue a NOFA.
- Harsha Ramchandani notes that nonprofits have shown limited interest in CDBG capital funds in recent years and that Mountain View’s capital allocation is relatively small compared to typical project needs.
- Harsha Ramchandani states that the third principle is to establish minimum funding thresholds for grants because of substantial ongoing reporting and monitoring requirements for both the City and recipients.
- Assistant Community Development Director Wayne Chen notes that staff reviewed HUD best practices and consulted with HUD consultants, grant recipients, and HUD staff when formulating these principles.
#### Minimum grant thresholds and public services funding
Staff recommend establishing a minimum grant size for public service awards to balance administrative workload with breadth of support. While HUD previously allowed CDBG public service grants as low as $5,000, staff propose a recommended minimum request of $7,500 for both CDBG and General Fund public service grants under Council Policy A‑8. They report that stakeholders view $5,000 as too small given compliance requirements, while a $10,000 minimum could exclude too many agencies in an already oversubscribed funding environment. Commissioners express general support and focus questions on flexibility and the ability to respond to exceptional proposals.
- Harsha Ramchandani recommends a hard minimum of $5,000 for CDBG public service grants and a staff‑recommended minimum application size of $7,500 for both CDBG and General Fund public service awards.
- Harsha Ramchandani reports that feedback from agencies and consultants indicates that the administrative burden for a $5,000 grant is relatively high compared to the benefit.
- Harsha Ramchandani notes that a $10,000 minimum could significantly reduce the number of agencies that can be funded, given historically oversubscribed demand for public service funds.
- Chair Lin remarks that having clear funding thresholds will help guide HRC decisions during future grant allocation cycles while asking about flexibility for exceptional cases.
- Housing Manager Michaela Helman‑Tincher states that these thresholds function as guidelines within the NOFA and that the HRC and City Council retain discretion in special circumstances.
#### Minor home repair funding set‑aside
Staff propose reintroducing and funding a minor home repair program with a $30,000 CDBG capital set‑aside, offering grants of up to $5,000 per qualifying low‑income household. The allocation is based on discussions with regional nonprofits about anticipated demand and delivery capacity in Mountain View. Commissioner Rakestraw questions whether $30,000 is sufficient given visible needs among seniors and low‑income homeowners, and staff explain their concern about over‑allocating funds they might not be able to spend under HUD timeliness rules. Staff indicate they are open to adjusting the amount in future years after monitoring actual uptake and nonprofit capacity.
- Harsha Ramchandani recommends reserving $30,000 in CDBG capital funds for nonprofits that provide minor home repairs for low‑income homeowners, with grants capped at $5,000 per household.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher explains that the $30,000 figure is derived from nonprofit estimates of likely Mountain View demand based on past experience.
- Commissioner Rakestraw questions whether a $30,000 allocation is too low given that it may only fully serve about six households at the $5,000 grant maximum and notes unmet needs among seniors with limited resources.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher responds that staff must avoid allocating more capital funding than can be spent, due to HUD penalties for under‑utilization, but are open to modifying the allocation in later years if demand proves higher.
- Wayne Chen adds that nonprofits delivering minor repair services operate region‑wide and that the set‑aside also reflects realistic service capacity in Mountain View.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher states that reintroducing the program now will help staff gauge demand, refine outreach, and potentially support a larger allocation in future NOFA cycles.
#### Capital project thresholds and allocation strategy
To improve efficiency and impact of CDBG capital funding, staff propose issuing two CDBG capital NOFAs for FY 2023–24 and establishing a minimum capital request threshold of $300,000. The minimum is designed to ensure that funded capital projects are large enough to justify extensive federal compliance requirements. Staff also recommend that smaller capital requests under $300,000 be evaluated case‑by‑case and that any remaining CDBG capital funds after addressing suitable applications be allocated to City affordable housing pipeline projects. Commissioners ask about fairness, priority setting, and ensuring that the same large developers do not receive all the funds, and staff respond by referencing existing competitive procurement processes and evaluation criteria.
- Harsha Ramchandani recommends establishing a minimum CDBG capital funding request threshold of $300,000, with sub‑threshold applications to be considered case by case.
- Harsha Ramchandani proposes that any remaining CDBG capital funds after funding appropriate applications be redirected to affordable housing projects in the City’s pipeline.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher confirms that capital projects typically involve affordable housing developments or nonprofits serving low‑income households or people with special needs for acquisition or rehabilitation activities.
- Commissioner Rakestraw asks how staff will prioritize among multiple capital applicants and expresses concern that familiar large developers might receive the majority of funds.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher explains that all pipeline projects have undergone their own competitive or formal selection processes, such as RFPs or City NOFAs, before being considered for CDBG or HOME funds.
- Wayne Chen notes that staff use a defined evaluation and scoring framework—covering organizational capacity, compliance with CDBG/HOME requirements, and alignment with Consolidated Plan priorities—when forming funding recommendations for the HRC and Council.
#### Shift in HOME funding approach toward pipeline projects
Staff recommend discontinuing a competitive NOFA for HOME capital funds and instead directly allocating HOME funds to shovel‑ready affordable housing projects in the City’s pipeline. They note that past HOME NOFAs have had limited success and that HOME dollars are subject to tight commitment and expenditure deadlines. By integrating HOME dollars into the broader affordable housing pipeline financing strategy, staff aim to both comply with HUD timeliness requirements and help close known funding gaps in multiple approved or pending developments. Commissioners seek clarity on what projects might benefit, and staff describe an active pipeline including the Crestview conversion, La Avenida, Montecito, Lot 12, Terra Bella, and anticipated preservation efforts.
- Harsha Ramchandani explains that HOME capital funds are restricted to affordable housing and must be committed and used within tight HUD timelines, making flexible deployment critical.
- Harsha Ramchandani recommends not issuing a HOME NOFA and instead working directly with developers of shovel‑ready affordable housing projects in the City’s pipeline to address existing funding gaps.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher states that prior HOME NOFAs have been relatively unsuccessful, in part because the funding amounts are small compared to total project needs.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher notes that the City has several approved or soon‑to‑be‑approved affordable housing projects, including Crestview, La Avenida, Montecito, Lot 12, and Terra Bella, as well as expected preservation and rehabilitation efforts.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher explains that using HOME funds on pipeline projects may allow the City to reassign local fees to later developments, since local funds are more flexible and not subject to strict HUD timelines.
- Wayne Chen adds that staff discussed this approach with HUD consultants and evaluated best practices from other jurisdictions before recommending a shift away from competitive HOME NOFAs.
#### Commission questions on policy change magnitude and flexibility
Commissioners ask how different the proposed policies are from prior practice and how much flexibility remains to deviate from guidelines in unusual but compelling cases. Staff explain that the recommendations mostly formalize existing practices, such as two‑year public services cycles, case‑by‑case capital allocations, and the prior direct allocation for the Crestview project. They emphasize that the HRC and City Council retain discretion to make exceptions within HUD rules, and they plan to reflect some flexibility explicitly in the NOFA language. Commissioners stress the importance of avoiding overly rigid local rules that might prevent funding of high‑value projects that slightly diverge from the guidelines.
- Chair Lin asks how much the new policy recommendations differ from existing practice and whether they introduce significant new constraints.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher responds that most recommendations formalize previous discussions and practices—such as minimum thresholds and flexible capital allocations—rather than dramatically changing course.
- Chair Lin requests assurance that the HRC will retain discretion to consider exceptional projects that do not fully align with the new guidelines but still meet HUD requirements and local priorities.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher confirms that the policies will be framed as guidelines within the NOFA and that the HRC and Council can exercise judgment in special circumstances.
- Wayne Chen emphasizes that the new framework actually adds flexibility for capital projects by explicitly allowing non‑NOFA allocations and case‑by‑case consideration of smaller projects.
- Commissioner Rakestraw reiterates concern that the City remain open to new or smaller organizations seeking capital support, and staff respond that all pipeline projects have already been selected through open processes and will still be evaluated against objective criteria.
#### Earthquake retrofitting and seismic safety funding possibilities
Commissioner Solomon raises whether CDBG or HOME funds could support earthquake retrofitting, especially for buildings housing lower‑income residents who are particularly vulnerable. Staff respond that they are uncertain about eligibility and willing to research the possibility, noting that federal capital funding rules and ownership structures may pose barriers for private multifamily buildings. Assistant Director Chen provides context that City Council previously explored a soft‑story retrofit program but paused work during the pandemic due to cost and staffing constraints. Staff also describe ongoing efforts to design an acquisition and preservation strategy that could eventually intersect with seismic upgrades for older buildings.
- Commissioner Solomon asks whether CDBG or HOME funds could support earthquake retrofitting in Mountain View, particularly in vulnerable buildings with low‑income residents.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher replies that she is not sure if federal rules would allow retrofit funding for private multifamily buildings and commits to researching the issue further.
- Michaela Helman‑Tincher notes that the minor home repair program likely cannot address large‑scale seismic retrofits because of grant size limits and scope.
- Wayne Chen explains that City Council directed staff about three years ago to evaluate a soft‑story retrofit program and that an initial survey was conducted before the effort was paused due to high costs, staffing limits, and the pandemic.
- Wayne Chen notes that discussions at the time included whether City funds should support buildings covered by the rent stabilization program and how such investments would interact with that framework.
- Wayne Chen adds that staff are developing an acquisition and preservation strategy—including potential partnerships and external funding sources—that could eventually support rehabilitation, including possible seismic work, on older apartment buildings.
