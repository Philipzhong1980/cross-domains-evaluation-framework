### Overview
The briefing, led by Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and National Security Council spokesman Admiral John Kirby, covers three primary areas: the Biden administration’s interpretation of recent U.S. election results, extensive updates on the Israel–Hamas war and related regional dynamics, and domestic legislative and political challenges. Jean-Pierre frames Democratic and issue victories in states like Ohio and Kentucky as validations of President Biden’s agenda on reproductive rights, infrastructure, and middle-class economics, while dismissing negative polling and emphasizing that the White House focuses on delivering results rather than reacting to surveys. Kirby provides detailed updates on U.S.–Israeli coordination, G7 alignment on condemning Hamas and endorsing humanitarian pauses, ongoing efforts to increase aid into Gaza and evacuate U.S. citizens, and the delicate, partner-mediated negotiations to free hostages through localized, time-bound pauses rather than a broader ceasefire that he says would benefit and legitimize Hamas. He reiterates U.S. opposition to permanent displacement of Palestinians, insists that Palestinians should shape Gaza’s post-war governance, and underscores parallel commitments to counter antisemitism and Islamophobia at home. Kirby also addresses questions on Ukraine, noting that the front remains active, that Biden still believes Ukraine can regain its territory, and that U.S. assistance funds are nearly exhausted, heightening pressure on Congress to pass the supplemental funding package that also includes border security resources. Jean-Pierre, meanwhile, presses Congress to avert a looming government shutdown, defends Biden’s foreign leadership record despite polls, responds cautiously to the House censure of Rep. Rashida Tlaib while rejecting the phrase 'from the river to the sea' as divisive and antisemitic, previews Biden’s upcoming APEC and Illinois trips, and criticizes Senator Tuberville’s holds on military promotions and GOP investigations of the Biden family as politically driven distractions from substantive policy priorities.

The White House emphasizes that voters, not polls, are what matter and that Biden’s agenda continues to resonate despite negative survey data. They also note specific races, like Kentucky’s governor race and Ohio’s abortion and marijuana initiatives, as evidence of support for Biden’s priorities.

He underscores that safe passage through Rafah without risk of fire is one of several complicating factors in evacuation efforts.

#### Post-war governance and transition in Gaza
Kirby responds to questions about Secretary Blinken’s comments on Gaza’s post-war governance and a potential transition period. The U.S. position is that Palestinians should have a determining voice in their future and governance in both Gaza and the West Bank, with long-term arrangements still under discussion. While Blinken referenced Palestinian-led governance and possible unification under the Palestinian Authority, Kirby avoids committing to a specific model but reiterates that Israel should not pursue a long-term reoccupation. He acknowledges that a short-term period in which Israeli forces remain on the ground after the conflict to manage security is plausible, but stresses this should not become a permanent governance structure.
- Mr. Kirby says U.S. policy is that Gaza and the West Bank are for the Palestinian people and that Palestinians should have a vote and voice in their governance.
- Mr. Kirby notes that specific governance structures, timelines, and implementing players are still being worked out with partners and that no final model is set.
- Mr. Kirby explains that Secretary Blinken’s reference to a 'transition period' acknowledges the likelihood that Israeli forces may remain in Gaza for some time after hostilities to manage immediate security needs.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that the U.S. does not support an Israeli Defense Forces reoccupation of Gaza as a long-term governance solution.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates in multiple answers that the Palestinian people should be in charge of their destiny and future.

At the same time, the U.S. maintains its commitment to providing Israel with defensive support while pressing it privately on operational conduct.

#### Hostage negotiations and humanitarian pauses mechanics
Kirby fields extensive questions about hostage negotiations, proof of life, and how humanitarian pauses might operate on the ground. He confirms the U.S. lacks direct channels to Hamas and instead works through partners like Qatar and Egypt, declining to discuss specific negotiating positions or demands to protect delicate talks. The U.S. does not have specific proof of life for each hostage but assumes American hostages are alive absent contrary evidence. Humanitarian pauses are envisioned as localized, time-bound cessations of hostilities in defined areas, used flexibly over potentially multiple rounds to move aid, evacuate civilians, and secure hostages’ release without implying a broader ceasefire or political endgame.
- Mr. Kirby states that the U.S. does not communicate directly with Hamas but uses partners who maintain such channels.
- Mr. Kirby refuses to discuss reported Israeli positions on minimum hostage numbers for a deal, saying he will not negotiate in public.
- Mr. Kirby explains that prior hostage releases required pauses in fighting and that the U.S. seeks an agreement allowing as many such pauses as necessary to free all hostages.
- Mr. Kirby says he is not aware of specific proof of life for individual hostages, including Americans, but notes there is no indication they are dead and the U.S. operates under the assumption they are alive.
- Mr. Kirby describes humanitarian pauses as temporary, fixed in time and geography, potentially lasting hours to days, and limited to defined grid coordinates where fighting would stop while operations continue elsewhere.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that humanitarian pauses may need to occur multiple times and be tailored based on how many people can move or how much aid can enter in each interval.

The U.S. has observed rocket fire from southern Lebanon into northern Israel but does not see a full-scale second front opened by Hezbollah. He acknowledges that Iran continues to train, resource, and equip Hezbollah, and that the U.S. is not blind to Tehran’s role. The administration remains focused on deterring broader regional escalation even as it prioritizes the Gaza conflict.

- Mr. Kirby confirms that Hezbollah is supported, trained, and resourced by Tehran and that the U.S. is fully aware of that relationship.
- Mr. Kirby indicates no new overt Iranian actions he can publicly discuss but underscores that the U.S. is closely watching the northern front for escalation.

He notes that different militant groups besides Hamas may hold some hostages. He cannot estimate how many pauses will be needed, saying it depends on deal terms and batch sizes, but stresses openness to multiple pauses to accomplish the mission.

Kirby also warns that U.S. assistance funds are nearly exhausted, with approximately 96% of overall Ukraine support allocated and only about $1.1 billion in defense replenishment funds remaining, reinforcing the urgency of Congress approving the supplemental request.

#### Humanitarian pauses versus ceasefire
Kirby elaborates on the conceptual distinction between humanitarian pauses and a broader ceasefire in Gaza. He defines a ceasefire as an indefinite cessation of hostilities typically associated with negotiating an end to a conflict, arguing that such a move now would benefit and legitimize Hamas by treating it as an equal party after its October 7 attacks. In contrast, humanitarian pauses are described as temporary, localized, and purpose-specific, calibrated to allow hostages out, aid in, or civilians to move. The administration explicitly opposes a general ceasefire at this stage while advocating for tailored pauses.
- Mr. Kirby explains that a general ceasefire usually implies an indefinite halt to fighting aimed at negotiating the end of a war.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. does not support a ceasefire now because it would benefit Hamas, give it time to regroup, and legitimize its October 7 attacks.
- Mr. Kirby argues a ceasefire would allow Hamas to claim parity and a right to continue governing Gaza, which the U.S. rejects.
- Mr. Kirby defines humanitarian pauses as temporary, localized, and tied to specific goals, such as hostage releases, aid deliveries, or civilian evacuations.
