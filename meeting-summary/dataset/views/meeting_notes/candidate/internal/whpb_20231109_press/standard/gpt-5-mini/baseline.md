### Overview
  Karine Jean-Pierre opens by framing recent state election outcomes as endorsements of President Biden’s agenda, highlighting Ohio’s vote to enshrine reproductive care and previewing a briefing on antisemitism and a new strategy to counter Islamophobia. Admiral John Kirby then provides a foreign-policy update: he summarizes the G7’s joint condemnation of Hamas and endorsement of humanitarian pauses, reports that 650 aid trucks have entered Gaza and that roughly 400 Americans have departed with embassy support, and describes ongoing diplomatic efforts to secure additional pauses and hostage releases while urging Israeli caution to reduce civilian harm. On governance, Kirby emphasizes a U.S. policy goal that Palestinians determine Gaza’s future while noting details remain to be worked out, and he reiterates no change on Golan Heights policy and no plans to send U.S. combat troops into Gaza. The briefing also covers limited Hezbollah activity and Iran’s support for the group, the operational character and limits of humanitarian pauses versus a general ceasefire, and U.S. urgency to replenish assistance for Ukraine as Defense Department replenishment funds run low. Domestically, Jean-Pierre stresses OMB outreach to Congress on a supplemental funding package tied to national security and humanitarian needs and urges lawmakers to prevent a government shutdown, addresses congressional actions and rhetoric, previews the President’s UAW visit to mark factory reopening, and responds to breaking Oversight Committee subpoenas by referring inquiries to personal representatives while reiterating the Administration’s position that no evidence has implicated the President. Overall, the briefing balances immediate crisis management in the Middle East and support for allied security with ongoing resource constraints for Ukraine and urgent fiscal and political matters at home.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Election results impact
  MS. JEAN-PIERRE opens with the previous night’s election outcomes, highlighting wins in Kentucky, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Virginia and framing them as endorsements of the President’s agenda. She emphasizes Ohio voters enshrining reproductive care as a constitutional right and interprets results as rejection of restrictive abortion measures and conspiracy-driven politics. The administration frames these outcomes as validation for continuing to focus on economic growth, lowering costs, and protecting fundamental freedoms.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said voters in Kentucky, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Virginia sided with the President’s agenda and values.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said Ohio voters made reproductive care a constitutional right in the state.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the results show voters rejected extreme abortion bans and conspiratorial politics.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration will remain focused on growing the economy and lowering costs for families.
  #### National hate strategies
  MS. JEAN-PIERRE previews a 2:00 p.m. briefing by Domestic Policy Advisor Neera Tanden on the National Strategy to Counter Antisemitism and the administration’s response to rising antisemitic incidents. She reiterates the President’s condemnation of antisemitism and announces the administration will develop the first U.S. National Strategy to Counter Islamophobia. The administration frames antisemitism, Islamophobia, and other hate as unacceptable and pledges ongoing public and private engagement with affected communities.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said Domestic Policy Advisor Neera Tanden will brief the American Jewish community on the National Strategy to Counter Antisemitism.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration will develop the first U.S. National Strategy to Counter Islamophobia.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President strongly condemns antisemitic threats and attacks targeting Jewish communities.
  #### G7 statement and Israel
  MR. KIRBY summarizes Secretary Blinken’s participation in the G7 foreign ministers’ meeting in Japan and a joint statement condemning Hamas’s terror attacks. He says the G7 reaffirmed support for Israel’s right to defend itself consistent with international law and agreed that humanitarian pauses can help protect civilians, increase aid access, facilitate evacuations, and help secure hostage releases. The administration continues diplomatic engagement with partners, including a meeting between Jake Sullivan and former Israeli Prime Minister Naftali Bennett.
- MR. KIRBY said G7 foreign ministers issued a joint statement that unequivocally condemns Hamas’s terror attacks.
- MR. KIRBY said the G7 reaffirmed support for Israel’s right and responsibility to defend itself in accordance with international law.
- MR. KIRBY said the G7 agreed that humanitarian pauses can advance protection of civilians, delivery of assistance, exits, and hostage facilitation.
- MR. KIRBY said National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan met with former Prime Minister Naftali Bennett to discuss these issues.
  #### Rafah aid and evacuations
  MR. KIRBY provides an operational update on the Rafah crossing and evacuations, reporting that more than 80 additional aid trucks entered Gaza in the last 24 hours, bringing the total to 650 trucks. He reports awareness of more than 400 Americans and family members who have departed with embassy support and stresses the overall aid flow remains insufficient. The administration says it will continue pushing for sustained access and evacuations.
- MR. KIRBY said over the last 24 hours more than 80 additional humanitarian trucks entered Gaza, bringing the total to 650.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration is aware of more than 400 Americans and family members who have departed with embassy support.
- MR. KIRBY said the current level of aid is not enough and the U.S. will continue to press for increased sustained delivery.
  #### Americans remaining in Gaza
  Reporters press MR. KIRBY on why more U.S. citizens have not left Gaza and how many remain, and he estimates roughly 500–600 people remain when counting family members. He attributes varying daily numbers to the dynamic situation, including vetting, procedural constraints, and obstacles posed by Hamas. The administration emphasizes ongoing, daily efforts to facilitate departures and cautions against assuming consistent progress day to day.
- MR. KIRBY said about 5- to 600 people likely remain in Gaza when counting family members after roughly 400 were evacuated.
- MR. KIRBY said daily variations occur because the situation is dynamic and often depends on vetting and obstacles that Hamas may pose.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. is confident the flow will continue but that each day’s progress must be negotiated anew.
  #### Gaza governance debate
  Reporters ask whether U.S. policy now endorses reunifying Gaza and the West Bank under Palestinian Authority control following Secretary Blinken’s remarks. MR. KIRBY says the U.S. wants Palestinians to determine governance and be the primary voice in their future, but he acknowledges the administration has not settled on a specific governance structure or timeline. The administration frames this as an objective rather than a finalized plan, with details to be worked out with partners.
- MR. KIRBY said the United States believes Palestinians should be the determining voice in their governance and future.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration has not defined a specific final governance structure for Gaza and the West Bank.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. is working with partners to answer questions about who will help shape post-conflict governance.
  #### Humanitarian pauses and hostages
  Multiple exchanges focus on negotiations for humanitarian pauses—how they work, whether they facilitate hostage releases, and their potential duration. MR. KIRBY confirms the administration is discussing pauses with Israeli counterparts, notes small pauses have enabled some hostage releases, and describes pauses as temporary, localized, and purpose-driven for aid, exits, and hostage movements. He declines to confirm specific media reports about discrete pause proposals or to negotiate public terms that could jeopardize sensitive processes.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. is discussing the benefit of humanitarian pauses with Israeli counterparts and partners.
- MR. KIRBY said prior small pauses have allowed some hostages to be released and enabled people to exit Gaza.
- MR. KIRBY said humanitarian pauses are temporary, localized, and tailored to specific objectives like getting aid in and hostages out.
- MR. KIRBY said he will not negotiate or discuss sensitive terms in public to avoid putting processes at risk.
  #### Transition period after conflict
  Reporters seek clarification about Secretary Blinken’s reference to a “transition period,” and MR. KIRBY explains that an immediate post-conflict period could plausibly involve Israeli forces remaining in Gaza for a time to manage security. He stresses that the administration does not view a long-term Israeli reoccupation as the desired governance solution and reiterates the goal of a Palestinian-led future. The specifics of timing, roles, and partners remain under discussion.
- MR. KIRBY said a near-term transition could involve Israeli forces managing the immediate aftermath and security in Gaza.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. does not support a long-term IDF reoccupation of Gaza as a governance solution.
- MR. KIRBY said details about timing and the players involved in transition remain to be worked out.
  #### Civilian harm concerns
  Reporters cite U.N. and international criticism about civilian casualties and possible collective punishment, and MR. KIRBY stresses the U.S. is closely watching events and repeatedly urges Israeli counterparts to be cautious and deliberate to avoid civilian harm. He emphasizes adherence to the law of armed conflict and expresses mourning for Palestinian civilian casualties while reaffirming U.S. support for Israel’s right to defend itself against Hamas. The administration says it raises concerns privately and publicly when events cause alarm.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. is watching battlefield events closely and remains in constant touch with Israeli counterparts.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration urges Israel to be as cautious and deliberate as possible to avoid civilian casualties.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. recognizes the many Palestinian deaths and stresses compliance with the law of armed conflict.
  #### Golan Heights position
  In response to a reporter’s question, MR. KIRBY reiterates there is no change in U.S. policy on the Golan Heights and references prior statements by Secretary Blinken. He frames the position as recognizing Israel’s legitimate security needs given the situation in Syria. The administration does not signal any policy shift on the Golan.
- MR. KIRBY said there has been no change in U.S. policy on the Golan Heights.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. understands Israel’s legitimate security concerns on the Golan given the situation in Syria.
  #### Hezbollah and Iran role
  Reporters ask about activity along the northern front and Tehran’s role, and MR. KIRBY reports limited rocket attacks from southern Lebanon into northern Israel but no wholesale Hezbollah opening of a second front. He notes Iran continues to resource and train Hezbollah and says the U.S. monitors those ties, without pointing to overt new escalatory actions. The administration warns it is not blind to Iran’s support for Hezbollah while noting current activity remains limited.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. has tracked rocket attacks from southern Lebanon into northern Israel but not a full-scale Hezbollah offensive.
- MR. KIRBY said Iran continues to resource and provide capabilities to Hezbollah, which the U.S. monitors closely.
- MR. KIRBY said there is no overt, new, demonstrable escalation by Hezbollah that he can point to publicly today.
  #### Hostage negotiations and visibility
  Reporters press on the composition, numbers, and proof-of-life for hostages; MR. KIRBY declines public negotiation details, says U.S. channels through partners are working hostage issues, and acknowledges imperfect visibility on the full universe of hostages. He says some partners maintain channels with Hamas, and the U.S. continues efforts to secure releases for American citizens and others. MR. KIRBY says he has no specific proof-of-life updates to announce and is operating on the assumption hostages remain alive absent information to the contrary.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. does not have perfect visibility on all hostages and is working with Israeli counterparts to refine that picture.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. does not maintain direct communications with Hamas but uses partner channels that have been helpful.
- MR. KIRBY said he is not aware of specific proof-of-life documentation for American hostages and is operating under the assumption they remain alive.
  #### Humanitarian pause logistics
  Reporters ask operational questions about how pauses would work for forces already on the ground, and MR. KIRBY characterizes pauses as temporary, fixed in time, and potentially localized to specific grid coordinates. He notes that pauses could last hours to days depending on objectives and would not preclude fighting outside agreed zones, and he emphasizes Israeli operational considerations in reviewing proposals. The administration frames pauses as practical, limited tools to accomplish discrete objectives rather than broad military stand-downs.
- MR. KIRBY said humanitarian pauses would be temporary, fixed in time, and localized to defined grid coordinates.
- MR. KIRBY said pauses might last hours to days depending on the purpose and would not necessarily halt fighting outside designated zones.
- MR. KIRBY said Israeli military and political considerations will factor into how each pause proposal is evaluated.
  #### Ukraine front and assistance
  Reporters question whether Ukraine faces a stalemate and about continued U.S. support; MR. KIRBY declines to characterize Ukrainian operations but says the front from Donbass toward Zaporizhzhia remains active. He affirms the U.S. believes Ukraine can win back its territory and reiterates the need to provide security assistance before weather and mines limit movement. He warns the Defense Department’s replenishment funds are largely spent and stresses the urgency of the President’s supplemental request.
- MR. KIRBY said the front in Ukraine remains active from Donbass toward Zaporizhzhia.
- MR. KIRBY said the United States believes Ukraine can win back its territory and is capable and resilient.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. has expended the vast majority of assistance funds and that Defense Department replenishment money is down to about $1.1 billion, underscoring the need for the supplemental.
  #### Polling and leadership perceptions
  A reporter cites a CNN poll showing low public confidence in President Biden as an effective world leader, and MR. KIRBY responds that the National Security Council does not base policy on polls. He emphasizes that the administration remains focused on defending national security interests and refers broadly to the President’s track record on the world stage. The NSC frames operational priorities as driven by principles and national security requirements rather than polling data.
- MR. KIRBY said the National Security Council does not let polls govern policy development or execution.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration remains fixated on defending U.S. national security interests, not on poll numbers.
- MR. KIRBY cited the President’s international actions as evidence of leadership without relying on polling as a guide.
  #### U.S. troops and direct intervention
  Reporters ask whether the U.S. would send troops into Gaza to retrieve hostages, and MR. KIRBY states there are no plans to put American troops on the ground in combat in this conflict. He says the administration pursues hostage recovery largely through diplomatic and negotiated channels. The White House emphasizes it is not militarily engaged inside Gaza.
- MR. KIRBY said there are no plans to deploy American combat troops into Gaza to recover hostages.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration is pursuing negotiations and partner channels to secure hostage releases.
  #### Displacement and refugee policies
  Reporters question whether the U.S. supports permanent displacement of Palestinians and whether it pressures neighboring countries to accept temporary refugees. MR. KIRBY reiterates the U.S. does not support permanent displacement outside Gaza and says decisions on refugee policies rest with sovereign countries. He emphasizes the immediate priority is protecting and assisting internally displaced Palestinians inside Gaza where more than a million people have moved.
- MR. KIRBY said the United States does not support permanent displacement of Palestinians outside Gaza.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. is not making specific asks for other countries to take in refugees and that resettlement decisions remain sovereign choices.
- MR. KIRBY said the most pressing issue is protecting internally displaced Palestinians inside Gaza.
  #### Indo-Pacific diplomacy and meetings
  Reporters raise East Asia diplomacy ahead of President Biden’s meeting with Indonesia’s President and speculation about a Biden-Xi meeting; MR. KIRBY says the Middle East will likely come up in bilateral meetings but declines to over-parse advance statements. He confirms the administration treats Indo-Pacific alliances and deterrence with high priority and frames upcoming forums like APEC as important venues to reinforce those partnerships. He does not confirm any specific Biden-Xi meeting date.
- MR. KIRBY said the situation in the Middle East is likely to come up during the President’s meetings with Indo-Pacific leaders.
- MR. KIRBY said he cannot confirm a Biden-Xi meeting date at this time.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration is devoting resources to strengthen alliances and readiness in the Indo-Pacific, including exercises and intelligence capabilities.
  #### DFC investment and PGI
  A reporter asks about the Development Finance Corporation’s $553 million investment in a Colombo port terminal and how it fits U.S. strategy versus China. MR. KIRBY frames the President’s Program for Global Investment and Infrastructure (PGI) as offering fairer, more transparent alternatives that help lower- and middle-income countries finance infrastructure. He says the administration is not telling countries whom to partner with but is offering alternatives to high-interest approaches like the Belt and Road Initiative.
- MR. KIRBY said the PGI provides alternative investment options that aim to be more transparent and economically beneficial than some other offers.
- MR. KIRBY said the U.S. is not telling countries they cannot engage with China but is offering competitive, fairer financing alternatives.
  #### Ceasefire versus pauses
  In extended questioning MR. KIRBY distinguishes humanitarian pauses from a general ceasefire, arguing a general ceasefire now would benefit Hamas by granting it political legitimacy and operational breathing room. He states the administration supports temporary, localized pauses for concrete objectives but does not support an indefinite cessation of hostilities at this stage. The White House frames its policy as preserving pressure on Hamas while seeking ways to protect civilians and secure hostages.
- MR. KIRBY said a general ceasefire implies an indefinite cessation of hostilities and is not supported at this time.
- MR. KIRBY said a ceasefire now would give Hamas a propaganda and operational advantage and thus is unacceptable to the President.
- MR. KIRBY said the administration supports temporary, localized humanitarian pauses to achieve specific objectives like aid delivery and hostage release.
  #### Government funding and supplemental outreach
  MS. JEAN-PIERRE addresses a looming government funding deadline and says the White House has no public schedule for presidential outreach but confirms OMB Director Shalanda Young is actively conducting outreach to Congress. She stresses bipartisan support for the President’s supplemental requests, which include assistance for Ukraine and Israel and humanitarian needs, and urges Congress to keep the government open. The administration frames the funding request as urgent and necessary for national security and domestic programs.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said OMB Director Shalanda Young is on the Hill conducting outreach about the urgent need to avoid a government shutdown.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the supplemental request enjoys bipartisan support and includes emergency needs for Ukraine, Israel, and humanitarian assistance.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said Congress must act to keep the government open and fund critical programs.
  #### Congressional actions and rhetoric

JEAN-PIERRE declines to comment on congressional disciplinary actions but says the White House strongly disagrees with applying that phrase to the conflict because many find it divisive and antisemitic. She also addresses breaking news about Oversight Committee subpoenas for Hunter Biden and associates by noting the White House sees the probe as long-running and without evidence of wrongdoing by the President. The White House reiterates its priority focus on national issues rather than partisan investigations.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the White House will not speak to congressional disciplinary actions and that such actions are Congress’s prerogative.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration strongly disagrees with using the phrase “from the river to the sea” in this context and noted many find it divisive and antisemitic.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the Administration views the long-running investigation into the President’s family as producing no evidence of wrongdoing by the President.
  #### President’s domestic engagements
  MS. She frames the event as demonstrating the President’s commitment to working families and domestic economic priorities. The White House credits these policy themes as part of the broader electoral messaging referenced earlier in the briefing.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President will meet UAW workers and mark the reopening of an assembly plant in Belvidere, Illinois.

She closes the briefing and thanks Admiral Kirby for his appearance.
