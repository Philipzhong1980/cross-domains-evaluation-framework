### Overview
  Senior administration officials brief reporters on a new executive order by President Biden that prohibits U.S. federal departments and agencies from operationally using commercial spyware tools that pose significant counterintelligence, security, or human rights risks, explaining that it is a consensus product across law enforcement, defense, and intelligence components and builds on earlier export controls, Commerce Entity List designations, and recent congressional authorities. They recount how a National Security Council-led review begun in 2021 identified growing misuse of invasive spyware by both authoritarian and democratic governments, confirmed that U.S. personnel overseas have been targeted—including devices associated with 50 U.S. government personnel in at least 10 countries—and exposed gaps in U.S. policy amid aggressive marketing by vendors. The officials describe the EO’s risk framework, which focuses on unauthorized targeting of U.S. devices, vendor trustworthiness, misuse against activists and journalists, and provision of tools to repressive regimes, while allowing for concrete remedial steps by vendors and requiring enhanced reporting, information sharing, and semiannual intelligence assessments, with non-delegable determinations made by a limited set of agency heads and no public blacklist mandated. They position the EO as a cornerstone deliverable for the upcoming second Summit for Democracy—co-hosted by the United States with Costa Rica, the Netherlands, the Republic of Korea, and Zambia—where the U.S. will host events on advancing technology for democracy, countering digital authoritarianism, and shaping emerging technologies, and will unveil additional initiatives, including export controls and human rights measures and new guiding principles for government use of surveillance technology, to align global technology governance with democratic values and human rights.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Announcement of spyware executive order
  Senior administration officials announce that President Biden will issue a new executive order (EO) prohibiting U.S. federal departments and agencies from operational use of commercial spyware that poses significant national security, counterintelligence, or human rights risks. The order responds to the global proliferation and misuse of advanced commercial spyware, including against U.S. personnel and civil society worldwide. Officials frame the EO as a landmark step to counter misuse of surveillance technologies and as part of a broader, multi-pronged U.S. strategy. The EO is timed to coincide with the second Summit for Democracy to underscore U.S. leadership on responsible technology governance.
- A senior administration official states that President Biden will issue an executive order prohibiting operational use by U.S. departments and agencies of commercial spyware that poses risks to U.S. national security, foreign policy, or has been used to target U.S. personnel or enable human rights abuses.
- A senior administration official explains that the EO is intended as a concrete demonstration of U.S. leadership in countering misuse of commercial spyware as the President co-hosts the second Summit for Democracy.
- A senior administration official notes that the EO is one element of a multifaceted response to the multifaceted challenge posed by proliferation and misuse of commercial spyware.
- A senior administration official outlines that the briefing will cover why the administration decided to issue the EO, its objectives grounded in national security and human rights concerns, its risk-evaluation structure, and its connection to the Summit for Democracy.
#### Background on spyware threat assessment
  Officials describe a policy process launched in late summer/early fall 2021 by the National Security Council to assess the threat from commercial spyware and develop countermeasures. The focus is on highly advanced, invasive tools capable of remotely compromising and manipulating devices without user knowledge. They emphasize that both authoritarian and democratic governments have misused such tools to repress dissent, target activists and journalists, and bypass legal safeguards. The U.S. concluded that these tools create distinct and growing counterintelligence and security risks, especially to U.S. personnel and government systems.
- A senior administration official explains that in late summer or early fall 2021, the National Security Council began a government-wide process to assess the threat from misuse of commercial spyware and to craft policy responses.
- A senior administration official describes the focus on end-to-end spyware suites that enable remote access to devices, extraction of contents, and manipulation of components without the user’s knowledge or consent.
- A senior administration official reports that numerous foreign governments have used commercial spyware to facilitate repression, intimidate political opponents, curb dissent, and target activists and journalists worldwide.
- A senior administration official notes that misuse is not confined to authoritarian regimes, as democratic governments have also faced revelations of domestic targeting without adequate legal authorization, safeguards, or oversight.
- A senior administration official states that proliferation of commercial spyware poses distinct counterintelligence and security risks to the United States, including to the safety of U.S. personnel and the integrity of U.S. government devices and information, and confirms that U.S. personnel overseas have been targeted.
#### Early U.S. actions and policy gaps
  Before the EO, the U.S. had already taken several steps, including new export controls on spyware-related software and adding certain foreign spyware companies to the Commerce Department Entity List. However, officials identified a major gap: federal departments and agencies lacked clear, consistent guidance on whether they could use spyware tools that were simultaneously being misused abroad, including against U.S. personnel. Vendors were actively marketing to U.S. law enforcement, defense, and intelligence entities, sometimes obscuring their business practices. This combination of aggressive marketing and policy ambiguity drove the decision to pursue a formal prohibition framework.
- A senior administration official recounts that the administration moved quickly to impose new export controls on spyware-related intrusion software and to add several foreign spyware companies to the Commerce Department Entity List.
- A senior administration official identifies a key gap in that U.S. departments and agencies lacked clear direction on whether they could use commercial spyware tools, even when those tools were being used to target U.S. diplomats or to facilitate human rights abuses.
- A senior administration official notes that commercial spyware vendors were aggressively marketing to U.S. law enforcement, defense, and intelligence components and sometimes obscuring their business ties and practices.
- A senior administration official recalls that nearly a year ago the administration announced it would work to institute a prohibition on risky spyware and that Congress subsequently passed bipartisan measures giving the Director of National Intelligence new authorities concerning commercial spyware and restricting intelligence community personnel from working for foreign entities such as DarkMatter.
- A senior administration official highlights that the Director of National Intelligence issued binding guidance just days earlier to implement statutory restrictions on former intelligence officials seeking employment with foreign governments or commercial spyware entities.
#### Scope and objectives of the executive order
  Officials describe the EO as a consensus product across key U.S. departments and agencies, including law enforcement, defense, and intelligence. The EO prohibits federal departments and agencies from operationally using commercial spyware tools that pose significant counterintelligence or security risks or significant risks of improper use by foreign actors, whether the vendors are foreign or domestic. Its core objectives are to align U.S. government spyware use with democratic and human rights values, avoid contributing to proliferation and abuse, protect U.S. personnel and systems, and catalyze international cooperation and industry reform. The EO also aims to prevent incentives for vendors to relocate to the U.S. to evade restrictions.
- A senior administration official says the EO represents consensus among all key departments and agencies and prohibits operational use of commercial spyware that poses significant counterintelligence or security risks or risks of improper use by foreign governments or persons.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that the EO applies to spyware furnished by both foreign and domestic commercial entities to avoid creating incentives for vendors to relocate to the United States to evade controls.
- A senior administration official states that the first objective of the EO is to ensure any U.S. government use of commercial spyware aligns with U.S. national security, foreign policy, democracy, rule of law, and human rights interests.
- A senior administration official explains that the second objective is to ensure the United States does not directly or indirectly contribute to the proliferation and misuse of commercial spyware.
- A senior administration official states that the third objective is to better protect U.S. government personnel, information systems, and sensitive activities against counterintelligence and security risks.
- A senior administration official notes that the fourth objective is to provide a foundation for expanded international cooperation on countering spyware proliferation by having the United States lead by example.
- A senior administration official argues that the EO should spur reform in a largely unregulated industry by defining responsible use and remedial factors intended to prevent misuse and reduce national security risks.
#### Risk factors and human rights criteria
  The EO introduces specific counterintelligence, security, and human-rights-based factors to assess whether a spyware tool is too risky for U.S. operational use. These include prior unauthorized targeting of U.S. government devices or personnel, vendor trustworthiness concerns such as unauthorized data access, backdoors, or foreign intelligence control, and misuse against activists, dissidents, journalists, or other civil society actors. The EO also covers situations in which spyware is deployed to track U.S. persons without proper legal safeguards and circumstances where vendors supply governments credibly reported to commit systematic political repression or gross human rights violations, even if spyware-related abuses have not yet occurred. The goal is to ensure broad, forward-looking coverage of abusive use cases while allowing for clear, documented risk assessments.
- A senior administration official explains that the EO establishes novel counterintelligence, security, and improper use factors to evaluate the risk of commercial spyware tools.
- A senior administration official highlights that one key factor is whether a foreign government or person has used or acquired the spyware to access U.S. government devices or devices of U.S. government personnel without U.S. authorization.
- A senior administration official notes that counterintelligence and security factors include whether a vendor maintains, transfers, or uses spyware-derived data without authorization, such as via backdoors, whether it has disclosed or plans to disclose nonpublic U.S. government information, or whether it is under the control of a foreign intelligence-linked actor.
- A senior administration official states that human rights factors include whether foreign actors have used the spyware against activists, dissidents, journalists, or similar actors to intimidate, suppress dissent, limit freedoms of expression, assembly, or association, or enable other abuses and civil liberties violations.
- A senior administration official adds that the EO aims to capture use of commercial spyware against U.S. persons without proper legal authorization, safeguards, oversight, or consent.
- A senior administration official explains that the EO also applies when spyware is furnished to governments credibly reported to engage in systematic political repression or gross human rights violations, even if specific spyware-related abuses have not yet been documented, to enable preventive application.
#### Remedial steps, reporting, and intelligence assessment
  Officials stress that the EO is designed to strike a balance by defining concrete remedial actions vendors can take to mitigate identified risks and potentially remain eligible for U.S. use. Remedial steps may include canceling risky licensing agreements or contracts and implementing verifiable controls against misuse. The EO sets out enhanced reporting and information-sharing requirements across the executive branch so agencies can make consistent, informed determinations based on up-to-date all-source intelligence. It also mandates development of a semiannual comprehensive intelligence assessment on commercial spyware threats to provide an ongoing picture for policymakers.
- A senior administration official states that the EO identifies specific remedial steps spyware vendors can take to remove risks, such as canceling licensing agreements or contracts that pose concerns.
- A senior administration official says the EO seeks to strike a balance by combining strict risk criteria with pathways for vendors to address and reduce those risks.
- A senior administration official explains that the EO directs new reporting and information-sharing requirements within the executive branch so departments and agencies can make informed and consistent decisions based on current all-source information.
- A senior administration official notes that the EO calls for a semiannual comprehensive intelligence assessment to track spyware threats and inform ongoing implementation.
#### Broader U.S. toolkit against spyware misuse
  The EO is positioned as one pillar within a broader U.S. strategy to counter spyware proliferation and misuse. This strategy includes preventing recruitment of U.S. intelligence professionals by foreign spyware vendors, leveraging export controls and Entity List designations, and using diplomatic engagement and other tools. Officials point to recent congressional action and intelligence community guidance restricting post-employment with foreign spyware-linked firms as part of this effort. The combination of internal guardrails, external restrictions, and international cooperation is intended to address the threat from multiple angles.
- A senior administration official reiterates that the EO is part of a series of actions addressing the multifaceted threat posed by commercial spyware proliferation and misuse.
- A senior administration official notes that the U.S. response includes measures to prevent entities from recruiting U.S. intelligence personnel to develop spyware tools, supported by recent congressional action and DNI guidance.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that export controls and Commerce Department Entity List designations are key tools the United States already uses against problematic spyware entities.
- A senior administration official frames the EO as the U.S. leading by example by establishing robust internal guardrails while also seeking to spur industry reform and cooperation with like-minded global partners.
#### Linkage to Summit for Democracy agenda
  Senior Administration Official 2 connects the EO to the upcoming second Summit for Democracy, arguing that it will serve as a cornerstone deliverable that showcases the U.S. commitment to responsible technology use. The summit, co-hosted with Costa Rica, the Netherlands, the Republic of Korea, and Zambia, is designed to advance an affirmative, rights-respecting vision for technology while countering digital authoritarianism. Over several days, cabinet and senior-level events will address advancing internet freedom, countering misuse of technology, and shaping emerging technologies to uphold human rights and democratic principles. The EO fits squarely into the summit’s track on countering misuse and is intended to catalyze joint efforts with the 121 invited partner governments.
- Senior Administration Official 2 states that the EO is a cornerstone deliverable at the second Summit for Democracy and a demonstration of U.S. leadership on countering misuse of commercial spyware.
- Senior Administration Official 2 notes that the United States is co-hosting the summit with Costa Rica, the Netherlands, the Republic of Korea, and Zambia from March 29–30, preceded by high-level events starting March 28.
- Senior Administration Official 2 reports that 121 foreign partners have been invited and expresses the expectation that the summit will spur efforts to strengthen democratic governance and show how democracies are addressing global challenges.
- Senior Administration Official 2 explains that the U.S. in-person event on March 30 at the Washington Convention Center will focus on advancing technology for democracy, with three sessions on internet freedom, countering misuse and digital authoritarianism, and shaping emerging technologies to promote human rights and democratic principles.

#### Clarifying scope and mechanisms of the EO
  During Q&A, reporters seek clarity on which spyware tools are covered, whether all spyware is banned, and whether there will be a public blacklist. Officials explain that the EO bans only commercial spyware that meets specific thresholds for counterintelligence, security, or misuse risks and builds on prior guidance and FY23 NDAA authorities, while expanding coverage beyond the intelligence community to the whole federal government. Determinations will be made on a case-by-case basis by a limited set of non-delegable, Senate-confirmed agency heads, who must rely on enhanced information-sharing processes. The EO does not create a mandatory public list of banned vendors, and it is not designed to publicly disclose individual determinations, although other tools like export controls and Entity List designations remain available for public action.
- A senior administration official clarifies that the EO does not ban all spyware but prohibits U.S. government use of commercial spyware that presents significant counterintelligence or security risks or significant risks of misuse abroad.
- A senior administration official notes that the EO builds on FY23 NDAA legislation that granted discretionary authority to the Director of National Intelligence on spyware in counterintelligence contexts but extends beyond that to cover human rights risks and all federal departments and agencies.
- A senior administration official explains that the EO creates an information-sharing process so agencies can evaluate whether derogatory information meets the specified criteria, and it sets a high bar while allowing for verifiable remedial steps by companies.
- A senior administration official states that only specified, enumerated heads of agencies and departments, whose authority is not delegable, can make determinations about spyware use, ensuring accountability to the President and Congress.
- In response to a question, a senior administration official confirms that the EO does not mandate creation of a public list of banned spyware vendors and that the public will not be notified of individual bans through the EO itself.
#### Extent of past U.S. spyware use and EO timing
  Reporters press for transparency about past use of commercial spyware by U.S. federal law enforcement or intelligence agencies and how widespread such use has been. Officials decline to provide detailed examples, pointing to prior limited disclosures to Congress, but emphasize that the EO process was intentionally launched early to get ahead of a growing risk rather than respond to widespread existing use. They recount a timeline beginning with the 2021 policy review, followed by export control measures and Entity List additions, culminating in the EO as standardized guardrails. The administration presents the EO as proactive governance aimed at preventing problematic adoption before it becomes entrenched.
- A senior administration official explains that the EO effort was initiated to get ahead of a challenge in which commercial spyware vendors were trying to make inroads across the U.S. federal government, rather than to respond to widespread existing use.
- A senior administration official notes that the administration publicly announced about a year earlier that it would pursue a ban, then started an interagency process to develop the EO involving all key departments and agencies.
- A senior administration official reiterates that policy steps began in late summer or early fall 2021 with export control rules on cyber surveillance tools and adding certain companies to the Commerce Department Entity List.
- In response to questions about specific past U.S. use, a senior administration official declines to provide further detail but indicates that the few instances on record have been addressed with Congress.
#### EO enforcement, vendor monitoring, and other tools
  Another line of questioning explores how the EO would address incidents such as an American reportedly hacked by the Predator spyware and how the government decides which vendors are acceptable. Officials clarify that the EO regulates only U.S. government operational use, while other tools such as export controls, Entity List designations, and diplomacy address foreign misuse cases. For vendors like Paragon, they explain that agencies must conduct reviews guided by the EO’s criteria, certify whether tools pose significant risks, and terminate use if new derogatory information emerges after procurement. This framework institutionalizes ongoing risk monitoring and provides a mechanism to cease use when misuse or security concerns arise.
- A senior administration official states that the EO focuses on regulating U.S. government operational use of commercial spyware and does not, by itself, impose foreign sanctions such as Entity List designations, which are handled through other authorities.
- A senior administration official notes that the U.S. also relies on export controls, diplomatic engagement, and recent restrictions on recruitment of U.S. intelligence professionals by foreign spyware firms as complementary tools to address misuse abroad.
- In response to a question about how acceptable vendors are evaluated, a senior administration official points to detailed EO provisions requiring agency heads to review relevant activity, determine whether a tool poses significant counterintelligence or security risks or risks of improper use, and certify those findings.
- A senior administration official adds that if an agency later obtains information indicating that a spyware tool meets the risk factors after it has been procured, the EO requires that the agency terminate operational use.
#### Targeting of U.S. personnel by spyware
  In response to a direct question about the scale of targeting, officials reveal that the U.S. government has identified a significant number of U.S. personnel overseas who have been targeted or potentially targeted by commercial spyware. Public reporting previously mentioned fewer than a dozen diplomats, but internal investigations show that devices associated with 50 U.S. government personnel in at least 10 countries on multiple continents are confirmed or suspected to have been targeted. This ongoing effort underscores the national security and counterintelligence rationale behind the EO and its emphasis on targeting of U.S. personnel as a key risk factor. Officials note the numbers may increase as investigations continue.
- A senior administration official confirms that U.S. personnel overseas have been targeted by commercial spyware, beyond what has been publicly reported.
- A senior administration official notes that public reports to date have identified fewer than a dozen U.S. diplomats targeted by commercial spyware.
- A senior administration official reports that the U.S. government has identified devices associated with 50 U.S. government personnel overseas in at least 10 countries on multiple continents that are confirmed or suspected to have been targeted by commercial spyware.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that efforts to identify additional targeted personnel are ongoing and that more instances may be uncovered, which is why targeting of U.S. personnel is explicitly incorporated as a key factor in the EO.
