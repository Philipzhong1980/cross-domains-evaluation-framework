### Overview
Senior administration officials briefed on a new executive order issued by President Biden that prohibits U.S. government departments and agencies from operationally using commercial spyware posing significant national security, counterintelligence, or human rights risks. This order addresses gaps in existing policies by establishing clear criteria and accountability mechanisms for evaluating spyware risks, including unauthorized targeting of U.S. personnel and misuse abroad. The officials detailed the multifaceted approach encompassing export controls, legislative actions, and intelligence community guidance to counter spyware threats. The executive order also serves as a foundational element for the upcoming second Summit for Democracy, where the U.S. and partner nations will focus on advancing democratic governance and countering digital authoritarianism through technology. The briefing highlighted confirmed targeting of approximately 50 U.S. government personnel overseas by commercial spyware, underscoring the order's protective intent. Additionally, the administration announced plans for further technology-related initiatives and guiding principles to be unveiled at the summit, reinforcing a values-driven vision for technology aligned with human rights and democratic principles.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Executive Order on Commercial Spyware
The administration announces a groundbreaking executive order prohibiting U.S. government departments and agencies from operationally using commercial spyware that poses national security or foreign policy risks, including spyware used to target U.S. personnel or enable human rights abuses. This order reflects a multifaceted response to the proliferation and misuse of commercial spyware, aiming to set clear guardrails and demonstrate U.S. leadership ahead of the Summit for Democracy.
- Senior Administration Official states President Biden will issue an executive order banning operational use of risky commercial spyware by U.S. government entities.
- The order addresses spyware misuse that threatens national security and human rights globally.
- It is part of a broader strategy including export controls and legislative actions to counter spyware threats.
- The executive order aims to lead by example internationally and spur industry reform.
#### Threat Assessment and Policy Development
Since late 2021, the National Security Council initiated a government-wide policy process to assess and counter the threats posed by advanced commercial spyware, focusing on invasive tools that can remotely access and manipulate devices without user consent. The administration identified misuse by authoritarian and democratic governments alike, including targeting of U.S. personnel, prompting actions such as export controls and entity listings.
- Senior Administration Official explains the policy process began in late 2021 to address spyware threats.
- Advanced spyware tools allow remote access and manipulation of devices covertly.
- Both authoritarian and democratic governments have misused spyware, sometimes without proper legal oversight.
- U.S. personnel overseas have been confirmed targets of commercial spyware.
#### Gaps in U.S. Government Use and Vendor Practices
The administration recognized a lack of clear, consistent guidance for U.S. agencies on using commercial spyware, even when such tools were misused abroad or against U.S. diplomats. Some vendors aggressively marketed spyware to U.S. law enforcement and intelligence agencies, sometimes obscuring business ties, highlighting the need for prohibitive measures and oversight.
- Senior Administration Official notes absence of clear direction for U.S. agencies on spyware use.
- Commercial spyware vendors sought to expand sales within U.S. government sectors, sometimes obfuscating ties.
- Congress enacted bipartisan legislation granting new authorities to the Director of National Intelligence regarding spyware.
- The intelligence community issued binding guidance restricting former officials from working with foreign spyware entities.
#### Executive Order Objectives and Structure
The executive order prohibits operational use of commercial spyware posing significant counterintelligence, security, or misuse risks, regardless of vendor origin. Its objectives include aligning spyware use with U.S. values, preventing proliferation, protecting personnel and information, and fostering international cooperation. The order introduces novel risk factors related to counterintelligence, security, and human rights abuses, and outlines remedial steps for vendors to mitigate risks.
- The order applies to spyware from both foreign and domestic entities to prevent circumvention.
- Objectives include upholding democracy, preventing misuse, protecting U.S. personnel, and leading international efforts.
- Risk factors include unauthorized access to U.S. devices, untrustworthy vendors, and use against activists or journalists.
- Remedial measures allow vendors to cancel risky contracts to reduce threats.
- The order mandates reporting, information sharing, and semiannual intelligence assessments within the executive branch.
#### Integration with Summit for Democracy
The executive order serves as a cornerstone for the upcoming second Summit for Democracy, co-hosted by the U.S. and other nations, aiming to advance democratic governance and counter digital authoritarianism. The summit will feature high-level events focusing on technology's role in democracy, misuse of technology, and shaping emerging technologies to respect human rights and democratic principles.
- Senior Administration Official 2 highlights the executive order as a key deliverable at the Summit for Democracy.
- The summit includes 121 foreign partners and multiple high-level events from March 28-30.
- Sessions will address advancing democracy online, countering technology misuse, and shaping emerging tech.
- The administration emphasizes a values-driven vision for technology supporting democratic norms.
- Additional technology-related announcements are planned during the summit.
#### Implementation and Enforcement Details
The executive order establishes a high bar for prohibiting spyware use, requiring determinations by specified agency heads based on detailed risk factors. It does not mandate a public blacklist but relies on case-by-case evaluations and internal accountability. Agencies must terminate use if risks emerge post-procurement. The order complements other tools like export controls and diplomatic engagement to address spyware misuse.
- Determinations on spyware use are made by Senate-confirmed agency heads and are non-delegable.
- No public list of banned spyware vendors will be created under the order.
- Agencies must review and terminate spyware use if new risks are identified.
- The order complements export controls, diplomatic efforts, and intelligence community guidance.
- It addresses concerns about spyware marketing and recruitment of U.S. personnel by foreign entities.
#### Extent of U.S. Personnel Targeting
The administration confirms that U.S. government personnel overseas have been targeted by commercial spyware, with identified cases involving devices of approximately 50 personnel across at least 10 countries. This ongoing effort underscores the importance of the executive order's protective measures and risk factors related to targeting U.S. individuals.
- Senior Administration Official confirms confirmed or suspected targeting of 50 U.S. government personnel overseas.
- Targets span multiple continents and at least 10 countries.
- Efforts to identify additional cases continue as part of ongoing investigations.
- Targeting of U.S. personnel is a key factor in the executive order's risk assessment.
#### Future Announcements and Guiding Principles
Building on initiatives from the first Summit for Democracy, the administration plans to release additional announcements focused on technology, including export controls, human rights initiatives, and guiding principles for government use of surveillance technology. These efforts aim to advance an affirmative vision for technology aligned with human rights and democratic values.
- Senior Administration Official 2 confirms upcoming announcements on technology at the summit.
- Planned topics include export controls, human rights initiatives, and surveillance technology principles.
- These initiatives build on commitments made at the first Summit for Democracy.
- The administration emphasizes countering digital authoritarianism and promoting responsible technology use globally.
