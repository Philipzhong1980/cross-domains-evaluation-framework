### Overview
  Two senior administration officials briefed press on a new executive order that prohibits U.S. departments and agencies from operationally using commercial spyware that poses significant counterintelligence, security, or human-rights risks. They trace the policy’s origins to a National Security Council–led interagency process begun in 2021, note prior steps such as export controls and Entity List designations, and describe complementary congressional action and recent DNI guidance to restrict post‑employment ties between intelligence personnel and foreign spyware firms. The officials explain the EO’s objectives—protecting U.S. personnel and information, preventing U.S. contribution to proliferation, aligning usage with democratic values, and enabling international cooperation—and outline its structure, including specific counterintelligence and human‑rights risk factors, non‑delegable determinations by specified agency heads, remedial steps vendors can take, internal reporting and information‑sharing, and a semiannual intelligence assessment. They frame the EO as a cornerstone deliverable for the Summit for Democracy and preview additional summit announcements on export controls, human‑rights initiatives, and guiding principles for governments’ use of surveillance technology. In Q&A, officials clarify the EO does not create a public blacklist, say determinations are case‑by‑case and non‑delegable, reiterate that some operational details remain sensitive, and confirm the government has identified devices associated with 50 U.S. government personnel in at least 10 countries (and that public reporting has named fewer than a dozen diplomats) as confirmed or suspected spyware targets.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Executive order announcement
  Senior administration officials announce a new executive order that prohibits U.S. departments and agencies from operationally using commercial spyware that poses national security, counterintelligence, or human rights risks. They frame the order as a first-of-its-kind policy to prevent misuse of invasive surveillance tools and as a demonstration of U.S. leadership ahead of the Summit for Democracy. The announcement signals a government-wide prohibition meant to close gaps in existing guidance and to align U.S. practice with values on democracy and human rights.
- Senior administration official says President Biden will issue an executive order prohibiting operational use of commercial spyware that poses national security and foreign policy risks.
- Senior administration official says the executive order demonstrates U.S. leadership and commitment to countering misuse of commercial spyware ahead of the Summit for Democracy.
- Senior administration official says the order applies to spyware furnished by both foreign and domestic commercial entities to avoid creating incentives to relocate vendors.
  #### Policy development background
  Officials describe a government-wide policy process initiated by the National Security Council in late 2021 to assess threats from commercial spyware and to craft policy responses. They explain the focus on highly invasive end-to-end intrusion suites that can remotely access and manipulate devices without user knowledge. The process led to export controls and placement of some companies on the Commerce Department Entity List as early steps in a broader strategy.
- Senior administration official says the National Security Council began an interagency policy process in late summer/early fall 2021 to assess the spyware threat.
- Senior administration official says the policy effort targeted end-to-end intrusion suites that extract and manipulate device contents without user consent.
- Senior administration official says the administration already implemented export controls and added several spyware companies to the Commerce Department’s Entity List.
  #### Misuse by governments worldwide
  Speakers outline that both authoritarian and some democratic governments have misused commercial spyware to repress dissent, intimidate journalists and activists, and target political opponents. They emphasize that misuse contributes to global human rights abuses and undermines democratic norms. Officials stress that these observed abuses inform the human-rights-related criteria in the executive order.
- Senior administration official says many foreign governments have deployed commercial spyware to facilitate repression and human rights abuses.
- Senior administration official says democratic governments have also confronted revelations of improper commercial spyware use without proper legal authorization and oversight.
- Senior administration official says human-rights-related misuse directly informs factors included in the executive order.
  #### Security and counterintelligence risks
  Officials identify distinct counterintelligence and security risks from untrustworthy spyware vendors and tools, including threats to U.S. government devices, information integrity, and the safety of U.S. personnel overseas. They confirm that U.S. personnel have been targeted and that these risks motivated the policy. The administration frames the prohibition as necessary to protect U.S. personnel and sensitive systems.
- Senior administration official says untrustworthy commercial vendors and tools can threaten the security and integrity of U.S. government devices and information.
- Senior administration official says the government has confirmed that U.S. personnel overseas have been targeted by commercial spyware.
- Senior administration official says protecting U.S. personnel and information systems is a primary objective of the executive order.
  #### Congressional and DNI actions
  Officials note bipartisan Congressional action in the FY23 NDAA that gave the Director of National Intelligence (DNI) new authorities within the intelligence community and imposed new post-employment restrictions, motivated by prior controversies. They report that the DNI issued binding guidance to implement those statutory restrictions just last Thursday. These legislative and agency steps complement the executive order’s government-wide prohibition.
- Senior administration official says Congress acted on a bipartisan basis to give the DNI new authorities on commercial spyware in the FY23 NDAA.
- Senior administration official says Congress imposed new restrictions on intelligence personnel working for foreign entities following controversies around specific companies.
- Senior administration official says the Director of National Intelligence issued binding guidance to implement new statutory restrictions last Thursday.
  #### Executive order objectives
  The officials outline the executive order’s core objectives: ensure U.S. government use of spyware upholds democracy and human rights, avoid contributing to proliferation and misuse, protect personnel and systems from counterintelligence risks, and provide a foundation for international cooperation. They also expect the order to spur industry reform by outlining responsible-use and remedial factors. These objectives shape the order’s risk-based approach.
- Senior administration official says the EO aims to align U.S. government spyware use with democratic values, the rule of law, and respect for human rights.
- Senior administration official says the EO seeks to prevent the U.S. from contributing to the proliferation and misuse of commercial spyware.
- Senior administration official says the EO intends to protect U.S. government personnel and information and to serve as a foundation for international cooperation.
  #### EO structure and risk factors
  Officials describe novel counterintelligence, security, and human-rights factors the executive order establishes for evaluating spyware risk, including use against U.S. devices, vendor trustworthiness, and documented human rights abuses. The order includes remedial steps vendors can take, non-delegable determinations by specified agency heads, internal reporting and information-sharing requirements, and a semiannual comprehensive intelligence assessment. These structural elements operationalize how agencies will assess and act on risk.
- Senior administration official says the EO sets factors such as whether spyware was used to access U.S. government devices without authorization.
- Senior administration official says the EO flags vendor behaviors like unauthorized data transfers, disclosures of nonpublic U.S. government information, or control by foreign intelligence actors as security factors.
- Senior administration official says the EO identifies human-rights factors including use against activists, journalists, or dissidents and credible reports of systematic political repression.
- Senior administration official says the EO requires specified Senate-confirmed agency heads to make determinations and makes that authority non-delegable.
- Senior administration official says the EO directs new internal reporting, information-sharing, and a semiannual comprehensive intelligence assessment.
  #### Remedial steps and enforcement
  Officials explain the EO permits concrete remedial steps vendors can take to remove identified risks—such as canceling licenses or contracts—and requires agencies to terminate operational use if new relevant derogatory information arises. The order does not itself create a public banned list; instead it establishes case-by-case determinations and internal processes to ensure accountability. Other government tools, like export controls, entity listings, diplomatic measures, and DNI guidance, remain available to address misuse.
- Senior administration official says the EO outlines concrete remedial steps vendors can take to mitigate risks, such as canceling offending licensing agreements.
- Senior administration official says agencies must terminate operational use if they later obtain information meeting the EO’s risk factors.
- Senior administration official says the EO does not mandate creation of a public list of banned vendors and emphasizes case-by-case determinations.
- Senior administration official says the government also uses export controls, entity listings, diplomatic engagement, and DNI guidance as additional tools to address misuse.
  #### Summit for Democracy linkage
  Officials frame the executive order as a cornerstone deliverable for the second Summit for Democracy, which the U.S. co-hosts and expects to use as momentum to advance a values-driven vision for technology. Senior administration official 2 outlines summit logistics, co-hosts, invited partners, and the U.S. event’s focus on advancing technology for democracy across three session themes. The administration plans additional technology-related announcements at the summit to build on this EO.
- Senior administration official 2 says the EO is a cornerstone deliverable at the Summit for Democracy and demonstrates U.S. leadership.
- Senior administration official 2 says the U.S. is co-hosting the summit with Costa Rica, the Netherlands, the Republic of Korea, and the Republic of Zambia and has invited 121 foreign partners.
- Senior administration official 2 says the U.S. summit event focuses on advancing technology for democracy in three sessions including countering misuse of technology.
  #### Public visibility and determinations process
  During Q&A officials clarify that the executive order intentionally does not require a public blacklist and that determinations will be made on a case-by-case basis by a limited set of non-delegable, Senate-confirmed agency heads. They emphasize an interagency information-sharing process to ensure determinations reflect up-to-date, all-source intelligence and that the EO sets a high bar while allowing verifiable remedial actions. Public notification of determinations is not mandated by the order.
- Senior administration official says the EO does not mandate creation of a public list of banned spyware vendors.
- Senior administration official says determinations about risk will be made only by specific enumerated heads of agencies and departments and that the authority is non-delegable.
- Senior administration official says the EO builds an information-sharing process so departments can evaluate and certify determinations based on the best available information.
- Senior administration official says the EO intends to set a high bar for prohibitions while permitting concrete, verifiable remedial steps.
  #### Past federal use and transparency
  Reporters ask whether U.S. agencies previously used commercial spyware operationally in ways that would be banned; officials decline to provide classified operational details but say public instances have been addressed with Congress. They emphasize the EO is preventive—intended to set guardrails early to avoid inconsistent standards and to get ahead of potential misuse. Officials reiterate that some uses and incidents are sensitive and cannot be detailed on the call.
- Senior administration official says the government cannot provide additional operational details about past agency use in this forum.
- Senior administration official says a few instances in the record have been addressed with Congress.
- Senior administration official says the EO is intended to set guardrails early and get ahead of inconsistent standards across the federal government.
  #### Planned summit technology announcements
  Officials preview additional announcements at the summit that build on prior commitments and fall into three thematic buckets tied to the administration’s values-driven technology agenda. They confirm follow-up announcements will include export-control and human-rights initiatives and the release of guiding principles for governments’ use of surveillance technology promised at the first summit. Officials say other initiatives are being prepared and will be shared during summit events.
- Senior administration official 2 says the administration plans announcements across three buckets reflecting an affirmative vision for technology.
- Senior administration official 2 says follow-up summit announcements will include export-control and human-rights initiatives and guiding principles for government use of surveillance technology.
- Senior administration official 2 says additional initiatives are being prepared and will be unveiled later in the week.
  #### Enforcement mechanisms and examples
  In response to questions about specific cases, officials reiterate that the EO regulates U.S. government operational use while other tools—export controls, entity listings, DNI guidance, and diplomatic engagement—address misuse abroad. They explain that agencies must review acquisitions, make determinations, and terminate use if new derogatory information meets the EO’s factors, and that these internal mechanisms work alongside other enforcement authorities. Officials emphasize the multifaceted approach across policy levers.
- Senior administration official says the EO focuses on U.S. government operational use while export controls and diplomatic measures address misuse abroad.
- Senior administration official says agencies are required to review an acquisition, certify whether significant risks exist, and terminate operational use if those risks later materialize.
- Senior administration official says the administration uses a multifaceted set of tools across export controls, entity listings, and personnel restrictions to address misuse.
  #### Extent of targeting U.S. personnel
  Reporters ask about the prevalence of targeting against U.S. personnel; officials confirm that public reporting has identified fewer than a dozen diplomats and that the government has identified devices associated with 50 U.S. government personnel in at least 10 countries that are confirmed or suspected targets. They note the identification effort is ongoing and additional instances cannot be ruled out. This empirical assessment reinforces the EO’s focus on protecting personnel.
- Senior administration official says public reports have identified fewer than a dozen U.S. diplomats targeted by commercial spyware.
- Senior administration official says the U.S. government has identified devices associated with 50 U.S. government personnel in at least 10 countries that are confirmed or suspected targets.
- Senior administration official says the effort to identify additional targeted personnel continues and more instances may emerge.
