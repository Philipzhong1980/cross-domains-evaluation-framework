### Overview
A senior administration official briefs reporters on President Biden’s upcoming White House meeting with Angolan President João Lourenço, describing it as the capstone of a historic year in U.S.–Angola relations and the 30th anniversary of diplomatic ties. They highlight unprecedented high-level engagement, including the first-ever visit by a U.S. defense secretary to Angola, expanding defense cooperation and a 2024 high-level security dialogue focused on military modernization, training, maritime security, and peacekeeping. Economically, the official emphasizes more than $1 billion in U.S. commitments to the Lobito Corridor under the Partnership for Global Infrastructure—covering rail, solar (notably a $900 million Sun Africa project), digital connectivity, and rural bridges—along with another $1 billion leveraged from the African Development Bank and the EU, positioning the corridor as a multi-sector development and investment platform that can move critical minerals, agricultural products, and other goods from the interior of Africa to global markets. They stress Angola’s diplomatic leadership in regional security, particularly through the Luanda Process on eastern DRC, and describe close coordination with the U.S., including DNI Avril Haines’s recent de-escalation mission to DRC and Rwanda, while also noting Angola’s broader SADC role in Mozambique and its strong stance on Zimbabwean elections. On global issues, the official underscores Angola’s positions on Russia’s war in Ukraine, its participation in the Partnership for Atlantic Cooperation, and its decision to sign the Artemis Accords as the third African country, reflecting a drive to include African partners in shaping future space norms. Responding to persistent questions about President Biden’s not yet visiting Africa despite earlier intentions, the official does not announce any travel but argues that a record of 16 Cabinet-level trips, major initiatives like Digital Transformation with Africa, support for AU membership in the G20, and efforts to enhance African representation in global institutions demonstrate sustained U.S. commitment, which the Biden–Lourenço meeting will further solidify by reviewing economic projects, discussing democracy and coups, and aligning on regional and global priorities.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Historic year in U.S.–Angola relations
The senior administration official outlines that President Biden’s meeting with President Lourenço caps a historic year in U.S.–Angola engagement, coinciding with the 30th anniversary of diplomatic ties. They emphasize a profound transformation in the relationship driven by Lourenço’s leadership and the Biden-Harris administration’s strategic investments and high-level contacts. The discussion frames the partnership as significantly strengthened across security, economic, and global issues. The current state is one of strong momentum that the White House intends to deepen further during the visit.
- The senior administration official states that President Biden looks forward to welcoming President Lourenço of Angola to the White House as the culmination of a historic year of partnership.
- The senior administration official notes that this transformation in relations stems from President Lourenço’s leadership and the administration’s commitment to invest in the partnership through engagements and deals.
- The senior administration official emphasizes that the U.S.–Angola relationship has gone from strength to strength over the past year.
- The senior administration official says the leaders will review progress and set a course to further deepen and expand the partnership.
#### Recent high-level U.S. engagement
The official details multiple high-level U.S. visits to Angola, including the first-ever trip by a U.S. Secretary of Defense and engagements by senior economic and development officials. These visits are presented as tangible proof of the administration’s “all-in on Africa” approach and its prioritization of Angola. The pattern of visits underscores both security cooperation and economic development as core pillars of the relationship. This extensive engagement creates the backdrop for the White House meeting.
- The senior administration official notes that Secretary of Defense Lloyd Austin made a historic first-ever visit by a U.S. defense secretary to Angola in September.
- The senior administration official reports that USAID Administrator Samantha Power, Secretary of Transportation Pete Buttigieg, EXIM Chair Reta Jo Lewis, DFC CEO Scott Nathan, and Amos Hochstein all engaged with Angolan officials this year.
- The senior administration official links these trips to the President’s directive at the African Leaders Summit to advance U.S. partnerships with African nations.
#### Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Lobito Corridor
A major focus is the U.S. flagship Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment (PGI), specifically over $1 billion in U.S. financing for the Lobito Corridor in 2023. The official explains that the corridor will connect Angola, the DRC, and Zambia to global markets through the Lobito port, addressing Africa’s low rail and road density. This rail-centered effort is described as the largest U.S. rail investment in sub-Saharan Africa and is intended to serve as a magnet for further investment. The corridor is positioned as both a development initiative and a strategic economic opportunity for the region and U.S. partners.
- The senior administration official states that the U.S. has committed over $1 billion in financing for the Lobito Corridor this year alone under PGI.
- The senior administration official explains that the Lobito Corridor will connect Angola, the DRC, and Zambia to global markets via Angola’s Lobito port.
- The senior administration official describes the Lobito Corridor as the biggest U.S. rail investment in sub-Saharan Africa.
- The senior administration official says this infrastructure responds to Africa’s very low road and rail density and the need for connectivity.
#### Regional security cooperation and Eastern DRC
The official highlights close U.S.–Angola collaboration on regional security, especially efforts to address the conflict in eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo. Angola’s diplomatic leadership, including through the Luanda Process, is acknowledged and tied to recent U.S. engagements, such as DNI Avril Haines’s travel to DRC and Rwanda. Later in the call, the official adds more detail on coordinated efforts to secure de-escalation commitments ahead of Congolese elections. The current state is one of ongoing close coordination to prevent escalation and stabilize eastern DRC.
- The senior administration official says the United States closely collaborates with Angola on regional security challenges, including the conflict in eastern Congo.
- The senior administration official notes that the U.S. supports and appreciates Angola’s diplomatic efforts in eastern DRC.
- The senior administration official reports that DNI Avril Haines recently traveled to DRC and Rwanda to support de-escalation efforts and that this was done in consultation with Angola.
- The senior administration official later explains that commitments to de-escalate tensions between DRC and Rwanda draw from the Luanda and Nairobi Processes.
- The senior administration official states that the U.S. debriefed Angolan Foreign Minister Tete on DNI Haines’s trip and will continue working with Angola, Kenya, and others to prevent escalation in eastern DRC.
#### Angola’s global leadership and Artemis Accords
The official underscores Angola’s role on global issues, including its position on Russia’s war in Ukraine and its engagement in new multilateral initiatives. Angola’s participation in the Partnership for Atlantic Cooperation and its decision to sign the Artemis Accords are cited as evidence of its evolving global profile. Later, the official elaborates that the Artemis Accords are non-binding principles guiding peaceful, transparent civil space exploration and notes Angola’s existing space program. Angola is presented as the third African country to sign, reflecting an effort to ensure African inclusion in shaping future space norms.
- The senior administration official praises Angola’s principled leadership on global issues, including Russia’s war in Ukraine.

- The senior administration official announces that Angola will sign the Artemis Accords, becoming the third African country to do so.

- The senior administration official highlights that Angola has a space program and is an important participant in shaping space norms.
- The senior administration official says Rwanda and Nigeria previously signed the Artemis Accords at the U.S.–Africa Leaders Summit.

When asked what message it sends if the President does not visit, the official again points to the record of concrete engagements, reforms, and high-level interactions with African leaders. The official avoids directly confirming or denying a 2023 visit while positioning existing actions as evidence of sustained commitment.

They argue the corridor will attract additional global and regional investment and note that U.S. tools such as the Development Finance Corporation and EXIM Bank are available to support U.S. companies. The current state is that financing mechanisms exist, and the administration expects growing private-sector engagement around the corridor.

#### Financial breakdown of Lobito-related investments
The Wall Street Journal asks for clarification on the claim of over $1 billion in U.S. financing for the Lobito Corridor and how it breaks down. The official cites two major projects: a $250 million DFC package currently under due diligence and a $900 million Sun Africa solar investment. They explain that the Lobito effort includes rail, solar energy, and digital infrastructure via Africell. Additionally, U.S. commitments have helped mobilize another $1 billion from partners: $500 million from the African Development Bank and $500 million from the European Union. The corridor is thus presented as a catalyst for significant multilateral financing.
