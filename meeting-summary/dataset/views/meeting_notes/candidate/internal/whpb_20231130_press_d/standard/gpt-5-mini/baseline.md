### Overview
  Senior administration official previews President Biden’s Oval Office meeting with Angolan President Lourenço as a capstone to a year of intensified U.S.-Angola engagement that marks the 30th anniversary of diplomatic ties. The call highlights the Lobito Corridor as a flagship Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment project—backed by U.S.-linked financing and associated upgrades in rail, solar, digital connectivity, and rural infrastructure—with major pieces including a $250 million DFC package and a large Sun Africa solar investment, and additional partner funding from the African Development Bank and the EU. Officials stress deepening security cooperation and ongoing consultations on the eastern DRC, including DNI Avril Haines’s recent diplomacy and planned 2024 defense dialogues to pursue mil-to-mil exchanges, modernization, and maritime and peacekeeping collaboration. The administration announces Angola’s signature of the Artemis Accords, frames the U.S. approach as sustained multi-agency engagement rather than a single presidential trip (no presidential visit to Africa is announced), and points reporters to available U.S. export and finance tools to enable private-sector participation in corridor opportunities while committing to continued consultation and follow-up on specific financial contributions and partner commitments.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Meeting purpose and context
  Senior administration official previews President Biden’s meeting with Angolan President Lourenço as a capstone to a year of deepening ties that coincides with the 30-year anniversary of diplomatic relations. The administration frames the relationship as transformed by Angolan leadership and extensive U.S. engagement across infrastructure, economic deals, and regional cooperation. The meeting is intended to review progress and set a course to expand the partnership.
- Senior administration official: President Biden is looking forward to welcoming President Lourenço and views the meeting as a capstone to a historic year of engagement.
- Senior administration official: The visit coincides with the 30-year anniversary of diplomatic ties and reflects a profound transformation in the bilateral relationship.
- Senior administration official: The meeting will review progress and set a course for deepening and expanding the partnership.
  #### High-level engagements overview
  The official highlights a series of high-level U.S. engagements with Angola this year as evidence of renewed U.S. commitment, including the first-ever Secretary of Defense visit and multiple Cabinet-level and agency delegations. These engagements underpin ongoing cooperation across defense, development, and economic initiatives. The visits demonstrate the administration’s strategy of sustained, multi-agency engagement rather than a single presidential trip.
- Senior administration official: The Secretary of Defense made a historic first-ever visit to Angola in September.
- Senior administration official: USAID Administrator Power, Secretary Buttigieg, EXIM Chair Rita Joe Lewis, DFC CEO Scott Nathan, and Deputy Assistant Amos Hochstein have engaged with Angolan officials this year.
- Senior administration official: Sixteen Cabinet-level or principal trips to the continent this year illustrate the administration’s renewed commitment.
  #### Lobito Corridor investment
  The administration highlights the Lobito Corridor under the Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment (PGI) as a flagship project, with over a billion dollars in U.S.-linked financing this year. The corridor aims to connect Angola, the DRC, and Zambia to global markets through Lobito port while driving broader upgrades in energy, digital connectivity, and rural infrastructure. The project is presented as catalytic—expected to attract additional public and private investment across sectors.
- Senior administration official: For PGI we have committed over a billion dollars in U.S.-linked financing to the Lobito Corridor this year.
- Senior administration official: The Lobito Corridor will connect Angola, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and Zambia to global markets via Lobito port.
- Senior administration official: The project includes rail investment and associated upgrades such as rural bridges, enhanced 4G/5G connectivity, mobile money introduction, and 500 megawatts of solar power.
  #### Presidential Africa trip status
  Reporters ask whether President Biden will announce a visit to Africa this year; the senior administration official reiterates the President’s strong commitment to the continent but has no trip to announce. The official emphasizes an alternative record of engagement via multiple senior-level visits and financial commitments. No timeline or forthcoming presidential travel is provided during the call.
- Zeke Miller (AP): Will the President provide an update on his previously stated commitment to visit Africa this year?
- Senior administration official: The President was 'all in' on Africa and directed extensive Cabinet-level engagement, but I do not have anything to announce today regarding a presidential trip.
- Senior administration official: The record of 16 principal-level trips and investment commitments is presented as evidence of ongoing presidential-level engagement even absent a visit announcement.
  #### U.S. business participation opportunities
  Reporters ask how U.S. businesses, including small firms, can participate in the Lobito Corridor and related supply-chain opportunities. The senior administration official describes project components that create commercial opportunities and highlights U.S. government financing tools—DFC and EXIM—available to support private sector engagement. The administration frames the corridor as a multi-sector opportunity attracting European and other partners as well.
- Kemi Osukoya (Africa Bazaar): How can American businesses, especially small businesses, participate in the Lobito Corridor?
- Senior administration official: The corridor supports projects such as rural bridges, digital upgrades, mobile money, and solar power that create opportunities for U.S. businesses.
- Senior administration official: U.S. agencies like the Development Finance Corporation and the Export-Import Bank offer tools to help U.S. companies invest in the corridor.
  #### Critical minerals and strategic access
  Reporters ask whether the Lobito Corridor improves U.S. access to critical minerals and whether the initiative is a response to China’s Belt and Road Initiative. The senior administration official says the corridor will facilitate export of critical minerals and a broad range of commodities, supporting U.S. investment and access, but stops short of framing it as a direct geopolitical countermeasure. The official does not have immediate information on the extent of Angolan financial contributions and offers to follow up.
- Chase Winter (Energy Intelligence): Does the Lobito Corridor expand U.S. access to critical minerals and is it a response to China’s BRI?
- Senior administration official: The corridor can help export critical minerals and multiple commodities, creating opportunities for U.S. investment and access.
- Senior administration official: I will have to get back to you on the specific financial contributions Angola is making to these projects.
  #### Defense cooperation and 2024 dialogue
  A question about defense cooperation elicits confirmation that U.S.-Angola security ties are deepening and that a high-level defense dialogue is planned for 2024. The official cites past engagement—Angola’s former defense minister status of President Lourenço and Secretary Austin’s September trip—and describes prospective mil-to-mil exchanges focused on modernization, education, language capacity, maritime security, and peacekeeping. No specifics on deployments or instructor programs are provided.
- Mida (Voice of America): What is the status of a defense cooperation agreement and potential deployment of U.S. military instructors?
- Senior administration official: Angola and the U.S. will hold a high-level defense dialogue in 2024 to discuss agreements and deepen mil-to-mil exchanges.
- Senior administration official: Areas of focus include military modernization, education, English language capacity, maritime security, and peacekeeping.
  #### Financing breakdown for projects
  Reporters request specifics on the over-a-billion-dollar financing figure for the Lobito Corridor; the senior administration official provides a breakdown, naming a $250 million DFC package and a $900 million Sun Africa solar investment. The official also says the U.S. investment has catalyzed additional contributions, citing roughly $500 million from the African Development Bank and another $500 million from the EU. The summary frames the U.S. financing as both direct investment and a catalyst for partner funding.
- Michael Phillips (Wall Street Journal): Can you break down the stated over-$1 billion financing for the Lobito Corridor?
- Senior administration official: Major items include a $250 million DFC package and a roughly $900 million Sun Africa solar investment.
- Senior administration official: The U.S. investment has corralled additional partner funding, including about $500 million from the African Development Bank and about $500 million from the EU.
  #### Deliverables and democracy issues
  Reporters ask whether any deliverables will be announced and whether the presidents will discuss democracy. The official confirms that the signature of the Artemis Accords is a concrete deliverable and says both presidents will discuss the state of democracy and concerns about coups in the region. The official frames these discussions as part of a broader agenda on governance and regional stability.
- Raquel (Global Brazil): Will there be any deliverables announced and will the President talk about democracy?
- Senior administration official: One deliverable to share is Angola’s signature of the Artemis Accords, which will be announced with the President’s meetings.
- Senior administration official: The presidents will discuss the state of democracy globally and in Africa, including regional concerns about coups.
  #### Message about presidential travel decisions
  Reporters press on what it signals that President Biden has not yet visited Africa despite earlier indications; the official responds by enumerating extensive U.S. engagement and initiatives since the Africa Leaders Summit. The administration cites record-breaking personnel visits, major investments, the Digital Transformation initiative, efforts to include African voices in global governance, and the Vice President’s diaspora advisory council as the substantive message. The official frames these actions as a strong demonstration of commitment despite the absence of a presidential trip announcement.
- AFP: By not visiting Africa this year, what message does the President send to the continent?
- Senior administration official: The administration points to a record of engagement including 16 Cabinet-level trips, multi-billion-dollar investments, and the Digital Transformation initiative as the clearest message of commitment.
- Senior administration official: The Vice President launched a Presidential Advisory Council on African Diaspora Engagement to deepen people-to-people ties, underscoring broader engagement beyond presidential travel.
  #### Trafigura, OPEC+, and DRC consultations
  Reporters ask whether Trafigura or local mining firms have committed to using the railway and whether OPEC+ or Congo-related topics are on the agenda. The senior administration official has no update on Trafigura commitments but confirms that the leaders will consult on economic investments and regional issues including the DRC, with emphasis on aligning efforts like the Luanda process and recent DNI engagement. The official underscores that territorial integrity, sovereignty, and U.N. Charter principles will be part of broader consultations.
- Trevor Hunnicutt (Reuters): Do Trafigura or local miners have commitments to use the railway, and will OPEC+ or Congo-related topics be discussed?
- Senior administration official: I do not have an update on commitments from Trafigura or local mining companies at this time.
- Senior administration official: The two presidents will review economic investments and consult on regional issues, including the eastern DRC and alignment with the Luanda process.
  #### Artemis Accords details and historical ties
  Reporters ask for more detail on the Artemis Accords and whether President Biden is aware of Angola’s historical ties to the United States. The senior administration official explains that the Artemis Accords are non-binding principles to guide peaceful, sustainable, transparent civil space cooperation and notes Angola as the third African signatory. The official also says President Biden has long experience with Angola through his Senate work and is well informed about the bilateral history.
- Hariana Veras (TPA): Can you give more details on the Artemis Accords and does the President understand Angola’s historical ties to the U.S.?
- Senior administration official: The Artemis Accords are non-binding principles that set norms for peaceful, sustainable, and transparent civil space cooperation.
- Senior administration official: Angola is the third African country to sign the Accords, and President Biden is well informed about Angolan history and the bilateral relationship from his long experience.
  #### Zimbabwe sanctions and Angola’s SADC role
  Reporters ask whether Angola’s regional role influenced recent U.S. Treasury actions on Zimbabwe sanctions and whether there will be more briefings on DRC outcomes. The official notes close consultation with Angolan partners, describes DNI Haines’s recent de-escalation efforts in the DRC and Rwanda, and says the administration briefed Angolan Foreign Minister Tete. The senior administration official emphasizes Angola’s leadership in SADC and its involvement in regional security issues.
- Pearl Matibe (defenceWeb): Did Angola’s regional role factor into the Treasury’s removal of Zimbabwean sanctions and will there be a briefing on DRC outcomes?
- Senior administration official: DNI Haines traveled to Kinshasa and Kigali to secure commitments to deescalate tensions and debriefed Angolan Foreign Minister Tete yesterday.
- Senior administration official: Angola is a prominent SADC leader and has been active on regional security, including in Mozambique and critiques of Zimbabwean elections.
  #### Final follow-up on presidential visit
  A final question revisits the absence of a presidential trip to Africa; the official reiterates there is no announcement but emphasizes the administration’s record of concrete investments, institutional reforms, and multilateral actions as the substantive signal of commitment. The official cites recent calls and multilateral engagements as examples of continuing high-level attention to African partners. No new commitments or travel plans are provided on the call.
- Andrew (The Independent): What does it say that the President said he would go to Africa this year but has not done so?
- Senior administration official: There is nothing to announce on a presidential trip today, and the administration points to its record of engagement and concrete outcomes as the message to African partners.
- Senior administration official: Recent calls with African leaders and sustained multilateral engagement, including work at the G20 and IFI reforms, demonstrate ongoing presidential-level attention.
