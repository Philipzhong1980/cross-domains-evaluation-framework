### Overview
National Economic Adviser Lael Brainard uses the call to frame how the pandemic exposed severe supply chain vulnerabilities and drove inflation, then outlines how President Biden’s early executive actions, taskforces, and major legislation like the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, CHIPS and Science Act, and Inflation Reduction Act underpin the administration’s response and have helped reduce supply chain stress and inflation. She previews that the President will launch a White House Council on Supplier Resilience and roll out 30 actions across agencies, including a Defense Production Act determination empowering HHS to tackle drug shortages, $35 million for domestic production of key starting materials for sterile injectables, a new HHS supply chain resilience and shortage coordinator, and an upcoming Department of Defense report on pharmaceutical supply chain resilience. Brainard also announces new institutional infrastructure: a DHS Supply Chain Resilience Center with tabletop exercises focused on port and cross-border risks, and a first-of-its-kind Commerce supply chain center that uses industry expertise and data analytics, alongside a DHS–Commerce partnership to secure semiconductor supply chains and support CHIPS implementation. In the background Q&A, senior administration officials stress that the overarching strategy is to diversify critical supply chains using both domestic investments and international partnerships to avoid dangerous hyperconcentration, describe how DHS’s data and security capabilities complement Commerce’s economic authorities in semiconductors, address questions about FLOW data-sharing and cybersecurity in ports, and clarify that HHS has secured multiyear funding for the new coordinator while the DPA determination will be issued this week as one of several steps to strengthen domestic manufacturing and reduce drug shortages.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Pandemic supply chain impacts
Director Lael Brainard explains that the COVID-19 pandemic causes severe disruptions across global and domestic supply chains, leading to shortages in semiconductors, vehicles, appliances, and everyday goods, which in turn contributes to inflation. She notes that some corporations raise consumer prices more than their own input costs, intensifying the inflation surge. This context underscores why the administration makes supply chain resilience a central economic priority. The discussion frames subsequent policy actions as responses to these vulnerabilities and inflationary pressures.
- Director Brainard states that the pandemic brings tremendous human and economic hardship and creates unprecedented stress in supply chains, resulting in shortages from semiconductors to household goods.
- Director Brainard explains that when supply chain stress intensifies and input prices rise, some corporations increase consumer prices by even more, contributing to a surge in inflation.
#### Early Biden supply chain actions
Brainard outlines the administration’s initial policy steps to tackle supply chain stress, including a day-one executive order, the creation of a Supply Chain Disruptions Taskforce, and sector-specific measures in shipping and trucking. She highlights legislative achievements such as the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, the CHIPS and Science Act, and the Inflation Reduction Act as foundational investments in transportation systems and key inputs. These actions aim to reduce bottlenecks, support domestic production, and enhance long-term resilience. The current state is that these measures are in place and underpin the improved conditions she describes.
- Director Brainard recalls that in his first month in office, President Biden signs an executive order on America’s supply chains and establishes a Supply Chain Disruptions Taskforce.
- Director Brainard notes that the President secures important actions on shipping and trucking through the Ocean Shipping Reform Act and the Trucking Action Plan.
- Director Brainard says the President secures the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law in his first year and the CHIPS and Science and Inflation Reduction Acts in his second year to enable investments in transportation systems and key inputs.
#### Progress on inflation and supply chains
Brainard reports that supply chain stress has fallen from record highs to record lows and links this improvement to a 65 percent decline in inflation from its peak. She points to the Global Supply Chain Pressure Index and CPI inflation as visual evidence of this relationship. While noting positive impacts on prices for items like turkeys and gasoline over Thanksgiving, she stresses that work remains to ensure corporations pass savings on to consumers. The administration positions this progress as validation of its policies but emphasizes ongoing efforts.
- Director Brainard says that as a result of President Biden’s actions, supply chain stress is reduced from record highs to record lows and inflation declines by 65 percent from its peak.
- Director Brainard notes that the relationship between the Global Supply Chain Pressure Index and CPI inflation visually demonstrates the link between easing supply chain pressures and lower inflation.
- Director Brainard highlights that lower supply chain stress is showing up in lower prices from turkeys to gas prices this Thanksgiving, while emphasizing determination to keep working to bring down prices further.
#### Launch of White House Council on Supplier Resilience
Brainard announces that President Biden will launch a White House Council on Supplier Resilience to coordinate and advance a set of 30 new actions to strengthen U.S. supply chains and reduce costs for families. The council will draw on multiple agencies’ expertise and serve as a central mechanism to drive long-term resilience efforts. This initiative reflects the administration’s shift from crisis response to structural reform of critical supply chains. The council’s work is ongoing and will guide many of the actions described in the fact sheet and call.
- Director Brainard states that President Biden will launch a White House Council on Supplier Resilience and announce 30 new actions to strengthen American supply chains and bring down costs for families.
- A senior administration official explains that these actions are part of the President’s long-term strategy to support resilience of critical supply chains, building on work done over the last two years, including a day-one executive order.
#### Defense Production Act for essential medicines
Brainard details a forthcoming presidential determination that expands the Department of Health and Human Services’ authorities under the Defense Production Act (DPA) to address drug shortages and invest in domestic production of essential medicines. HHS will dedicate $35 million to bolster U.S. production of key starting materials for sterile injectable medicines and create a new supply chain resilience and shortage coordinator role. In the Q&A, officials clarify that HHS has secured multiyear funding for the coordinator despite congressional rescissions and will name the individual later. The DPA determination will be issued this week and is one of several steps to improve domestic pharmaceutical manufacturing and reduce drug shortages over time.
- Director Brainard says the President will issue a presidential determination to broaden HHS authorities under the Defense Production Act to mitigate drug shortages and invest in essential medicines in America.
- Director Brainard notes that HHS has identified $35 million for investments in domestic production of key starting materials for sterile injectable medicines and will designate a new supply chain resilience and shortage coordinator.
- A senior administration official explains that HHS is announcing the creation of the coordinator position, has secured multiyear funding despite congressional rescissions, and will announce the actual coordinator at a later date.
- A senior administration official states that the presidential determination under the Defense Production Act will be released this week and that the administration hopes to see results as soon as possible, while acknowledging that addressing drug shortages is a very hard problem.
- A senior administration official adds that the DPA determination is one of many steps the administration will take to invest in domestic manufacturing capabilities and address overall drug shortage issues.
#### Pharmaceutical supply chain diversification strategy
In response to questions about pharmaceutical inputs from China and possible shifts to India, officials emphasize a broader strategy to diversify critical supply chains using both domestic tools and international partnerships, rather than naming specific country substitutions. They stress that hyperconcentration of production in a single region creates geopolitical, economic, and climate-related risks, as seen during the pandemic. The Department of Defense’s upcoming report on pharmaceutical supply chain resilience will contribute to this effort. The current approach focuses on working with allies and partners to reduce regional concentration while strengthening domestic capacity.
- A senior administration official responds that the goal of actions, including the Department of Defense’s report on pharmaceutical supply chains, is to use domestic tools and international partnerships to diversify supply chains for critical goods rather than rely on a single country.
- A senior administration official states that hyperconcentration of production in one region can become a problem because it heightens exposure to geopolitical, economic, and climate shocks, as experienced during the pandemic.
- A senior administration official notes that agencies with specific expertise are leveraging their strengths through the council’s work and related initiatives to secure pharmaceutical supply chains.
#### DOD report on pharmaceutical resilience
Brainard notes that the Department of Defense will soon release a report on pharmaceutical supply chain resilience, which will inform government planning and risk mitigation. This report will examine vulnerabilities and outline strategies to ensure reliable access to medicines. In the broader discussion, officials describe it as part of the diversification and resilience strategy for critical health-related goods. The report is pending release and will complement DPA actions and HHS initiatives.
- Director Brainard announces that the Department of Defense will soon release a new report on pharmaceutical supply chain resilience.
- A senior administration official links the forthcoming DOD report to the broader goal of diversifying supply chains for critical goods using both domestic measures and international partnerships.
#### Semiconductor supply chain resilience and CHIPS Act
Both Brainard and senior officials highlight a coordinated effort by the Department of Commerce and the Department of Homeland Security to strengthen semiconductor supply chains and support CHIPS and Science Act implementation. Officials explain that DHS brings access to cross-border data and security-focused industry partnerships, while Commerce contributes trade and economic authorities; together, they will support the resurgence of chip production in the United States. In Q&A, officials frame this collaboration as a response to risks from hyperconcentration in certain regions, including concerns raised about Taiwan and Chinese threats, without naming specific geopolitical scenarios. The partnership is ongoing and will evolve as CHIPS funding identifies major U.S. semiconductor players.
- Director Brainard states that DHS and the Department of Commerce will collaborate to strengthen the semiconductor supply chain and further implementation of the CHIPS and Science Act.
- A senior administration official tells Paris Huang that the goal of the Commerce–DHS collaboration on semiconductor supply chains is to diversify supply chains for critical goods using domestic tools and international partnerships, in light of risks from hyperconcentration in one region.
- A senior administration official explains to Jeff Mason that DHS has unprecedented access to cross-border information and unique industry partnerships from a security standpoint, while Commerce has trade-focused economic authorities, making their partnership important and complementary.
- A senior administration official notes that as CHIPS funding progresses and the government learns who the major U.S. semiconductor players are, the agencies will work to fully support the resurgence of chip production in the United States.
#### DHS Supply Chain Resilience Center and port risks
Brainard announces that the Department of Homeland Security will establish a new Supply Chain Resilience Center and run tabletop exercises to test critical cross-border supply chain resilience, with near-term priorities focused on risks in U.S. ports. In discussion with reporters, senior officials add that this center will particularly focus on critical infrastructure risks that affect the flow of goods. These efforts aim to anticipate and mitigate disruptions, including cyber or physical threats, across ports and logistics networks. The center is being set up and will coordinate with other initiatives like the FLOW data-sharing effort.
- Director Brainard states that the Department of Homeland Security will launch a new Supply Chain Resilience Center and new tabletop exercises designed to test the resilience of critical cross-border supply chains.
- Director Brainard notes that near-term priorities for DHS will include addressing supply chain risks resulting from threats and vulnerabilities inside U.S. ports.
- A senior administration official says the new DHS Supply Chain Resilience Center will focus on risks from critical infrastructure as they relate to the flow of goods.
- A senior administration official emphasizes that this critical infrastructure risk focus will be crucial as DHS works together with partners referenced in the FLOW initiative.
#### Commerce supply chain center and risk analytics
Brainard announces that the Department of Commerce will create a new, first-of-its-kind supply chain center to integrate industry expertise and data analytics to develop innovative risk-assessment tools. This center will enhance the government’s ability to identify vulnerabilities and support policy decisions across critical sectors, including semiconductors. In combination with DHS’s efforts, it reflects a more data-driven approach to supply chain governance. The center is in the launch phase and will underpin ongoing cross-agency collaboration.
- Director Brainard states that the Department of Commerce will launch a new, first-of-its-kind supply chain center to integrate industry expertise and data analytics for innovative supply chain risk-assessment tools.
- A senior administration official underscores that Commerce’s economic and trade authorities complement DHS’s data access and security partnerships in their joint work on supply chains, including semiconductors.
#### FLOW data-sharing and cybersecurity concerns
In response to a question about interoperability, data sharing, and hacking risks in port systems, a senior official describes the FLOW initiative as a collaborative, pay-to-play data-sharing environment where multiple industry players and the federal government contribute and benefit. The official argues that broader participation yields a fuller picture of port operations and is designed to strengthen, not weaken, supply chain links. Another official adds that addressing these data and infrastructure risks will also be a focus of the new DHS Supply Chain Resilience Center. Specific cybersecurity technical controls are not detailed on the call, but the emphasis is on cross-collaboration and risk-focused design.
- Lori Anne asks how the government ensures that data interoperability and sharing under initiatives like FLOW do not create cybersecurity vulnerabilities in port systems.
- A senior administration official answers that FLOW’s strength lies in collaboration across industry and the federal government to create an interoperable data set, with participants incentivized to join by gaining insights from competitors and improving visibility into port operations.
- A senior administration official explains that participation in FLOW is effectively pay-to-play, where industry players benefit from shared data while contributing their own, yielding a fuller picture of what is happening in the port system.
- A senior administration official notes that managing risks from critical infrastructure and data flows related to the movement of goods will be a key focus area for the new DHS Supply Chain Resilience Center.
#### Creation and funding of HHS shortage coordinator
During Q&A, officials elaborate on the new HHS supply chain resilience and shortage coordinator role previously mentioned by Brainard. They clarify that HHS has secured multiyear funding to make the position durable, despite congressional rescissions of some pandemic-related funds. The individual to fill the role will be announced later, and the coordinator will help lead efforts to mitigate drug shortages and oversee resilience investments, including the $35 million for key starting materials. This role is central to institutionalizing lessons from the pandemic in the health supply chain domain.

- A senior administration official replies that HHS is announcing the creation of the coordinator position now, has secured multiyear funding despite congressional rescissions, and will announce the coordinator at a later date.
- A senior administration official reiterates that HHS has identified $35 million for investments in domestic production of key starting materials as part of this broader effort to address drug shortages.
#### Use of domestic and international partnerships
Throughout the Q&A, senior officials repeatedly stress that the administration’s supply chain strategy combines domestic investments and tools with international partnerships and alliances. They position diversification—away from hyperconcentration in any single region—as the core principle guiding actions in pharmaceuticals, semiconductors, and other critical goods. This approach aims to mitigate geopolitical, economic, and climate-related risks while supporting U.S. manufacturing. The strategy is ongoing and informs the work of the new council, DHS and Commerce centers, and agency reports.

- A senior administration official notes that multiple agencies with different expertise are leveraging their authorities through the council and related initiatives to secure and diversify critical supply chains.
