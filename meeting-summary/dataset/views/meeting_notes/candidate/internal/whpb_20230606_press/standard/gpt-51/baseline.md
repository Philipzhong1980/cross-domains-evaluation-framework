### Overview
The briefing, led by Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre with extensive participation from NSC spokesman John Kirby, centers on domestic economic initiatives and major foreign policy developments, particularly in Ukraine. Jean-Pierre introduces Invest.gov as a transparency tool for President Biden’s “Investing in America” agenda, highlighting substantial private investment and tens of thousands of infrastructure projects, and uses a new $115 million EPA commitment to Jackson, Mississippi’s water system to illustrate how infrastructure and rescue funds address long-neglected communities nationwide. She defends the Inflation Reduction Act’s Medicare drug price negotiations against Merck’s lawsuit and broader Big Pharma criticism, asserting constitutional authority and a continued focus on lowering healthcare costs despite anticipated litigation, and fields questions on border policy, city-level migrant pressures, and the President’s recent fall, stressing policy results and continuity of operations. Kirby provides a detailed but cautious update on the Nova Kakhovka dam’s destruction in Ukraine, emphasizing severe flooding, humanitarian needs, risks to energy and canal infrastructure, and the legal implications under the Geneva Conventions while noting the U.S. has not yet determined responsibility, and he places this event within sustained U.S. military, humanitarian, and financial support for Kyiv that remains bounded by opposition to Ukrainian strikes inside Russia. He refuses to comment on leaked intelligence or U.S. investigations regarding the Nord Stream sabotage, points to European-led inquiries, outlines ongoing efforts to manage tensions with Iran and China through sanctions and diplomacy, and underscores that F-16s for Ukraine are for long-term conventional defense amid Russian nuclear rhetoric. Across topics—from Ukraine funding timelines and NATO leadership succession to Biden’s planned Africa trip, Indo-Pacific tensions, and the PGA–LIV merger with Saudi backing—the administration consistently emphasizes reliance on allies, diplomacy, and existing legal processes while declining to prejudge ongoing investigations, lawsuits, and regulatory questions.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Launch of Invest.gov and agenda
Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre announces the launch of Invest.gov, a new website showcasing how President Biden’s “Investing in America” agenda is driving job creation and infrastructure investment nationwide. She emphasizes the scale of private sector commitments and infrastructure projects and notes that the bipartisan budget agreement protects these investments. The administration positions this as core to rebuilding manufacturing, strengthening energy security, and lowering costs. The site is intended as a state-by-state transparency tool illustrating tangible local benefits from federal policy.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that Invest.gov went live to show how President Biden’s Investing in America agenda is bringing jobs and investments to every U.S. state.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that Invest.gov’s interactive map tracks about $470 billion in private sector manufacturing and clean energy investments mobilized by the President’s agenda.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre highlights that the site reflects roughly 32,000 infrastructure projects announced or underway due to the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre explains that Invest.gov presents state and territory-level statistics on infrastructure, investment, job creation, and cost-lowering impacts.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the bipartisan budget agreement the President secured protects these investments and will sustain recent economic progress.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre announces that officials will conduct a second “Investing in America” tour around the July 4th recess to showcase local impacts.
#### Jackson, Mississippi water investment
Jean-Pierre highlights a new federal commitment to support Jackson, Mississippi’s long-troubled water system as a tangible example of the Investing in America agenda. President Biden directs EPA to deploy $115 million in initial funding to stabilize and rebuild the local water infrastructure. The administration frames this as part of a broader effort to ensure all Americans have access to safe drinking water, especially in communities that have faced repeated crises. She later connects this investment to larger funding streams from the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, the American Rescue Plan, and a $600M congressional allocation for Jackson’s water system.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre announces that President Biden directed EPA to deploy $115 million to help repair Jackson, Mississippi’s water system and deliver clean water to residents.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre recalls that Jackson’s water system was in crisis last summer and that residents have long endured frequent boil-water orders.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says this initial investment will help stabilize and rebuild Jackson’s system and reflects the President’s commitment to universal access to safe drinking water.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre later notes that the $115 million is part of more than $600 million Congress has made available for Jackson’s water system in the FY 2023 funding bill.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre stresses that the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law and the American Rescue Plan are key vehicles for supporting Jackson and similar communities.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre confirms that infrastructure funds are designed to address aging water systems, roads, and bridges nationwide, including cities like Flint and Jackson.
#### Merck lawsuit and Medicare negotiation
Jean-Pierre addresses a new lawsuit by Merck and broader Big Pharma opposition to Medicare drug price negotiations enabled by the Inflation Reduction Act. She underscores that the law allows Medicare to negotiate lower prices, caps insulin for seniors at $35, and requires rebates when drug prices rise faster than inflation. The administration argues there is no constitutional barrier to these negotiations and rejects industry claims that lower prices will harm innovation. While deferring legal specifics to DOJ, she insists the President remains focused on lowering healthcare costs and expects to continue implementing the law despite litigation.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre recalls that the Inflation Reduction Act authorizes Medicare to negotiate drug prices for the first time, caps seniors’ insulin costs at $35, and mandates rebates when prices rise faster than inflation.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre criticizes pharmaceutical companies for earning record profits while charging middle-class families high prices for often decades-old drugs.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre argues there is nothing in the Constitution that prevents Medicare from negotiating lower drug prices for seniors.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the Biden administration will fight in court against Big Pharma’s attempts to roll back historic drug pricing reforms.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre maintains that the administration is confident it will prevail in the Merck lawsuit but declines to discuss legal strategy, citing ongoing litigation.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre dismisses Merck’s argument that negotiations will hurt innovation and reiterates that the President prioritizes lowering costs so families do not have to choose between bills and lifesaving medications.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre refuses to speculate about additional lawsuits from other drug makers but emphasizes the President will keep pursuing policies that deliver lower healthcare costs.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre restates that DOJ will handle the legal arguments, including Merck’s Fifth and First Amendment claims, while the White House continues implementing the law.
#### Nova Kakhovka dam destruction
National Security Council spokesman John Kirby briefs on the destruction of Ukraine’s Nova Kakhovka dam, which is causing extensive flooding and humanitarian impacts. He explains the scale of the infrastructure, its role in supplying water and power, and notes Russian forces have occupied it since last year. The U.S. has not reached a conclusive assessment on responsibility or intent, but stresses that the damage to civilians, the energy grid, canals, and nearby nuclear safety considerations is significant. The U.S. is coordinating with Ukraine and humanitarian partners to provide assistance and will share more information as assessments develop.
- Mr. Kirby says the Nova Kakhovka dam’s destruction has triggered massive flooding and forced the evacuation of at least thousands of Ukrainians, with likely casualties and deaths that are not yet quantifiable.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Russian forces illegally seized and have occupied the dam and reservoir since last year.
- Mr. Kirby confirms the U.S. has seen reports blaming Russia for the explosion but says Washington cannot yet conclusively determine what happened or who is responsible.
- Mr. Kirby describes the dam as about 30 yards high and 100 yards wide, protecting a reservoir holding roughly as much water as Utah’s Great Salt Lake, which supplies southern Ukraine, Crimea, and the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant.
- Mr. Kirby welcomes the IAEA director’s statement that there is no immediate risk to the safety of the Zaporizhzhia plant from the dam incident.
- Mr. Kirby warns that the dam’s destruction could seriously damage Ukraine’s energy security and canal systems, in addition to causing loss of life and livelihoods.
- Mr. Kirby states the U.S. is in touch with Ukrainian authorities and humanitarian partners to provide aid to displaced civilians.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that Russia’s unprovoked war has brought enormous suffering to Ukraine and that the United States will continue military, humanitarian, and economic support.
- Mr. Kirby tells reporters it is too soon to assess how the dam’s destruction might affect Ukrainian counteroffensive operations and reiterates he will not comment on Ukrainian military plans.
- Mr. Kirby declines to say whether the U.S. has determined the attack was intentional or whether it had to be conducted from inside the structure, noting assessments are ongoing.
- Mr. Kirby later underscores that deliberate destruction of civilian infrastructure, including dams, is prohibited under the Additional Protocol to the Geneva Conventions that Russia has signed, but he stops short of declaring the incident a war crime pending further assessment.
- Mr. Kirby says President Biden was briefed earlier in the day on the dam explosion but notes there is no decision yet to declassify related intelligence.
