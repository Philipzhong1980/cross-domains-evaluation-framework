### Overview
  National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan and White House Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre provide a Hiroshima briefing that covers the G7 summit's unified approach to Ukraine, China, economic security, and climate action, and explains several concrete outcomes and commitments. Sullivan details an expanded, coordinated sanctions campaign against Russia, announces U.S. support for allied training of Ukrainian pilots on fourth‑generation fighters (including F‑16s) while noting delivery decisions and timelines will be determined with partners, and describes the sequencing of military aid based on battlefield needs. He previews G7 economic-security tools—including supply‑chain resilience, export controls, and a referenced outbound investment screening—explains the communiqué's candid but non‑hostile approach to the PRC (de‑risk, not decouple), highlights clean‑energy commitments and PGII project announcements, and summarizes Quad priorities such as secure digital technology and submarine cables; he also says leaders began work on international AI norms and reiterated support for denuclearization of the Korean Peninsula. Jean‑Pierre shifts focus to the domestic front, reiterating President Biden’s March budget priorities (a stated $3 trillion deficit reduction over 10 years), emphasizes that default is not negotiable, and explains the President is returning early to lead bipartisan budget talks while giving negotiators space to work; she stresses optimism that a negotiated outcome is possible if both sides negotiate in good faith and that the administration continues to engage regularly with congressional leaders and allies. The briefing attributes decisions and positions to Sullivan and Jean‑Pierre, notes key follow-ups (training implementation and outbound investment authorities to be worked out), and underscores the administration’s twin goals of sustaining support for Ukraine and avoiding a U.S. default.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### G7 summit unity
  National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan frames the Hiroshima summit as a high-water mark demonstrating close alignment among G7 democracies across security, economic, and climate issues. He says the leaders’ joint communiqué will reflect that unity and outline common approaches to current global challenges. The summit sets the stage for coordinated action on multiple fronts and signals sustained transatlantic and allied cooperation.
- National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan says the summit is a high-water mark demonstrating real unity among key democracies.
- Jake Sullivan says the joint leaders' statement will show close alignment on Ukraine, China, economic security, and the clean energy transition.
  #### Sanctions on Russia
  Sullivan outlines an expanded, coordinated sanctions campaign against Russia, noting roughly 300 recent sanctions targeting individuals, entities, vessels, and aircraft. He emphasizes focus on circumvention and evasion, financial facilitators, and restrictions to limit Russia's energy and defense-industrial capabilities. The U.S. actions are presented as closely coordinated with the EU and UK to maximize economic pressure.
- Jake Sullivan says the United States has announced about 300 sanctions against Russian individuals, entities, vessels, and aircraft.
- Jake Sullivan says sanctions now target circumvention and evasion mechanisms and financial facilitators to tighten economic pressure on Russia.
- Jake Sullivan says U.S. sanctions are more tightly coordinated with the EU and UK to ensure aligned economic pressure.
  #### F-16 pilot training
  Sullivan confirms President Biden informed G7 counterparts the United States will support a joint effort to train Ukrainian pilots on fourth-generation fighters, including F-16s. He frames training as the initial step while allies and partners determine which countries will deliver aircraft, how many, and delivery timing. The training decision reflects planning for Ukraine’s longer-term air capability rather than immediate battlefield needs.
- Jake Sullivan says the United States will support a joint effort to train Ukrainian pilots on fourth-generation fighter aircraft, including F-16s.
- Jake Sullivan says allies will determine over the coming months when planes will be delivered, who will deliver them, and how many.
- Jake Sullivan says training on F-16s is intended to build Ukraine's future force capability rather than to meet immediate counteroffensive needs.
  #### Timing and escalation concerns
  Reporters press Sullivan on why the training decision comes now, whether it could escalate the conflict, and how long training will take. Sullivan explains the U.S. approach has adapted to battlefield phases—initial air-defense, then artillery and ground systems—and that F-16s were not a top priority for the current counteroffensive. He reiterates the U.S. policy that capability transfers are not to enable strikes on Russian territory and that Ukrainians have indicated they will abide by that principle.
- Jake Sullivan says the U.S. provision of weapons and training evolves with the conflict's exigencies and that F-16s were not a top priority for the spring counteroffensive.
- Jake Sullivan says U.S. support for capabilities to Ukraine comes with the proposition that the U.S. will not enable attacks on Russian territory.
- Jake Sullivan declines to fix a training timeline and says military experts will set the detailed schedule for F-16 training.
  #### Military aid sequencing
  Sullivan outlines how U.S. assistance has shifted through phases—Stinger and Javelin anti-armor for early defense of Kyiv, artillery and ammunition for the Donbas campaign, and then tanks and other systems for the counteroffensive. He notes Western partners have provided spare parts and Soviet-era aircraft that sustain Ukraine's current air capability while preparations for a longer-term transition to Western fighters proceed. The intention is to sustain air capability as stocks of legacy aircraft decline.
- Jake Sullivan says U.S. assistance has followed battlefield phases, from Stingers and Javelins to artillery and, later, tanks and ammunition.
- Jake Sullivan says Western partners have provided spare parts and Soviet-era aircraft to sustain Ukraine's current air capability as planning for a longer-term transition continues.
  #### Economic security tools
  Sullivan says the G7 will outline common economic-security tools to counter economic coercion and protect sensitive technology, including supply-chain resilience measures, export controls, and outbound investment measures. He indicates the leaders' communiqué will reference outbound investment screening as a broad tool while leaving legal specifics to each country. The United States is developing legal authorities for targeted outbound investment controls and views the G7 statement as a milestone.
- Jake Sullivan says G7 leaders will outline tools to address economic coercion, including supply-chain resilience, export controls, and outbound investment measures.
- Jake Sullivan says the leaders' statement will reference outbound investment screening at a high level and that legal details will be determined by each country.
- Jake Sullivan says the United States is developing legal authorities for targeted outbound investment controls and will lay them out at an appropriate moment.
  #### Approach to China (PRC)
  Sullivan explains the G7 communiqué will set out key elements of a common approach to China while recognizing that each country retains an independent relationship. He reiterates the U.S. position of 'de-risk, not decouple' and characterizes the communiqué language as candid but not hostile. He attributes the convergence of views to intensive consultations over the past two and a half years.
- Jake Sullivan says the G7 communiqué will reflect shared key elements for approaching China while acknowledging each country's independent relationship.
- Jake Sullivan says the official line is to 'de-risk, not decouple' from China and that the communiqué will be direct but not gratuitously hostile.
- Jake Sullivan says the alignment on China reflects intensive consultations and convergence over the past two and a half years.
  #### Clean energy and PGII
  Sullivan says G7 leaders will commit to bolder action on the clean energy transition, linking public and private investment to resilient supply chains for critical minerals and technologies. He frames the G7 clean energy statement as extending President Biden's economic agenda and notes coordination with partners on aligning the Inflation Reduction Act with other members' policies. Sullivan also highlights forthcoming Project on Global Infrastructure and Investment (PGII) announcements to mobilize public and private finance for infrastructure projects.
- Jake Sullivan says G7 leaders will deliver bold action to accelerate the clean energy transition and build resilient supply chains for critical minerals.
- Jake Sullivan says the G7 clean energy statement will align elements of the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act with steps other members are taking.
- Jake Sullivan says the PGII will announce projects and aim to mobilize private-sector investment alongside public funding.
  #### Quad summit and submarine cables
  Sullivan previews the in-person Quad meeting with Australia, Japan, and India, expecting outcomes on secure digital technology, submarine cables, and infrastructure capacity building. He defers specific submarine-cable language to the Quad outcome document but says the Quad wants to finance and build trusted, secure cable infrastructure. The Quad meeting is presented as part of broader regional infrastructure and digital connectivity priorities.
- Jake Sullivan says the President will join Australia, Japan, and India for the Quad summit, with expected outcomes on secure digital technology and submarine cables.
- Jake Sullivan says the Quad seeks to play a role in financing and constructing trusted and secure submarine cable infrastructure.
  #### Artificial intelligence discussion
  Sullivan notes that G7 leaders discussed frontier artificial intelligence at a previous high-level meeting and that President Biden briefed peers on engagements with major AI companies. Leaders tasked their teams to work on an international format to align norms and standards for AI risk management. The discussion is an initial step toward international coordination on fast-moving AI technologies.
- Jake Sullivan says leaders discussed artificial intelligence and that the President briefed them on a White House meeting with frontier AI CEOs.
- Jake Sullivan says leaders have tasked their teams to work together on an international format for AI norms and standards.
  #### Nuclear disarmament concerns
  Leaders addressed nuclear disarmament with a focus on the goal of a world without nuclear weapons and the specific threat from the DPRK. Sullivan says the G7 supports complete denuclearization of the Korean Peninsula and will continue to align approaches, particularly with Japan and the Republic of Korea. He notes there is no set timeframe and that coordination will continue.
- Jake Sullivan says G7 leaders support the objective of complete denuclearization of the Korean Peninsula.
- Jake Sullivan says leaders will continue aligning their approaches with Japan and the Republic of Korea on DPRK threats.
  #### Arms sourcing and allied support
  Sullivan describes the multinational sourcing effort for Ukraine's needs—artillery ammunition, air defense supplies, and other systems—coordinated through Ramstein and U.S. engagement with dozens of countries. He declines to discuss specifics for individual countries, noting sensitivities, and reiterates that securing materiel for Ukraine remains a high priority. The U.S. continues to work with partners to identify available and necessary materiel.
- Jake Sullivan says the U.S. has worked with dozens of countries to source artillery ammunition, air defense supplies, and other capabilities for Ukraine.
- Jake Sullivan says specifics for individual countries can be sensitive and declines to comment on any single partner's contributions.
  #### Zelenskyy meeting logistics
  Reporters ask about President Zelenskyy's travel to Japan and whether President Biden will meet him. Sullivan says the U.S. did not fly Zelenskyy and that it is a safe bet President Biden will meet him, though he has no formal announcement. Jean-Pierre reiterates the President looks forward to meeting Zelenskyy and defers travel details to the Ukrainians.
- Jake Sullivan says the United States was not the country that flew President Zelenskyy to Japan and expects President Biden will meet him.
- White House Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre says the President looks forward to the opportunity to meet with President Zelenskyy.
  #### Engagements with Lula and Modi
  Sullivan rejects the term 'pressure' for bilateral engagements and says President Biden will speak constructively with leaders like President Lula and Prime Minister Modi about supporting sovereignty, territorial integrity, and other cooperative topics such as PGII and debt burdens. He says the President will thank partners for constructive actions and discuss a range of issues beyond Ukraine. The tone is described as relationship-driven rather than coercive.
- Jake Sullivan says President Biden will speak with President Lula and Prime Minister Modi constructively about the role their countries can play in supporting sovereignty and territorial integrity.
- Jake Sullivan says the President will also discuss PGII, mobilizing investment, and multilateral development bank evolution with emerging-economy leaders.
  #### U.S. debt-limit and budget talks
  Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre addresses continued differences between the White House and House Republicans over the debt limit and budget. She reiterates the President's budget priorities—including a March proposal that reduces the deficit by $3 trillion over 10 years by cutting subsidies and investing in growth—and emphasizes that default is not negotiable. The President returns early from the trip to help lead negotiations, the White House expresses cautious optimism about a bipartisan outcome, and negotiators are given space to continue talks.
- White House Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre says the President's budget reduces the deficit by $3 trillion over 10 years and targets wasteful subsidies.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says default is not negotiable and warns that a default would plunge the economy into recession and risk jobs and retirement savings.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the President is confident there is a path to a bipartisan budget agreement if both sides negotiate in good faith.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the President is returning from Hiroshima early to lead efforts to resolve the debt-limit negotiations.
  #### Debt negotiations: process and posture
  Reporters press for details on transparency, spending caps, short-term extensions, and whether the White House is negotiating over the debt ceiling. Jean-Pierre repeatedly distinguishes between the non-negotiable debt ceiling (must be lifted) and ongoing budget negotiations over spending priorities, declines to negotiate in public, and says negotiators should have space to work. She emphasizes the President's directive that the debt limit not be used as leverage and that the administration will stay engaged and updated.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the White House will not negotiate about specifics in public and insists the debt limit must be addressed without conditions.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says budget discussions are regular-order negotiations about spending priorities and that negotiators need space to reach a bipartisan agreement.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says she has no new details to share about potential short-term extensions or timelines and that the President is being updated regularly.
  #### Vice President's role and updates
  Reporters ask why Vice President Harris is not leading negotiations on the Hill while the President is abroad. Jean-Pierre rejects the premise that the President does not trust the Vice President, noting Harris has participated in recent meetings and remains engaged. She reiterates that the President receives frequent updates and has been directly involved in leader-level discussions with congressional leaders.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says Vice President Harris has been involved in recent negotiations and has regular conversations with the President.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the President is being updated multiple times daily and remains directly engaged in the negotiation process.
  #### Allied interest in U.S. negotiations
  Reporters ask whether allied leaders view U.S. debt-limit talks with alarm. Sullivan and Jean-Pierre both characterize allied interest as keen but not panicked; Sullivan says leaders want a sense of how negotiations will play out, while Jean-Pierre says the situation has not diminished the President's stature. The White House reiterates its message that the U.S. will not default and is confident it can reach a resolution.
- Jake Sullivan says G7 partners are keenly interested in the outcome of U.S. debt-limit negotiations and want a sense of how they will play out.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the debt-limit talks have not damaged the President's stature among foreign leaders and reiterates the U.S. commitment to avoid default.
  #### President's travel and calendar
  Jean-Pierre reviews the President's immediate schedule: return from Hiroshima on Sunday evening, East Room ceremonies for NCAA champions on Friday, travel to Camp David, and Memorial Day events the following week. She frames the truncated trip as necessary to allow the President to lead on the debt-limit effort. The White House will provide additional calendar details as plans firm up.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the President will return to the White House from Hiroshima on Sunday and will host ceremonial events and Memorial Day observances the following week.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the President shortened his trip to be back in the U.S. to help lead debt-limit negotiations.
