### Overview
Senior U.S. officials use the Hiroshima press briefing to present the G7 as highly unified on major global challenges, with National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan detailing new, closely coordinated sanctions on Russia, long‑term F‑16 pilot training and broader arms support for Ukraine, a shared G7 framework on economic security and China built around "de‑risking, not decoupling," and joint commitments on clean energy, infrastructure (via PGII), AI governance, submarine cable security, and nuclear and North Korea policy, while also highlighting President Biden’s engagement with leaders such as Zelenskyy, Lula, Modi, and other emerging powers. Sullivan underscores that other G7 members follow U.S. debt ceiling negotiations with keen interest but not alarm as Biden cuts short his trip and expresses confidence in avoiding default. Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre then focuses on the domestic fiscal picture, repeatedly stressing that default is unacceptable and should be resolved without conditions while describing separate, difficult but ongoing budget talks aimed at a "reasonable bipartisan" agreement that reduces the deficit along Biden’s preferred lines, defending the President’s and Vice President Harris’s active roles, rejecting claims that the White House is negotiating over the debt limit itself, and insisting that despite tactical setbacks, the administration remains optimistic about reaching a deal that can pass both the House and Senate without undermining U.S. credibility at home or abroad.

He explains that additional sectors tied to Russia’s military-industrial complex will fall under expanded authorities, and new bans will limit Russia’s access to Western services. These measures are tightly coordinated with EU and UK actions to maximize impact. The goal is to further constrain Russia’s ability to sustain its war effort and long-term energy and extractive ambitions.
