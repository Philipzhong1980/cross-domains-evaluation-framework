### Overview
The briefing centers on President Biden’s imminent executive order launching the first whole-of-government strategy to build resilient U.S. supply chains, with NSC official Peter Harrell and NEC Deputy Director Sameera Fazili explaining that COVID-19 and recent shortages of PPE and semiconductors exposed structural vulnerabilities the administration now intends to prevent through 100‑day product reviews, year-long sectoral reviews, targeted incentives, stockpiling, and robust public‑private and allied partnerships that also create high-quality jobs. Reporters probe the economic implications, funding plans for semiconductors, and how success will be measured, while Psaki repeatedly underscores that the initiative is about long-term preparedness, workforce development, and rebuilding domestic and allied capacity rather than “going it alone.” Beyond supply chains, Psaki outlines several immediate domestic actions: a program to distribute more than 25 million American-made masks to vulnerable communities via health centers and food banks; ongoing federal support for states recovering from severe winter storms, including Texas, ahead of Biden’s trip to Houston; and continued progress on COVID vaccines, tempered by surprise at Johnson & Johnson’s lower‑than‑expected initial output, emerging virus variants, and state-level deviations from federal vaccination guidance. She defends the administration’s border policy on unaccompanied minors by contrasting it with the previous administration’s family separations and describing HHS shelters like Carrizo Springs as a difficult but necessary interim solution while children are vetted and placed with sponsors, and she fields extensive questions on the embattled nomination of Neera Tanden, insisting that Biden is still “fighting for” her confirmation, that outreach to senators continues, and that talk of replacements is premature. On foreign policy, Psaki previews a recalibration of U.S.–Saudi relations built around leader-to-leader engagement with King Salman, an imminent declassification of the Khashoggi report, and a willingness by Biden to speak out on human rights even as security cooperation continues; she also outlines an Iran approach that seeks a return to JCPOA compliance as a base for addressing missiles and regional behavior while carefully managing responses to proxy attacks in Iraq. Additional exchanges touch on tensions with Russia and the ongoing U.S. review of cyber and election interference, questions about Yemen and the Houthis, voting rights and potential federal legislation, targeted support for Black communities, the minimum wage in the COVID relief bill, military vaccine hesitancy, and governors’ discretion in vaccine prioritization, collectively portraying an administration trying to advance an ambitious domestic and foreign agenda while navigating legislative constraints and complex crises in real time.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Executive order on supply chains
White House Press Secretary Jen Psaki opens by announcing that President Biden will sign an executive order launching a whole-of-government initiative to secure critical U.S. supply chains. She frames the COVID-19 pandemic and recent shortages as evidence that the United States needs more resilient supply chains and stronger domestic manufacturing to avoid crises and create good-paying jobs. Psaki also notes the President will meet with a bipartisan group of lawmakers to discuss supply chain resilience, underscoring broad political support. She introduces Peter Harrell and Sameera Fazili as key officials to explain the order’s details.
- Ms. Psaki states that President Biden will sign an executive order to secure critical U.S. supply chains and support good-paying jobs.
- Ms. Psaki explains that COVID-19 exposed vulnerabilities in supply chains and domestic manufacturing, making resilience a national priority.
- Ms. Psaki notes that the order has strong bipartisan support and that the President will meet with a bipartisan group of House and Senate members on supply chains later in the day.
- Ms. Psaki introduces Sameera Fazili and Peter Harrell as administration experts to brief on the executive order and answer questions.
#### Scope and structure of supply chain initiative
Peter Harrell details the executive order as the first comprehensive, whole-of-government strategy to build resilient supply chains across critical sectors. He cites past shortages of PPE and current chip shortages in auto manufacturing as examples of vulnerabilities that the initiative aims to prevent rather than react to. He explains that the EO launches immediate 100‑day reviews of four critical product supply chains and mandates six sectoral reviews within a year, modeled on Defense Department industrial base assessments. Harrell stresses that the administration intends to act on findings, not just generate reports, using tools like incentives, stockpiling, surge capacity, and allied coordination.
- Mr. Harrell recalls that frontline health workers lacked masks and PPE early in the pandemic and that automakers now face production cuts due to chip shortages.
- Mr. Harrell says President Biden previously pledged to secure supply chains so America never again faces shortages of critical products in crises.
- Mr. Harrell describes the EO as the first whole-of-government approach to supply chain resilience, aiming to move from crisis reaction to prevention.
- Mr. Harrell explains that the EO orders 100‑day reviews of four critical product supply chains: semiconductors, large-capacity batteries, pharmaceuticals and active ingredients, and critical minerals including rare earths.
- Mr. Harrell adds that six sector-specific reviews will assess defense, public health and biological preparedness, ICT, transportation, energy, and food production within one year.
- Mr. Harrell emphasizes the administration will take actions to close identified gaps and is already working with industry to address the auto chip shortage.
- Mr. Harrell notes the administration expects to use a mix of tools, including incentives for domestic production, stockpiling, surge capacity planning, and coordination with allies.
- Mr. Harrell says all tools, including potentially the Defense Production Act, are on the table for building resilient supply chains, though he declines to discuss DPA use for specific sectors.
- Mr. Harrell characterizes U.S. discussions with Taiwan on semiconductor supply as constructive but declines to detail diplomatic conversations.
#### Economic and workforce framing of supply chain policy
Sameera Fazili positions supply chain resilience as an economic opportunity to create well-paying jobs, rebuild the middle class, and fulfill the President’s Build Back Better agenda. She argues that pre-pandemic economic conditions—low wages and job losses from technological and global shifts—left workers vulnerable, and that constant crises show the need for preparedness. Fazili outlines that sector reviews will examine broad categories of risk, including climate, geopolitical, workforce, and industrial capacity, and will recommend actions that may use federal data, procurement, stockpiling, and new legislative tools. She stresses extensive stakeholder engagement, public-private partnerships, small-business development, worker training, geographic and racial equity, and collaboration with allies rather than isolationism.
- Ms. Fazili states that building resilient supply chains is an opportunity to create well-paying jobs and supports the President’s Build Back Better commitment.
- Ms. Fazili notes that even before COVID-19, many Americans faced low wages and the loss of core middle-class jobs due to technology and global economic shifts.
- Ms. Fazili says the U.S. has lurched from crisis to crisis when essential goods ran short, and argues the EO moves government toward better preparedness.
- Ms. Fazili explains that sector-specific reviews will assess risks across supply chains and the domestic industrial base, including climate risk, geopolitical risk, worker shortages, and inadequate factory capacity or equipment.
- Ms. Fazili says agencies will recommend resiliency actions, ranging from publishing data to using procurement authority to support stockpiles or domestic production.
- Ms. Fazili indicates the administration will seek additional tools from Congress and will treat the day’s meeting with lawmakers as part of an ongoing dialogue.
- Ms. Fazili emphasizes broad stakeholder consultation, including business, labor, local communities, and academia, and says public-private partnerships will be critical to solving complex supply chain issues.
- Ms. Fazili highlights opportunities for small businesses to diversify supplier networks and reduce “too big to fail” risks in critical goods supply chains.
- Ms. Fazili stresses the need to improve worker readiness and training to support rapid scaling of research, production, and distribution for key products.
- Ms. Fazili underscores the goal of bringing jobs to communities nationwide, including communities of color, and leveraging U.S. scientific leadership and R&D by pairing “thinkers and doers.”
- Ms. Fazili clarifies that the strategy is not about America “going it alone” and that ramping up domestic production is not always the answer, given global supply chain interdependence.
- Ms. Fazili states the U.S. will work with allies and partners to reduce shared supply chain vulnerabilities and sees this effort as part of a historic pattern of strategic investments that drive broad-based growth.
- Ms. Fazili notes that supply chain weaknesses developed over decades and argues they can be addressed through long-term, smart investments in research, manufacturing, and the workforce.
#### Jobs impact and measures of success
In response to questions on manufacturing job creation, Fazili declines to give a numerical jobs forecast for the supply chain initiative. Instead, she argues that traditional manufacturing job counts understate total employment impact because manufacturing activity generates R&D, supplier, and service jobs. The administration signals it will adopt a broader lens when measuring success, looking at the wider economic ecosystem rather than just factory headcount.
- An unidentified reporter asks how many factory jobs the initiative can create and what metrics will define success, noting manufacturing jobs declined from 17 million in 2000 to 12.2 million.
- Ms. Fazili responds that manufacturing’s impact goes beyond factory jobs to include R&D, supplier networks, and services employment, suggesting metrics should capture broader economic spillovers.
#### Funding tools and semiconductor support
Questioned about federal funding for semiconductors in the forthcoming budget, Fazili says the budget process is still underway and declines to specify proposed allocations. She reiterates the administration’s intent to work with Congress on funding to strengthen supply chains, including chips. Harrell adds that solutions will differ by supply chain and that the administration will present a comprehensive, tailored toolkit for each critical product. He indicates that potential tools encompass incentives for domestic production, surge capacity planning, stockpiling, and cross-border coordination with allies.
- An unidentified reporter asks if the upcoming budget will propose federal funding to boost semiconductor supply.
- Ms. Fazili says she cannot yet detail budget specifics but confirms the administration wants to work with Congress to secure needed funds.
- Mr. Harrell explains that supply chains such as semiconductors and rare earths differ structurally and will require tailored policy solutions.
- Mr. Harrell says the administration will use a mix of tools, including incentives for U.S. production, planning for surge capacity, stockpiling, and coordination with allies to maintain open cross-border flows for critical goods.
#### Mask distribution to vulnerable communities
Psaki announces a federal program to distribute over 25 million high-quality, American-made masks to low-income and hard-hit populations through community health centers and food banks starting next month. She ties the program to existing HHS funding and CDC standards, positioning it as a targeted effort to close gaps in basic protective equipment. The aim is to ensure any American in need can obtain a free, well-fitting mask through these networks, thereby improving equity in COVID protection.
- Ms. Psaki says the administration will deliver more than 25 million masks to over 1,300 community health centers and about 60,000 food pantries nationwide starting next month.
- Ms. Psaki notes the program targets hard-hit, low-income populations that still lack access to basic protective masks.
- Ms. Psaki emphasizes that the masks are American-made and compliant with CDC guidelines and that any American can walk into participating sites and receive one.
- Ms. Psaki explains that the program uses existing HHS funding and is intended to ‘level the playing field’ by giving vulnerable communities access to quality, well-fitting masks.
- In response to a question, Ms. Psaki confirms the masks are cloth-based and CDC-compliant but says she does not have a public list of manufacturers to share yet.
#### Business support for the American Rescue Plan
Psaki highlights a letter from over 160 CEOs of major companies urging Congress to pass President Biden’s COVID relief package, the American Rescue Plan. She uses the letter to underscore growing national consensus, citing polling that shows broad bipartisan public support. The CEOs argue that prior relief was essential but insufficient to ensure a strong, durable recovery, aligning with the administration’s push for additional stimulus.
- Ms. Psaki reports that more than 160 CEOs of major U.S. companies have sent a letter calling on Congress to enact the President’s American Rescue Plan.
- Ms. Psaki says the CEOs’ letter argues that previous relief measures were essential but that more must be done to set the country on a path to strong, durable recovery.
- Ms. Psaki notes that public polling shows bipartisan majorities support the Rescue Plan, which she frames as evidence of broad national consensus.
- In response to a question, Ms. Psaki clarifies that outside business groups organized the CEO letter and that the White House did not play an organizational role, though officials regularly engage with business leaders.
#### Winter storm recovery and federal assistance
Psaki provides an operational update on recovery from the recent winter storm affecting multiple states, with a particular focus on Texas. She notes that temperatures have normalized and transportation systems largely returned to normal, but water outages and boil-water advisories remain significant problems. The administration is using FEMA and other federal resources to support ongoing needs, has approved additional county-level assistance, and is working to fill delayed vaccine shipments while vaccination sites make up missed appointments.
- Ms. Psaki states that temperatures in storm-affected states have returned to normal for the season, but water system outages and boil-water advisories remain a serious issue.
- Ms. Psaki says delayed COVID-19 vaccine shipments from the storm are expected to be made up in the coming days and that vaccination sites are doubling appointments to compensate for cancellations.
- Ms. Psaki reports that major transportation infrastructure, including airports, rail carriers, highways, transit agencies, and ports, has largely returned to normal operations.
- Ms. Psaki notes that federal assistance continues, including disaster declarations for additional counties, and that further requests will be considered.
#### President’s COVID testing and White House protocols
To maintain transparency, Psaki discloses that President Biden tested negative for COVID-19 on Monday, part of his regular testing regimen. She indicates the White House will continue to provide next-day updates on test results going forward. She also reiterates that the White House complex continues to follow strict mitigation measures, including masks and social distancing, to protect staff and leadership.
- Ms. Psaki reports that President Biden tested negative for COVID-19 on Monday as part of regular safety protocols.
- Ms. Psaki says the White House will aim to provide test-result updates the following day in the future to maintain transparency.
- Ms. Psaki reiterates that the White House complex adheres to strict mask-wearing, social distancing, and other mitigation measures.
#### Russia tensions and cyber and election interference
Responding to questions about Vladimir Putin’s comments on countering foreign powers, Psaki reiterates U.S. concern over Russia’s recent actions, including election interference, hacking, and alleged bounties on U.S. troops. She notes that President Biden has ordered a comprehensive review of Russian activities, including the SolarWinds cyber intrusion and 2020 election interference. Biden has already raised concerns directly with Putin and plans to respond after the review concludes, which she says will take weeks, not months.
- An unidentified reporter asks whether the White House sees tensions with Russia increasing after President Putin vowed to counter foreign interference.
- Ms. Psaki says the administration views recent Russian actions, including election interference, alleged bounties on U.S. troops, and the SolarWinds hack, as deeply concerning.
- Ms. Psaki notes that President Biden has directed his national security team to assess Russian activities around the 2020 election, cyber intrusions, and other malign behavior.
- Ms. Psaki recalls that President Biden spoke with President Putin recently and did not hold back in expressing concerns about Kremlin actions.
- Ms. Psaki states that the review of Russia-related issues will conclude in weeks, not months, and that the U.S. will respond once its own assessment is complete.
#### Forthcoming Khashoggi report and U.S.–Saudi relations

Psaki confirms the administration remains committed to releasing an unclassified report “soon” via the DNI and expects a Biden–King Salman call in the near future, with a readout afterward. She reiterates that Biden intends to recalibrate relations by having counterparts communicate with counterparts, meaning the President will speak with the King while Defense Secretary Austin engages Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS). While declining to preview policy responses until after the report’s release and the call, Psaki notes Biden’s long-held critical views of Saudi leadership and his readiness to speak out on human rights, balanced against ongoing security cooperation with Riyadh.

#### Neera Tanden nomination status

She stresses that President Biden remains committed to Tanden, describing her as highly qualified and actively engaging senators and stakeholders to build support. Psaki downplays committee vote delays as part of due diligence rather than a setback, rejects discussion of potential replacements such as Shalanda Young, and emphasizes that the confirmation is a “numbers game” focused on securing at least one Republican vote. She declines to say whether Tanden has offered to withdraw and confirms that the White House conducts wide but not universally pre-announced consultation on nominees, including with Senator Sanders after the fact.
- Ms. Psaki says President Biden is ‘fighting for’ Neera Tanden’s nomination and underscores her expertise as critical during an unprecedented economic crisis.
- Ms. Psaki notes that Tanden and the White House are in close touch with senators and key constituencies, conducting outreach, answering questions, and reiterating her qualifications.
- Ms. Psaki characterizes the postponement of a committee vote on Tanden as an opportunity for further outreach, not a setback, pointing to committee leadership’s statement about building support.
- Asked whether delays undercut Biden’s budget agenda, Ms. Psaki stresses Tanden’s record of working with people who disagree with her and says that skill will be valuable if she is confirmed.
- Ms. Psaki rejects speculation about Shalanda Young as a replacement, reiterating that “there’s one nominee” for OMB and that the administration is focused on Tanden.

Psaki addresses concerns that Johnson & Johnson will initially ship far fewer vaccine doses than its federal contract envisioned. She confirms that the administration was surprised to learn that J&J is behind on manufacturing and now expects only 3–4 million doses ready to ship upon FDA authorization, rather than the roughly 10 million initially projected for February. Psaki says the White House is working with the company to accelerate production, while recognizing that FDA authorization is still pending.
- Kaitlan asks whether President Biden is disappointed that Johnson & Johnson will have only 3–4 million doses ready to ship, versus the 10 million its contract anticipated for February.
- Ms. Psaki says the administration was surprised by Johnson & Johnson’s production delays, which reduced the initial expected supply from about 10 million to 3–4 million doses.
- Ms. Psaki notes that the Johnson & Johnson vaccine is still undergoing FDA review and that no shipments will occur until authorization is granted.
- Ms. Psaki says the administration will continue working with Johnson & Johnson to expedite manufacturing if and when the vaccine is approved.

At the same time, she acknowledges a longstanding bilateral relationship and ongoing regional threats facing Saudi Arabia where cooperation continues. #### New York nursing homes and federal lessons learned

She acknowledges that many lessons will emerge from the pandemic and notes New York’s early, severe impact as a key context. However, she stresses that the administration is currently focused on working with governors from all states to expand vaccine distribution, improve communication, and increase vaccination sites, leaving retrospective policy debates for later.
- A reporter asks whether New York’s nursing home reporting controversy should spur federal standards for nursing home data and outbreak guidance.

#### Unaccompanied minors and border facilities
Psaki faces detailed questioning about the administration’s handling of unaccompanied migrant children and the use of HHS and CBP facilities that resemble those used under the previous administration. She draws a sharp distinction between Biden’s policies and prior “kids in cages” family separations, emphasizing that the administration does not separate children from their parents at the border and does not summarily return unaccompanied minors to dangerous journeys. Instead, she says, CBP facilities serve as short-term intake centers and HHS facilities—like the reopened Carrizo Springs site—provide schooling, medical care, and revamped conditions while children are vetted and placed with sponsors. She acknowledges some minors stayed over the 72-hour legal limit due to weather-related delays and limited COVID-safe capacity and frames the use of HHS shelters as a difficult but necessary middle option while the system is under strain.
- A reporter asks how the White House describes the Carrizo Springs HHS facility, noting photos of container-like units and comparisons to ‘kids in cages.’
- Ms. Psaki stresses that unlike the previous administration, the Biden administration is not separating children from their parents at the border, calling family separation immoral.
- Ms. Psaki explains that the government faces three options for unaccompanied minors: sending them back on a dangerous journey, placing them with sponsors without vetting, or housing them temporarily in HHS facilities while vetted placements are arranged, and says the administration has chosen the third option.
- Ms. Psaki describes the Carrizo Springs HHS facility as revamped, with teachers and medical services, and says the goal is to move children quickly to vetted families or sponsors.
- A reporter notes reports that CBP is using tent facilities with chain-link fencing in Donna, Texas, similar to prior years, and asks why some children have been held over 72 hours.
- Ms. Psaki distinguishes CBP intake centers from HHS shelters and says the goal is to transfer children from CBP to HHS within 72 hours.
- Ms. Psaki acknowledges that due to severe weather and power issues at destination facilities, some children remained in CBP custody for four to five days or longer, contrary to the administration’s objectives.
- Asked about Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez’s criticism that the Carrizo Springs facility is “not okay,” Ms. Psaki calls the situation heartbreaking and reiterates that the administration is working to process children quickly into safer HHS settings and then into family placements.
- In response to a question about press access, Ms. Psaki suggests reporters contact DHS regarding potential media visits, citing safety and privacy constraints.

She says Houston was chosen partly so Biden can also visit a vaccine distribution site and that the trip’s timing and location were coordinated with FEMA and local officials. Psaki notes that announcements of new aid are being made continuously as resources are approved and says nothing is being held back specifically for the trip.
- A reporter asks if Biden’s infrastructure plan to modernize the grid can proceed without federal oversight of Texas’s largely independent electric grid.

- Ms. Psaki says long-term discussions about weatherization and infrastructure resilience will come later and may be addressed as part of the President’s broader Build Back Better agenda.

- A reporter asks if the choice of Houston is related to Senator Ted Cruz; Ms. Psaki laughs and says no, explaining that Houston allows the President to visit both storm-affected communities and a vaccine distribution site.

#### Iran nuclear diplomacy and regional security concerns
Later in the briefing, Psaki addresses questions about U.S. policy toward Iran, especially whether ballistic missiles and regional activities will be included in nuclear negotiations. She reiterates that Iran is currently far from compliance with the JCPOA and that the U.S. has merely expressed openness to an EU-facilitated diplomatic meeting, without offering sanctions relief. Biden’s strategy, she says, is to restore mutual compliance with the nuclear deal as a platform for a “longer and stronger” agreement that also addresses missiles and regional behavior. On recent rocket attacks in Iraq, she confirms that the U.S. holds Iran responsible for its proxies and reserves the right to respond at a time and manner of its choosing, while avoiding uncalculated escalation that could destabilize Iraq.
- A reporter asks whether the administration will link Iran’s ballistic missiles and regional interference to nuclear negotiations or treat them separately.
- Ms. Psaki says Iran is a long way from nuclear compliance and that if Iran returns to full compliance, the U.S. will do the same and then seek a ‘longer and stronger’ agreement that includes ballistic missiles and regional concerns.
- Ms. Psaki notes that the U.S. has only expressed openness to an EU invitation for talks and has not offered sanctions relief, while awaiting Iran’s response.
- Asked about rocket attacks in Iraq, the reporter inquires whether Iran is testing U.S. resolve.
- Ms. Psaki states that the U.S. holds Iran accountable for actions of its proxies and reserves the right to respond at a time and in a manner of its choosing, using both seen and unseen tools.
- Ms. Psaki emphasizes that the U.S. will not lash out in ways that risk escalation and further destabilize Iraq, contrasting this approach with past responses.

She refuses to speculate on whether Biden would sign a bill without the wage hike, saying the process must play out first and that any compromise will occur among senators after the “Byrd bath” review.
