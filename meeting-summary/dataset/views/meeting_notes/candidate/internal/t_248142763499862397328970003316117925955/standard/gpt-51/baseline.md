### Overview
The Human Relations Commission meeting focuses on integrating three new commissioners, reviewing midyear progress on its FY 2022–2023 work plan, filling subcommittee vacancies, and electing new leadership as the body prepares to return to in‑person meetings. Principal Management Analyst Laurel James first announces the immediate resignation of Commissioner Ragstraw, then reviews ongoing annual tasks such as participating in the coming Spring Family Parade and observing Council Neighborhoods Committee sessions, and outlines the status of three priority projects: a May 25 housing insecurity storytelling event at the Community Center, a bystander intervention training effort initiated by the Council’s Race, Equity and Inclusion Subcommittee, and a tentative late‑June mental health community dialogue led by Commissioners Nwokido and Sylvester. After detailed introductions in which continuing commissioners (Sylvester, Webb, and Nwokido) share advice and new commissioners (Baird, Smith, and Poicon) describe their backgrounds and goals, the Commission hears deeper updates on the housing and mental health subcommittees and clarifies that bystander training will adapt existing models rather than create curriculum from scratch. The group then assigns all members to subcommittees—Housing Insecurity (Sylvester, Webb, Poicon), Mental Health (Sylvester, Nwokido, Smith), and Bystander Intervention (Webb, Baird, Poicon)—ensuring each project has sufficient capacity. In officer elections, the Commission unanimously elects Ida Rose Sylvester as Chair and Nirvana Nwokido as Vice Chair for 2023 after Laurel explains their responsibilities. Laurel also reminds members about upcoming Brown Act training, Form 700 filings via NetFile, and confirms that the next regular meeting on February 2, 2023, will be held in person at City Hall’s Plaza Conference Room. The session closes with commissioners expressing enthusiasm about the energized Commission and the opportunity to advance housing, equity, and mental health initiatives together in the coming months.

#### Housing insecurity storytelling project update
Ida Rose Sylvester and Xavier Webb provide more detail on the housing insecurity storytelling subcommittee and planned event. The concept is to host a community forum where unhoused, unstably housed, and stably housed residents sit together at mixed tables to discuss experiences of housing, initially without disclosing individuals’ housing status, followed by a segment where those experiencing housing insecurity share their stories publicly and engage in Q&A. The subcommittee is partnering with Reach Potential Movement and plans to recruit additional community organizations to ensure respectful participation and representation. The event is set for May and still has one open commissioner seat.
- Ida Rose Sylvester explains that the housing insecurity event will bring together residents with a range of housing stability, including unhoused and unstably housed community members, for facilitated round‑table conversations about what it means to be housed.
- Ida Rose Sylvester describes a planned design in which unhoused or unstably housed participants sit at mixed tables without their housing status being identified, to foster candid dialogue across the community.
- Ida Rose Sylvester notes that a second portion of the event would invite participants experiencing housing insecurity to share their stories from the front of the room, respond to audience questions, and potentially pose questions back to the community.
- Ida Rose Sylvester reports that the subcommittee has been working closely with Reach Potential Movement and intends to bring in additional partners in the coming weeks to identify and support participants.
- Ida Rose Sylvester confirms that the housing insecurity event is slated for May 25, 2023 at the Community Center and that the subcommittee currently consists of herself and Xavier Webb with one commissioner opening.
- Jocelyn Baird asks whether the subcommittee is already partnering with local safety‑net service providers or still designing its outreach approach, and Ida Rose Sylvester responds that new partners will be engaged soon to ensure participants feel respected and included.
#### Mental health subcommittee scope and direction
Nirvana Nwokido and Ida Rose Sylvester describe the early-stage work of the mental health subcommittee, which aims to destigmatize mental health discussions and connect residents with resources. The subcommittee has met twice and is still defining its focus and event format, recognizing the breadth and sensitivity of the topic. Ideas under consideration include a panel of experts, community testimonies on accessing services, curated local resource lists, and practical strategies for day‑to‑day coping. One commissioner seat is open, and the final emphasis (e.g., general adult support versus youth or school‑specific issues) will depend partially on the third member’s input and further planning.
- Nirvana Nwokido explains that the mental health subcommittee, currently comprised of herself and Ida Rose Sylvester, is in an early planning phase focused on creating a safe space for community discussions about mental health and reducing stigma.
- Nirvana Nwokido notes that the subcommittee is exploring possible formats such as expert panels, presentations, Q&A, and personal testimonies about using mental health services and their impact.
- Nirvana Nwokido emphasizes that the overarching goal is to destigmatize mental health so that residents feel more comfortable seeking help and accessing local resources.
- Nirvana Nwokido clarifies that the city does not have dedicated funding for this topic, which influences the scope and structure of what the subcommittee can deliver and reinforces the need for partnerships with local organizations.
- Ida Rose Sylvester adds that the subcommittee is still clarifying its specific objectives, but agrees that normalizing open conversation about mental health is core, with details to be shaped by the final subcommittee membership.
- In response to a question from Eric Poicon about whether the focus is more on expanding resources or improving access, Nirvana Nwokido explains that the group is considering both awareness and access, particularly around everyday coping and knowing when and how to seek professional support.
- Responding to a question from Jocelyn Baird about possible focus areas such as school mental health, Nirvana Nwokido says the topic is broad and that the subcommittee is leaning toward general day‑to‑day coping skills while recognizing youth and suicide ideation as important elements that may receive targeted attention.

#### Chair and vice chair roles and responsibilities
Before officer elections, Laurel James and Ida Rose Sylvester outline the duties and time commitments associated with the HRC Chair and Vice Chair roles. The Chair spends roughly 30–60 minutes per month working with staff to shape agendas and then reviews materials and chairs monthly meetings; the Vice Chair supports the Chair and steps in when the Chair is absent, as Sylvester is doing at this meeting. Sylvester adds that the role offers a rewarding opportunity to see and influence processes from the inside. This context informs commissioners’ willingness to nominate and vote for officers.
- Laurel James explains that the HRC Chair is responsible for coordinating with staff to develop meeting agendas and materials, typically requiring about 30 minutes to an hour of preparation each month.
- Laurel James notes that the Chair must also review agenda packets, chair notes, and then facilitate the Commission’s regular meetings.
- Laurel James states that the Vice Chair’s primary duty is to support the Chair and assume the Chair’s responsibilities if the Chair is absent.
- Ida Rose Sylvester adds that serving as Chair or Vice Chair provides the benefit of working closely with staff and understanding Commission processes from the inside, and that officers can choose how visible and involved they are beyond core duties.
