### Overview
The meeting of the City of Mountain View’s Visual Arts Committee (VAC), chaired by Jesse Cupp with staff support from John Lang, focuses on tightening governance basics, shaping future work, and advancing both project-level and strategic public art issues. After unanimously approving the September 14, 2022 minutes and confirming there is no public comment, the committee reviews ongoing agenda topics, including continued work on a public art strategy, a forthcoming session with consultant Bruce England on art/landscape integration, and exploration of placemaking in capital projects. Members add a specific request to revise the K5 policy so real-world CIP projects like bike infrastructure and McKelvey Ballpark can qualify for public art and emphasize Castro Street pedestrianization and downtown collaboration—especially storefront activation and vacant-space use—as central opportunities for art in everyday public life. Lang then reports that Council has voted to permanently close Castro Street as a pedestrian mall, created an interim CIP budget for ADA and related improvements, and formed an ad hoc pedestrian mall subcommittee; the VAC agrees to develop formal recommendations at its next meeting for Chair Cupp to present to that subcommittee. On project updates, Lang confirms that Rengstorff Park Aquatic Center is 25% complete and on schedule, Fayette Park has entered construction, and Rengstorff Park maintenance/tennis designs are nearing bid pending artist insurance issues, while Pyramid Park is complete; members who attended the Pyramid dedication praise the park and play equipment but express concerns about the mobile sculpture’s visual impact and a butterfly/bee piece’s placement and color blending, prompting suggestions for later-stage artist selection, interim design checkpoints, and joint site visits, which Lang agrees to explore with Public Works. The core of the meeting is a detailed review of Lang’s draft public art strategy funnel—from a vision centered on diverse public art, community vibrancy, and support for local artists, through goals of community-building, placemaking, heritage, and economic development, to strategies and initiatives including policy changes (e.g., private art fee, K5 refinement), more opportunities citywide, a public art fund, better jury processes, a public art inventory, and collaboration with arts and community organizations (potentially including schools). Members endorse a five-year horizon with measurable outcomes and a later review, stress the need to grow venues and demand for local artists (through city facilities, events, and partnerships with the Chamber, Business Improvement Area, and local businesses rather than heavy-handed city control), and ask that diversity be explicitly reflected in the vision while treated as a lens across all goals. On operations, the VAC votes to adopt AB 361 findings to preserve the option of virtual meetings even if Council returns to in-person sessions and then turns to the Center for the Performing Arts gallery Call for Artists. Drawing on experience with previous themed calls, the members recommend a broad, no-theme 2023 call focused on regional artists and artistic quality, while allowing Lang to use the call announcement to publicize the public art strategy effort. Finally, the committee unanimously adopts a 2023 meeting schedule (second Wednesdays in specified months at 6:00 PM), hears brief closing thanks from Lang, and adjourns at 8:04 PM.

#### Public art strategy framework and five-year horizon
Staff presents a structured framework for the emerging public art strategy, organized as a “funnel” from vision to goals, strategies, and specific initiatives. The committee discusses whether key themes such as support for local artists, collaboration with arts organizations, youth/school engagement, and diversity are adequately reflected. Members generally support a five-year time horizon with measurable outcomes and see this as a foundation for longer-term master planning, while emphasizing the need to grow opportunities and visibility for local artists and to leverage partnerships rather than rely solely on city-led programming.
- Staff liaison Lang reviews the previously drafted vision statement, which aims to create diverse public art that enriches Mountain View’s innovative and creative culture, enhances community vibrancy, supports local artists, and transforms Mountain View into an arts hub.
- Staff liaison Lang recalls four public art goals developed with the prior consultant and committee—building community, placemaking, reflecting heritage, and economic development—and explains how current work is aligning policies and initiatives to these goals.
- Staff liaison Lang outlines three high‑level strategy buckets emerging from prior VAC feedback: (1) develop, augment, or refine city policies and procedures (e.g., private art fees, donation and K5 policies) to support art appreciation and “placekeeping”; (2) create more public art opportunities across Mountain View; and (3) pursue new collaborations with artists and arts organizations.
- Staff liaison Lang presents draft initiative ideas, including elevating the need for spaces where artists can live and work, incorporating art into wayfinding (e.g., in the Castro Street pedestrian mall), developing a public art fund and seeking grants, improving the jury process to broaden mediums and backgrounds, creating a comprehensive public art inventory accessible to the public, aligning K5 with strategy recommendations, and collaborating with artist organizations.
- Chair Cupp mentions an existing but outdated Mountain View public art book and suggests the city could reissue or distribute it as part of building a public art inventory and public awareness.
- Committee member Hsu asks what time horizon the strategy is intended to cover and argues that the answer should shape the specificity and ambition of goals and initiatives.
- Staff liaison Lang recommends a five‑year time frame as a practical “crawl‑walk‑run” phase to grow the program from a relatively nascent state, with a later, more detailed master plan potentially addressing a ten‑year horizon once foundations and partnerships are established.
- Chair Cupp supports using the five‑year point as a formal review and refresh milestone rather than a hard expiration of the strategy, allowing future VAC members to evaluate progress and decide whether to extend or evolve it.
- Committee member Bryant requests that the strategy explicitly include success measures and a commitment to review outcomes at the end of the five‑year period when presented to Council.
- Staff liaison Lang agrees to return with measurable goals aligned to each initiative and to shape a work plan across the five years, after the VAC confirms the high‑level framework is correct.
- Committee member Whitebread stresses that a key goal should be to surround the public with art in parks and daily environments while also prioritizing support for local artists, noting that many local artists lack venues even if they have space and materials to create.
- Chair Cupp endorses the concept of small “micro‑grants” or stipends that would allow local artists to produce temporary public art installations in parks or plazas as a way to expand opportunities beyond large commissioned projects.
- Committee member Skalos argues that affordable studio space is also critical and points to neighboring cities (Palo Alto, Belmont, Redwood City, San Carlos) that provide artist studio programs as potential models.
- Committee member Hsu suggests that some issues—like studio and gallery space—may be best addressed by emphasizing Mountain View as a welcoming place for artists and by casting a wide net through the strategy, then working out specific mechanisms later.
- Committee member Bryant notes that earlier committee discussions raised school engagement and student art as a priority and asks why schools are not explicitly mentioned as partners in the draft strategy.
- Committee member Thomson reports that the Mountain View Public Library already exhibits student art on a dedicated wall, and that City Hall had previously hosted student art displays, highlighting existing school‑related efforts.
- Chair Cupp distinguishes between supporting school art education—which he argues should be funded by school district resources—and creating more entry‑level opportunities and stipends for older students (high school/college) to apply for city‑supported public art projects.
- Committee member Hsu clarifies that “diversity” should be treated as a lens applied to all goals—building a diverse community, reflecting diverse heritage, and supporting diverse economic development—rather than a separate stand‑alone goal, to avoid misinterpretation while still embedding it throughout the strategy.
- Committee member Bryant emphasizes that Mountain View’s demographic and economic diversity is a defining civic characteristic and urges that the strategy’s vision statement explicitly reference diversity so that it clearly informs all goals and initiatives.
- Staff liaison Lang suggests revising the vision statement wording (e.g., by explicitly noting Mountain View’s diverse innovative and creative culture and/or diversity as part of vibrancy) and flags this for refinement once the overall strategy draft is closer to completion.
- Chair Cupp notes that, in practice, achieving diversity in public art may require explicitly calling for certain cultural narratives or themes in calls for artists, as some artists might otherwise avoid foregrounding specific heritage motifs unless invited to do so.

#### Local artist ecosystem, venues, and business partnerships
A substantial portion of the strategy discussion focuses on how to foster a richer local art ecosystem, including venues, galleries, and sales opportunities. Members highlight that many artists already living in Mountain View lack places to show and sell work, and that culture change, demand creation, and partnerships with local businesses and organizations are likely more effective than heavy city programming. Ideas include more exhibits at city facilities, leveraging events like Art & Wine, engaging the Chamber and Business Improvement Area, encouraging retail displays, and exploring art walks, while recognizing that some commercialization depends on private galleries and collectors rather than municipal action.
- Committee member Whitebread describes a vibrant Los Altos First Friday scene with numerous bands and multiple galleries, noting that cooperative galleries of local artists sell accessible art and that such environments can be powerful public art experiences even when not city‑run.
- Committee member Whitebread observes that many local artists have space to create but nowhere in Mountain View to exhibit and sell, citing a neighbor whose show he attended in Los Altos as an example.
- Committee member Skalos reiterates the need for studio spaces and notes that other peninsula cities have built co‑operative studios, while acknowledging that Mountain View’s rents pose a barrier to similar models without city or landlord support.
- Committee member Hsu proposes that Mountain View think beyond traditional brick‑and‑mortar galleries and consider alternative mechanisms—including digital platforms or temporary events—that could enable hundreds or thousands of artists to show work over time.
- Chair Cupp suggests city-supported temporary events in large parks (e.g., Cuesta or Rengstorff) with many artist booths for a weekend, functioning as small art fairs to increase exposure and sales opportunities for local artists.
- Committee member Whitebread argues that long‑term success requires cultivating a culture of art buying and local collectors, observing that Silicon Valley tech culture is not traditionally strong in art purchasing, and that galleries and professional art sellers often play a key role in stimulating demand.
- Committee member Thomson proposes that retail stores dedicate wall space for local artists’ work, with stores allowed to choose from a pool of pieces and share in sales, creating a distributed exhibition network across downtown.
- Chair Cupp notes that Red Rock Coffee already operates this way and that similar informal artist‑store arrangements are common in San Jose and San Francisco, suggesting that culture and relationships drive those opportunities more than formal programs.
- Staff liaison Lang emphasizes that artists and business owners can already negotiate display arrangements without city involvement, and cautions that inserting municipal processes into those relationships may add complexity without clear benefit.
- Committee member Hsu suggests that a constructive city role might be to provide “pathways” rather than programs, such as publishing a directory of downtown businesses open to displaying art or facilitating introductions between artists and store owners.
- Committee member Thomson floats the idea of a one‑time city‑initiated downtown “art walk” in which volunteer retailers host temporary art displays, after which artists and businesses could build ongoing relationships independently.
- Committee member Bryant recommends engaging the Chamber of Commerce and the Downtown Business Improvement Area as key partners for promoting art display culture among businesses and integrating art into events like Taste of Mountain View.
- Staff liaison Lang suggests leveraging existing events such as Art & Wine or Taste of Mountain View by reserving dedicated spaces or booths for Mountain View artists, and notes that the city could pay local artists modest stipends to create event artwork instead of relying on staff‑generated graphics.
- Chair Cupp cites the Chamber’s recent Art & Wine Festival logo as an example of the value of commissioning professional local artists for event branding.
- Staff liaison Lang shares an example of a current CPA exhibitor who wants to be present on weekends to discuss his work, suggesting similar artist “receptions” at city spaces could deepen engagement and visibility.
- Committee member Thomson and others affirm that incremental, mutually reinforcing steps—more outlets, more exposure, and more sales—can create a self‑propelling cycle that strengthens the local art ecosystem over time.

#### Schools, youth art, and city role boundaries
The committee revisits previous ideas about engaging schools and youth in the city’s public art ecosystem, clarifying what is already happening and where the city should or should not lead. Members agree that there is value in providing exhibition space and pathways for student art, but they are cautious about having city public art funds substitute for school district art education funding. The emerging consensus is that school or youth collaborations should be treated as one category of broader organizational partnerships rather than a standalone pillar, and that low‑cost display opportunities (e.g., library and City Hall) are appropriate city roles.
- Committee member Bryant recalls earlier VAC conversations about building bridges with schools and asks why school partnerships are not explicitly called out in the strategy slides.
- Committee member Thomson reports that the Mountain View Public Library regularly exhibits artwork from local public school students on a prominent wall, and that her own students have had work displayed there.
- Chair Cupp notes that City Hall previously hosted student art exhibits before COVID and characterizes such uses of city walls as a low‑cost, high‑impact way for the city to support youth art without direct funding flows to school programs.
- Chair Cupp distinguishes between funding school art education, which he argues should be supported by school district tax revenues, and providing entry‑level public art opportunities and stipends for older students (e.g., high school and college) via VAC processes.
- Committee member Hsu asks whether the discussion is about arts education, student exhibition, or artist‑led beautification projects at schools, clarifying that these are different categories with different funding and governance implications.
- Staff liaison Lang proposes broadening the “collaborate with artists and arts organizations” strategy language to include organizations interested in art more generally, which would allow school districts and PTAs to be considered potential partners without over‑specifying them in the strategy.
- Staff liaison Lang stresses that while the city should be thoughtful about where it intervenes, partnerships with existing youth and school art programs can be part of a holistic approach to art appreciation if carefully framed.

#### CPA Call for Artists: theme, scope, and outreach
The committee reviews plans for the 2023 Center for the Performing Arts (CPA) gallery Call for Artists, which has not been run since 2019 due to COVID. Historically, calls have incorporated themes such as environmental sustainability, home, global culture, and ADA accessibility, with mixed success in attracting submissions that truly match the themes. After discussing the pros and cons, members recommend issuing a broad, no-theme call limited to regional artists, focusing on attracting more applicants and showcasing the highest-quality work, while also using the call notice to raise awareness of the city’s public art strategy effort.
- Staff liaison Lang explains that the CPA gallery call typically results in six to eight artists each receiving 6–8 week exhibition slots over a year, with artists submitting resumes and up to ten sample works for VAC review.
- Staff liaison Lang recounts that past calls were themed—environmental sustainability (2015), global culture (2016), home (2017), and ADA/accessibility for the ADA’s 30th anniversary (2019)—and shows the prior ADA call language as an example.
- Chair Cupp reports that the environmental sustainability theme was the most successful in attracting relevant submissions, that the “home” and “global culture” themes worked moderately well, and that the ADA/accessibility theme had very low alignment because most artists submitted pre‑existing work without adjusting to the concept.
- Chair Cupp notes that CPA space limitations and policies (e.g., no tactile art for touching) made it hard for artists to respond fully to accessibility themes such as tactile experiences for visually impaired patrons.
- Chair Cupp remarks that artists participating in the CPA program are typically receiving a modest stipend to show existing work rather than creating new commissioned pieces, so highly specific themes tend to reduce compatibility between portfolios and call requirements.
- Committee member Thomson asks whether artists ever create new work for the CPA themes, and Chair Cupp confirms that most rely on existing portfolios due to the nature of the arrangement.
- Committee member Bryant suggests the lived experience of COVID as a possible broad theme, encompassing wide-ranging emotional and experiential responses, but acknowledges potential constraints.
- Committee member Hsu argues against a theme for 2023, emphasizing that the priority should be to attract as many artists as possible after the COVID gap and to position the CPA as an accessible exhibition opportunity, with themes to be considered once submissions and engagement are higher.
- Committee member Hsu notes that the prior ADA‑focused call received very few submissions overall, which she sees as evidence that themes can inadvertently create barriers at this stage.
- Committee member Skalos supports an open, no‑theme call, pointing out that if artists are not creating new work specifically for the show, rigid themes are unlikely to be meaningfully reflected anyway.
- Chair Cupp adds that a non‑themed call allows the VAC to focus selection purely on artistic quality and suitability for the large CPA walls, which require substantial, visually strong pieces.
- Committee member Whitebread and Chair Cupp agree that the prior geographic scope—a “generous” definition of the Bay Area used in the last CPA call—worked well and should be retained, rather than narrowing to just Santa Clara County or expanding statewide.
- Staff liaison Lang confirms he will mirror the prior call’s geographic range and structural parameters when drafting the 2023 call.
- Staff liaison Lang proposes adding a brief note to the public call announcement about the city’s ongoing public art strategy development and including a link for artists to learn more or engage, which Chair Cupp supports as a way to increase awareness and leverage advertising spend.
