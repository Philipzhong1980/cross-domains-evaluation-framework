### Overview
  The Development Review Committee reviewed two projects. For 500 Ellis, Eris Studio presented a redevelopment with a six-story hotel and a two-story office linked by a landscaped paseo, a central automated valet parking core, and active street frontages; DRC praises tree preservation and pedestrian intentions but requests revisions to paseo entry grading and amenity design, bike-room access, corner hierarchy between the hotel and office, ground-floor materiality, window grouping, drop-off clarity, and rooftop screening—Tom Jess agrees to study simplified massing, material refinements, entry treatments, and coordination with the project planner. For 1040 Terra Bella, the applicant described a land transfer to Alta Housing and a two-phase storage redevelopment (6‑story and 4‑story buildings); the team reported elevation simplifications, increased glazing, a larger mural placeholder, reduced parking to add trees, and rooftop screening strategies, while DRC asks for a few proportional tweaks (especially Building 2 south elevation), verification and consolidation of rooftop equipment and solar relative to parapets, additional canopy where feasible, interim frontage planting options during Phase 1, setback of security gates, and physical material mockups to confirm colors (including a darker neutral alternative to the purple). Both applicants commit to continued, schematic-level coordination with planning staff, to provide material mockups, and to return with revised diagrams and details prior to their public hearings.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Site and tree preservation
  The applicant presents the existing site as two single-story office buildings with surface parking and emphasizes efforts to preserve perimeter trees and one central tree. Tree preservation matters for canopy retention and city objectives for native planting. The team and staff agree planting strategies must evolve with detailed landscape plans and potential additional larger-canopy trees in targeted locations.
- Tom Jess: The current site has two single-story office buildings and surface parking and we are preserving as many perimeter trees and a central tree as possible.
- Rebecca Shapiro: The City emphasizes maximizing tree canopy and encourages incorporating larger canopy trees as the landscape design progresses.
- Jonathan Thomas: For the storage project we revised the landscape plan to add trees and maximize canopy where site constraints allow.
  #### Program and site layout
  The 500 Ellis proposal includes a six-story hotel at the corner of Ellis and National and an adjacent two-story office building, connected by a landscaped paseo. The paseo and active frontages aim to create pedestrian engagement and circulation between buildings and adjacent parcels. The team highlights a tall hotel ground floor, office lobby access from the paseo, and second-story balconies overlooking the paseo.
- Tom Jess: The plan places a six-story hotel on the corner and a two-story office building down Ellis, linked by a landscaped paseo and active ground-floor uses.
- Tom Jess: The hotel lobby is at the prominent corner with guest rooms wrapping the mechanical parking core and the office lobby opens off the paseo.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC expects the paseo and public frontages to prioritize pedestrian engagement as East Wisman densifies.
  #### Parking and automated system
  The project uses a centrally located fully automated mechanical valet parking system to shield parking from frontages and enable active edges. Some surface stalls remain in the rear for accessible and oversized vehicles. Staff and the team discussed operational and design implications of automated parking and surface parking distribution.
- Tom Jess: We place a fully automated mechanical valet parking system at the center of the site with limited surface parking in the rear for accessible and oversized vehicles.
- Tom Jess: The automated system slides vehicles onto trays and stores them in the central stack, with valet operation anticipated.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC acknowledges the benefit of shielding parking to allow active building edges along Ellis.
  #### Paseo entry and landscaping
  DRC raises concerns that the paseo entry at Ellis currently reads as circulation-heavy and insufficiently inviting, recommending rethinking stairs, grading, and amenity design to create a clearer, more gradual and welcoming entry. Suggestions include shallow stairs with landings, strengthened seating/amenity elements, and stronger corner landscape treatments. The applicant agrees to study reconfigurations to better define hierarchy and invite pedestrian pause.
- Rebecca Shapiro: The paseo entry currently feels focused on circulation and needs to provide clearer, more inviting places to pause and more gradual transitions in elevation.
- Linda Pomancini: Consider pushing stairs and ramps further back to create a generous landing that reads as part of the public sidewalk rather than a private elevated space.
- Tom Jess: We will study softening the entrance with shallower stairs and more defined hierarchy so one entry reads as primary and other entries are downplayed.
  #### Bike parking access
  The secure long-term bike room is currently set back from Ellis, which raises DRC concern about bike access convenience and safety. Staff recommends relocating or redesigning the bike room to prevent bikes from riding the wrong way into vehicle aisles or forcing cyclists onto narrow sidewalks. The applicant agrees to reconsider bike room placement to prioritize safe, direct bike access.
- Rebecca Shapiro: The bike room's distance from the Ellis frontage risks unsafe circulation and could force bicyclists into vehicle aisles or narrow sidewalks.
- Tom Jess: We located long-term bike parking away from the paseo to keep the paseo pedestrian-only, but we will reconsider placement for better bike access.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC requests relocation or redesign so secure bicycle access is safe, convenient, and intuitive from the street.
  #### Massing and corner hierarchy
  DRC focuses on whether the two buildings read cohesively and how each building emphasizes its corner entry. Reviewers note the hotel corner is intended as the primary anchor but the office building's prominent framed volume currently competes for visual prominence. The team agrees to explore material, proportion, and massing adjustments so each building's diagrammatic hierarchy reads more clearly and entries are more legible.
- Russell Sherman: While the hotel corner is intended as the primary prominent element, the office building's framed volume reads equally or more prominent and competes for attention.
- Linda Pomancini: The office massing strategy is clearer than the hotel's, and the hotel's base may be interrupting corner prominence.
- Tom Jess: We will study simplification, material changes, and ways to strengthen the hotel corner and clarify the office lobby prominence to better reflect the diagrams.
  #### Office building base treatment
  DRC asks the team to strengthen the office ground-floor materiality and transparency so the base reads as an engaging, pedestrian-scaled street frontage rather than a plain plane. Reviewers suggest aligning materials and proportions with the intention shown in the massing diagrams and making the lobby entrance more apparent. The applicant agrees to explore more distinct ground-plane materials and increased transparency at the office lobby.
- Rebecca Shapiro: The office ground plane currently reads too plain and disconnected from the massing diagram and needs stronger material and landscape gestures to engage pedestrians.
- Russell Sherman: The office lobby element should be made more prominent so the eye is drawn to the entry, consistent with the diagram.
- Tom Jess: We will examine revised materials, increased glazing, and refined proportions to make the office base more legible and inviting.
  #### Hotel façade and window pattern
  DRC discusses how the hotel's façade composition and repetitive window patterns currently create vertical breaks that fragment the intended single street wall and distract from the key corner. Reviewers recommend simplifying vertical window grouping, increasing glazing proportion on the recessed upper white volume to reduce perceived mass, and adjusting the awning and frame proportions so the corner reads more vertical and prominent. The applicant responds that they will test window grouping and color/material simplifications to align the façade with the massing diagram.
- Russell Sherman: The hotel's fenestration emphasizes vertical segmentation and makes the façade read as multiple vertical pieces instead of a unified plane with a prominent corner.
- Rebecca Shapiro: Consider increasing glazing proportion on the recessed white upper volume and simplifying window groupings to reduce visual weight and support the corner hierarchy.
- Tom Jess: We will explore reducing vertical window emphasis away from the corner, simplifying color and material, and enhancing the awning and entrance treatments to strengthen the corner.
  #### Back-of-building mural and screening
  The team proposes a mural on the blank parking façade as an opportunity for public art, but no specific artwork is proposed yet. DRC encourages working with an artist and integrating the mural as genuine public art instead of purely decorative material treatments. The applicant states intent to collaborate with an artist in future design phases.
- Tom Jess: We propose a mural on the blank face of the parking facade and intend to work with an artist to create an actual public artwork rather than a patterned material.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC supports a genuine artist-led mural and encourages early coordination on concept and placement.
- Tom Jess: No specific artwork is proposed at this time, and we will return with an approach once an artist collaboration is confirmed.
  #### Drop-off and vehicle circulation
  The hotel vehicle drop-off (porte cochere) and circulation were discussed as a highly used area that currently reads semi-service and needs to feel more like a front-facing drop-off. DRC suggests improving the connection between the drop-off and lobby, enhancing paving, lighting, and overhead treatments, and possibly reconfiguring stairs to bring circulation toward the building face. The applicant agrees to study design moves to make the drop-off feel special and more legible as a primary guest arrival.
- Linda Pomancini: The drop-off area currently feels like a back entrance and should be emphasized so guests don't feel like they are entering through a service zone.
- Tom Jess: We will explore special paving, ceiling lighting, and other moves to make the drop-off feel intentional and connected to the lobby rather than a back alley.
- Rebecca Shapiro: Consider reorienting stairs and interior circulation so arrival flows more directly from porte cochere to the lobby public spaces.
  #### Rooftop open space and solar constraints
  DRC asks whether rooftop amenity is feasible above the parking area; the applicant explains structural constraints and code/fire separation make usable rooftop open space impractical, but solar and MEP equipment remain feasible. The team will place solar and rooftop equipment where structurally allowed and coordinate parapet screening and mechanical consolidation to minimize visibility. Staff requests verification that solar racking and equipment do not exceed lowest parapet heights and asks for consolidation or architectural screening where possible.
- Tom Jess: Structural and code constraints on the parking podium prevent heavy live loads, so usable rooftop open space is not feasible but solar and equipment can be accommodated.
- Rebecca Shapiro: Staff requests verification that solar racking and rooftop equipment will not exceed the lowest parapet height and urges consolidation and screening where possible.
- Tom Jess: We will confirm solar and equipment heights and work to inset and screen rooftop equipment within the parapet limits.
  #### Design next steps and coordination
  DRC encourages the applicant to iterate in schematic sketches with the project planner and staff to test massing, material, and entry revisions before producing full sets of construction drawings. The team plans to coordinate closely with planning staff (Ellen and the project planner) to refine the paseo, corner treatments, parking/bike access, and materials. The applicant acknowledges the recommendation and commits to continued work sessions and revisions prior to entitlements.
- Rebecca Shapiro: Staff recommends schematic-level iteration with the project planner to test revisions and avoid reworking many full sheets later in the process.
- Tom Jess: We will work with our project planner and staff to pursue additional schematic studies and refine massing, materials, and landscape based on today's feedback.
- Tom Jess: We thanked Ellen for her assistance and noted ongoing coordination with City staff will continue.
  #### Project overview and land transfer
  For 1040 Terra Bella the applicant explains a land donation to Alta Housing to increase affordable units while re-entitling the remaining property for two modern storage buildings. The proposal includes a six-story Phase 1 building and a four-story Phase 2 building with overall support from previous Planning Commission and Council reviews. Phase sequencing, easements, and the overpass context inform visibility, phasing decisions, and where frontage improvements are practical.
- Bryan Miranda: We are donating approximately a half acre and Terra Bella frontage to Alta Housing in exchange for re-entitling our site to two storage buildings.
- Bryan Miranda: The project is two-phase with a six-story phase one and a four-story phase two, and both received favorable review at the Planning Commission and City Council.
- Bryan Miranda: The freeway overpass limits visibility for some elevations and affects how the site will be perceived from adjacent rights-of-way.
  #### Design revisions and simplification
  Responding to prior DRC feedback, the team simplified elevations by reducing split-face CMU banding, clarifying vertical masking elements, mirroring features for symmetry, increasing glazing where appropriate, and enlarging the mural placeholder. The objectives were to harmonize rhythm across elevations and align ground-level loading and entry locations with upper-level fenestration. Staff acknowledges the refinements and asks for a few targeted adjustments, especially on Building 2's south elevation.
- Jonathan Thomas: We simplified the base by removing busy CMU banding, mirrored masking elements for symmetry, and adjusted glazing and entry alignments across both buildings.
- Jonathan Thomas: We enlarged the mural placeholder and rebalanced façade features to create a more holistic and simplified elevation vocabulary.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC finds the recent revisions largely successful but requests a few additional proportional tweaks, particularly on Building 2's south elevation.
  #### Roof equipment and screening
  The storage project team shows conservative rooftop MEP locations with six-foot screens and explains screening materials will match facade corrugated panels to visually integrate equipment. DRC asks for continued consolidation and confirmation that solar and mechanical equipment remain below parapet lines and are effectively screened from ground-level views. The applicant confirms intent to coordinate equipment heights and to use similar materials so screens blend with building architecture.
- Jonathan Thomas: We located rooftop MEP conservatively, show 6-foot screens and intend to clad screening in corrugated panels that relate to façade materials.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC requests consolidation where feasible and confirmation that solar racks and equipment do not exceed the lowest parapet heights.
- Jonathan Thomas: We will verify final equipment heights and work to set mechanical units back and screen them architecturally.
  #### Landscape, tree canopy, and parking
  The team reduced parking stalls to add tree canopy, relocated a trash enclosure for screening, and specified tree species that will thrive in narrow planting zones. DRC emphasizes maximizing canopy given city priorities and requests staff coordination to further evaluate opportunities for larger trees, especially along the San Rafael frontage and adjacent to the freeway sound wall. The applicant notes tradeoffs from operational needs and easement constraints but agrees to refine the planting palette and spacing.
- Jonathan Thomas: We reduced four parking stalls on Building 1 and removed peripheral stalls on Building 2 to provide space for additional trees and screening.
- Jonathan Thomas: The landscape architect specified species selected for survivability in constrained soil/bioretention areas while increasing overall canopy where possible.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC asks the team to explore adding a few larger-canopy trees along the San Rafael frontage and sound-wall edge where feasible to strengthen on-site canopy.
  #### Phase 1 frontage and interim planting
  DRC and the applicant discuss whether interim frontage planting is feasible where Phase 2 will later be built, recognizing grading and sidewalk reconfiguration during Phase 2 could conflict with early plantings. Staff asks the team to consider limited interim plantings (trees or planting beds) near the Phase 1 temporary office to soften the street during construction. The applicant notes functional and grading constraints but agrees to evaluate low-risk interim planting options and coordinate with staff on feasibility.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC recommends evaluating interim plantings along the Phase 1 San Rafael frontage to soften the street and provide a better condition during construction.
- Jonathan Thomas: Phase 2 grading and frontage changes limit where permanent trees can be placed, but we will explore limited interim planting that would not be destroyed by future work.
- Bryan Miranda: The team acknowledges the temporary trailer and interim conditions and will study planting options that balance constructability and aesthetic benefit.
  #### Sidewalk width and service access
  Staff asked about the width and function of the east-side sidewalk around Building 1, which reads constrained and primarily for emergency and building egress. The applicant explains the width (~9 feet) accommodates egress and fire apparatus buffers but agrees to study tree-grate planting or other urban-tree solutions for constrained zones. DRC requests exploration of tree-grate approaches in targeted locations where pedestrian circulation is limited but shading benefits are possible.
- Rebecca Shapiro: The east-side sidewalk appears constrained and DRC asks whether urban tree-grate planting could fit within the 9-foot pedestrian/egress corridor.
- Jonathan Thomas: The sidewalk is sized to meet egress and fire access clearances at approximately nine feet, but we will study tree-grate or similar low-profile planting options.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC encourages selective urban tree opportunities in constrained zones to increase canopy without impeding service or emergency access.
  #### Security gate and storefront relation
  DRC requests the security gate for Building 2 be set back so it does not read forward of the building face and compete with the storefront corner, improving the pedestrian experience. The applicant agrees to study moving the gate relative to the façade to keep the building corner/retail storefront as the dominant street presence. Staff will review feasibility and clearance needs during subsequent design coordination.
- Rebecca Shapiro: Please study setting the security gate further back so the building face and storefront remain the primary street-facing element rather than fencing.
- Jonathan Thomas: We will evaluate relocating the gate and fence so they do not encroach forward of the building face and storefront.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC requests coordination with staff on required clearances to ensure any gate relocation meets operational and safety requirements.
  #### Materials, color, and mockups
  The team presented a material palette and DRC highlighted screen color perception differences across monitors; reviewers expressed a preference to test a darker, more neutral accent in place of the current purple. DRC recommends a physical mockup or material board review to confirm final colors and recommends testing a neutral gray accent alternative. The applicant agrees to provide mockups and considers the neutral gray alternative for final approval.
- Jonathan Thomas: We provided a material board and digital colors but noted screen displays vary and the physical mockup is more reliable for final decisions.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC recommends testing a darker, more neutral accent alternative to the purple and reviewing materials in a full-size mockup.
- Jonathan Thomas: We will supply physical mockups and evaluate a neutral gray accent as an alternative to the purple.
  #### Public art and mural intent

DRC supports enlarging the mural and working with an artist to create meaningful public art appropriate to the site. The applicant will pursue artist collaboration and return with a specific artwork plan in later submittals.
- Jonathan Thomas: We enlarged the mural placeholder and confirm the mural will be a placeholder for artist-led public art rather than a material treatment.
- Rebecca Shapiro: DRC supports an artist collaboration and encourages early coordination on scale and content of the mural.
- Jonathan Thomas: We will pursue an artist and return with a specific mural approach in subsequent submittals.
