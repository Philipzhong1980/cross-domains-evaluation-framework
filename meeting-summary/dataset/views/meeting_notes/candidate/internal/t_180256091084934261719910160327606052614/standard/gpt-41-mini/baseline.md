### Overview
The Development Review Committee convenes to evaluate two key projects: a mixed-use hotel and office development at 500 Ellis St. and a two-phase personal storage facility at 1040 Terra Bella Ave. Tom Jess of Eris Studio Architects presents the 500 Ellis St. project, highlighting site plans, massing strategies, and pedestrian-focused design elements. The committee, led by Deputy Zoning Administrator Rebecca Shapiro and consulting architects Russell Sherman and Linda Pomancini, provides detailed feedback emphasizing pedestrian engagement, tree canopy preservation, and architectural clarity. They discuss massing hierarchy, facade articulation, and materiality, encouraging simplification and stronger corner prominence. The team agrees to refine the paseo entry, bike parking location, and drop-off area to enhance usability and aesthetics. For the 1040 Terra Bella project, Bryan Miranda and Jonathan Thomas report on design revisions that simplify elevations, increase glazing, and improve landscape planting with native species. The committee commends the architectural progress and recommends further refinement of facade proportions, color palettes, rooftop screening, and streetscape landscaping, including interim planting during phased construction. Both project teams commit to ongoing collaboration with city staff to address these recommendations ahead of upcoming public hearings, aiming to align with city goals for sustainable, pedestrian-friendly, and visually cohesive developments.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### 500 Ellis St. Project Overview
Tom Jess from Eris Studio Architects presents the proposed development at 500 Ellis St., involving demolition of existing single-story offices and surface parking to build a six-story hotel and a two-story office building. The design emphasizes tree preservation, active street frontages, and incorporates a mechanical automated parking system to maximize usable space. The project aims to enhance pedestrian connectivity with a landscaped paseo and outdoor patios.
- Tom Jess explains the site currently has two single-story office buildings and surface parking to be removed for new development.
- The hotel is six stories with guest rooms wrapping around a central automated parking system.
- The office building is two stories, designed to have its own presence and active frontage.
- The design includes a landscaped paseo connecting to adjacent properties, with efforts to preserve existing trees and add new canopy.
- Bike parking is separated from the paseo to maintain pedestrian focus, with short-term and long-term facilities.
#### Site and Frontage Design Feedback
Rebecca Shapiro and DRC members provide feedback emphasizing the need for pedestrian-oriented site and building design in East Wiseman. They commend tree preservation efforts but urge maximizing new canopy trees and incorporating 75% California native plants. Concerns are raised about the bike room's location potentially causing unsafe bicycle circulation and the paseo's entry feeling unwelcoming due to grading and circulation focus. Suggestions include rethinking the paseo entry to create a more inviting, usable space.
- Rebecca Shapiro highlights the importance of pedestrian engagement and tree canopy maximization in redevelopment.
- She recommends reconsidering the bike room location to ensure safe and convenient access.
- Concerns are expressed about the paseo entry being dominated by circulation rather than amenity space, suggesting design revisions.
- Linda Pomancini suggests pushing stairs and ramps further back to create a welcoming landing area at the paseo entrance.
- Tom Jess agrees to explore softening stairs and clarifying public versus semi-private circulation areas.
#### Building Massing and Architectural Strategy
The DRC discusses the relationship and design strategies of the hotel and office buildings. While acknowledging their distinct architectural languages, members emphasize the need for clarity in massing hierarchy and prominence of entry corners. The hotel’s key corner is recognized as prominent, but the office building’s entry corner is less visually emphasized. Suggestions include enhancing materiality and simplifying massing to better align with the design diagrams and improve pedestrian engagement.
- Russell Sherman appreciates the distinct architectural languages but stresses consistent prominence of entry corners.
- Linda Pomancini finds the office building’s massing clearer and simpler than the hotel’s, which feels less exciting.
- Rebecca Shapiro notes the office building’s ground plane feels disconnected and plain compared to its massing diagram.
- Tom Jess acknowledges the need to strengthen the office building’s lobby prominence and simplify massing.
- The team agrees to explore material and massing adjustments to better reflect design intent and improve pedestrian experience.
#### Hotel Building Facade and Massing Details
The committee critiques the hotel’s east elevation and facade articulation, noting that vertical breaks and window patterns create a fragmented appearance that detracts from the key corner’s prominence. They recommend simplifying massing breaks, adjusting window groupings, and enhancing materiality to create a cohesive street wall that highlights the corner. The relationship between the tower and lobby massing is also discussed, with suggestions to visually integrate these elements more effectively.
- Russell Sherman and Rebecca Shapiro observe that the hotel facade reads as three vertical pieces rather than a unified mass.
- They suggest reducing vertical window breaks and simplifying colors to emphasize the key corner.
- Tom Jess agrees to study window grouping and massing simplification to align with design diagrams.
- The team discusses enhancing the visual connection between the tower and lobby, possibly by extending the awning.
- Landscape treatment at the main corner is encouraged to further highlight this key area.
#### Secondary Entrances and Drop-off Area
Discussion focuses on the hotel’s Porte Cochere drop-off area, which is a primary access point but currently feels like a back entrance. The DRC suggests improving the spatial relationship between the drop-off and lobby to create a more welcoming arrival experience. Options include reconfiguring circulation and stair placement to bring movement closer to the building’s front facade and enhancing architectural and landscape features to activate this frontage.
- Linda Pomancini notes the drop-off area feels like a back door despite being the main arrival point.
- Rebecca Shapiro proposes relocating stairs and circulation closer to the front facade to improve visibility and access.
- Tom Jess acknowledges the challenge and commits to exploring design options to make the drop-off area more special and inviting.
- The team references a successful downtown hotel project with a well-designed drop-off area as inspiration.
- Landscape and lighting enhancements are planned to elevate the drop-off experience.
#### Public Art and Rooftop Use
Tom Jess confirms intent to collaborate with an artist to create a genuine mural on the hotel’s parking structure facade, enhancing public art presence. Regarding rooftop use, structural and code constraints limit active open space on the parking roof, restricting it to solar panels and equipment. The team explains challenges with load capacity and building code separations between the hotel and office structures.
- Tom Jess states the mural will be a true public art piece, not just decorative materials.
- Structural engineers advise against active rooftop use on the parking structure due to load limitations.
- Solar panels and equipment will be installed on the roof within code constraints.
- Building code requires firewall separation between the steel hotel building and wood-frame office building podium.
- The team plans to maximize solar installation while respecting structural and code limitations.
#### Window Design and Materiality Recommendations
The DRC encourages creative window design to add texture and shadow, reducing the typical hotel appearance. They emphasize the importance of material choices to highlight key architectural areas and support massing articulation. The metallic Trespa panel treatment on the hotel corner is noted as a strong element but may require additional differentiation. The team agrees to focus on massing clarity first, then refine materials and details.
- Rebecca Shapiro urges innovation in window design to soften the hotel’s typical look.
- Material use should emphasize prominent areas and support massing hierarchy.
- The metallic Trespa panel on the hotel corner is effective but may need further enhancement.
- Russell Sherman highlights the importance of detailed window and material treatments for architectural depth.
- Tom Jess confirms focus on massing clarity before advancing detailed material design.
#### 1040 Terra Bella Storage Project Update
Bryan Miranda and Jonathan Thomas present updates on the two-phase personal storage building project at 1040 Terra Bella Ave., including land donation to Alta Housing for affordable units. The project has received strong support for its architectural design from Planning Commission and City Council. Recent revisions simplify building elevations, increase glazing, and enhance landscape planting with native species to improve canopy and screening. The team addresses prior feedback and prepares for upcoming public hearings.
- Bryan Miranda explains the land donation to Alta Housing and site reconfiguration.
- The project includes a six-story and a four-story storage building with a combined area over 400,000 sq ft.
- Jonathan Thomas reviews elevation simplifications and symmetry improvements.
- Landscape plans incorporate more native trees and better screening of trash enclosures and utilities.
- The project has unanimous support from Planning Commission and City Council for its architectural design.
#### Building Elevation and Material Adjustments
The team details changes to building elevations, including simplifying CMU bases, aligning vertical elements, enlarging murals, and balancing facade features. They address concerns about proportion and rhythm, particularly on Building 2’s south elevation, by adjusting inverted L-shaped elements. Material boards are provided to clarify colors, with attention to digital display limitations. The team commits to refining colors, especially the purple accent, to better fit the overall palette.
- Elevations are simplified with more solid CMU bases and symmetrical vertical elements.
- The mural on Building 1’s west elevation is enlarged and balanced.
- Building 2’s south elevation proportions are adjusted by spacing inverted L elements further apart.
- Material boards help clarify true colors, acknowledging screen variability.
- The purple accent color is under review to possibly shift to a darker, more neutral tone.
#### Rooftop Mechanical and Solar Screening
Jonathan Thomas explains the rooftop mechanical equipment is double-screened and set back to minimize visibility, with screening materials matching facade colors. Solar panels are designed to remain below parapet heights to avoid visual intrusion. The team balances equipment placement to allow solar installation while maintaining architectural integration. The DRC emphasizes verifying final equipment heights and encourages minimizing rooftop clutter for aesthetic consistency.
- Mechanical equipment is grouped and screened with materials matching building facades.
- Solar panels are installed below parapet heights to remain visually unobtrusive.
- Equipment placement balances operational needs with solar panel space.
- DRC requests verification that solar structures do not exceed parapet heights.
- The team agrees to work with staff to ensure compliance and minimize rooftop visibility.
#### Landscape and Streetscape Enhancements
The landscape architect has increased native tree plantings and optimized species selection for sustainability and canopy coverage. The team reduced parking stalls to accommodate more trees and improved screening of trash enclosures. Discussion includes potential interim landscaping along San Rafael frontage during phased construction to enhance streetscape appearance. The DRC recommends exploring opportunities for taller trees and urban tree grates in constrained areas to improve pedestrian experience and visual appeal.
- Native species are prioritized to meet city goals for sustainability and canopy.
- Parking reductions allow for additional tree plantings and better screening.
- Interim landscaping is considered for phase one frontage to soften temporary conditions.
- DRC suggests incorporating taller trees and urban tree grates where feasible.
- Coordination with planning staff is encouraged to maximize planting and screening effectiveness.
#### Final Recommendations and Next Steps
The DRC commends the responsiveness of the project teams and encourages continued collaboration with staff on schematic revisions, particularly regarding massing, materiality, landscape, and pedestrian experience. They recommend early incorporation of phase two frontage planting if feasible and emphasize the importance of detailed design development before public hearings. The teams acknowledge the feedback and commit to refining designs accordingly to align with city objectives and community expectations.
- DRC appreciates the progress and encourages schematic-level iteration with staff.
- Early phase two frontage planting or interim landscaping is recommended for streetscape continuity.
- Focus areas include massing clarity, material differentiation, and pedestrian-oriented design.
- Teams commit to addressing rooftop screening, mechanical equipment placement, and landscape enhancements.
- Public hearings are anticipated early next year, with design refinements to precede them.
