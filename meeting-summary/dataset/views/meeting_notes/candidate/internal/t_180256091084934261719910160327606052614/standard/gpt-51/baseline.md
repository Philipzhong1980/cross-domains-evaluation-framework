### Overview
The meeting reviews two major development proposals: a hotel/office project at 500 Ellis Street in East Whisman and a two‑building self‑storage project at 1040 Terra Bella Avenue tied to an Alta Housing land donation. For 500 Ellis, architect Tom Jess presents a six‑story hotel and two‑story office wrapped around an automated parking core with a landscaped paseo, while Rebecca Shapiro and architects Russ Sherman and Linda Pomancini focus on strengthening pedestrian orientation, relocating long‑term bike parking for safer access, re‑designing the Ellis paseo entrance to feel more open and inviting, and realigning both buildings’ architecture with their massing diagrams so that key corners and streetwalls read clearly; they ask for simplification of the hotel’s Ellis elevation, a stronger and more integrated corner tower, lighter and more articulated upper masses, and a clearer, more contemporary expression of the office lobby on both Ellis and the paseo. For 1040 Terra Bella, Public Storage’s Bryan Miranda and architect Jonathan Thomas present refined elevations for a six‑story and four‑story storage complex that has already received strong Planning Commission and City Council support, and the DRC agrees that simplifications to the CMU base, framing elements, glazing alignment, and rooftop screening are successful while requesting proportion tweaks to Building 2’s south elevation, a potential shift from a branded purple accent band to a more neutral gray pending mock‑ups, confirmation that all rooftop mechanical and solar equipment will remain fully screened, and additional work on landscape: adding a few larger trees along the freeway edge, exploring tree‑grate plantings on a side walkway, carefully coordinating utilities with planting, and, if feasible, advancing Phase 2 frontage trees or interim plantings along San Rafael to support the adjacent Alta Housing project. Across both items, Shapiro emphasizes continued schematic‑level collaboration with staff to test revisions before full resubmittals and to ensure that final plans and conditions—especially around tree canopy, native planting targets, and utility screening—reflect Mountain View’s urban design priorities before the projects proceed to public hearings.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### 500 Ellis hotel and office project introduction
The committee reviews a proposed six-story hotel and two-story office project at 500 Ellis Street in the East Whisman neighborhood, replacing two existing one-story office buildings and surface parking. Architect Tom Jess describes the basic program, circulation, tree preservation strategy, and a central mechanical parking system meant to keep parking screened from public frontages. The project also features a landscaped paseo connecting Ellis to National and an emphasis on active frontages and hotel lobby visibility at the key corner. This framing sets up the DRC’s later comments on site planning, pedestrian orientation, and architectural massing.

- Tom Jess explains that the existing site consists of two single‑story office buildings and surface parking that will be demolished while preserving as many perimeter trees and one central tree as possible.
- Tom Jess describes the new program as a six‑story hotel on the Ellis/National corner with a two‑story office building further down Ellis, both wrapping a central automated parking system to keep parking away from public frontages.
- Tom Jess notes that vehicles will primarily access the site from National with an exit‑only drive onto Ellis, and that parking will be valet‑served using a fully automated mechanical system.
- Tom Jess states that short‑term bicycle parking is placed along Ellis while long‑term bike parking is located off the secondary driveway to keep the paseo predominantly pedestrian‑oriented.
- Tom Jess highlights an active ground floor along Ellis, including a semi‑recessed outdoor patio and a landscaped paseo that exceeds precise plan requirements for connectivity and amenity space.
#### Site design, trees, and planting policy
Rebecca Shapiro reiterates the city’s priorities for maximizing tree canopy and California native planting in redevelopment projects. She commends the applicant’s initial tree preservation efforts but urges further enhancement of canopy and compliance with a 75% native planting target. The DRC focuses on ensuring bike parking is safely and conveniently accessed and that landscaping is used to make the paseo and frontages inviting and functional, not just pass‑through spaces.

- Rebecca Shapiro praises the early work on tree preservation but stresses that the city wants projects to maximize new large‑canopy trees where feasible.
- Rebecca Shapiro reminds the team that City Council has set an objective for project planting palettes to be approximately 75% California native species and asks that the landscape plans be revised to reflect this target.
- Rebecca Shapiro expresses concern that the secure bike room is set too far back from Ellis and may lead riders to travel the wrong way in the drive aisle or onto narrow sidewalks, and she requests a relocation study to prioritize safe and convenient bike access.
- Rebecca Shapiro notes that while the paseo width is appreciated, its current design reads too circulation‑heavy and needs more space and features for people to linger, particularly at the Ellis access point.
#### Paseo entry design and grading concerns
The DRC focuses on the Ellis‑side entrance to the paseo, finding it visually and functionally uninviting due to immediate stairs/ramps, grade transitions, and planter configuration. Members recommend rethinking the hierarchy and locations of entries, softening stair runs, and providing a more generous, sidewalk‑like landing area that reads as a public place rather than a private forecourt. They also ask the team to enrich amenities and features along the paseo so it works as a place to pause as well as a circulation route.

- Linda Pomancini cautions that meeting stairs or ramps immediately at the paseo entrance signals a private, elevated zone and urges pushing grade changes further back to create a broad landing area that feels like an extension of the Ellis sidewalk.
- Linda Pomancini suggests adding more planting at the front edge and shifting ramps and stairs deeper into the paseo to soften the threshold and make the entrance more welcoming.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks the architect to simplify the many different access points and types of access at Ellis so there is a clearer, more legible public entry and a better balance between circulation and amenity space.
- Tom Jess proposes downplaying the secondary entry that leads into the semi‑private zone, widening and softening the main stair sequence, and creating a more gradual, less intimidating rise into the paseo as one way to respond.
#### Office building–hotel relationship and massing strategy
The committee discusses how the office and hotel buildings should relate architecturally given their different uses and scales. Russ Sherman accepts that they need not share a common language but asks that shared concepts—like what constitutes a prominent corner—be handled consistently. Both architects emphasize that the office building’s massing diagrams are clearer and more successful than the hotel’s, and they urge the design team to realign the architecture with the conceptual diagrams so that intended key elements truly read as primary.

- Russell Sherman accepts that the hotel and office need not share an identical architectural language, but he argues that concepts like a ‘prominent corner’ should be expressed convincingly on both buildings.
- Linda Pomancini observes that the massing strategy for the office building is simpler and more legible than for the hotel, and she finds the office’s proportions more successful at the diagram level.
- Russell Sherman points out that on the office building, the large framed volume facing Ellis reads as the most prominent element, not the lobby corner at the paseo, contradicting the designer’s own diagram labeling.
- Tom Jess explains that he intentionally gave the office’s framed volume more visual weight so the two‑story office is not visually overwhelmed by the six‑story hotel, but he agrees he can still strengthen the lobby entrance as a prominent element.
#### Office building Ellis frontage and base treatment
The DRC finds a disconnect between the office building’s Ellis‑front massing diagram and the built elevation, particularly in the way the building base and corner are treated. They note that the ground‑floor zone between the corner and the framed upper volume is visually plain and tied to the wrong massing piece, causing the diagrammed relationship to disappear. The committee recommends using material differentiation, glazing, and simplification to reassert the corner’s primacy and create a more engaging pedestrian base.

- Rebecca Shapiro notes that the Ellis elevation does not match the massing diagram, as the ground‑floor treatment visually aligns with the recessed upper volume instead of tying into the northeast corner mass, undermining the intended hierarchy.
- Rebecca Shapiro finds the office building base along Ellis overly plain despite its large windows and urges the team to use materials and detailing to give the ground plane more pedestrian interest and to stand up to the projecting frame above.
- Russell Sherman sketches over the elevation to show that, if the ground‑floor base and upper mass were treated as one continuous element, the corner entry would read more clearly as the diagram intends.
- Tom Jess agrees that simplifying the massing and reinforcing the corner zone with distinct materials or expression could better direct the eye to the lobby entrance without increasing height.
#### Office paseo elevation, lobby stair, and modern language
On the office’s paseo side, the committee feels the façade becomes busy and loses the clarity of the diagrams. They suggest that the lobby and its stair could be more strongly expressed and that glazing might help clarify massing. The architects also observe that the office building’s language is trending more contemporary than the hotel, and that the current lobby treatment feels ‘suburban retail’ rather than urban office, recommending a cleaner, more modern expression aligned with the overall concept.

- Russell Sherman comments that on the paseo elevation, the stair and lobby could serve as the primary visual break, but current massing and materials make the composition feel jumbled rather than diagram‑driven.
- Tom Jess acknowledges that the office building’s architecture is more contemporary than the hotel’s and even likens the current lobby composition to a ‘Pottery Barn or Williams‑Sonoma’ entry, suggesting it may be too suburban for the context.
- Linda Pomancini says that, based on the colored massing blocks Tom draws during the meeting, she would prefer the lobby plus second‑floor zone to become more ‘glassy’ to reinforce the simplified diagram and modern character.
- Russell Sherman advises paring back borrowed vocabulary from the hotel so the office can pursue a cleaner contemporary language consistent with its own massing logic.
#### Hotel streetwall massing and precise plan guidance
The DRC sees tension between the precise plan requirement to break up long façades and the hotel’s conceptual diagram that shows a strong, continuous streetwall supporting a key corner tower. In the current design, three vertical segments along Ellis, reinforced by window groupings, read as separate masses that compete with rather than support the corner. The committee asks the team to simplify the façade into one coherent plane, use subtler breaks or detailing to satisfy guideline intent, and adjust window patterns so the corner tower becomes the dominant element.

- Russell Sherman explains that the Ellis elevation currently reads as three distinct vertical elements because of the way the windows and pilasters are arranged, contradicting the diagram that shows a single mass supporting the corner tower.
- Tom Jess acknowledges that the team over‑emphasized façade breaks to avoid more than 100 feet of unbroken plane per the precise plan and agrees they can make those breaks more subtle or reconsider them if staff concurs.
- Rebecca Shapiro clarifies that the goal is not to eliminate modulation but to treat the lower streetwall as a coherent base whose articulation reinforces, rather than distracts from, the key corner mass.
- Russell Sherman notes that vertical window groupings at the ends and center emphasize segmentation and suggests reserving vertical emphasis for the tower while simplifying window expression on the main bar.
#### Hotel key corner tower and proportions
The committee generally supports the idea of a prominent metallic corner tower but feels the current relationship between the tower and the two‑story lobby below is weak. The tower appears to sit atop a wide lobby frame rather than rising from it, and the canopy height further diminishes vertical emphasis. The DRC recommends increasing the tower’s visual integration with the base—potentially by lowering the canopy frame, aligning elements, and enhancing the ground plane treatment—so that the corner clearly reads as the project’s primary urban marker.

- Tom Jess describes the corner at Ellis and National as the ‘key corner’ with metallic Trespa panels, vertical window slots, a framing element, and a two‑story glazed lobby meant to signal prominence.
- Russell Sherman observes that the tower feels disconnected from the lobby frame, as if it is sitting on top rather than emerging from it, and he suggests lowering the canopy frame to enhance the tower’s vertical reading.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks the team to coordinate building and landscape design at the main corner to create a more special, activity‑rich public realm that reinforces the tower’s importance.
- Russell Sherman notes that precise plan precedents show heavy, solid bases with lighter, more glazed upper masses and urges the team to study similar proportional moves so the corner mass feels grounded but not bulky.
#### Hotel upper massing, window design, and weight reduction
The upper two floors of the hotel, expressed as a lighter recessed mass, are intended to be subservient to the lower bar but currently appear heavy. The committee recommends increasing glazing or adjusting window proportions on this upper volume to reduce its visual weight and distinguish it from the more solid lower levels. More broadly, they encourage creative window detailing—such as fins, deeper punches, or varied patterns—to add shadow, richness, and a less generic ‘hotel’ feel while still respecting the constraints of repetitive guestroom modules.

- Russell Sherman points to precise plan images showing more glazed, lighter upper floors above solid bases and suggests that the hotel’s recessed white volume adopt a similar strategy to reduce apparent mass.
- Tom Jess agrees that additional glazing on the recessed upper two floors could lighten their visual weight and better express them as secondary to the lower bar.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks the team to explore window proportion changes on select areas—such as the upper mass or key corner—to differentiate masses and avoid the overly standardized ‘hotel window’ look.
- Russell Sherman encourages the use of fins, deeper recesses, or other detailing to generate shadow and visual interest across the large planes without resorting to excessive vertical segmentation.
#### Hotel secondary frontages and Porte Cochere
The DRC turns to the hotel’s National Avenue elevation and the internal drop‑off zone, recognizing that the porte cochere will be heavily used and therefore a key public frontage. While Tom Jess explains operational and code constraints around circulation and egress, Linda Pomancini and Rebecca Shapiro worry the main internal arrival sequence still feels like a ‘back entrance’ relative to the corner lobby. They ask the team to revisit internal layout and façade treatment so the drop‑off zone reads as a high‑quality, clearly legible arrival that relates strongly to the lobby and street.

- Rebecca Shapiro notes that National Avenue presents another public frontage opportunity and suggests designing a secondary focal area—with landscape and façade treatment—around the porte cochere to mirror the level of attention at the key corner.
- Linda Pomancini expresses concern that guests arriving via the drop‑off area might feel they are entering through a back corridor to reach the lobby, and she calls for a stronger spatial connection between drop‑off and reception.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks whether the interior stair and work area could be reconfigured so that more of the lobby circulation and activity fronts the porte cochere and National elevation.
- Tom Jess explains that the drop‑off layout has been heavily iterated to comply with limits on curb cuts and egress requirements but agrees to study how to elevate its architectural and landscape expression so it feels more like a primary arrival zone.
#### Hotel side and rear elevations, mural wall, and rooftop use
For the hotel’s rear elevation facing adjacent properties, the team proposes a large blank wall over the automated parking core to be treated as a future mural. The DRC supports using this as a true public art opportunity rather than a patterning exercise. They also ask about potential rooftop activation over the parking podium but accept the applicant’s explanation of structural and code constraints, while reiterating that rooftop utilities and solar must remain screened.

- Tom Jess notes that the rear elevation over the central parking core is a blank wall that the team proposes as a mural location, with specific artwork to be determined later in collaboration with an artist.
- Rebecca Shapiro confirms that the city expects this wall to host genuine public art rather than simply patterned materials and asks that this be carried through entitlement and implementation.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks whether the roof over the parking structure could be used for active open space, but Tom Jess explains that the parking podium is non‑load‑bearing and upgrading it to occupiable live loads would be prohibitively complex and costly.
- Tom Jess adds that rooftop active use was also constrained by building‑code separation between the steel parking structure and the wood‑frame hotel, and that the area will instead host solar panels and limited equipment, all of which must be fully screened by parapets.
#### Material strategy, accents, and detailing expectations
Although the DRC’s primary focus is on massing and architectural relationships, members briefly address materials and color. They find the general palette acceptable but warn that the metallic corner treatment and EFIS fields alone may not provide enough richness without careful detailing. The committee urges the team to use window surrounds, joints, and secondary materials strategically to emphasize key elements, and flags that some brand‑driven colors may need adjustment during mock‑up review for compatibility and visual balance.

- Rebecca Shapiro says the committee is prioritizing massing and articulation at this phase but still expects the team to refine material choices to ensure the right areas stand out and the buildings feel appropriately detailed at pedestrian scale.
- Russell Sherman notes that EFIS is the ‘default’ field material and urges the architect to use higher‑quality detailing around windows and feature elements to avoid a flat, generic appearance.
- Rebecca Shapiro observes that the metallic Trespa at the hotel corner may not be sufficient on its own to differentiate the tower and suggests that additional material or detail exploration could be needed.
- The committee signals that branding colors and accents will be scrutinized in future color mock‑up review to ensure they complement Mountain View’s context rather than dominate the composition.
#### Process and next steps for 500 Ellis
The meeting closes on the 500 Ellis item with an emphasis on iterative collaboration. The DRC wants the applicant to work at a schematic level with planning staff to test revisions on diagrams, sketches, and limited sheets before overhauling full plan sets. This approach aims to align the massing revisions, paseo redesign, and material emphasis with city objectives while avoiding unnecessary rework.

- Rebecca Shapiro encourages the design team to iterate at a sketch or schematic level with the project planner and her office before revising full drawing sets, to ensure the revised massing and façade concepts head in the right direction.
- Rebecca Shapiro notes that this collaborative approach typically prevents ‘paper trading’ cycles where teams revise many sheets before staff and the DRC can confirm alignment.
- Tom Jess agrees to work closely with the planner and appreciates the detailed feedback on massing and frontage design.
- Rebecca Shapiro reiterates that staff can facilitate targeted check‑ins on key issues such as the Ellis paseo entry, office lobby prominence, and hotel corner articulation as the design evolves.
#### 1040 Terra Bella self‑storage and Alta Housing partnership context
The second item covers a long‑running proposal for two modern self‑storage buildings at 1040 Terra Bella Avenue, tied to a land donation that enables an Alta Housing affordable project. Public Storage will replace existing ‘drive‑up’ storage with a six‑story and a four‑story building, while ceding Terra Bella frontage and land to Alta. Bryan Miranda emphasizes broad Planning Commission and City Council support for the architecture and frames this review as focused on detailed refinements sought by the DRC in a prior session.

- Bryan Miranda explains that Public Storage is donating roughly half an acre and the Terra Bella frontage to Alta Housing in exchange for re‑entitling its site from older drive‑up storage to two denser multi‑story storage buildings.
- Bryan Miranda notes that the six‑story building (phase one) is likely to be built regardless of market conditions, while the four‑story second building is more market‑dependent but still expected.
- Bryan Miranda reports that both the Planning Commission and City Council unanimously supported the architecture, expressing surprise that a storage building could look so good, and he acknowledges that DRC feedback focuses on fine‑grained design details.
- Bryan Miranda reminds the committee that the site is heavily influenced by a freeway overpass that visually covers much of the buildings, making perfect elevation views unlikely in reality.
#### Revised storage building elevations and massing simplification
Architect Jonathan Thomas presents updates to both storage buildings that respond to the previous DRC review. For each, he has simplified the split‑face CMU base, clarified the framing elements, improved symmetry where appropriate, and adjusted glazing to align with vertical massing moves. The DRC generally agrees that these changes are successful, while requesting a few proportion tweaks on the smaller building’s south elevation.

- Jonathan Thomas shows that the previous column of banded CMU base has been simplified into a more continuous, grounded base on all elevations, reducing visual clutter while preserving vertical elements that touch down to the ground.
- On Building 1’s south elevation, Jonathan Thomas realigns the inverted ‘L’ frames to correspond to major step‑backs in the building mass and makes the central vertical element symmetrical, responding to prior comments about inconsistent rhythms.
- On Building 2’s north elevation, Jonathan Thomas has aligned upper glazing with the loading lobby doors below to create a more coherent vertical relationship visible from the cul‑de‑sac.
- The DRC acknowledges that the revised elevations better match the earlier massing concepts and appreciates the overall simplification and alignment of elements.
#### Building 2 south elevation proportions and corner detailing
The most significant remaining architectural concern is the four‑story Building 2 south elevation, where the inverted ‘L’ brow elements are proportionally long and close together relative to the building’s height. This creates an awkward middle zone and a double‑reveal corner detail that feels atypical. The DRC asks the architect to widely space the inverted Ls and simplify the corner reveal system to improve rhythm and better match the taller Building 1’s successful composition.

- Rebecca Shapiro identifies Building 2’s south elevation as the main remaining issue, noting that the inverted ‘L’ elements feel too compressed around the central panel and throw off the façade’s proportions.
- Linda Pomancini recommends pushing the inverted Ls further apart so that the middle plaster field where the ‘7’ is located becomes wider, yielding a more comfortable rhythm without changing the rest of the massing.
- Russell Sherman points out that the length of the brow relative to its height is out of balance and suggests that increasing the central field’s width will visually shorten each brow run to a more appropriate proportion.
- Rebecca Shapiro notes that the corner at Building 2 has a double‑reveal panel treatment not used elsewhere and suggests revising it to a single reveal plus gray panel approach consistent with other corners.
#### Materials, accent color, and brand considerations
The committee briefly reviews the material palette and digital color board, recognizing that physical samples are a more reliable guide. They appreciate the coordination of corrugated metal as both façade and rooftop screen material, but raise concerns about the light, pastel‑like purple accent band used with the orange brand color. The DRC suggests that, at mock‑up stage, the team evaluate a darker, more neutral gray alternative to the purple to ensure the accent system appears refined and context‑appropriate while still accommodating Public Storage’s branding where feasible.

- Jonathan Thomas explains that digital color renderings are unreliable across monitors and reports that physical material boards with true color samples have been provided to staff.
- Rebecca Shapiro notes that the purple accent band appears too light and pastel in the digital documents, especially next to the orange, and may not provide the sophisticated contrast desired.
- Bryan Miranda explains that the purple is part of Public Storage’s brand palette and that earlier city feedback requested color beyond orange, which led the team to select purple as a secondary accent.
- Jonathan Thomas agrees that a more neutral, darker gray accent could be preferable and welcomes a DRC recommendation to test both purple and a gray alternative in on‑site mock‑ups before finalizing the accent color.
- The DRC signals that, during mock‑up review, staff may recommend reverting to a gray band if the branded purple reads too bright or incongruent with the rest of the palette.
#### Rooftop mechanical and solar screening
The DRC examines rooftop equipment layouts for both storage buildings, emphasizing the city’s requirement that all mechanical and solar infrastructure be fully screened. Jonathan Thomas explains that the team is using conservative equipment heights and six‑foot corrugated screens set well back from parapets, with parapet heights sufficient to block views from the ground. The committee accepts this approach but asks the applicant to confirm that solar racking will remain below the lowest parapet and to continue clustering and minimizing equipment as design advances.

- Jonathan Thomas describes the rooftop equipment locations as conservative placeholders developed with the MEP engineer, using six‑foot‑tall screens around units that are not expected to exceed five feet in height.
- Jonathan Thomas notes that the corrugated metal used for rooftop screens is the same material as parts of the façade, intentionally integrating mechanical screening into the architecture rather than treating it as an add‑on.
- Rebecca Shapiro reminds the applicant that city code requires full screening of rooftop mechanical and solar equipment on new buildings and expresses concern about the potential for solar panel structures to exceed parapet heights in later design stages.
- Bryan Miranda explains that HVAC loads in these storage buildings are relatively low because Public Storage cools to 85°F and heats to 55°F, which allows smaller, more dispersed units that are easier to screen and leave room for solar arrays.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks staff to include a condition requiring confirmation at building permit that solar panel racking remains below the lowest parapet and encourages further equipment consolidation while maintaining solar coverage.
#### Landscape revisions, tree canopy, and native planting targets
The DRC returns to landscape design for the Terra Bella project, acknowledging substantial improvements in tree quantity, placement, and species mix. Jonathan Thomas explains that the team reduced parking to add trees, introduced trees along the north property line, and adjusted species to thrive in stormwater planters. Rebecca Shapiro pushes for a few additional larger trees along the freeway sound wall, careful coordination with utilities, and exploration of tree opportunities in narrow walkways, while also requesting adherence to the city’s California native plant target.

- Jonathan Thomas reports that the team reduced four parking stalls at Building 1 and removed several spaces at Building 2 to free up room for additional trees and improve trash enclosure screening.
- Jonathan Thomas explains that stormwater treatment areas at the parking lot edges constrain tree selection, and the landscape architect has chosen species that can tolerate those conditions while still providing canopy.
- Jonathan Thomas notes that new low planting and smaller trees are now proposed along the north property line within a utility easement, where no planting previously existed.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks whether a few taller, larger‑canopy trees can be interspersed along the freeway sound wall edge, especially over the middle two‑thirds of Building 1, to strengthen long‑term canopy despite narrow planting zones.
- Rebecca Shapiro questions the feasibility of a tree shown adjacent to a transformer, and Jonathan Thomas confirms that clearances have been checked but agrees to maintain proper access while providing screening.
- Rebecca Shapiro suggests exploring tree‑grate plantings along the nine‑foot‑wide sidewalk on the east side of Building 1, arguing that this back‑of‑house walkway is lightly used and could accommodate trees without impeding operations.
- The committee reiterates that staff will be looking for a planting palette that meets the city’s 75% California native species objective.
#### Security gate placement and frontage character
The committee supports the applicant’s move to pull the security gate for Building 2 back from the street, but they remain concerned that the gate alignment may still sit slightly forward of the building corner and visually compete with a glazed façade. Rebecca Shapiro asks the team to study shifting the gate further back so that the building architecture, not the fence line, defines the San Rafael frontage. This adjustment would improve the street’s visual quality while maintaining site security.

- Jonathan Thomas explains that, in response to prior comments, the security gate for Building 2 has been rotated perpendicular to the façade and set back from the street to reduce its visual impact at the cul‑de‑sac.
- Rebecca Shapiro notes that the gate’s current start point appears slightly ahead of the building face and conflicts with a prominent glazed corner, risking the impression that fencing rather than architecture defines the frontage.
- Rebecca Shapiro requests that the team explore moving the gate origin further into the site, ideally behind the plane of the building’s glass corner, so that the building remains the primary visual element from San Rafael.
- Jonathan Thomas agrees to study this adjustment, balancing architectural clarity with operational and security needs.
#### Frontage planting for Phase 2 area and interim conditions
The DRC revisits whether any portion of Building 2’s San Rafael frontage landscaping can be delivered during Phase 1, given the Alta project’s timing and the desire for a complete streetscape. Public Storage explains that grading and demolition constraints make full buildout premature and potentially wasteful. The committee ultimately frames a two‑tier recommendation: ideally, implement the Phase 2 frontage tree planting early; if not feasible, coordinate with staff on meaningful interim planting in front of the temporary office trailer to improve the cul‑de‑sac environment during the multi‑year buildout.

- Rebecca Shapiro notes that Alta Housing’s project is moving on a similar timeline to Building 1 and asks whether tree planting along the Phase 2 San Rafael frontage could be advanced into Phase 1 to create a more complete streetscape.
- Jonathan Thomas and Bryan Miranda explain that Phase 2 will involve significant grading and building demolition, and that current grades in front of future Building 2 differ from the ultimate condition, making early installation of permanent frontage improvements risky.
- Jonathan Thomas adds that the location of the future sidewalk will shift relative to today’s curb line, so trees planted now in the ultimate locations would likely conflict with future sidewalk placement or grading.
- Rebecca Shapiro clarifies that the DRC is not asking for full off‑site frontage upgrades in Phase 1 but is primarily interested in tree and under‑story plantings along the current San Rafael edge in front of the temporary office trailer.
- Rebecca Shapiro recommends, as an ideal, that the applicant implement Phase 2‑type frontage tree plantings during Phase 1, but failing that, she asks the team to work with staff on an interim planting plan to soften the cul‑de‑sac frontage during the Alta and Building 1 construction period.
#### Utility coordination and landscape screening
Because utility locations often shift between entitlement and building permit, the DRC underscores the need to coordinate utility placement with landscape design to preserve planting opportunities. In particular, above‑ground transformers, backflow preventers, and similar equipment can easily consume key frontage areas if not planned early. The committee requests a strong condition that the applicant work with planning staff at permit stage to adjust layout and ensure utilities are screened by planting wherever practical.

- Rebecca Shapiro notes that in many projects, required clearances for above‑ground utilities discovered at permit stage significantly reduce available planting areas, undermining earlier landscape intentions.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks the applicant to proactively coordinate utility layouts with planning staff and the landscape architect as the design advances to maintain frontage trees and shrubs wherever possible.
- Rebecca Shapiro recommends adding or strengthening conditions of approval requiring landscape screening of all visible above‑ground equipment, especially along public frontages.
- Jonathan Thomas acknowledges the concern and agrees that coordination between civil, landscape, and planning will be needed to avoid last‑minute loss of planting area.
#### Closing direction and schedule for Terra Bella
The DRC concludes that the Terra Bella storage project has successfully addressed its earlier comments, with only targeted refinements outstanding on Building 2’s south elevation, accent color evaluation, rooftop screening confirmation, and landscape adjustments. Rebecca Shapiro encourages the applicant to incorporate these changes into the entitlement set where possible before upcoming Planning Commission and City Council hearings, noting the high priority elected officials place on trees and streetscape quality. Staff will continue to work with the team on mock‑ups and detailed conditions as the project moves forward.

- Rebecca Shapiro commends the applicant for being consistently responsive to DRC feedback and acknowledges that the latest revisions substantially address prior concerns.
- Rebecca Shapiro asks the team to implement key elevation and landscape refinements—especially for Building 2’s south elevation and tree layout—into the entitlement plans if timing allows, so decision‑makers can see a near‑final design.
- Rebecca Shapiro notes that City Council members have shown strong interest in tree canopy and streetscape quality on other projects and indicates that having updated drawings will facilitate their review.
- Jonathan Thomas and Bryan Miranda thank the committee for the guidance and confirm they will coordinate with planner Edgar on incorporating the final changes before public hearings.
