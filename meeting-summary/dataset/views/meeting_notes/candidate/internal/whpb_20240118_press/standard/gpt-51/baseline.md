### Overview
  The briefing aboard Air Force One, led by Olivia Dalton and John Kirby, covers President Biden’s trip to North Carolina to announce $82 million in American Rescue Plan broadband investments, the strategic significance of North Carolina’s fiber-optic industry and associated job growth, and ongoing savings for hundreds of thousands of residents on Internet bills. Kirby updates reporters on U.S. strikes to degrade Houthi missile capabilities in the Red Sea, the tense Pakistan-Iran missile exchanges, and the administration’s firm stance against an Iranian nuclear weapon, while outlining the legal basis for current military operations and previewing Jake Sullivan’s meeting with families of American hostages held by Hamas. He also reaffirms U.S. commitment to a two-state solution despite Prime Minister Netanyahu’s opposition, describes active engagement with Israel on minimizing civilian casualties and transitioning to lower-intensity operations in Gaza, and underscores that U.S. forces remain in Iraq at Baghdad’s invitation to fight ISIS. Dalton then addresses domestic and legislative issues, emphasizing Biden’s optimistic view of ongoing bipartisan border negotiations tied to the national security supplemental, the urgency of Ukraine aid for European and NATO security, and the need for Congress to fund additional border personnel, while also defending the administration’s EV agenda amid cold-weather performance questions and detailing the White House’s response to the DOJ’s Uvalde report, including reliance on the Office of Gun Violence Prevention and renewed calls for broader gun safety legislation.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### North Carolina broadband investments
  Ms. Dalton outlines President Biden’s trip to Raleigh to announce $82 million from the American Rescue Plan’s Capital Projects Fund to expand high-speed Internet access in North Carolina. She frames this as part of the administration’s broader goal to close the digital divide by 2030, noting the large number of Americans who lacked access when Biden took office. Dalton highlights the importance for rural communities, families, seniors, and small businesses, and connects the announcement to existing savings on monthly Internet bills for North Carolinians. She also stresses the economic benefits for the state, including local fiber-optic manufacturing and job creation.
- Ms. Dalton states that the President will announce $82 million in American Rescue Plan Capital Project Fund investments to connect additional North Carolina homes and businesses to high-speed Internet.
- Ms. Dalton recalls that when President Biden entered office, 24 million Americans lacked Internet access and many more lacked affordable, reliable connectivity.
- Ms. Dalton says the President committed to ensuring every American and small business has affordable, reliable high-speed Internet by 2030.
- Ms. Dalton notes that one in three North Carolinians lives in a rural area, making expanded broadband access particularly significant for the state.
- Ms. Dalton explains that more than 880,000 North Carolinians are already saving money on monthly Internet bills due to American Rescue Plan measures.
- Ms. Dalton emphasizes that North Carolina produces about 40 percent of U.S. fiber-optic cable and cites companies like CommScope and Corning investing over $550 billion and creating 650-plus local jobs.
- Ms. Dalton characterizes the broadband and manufacturing investments as a win for workers, families, small businesses, and the North Carolina economy and notes that Governor Cooper will join the President.
#### U.S. strikes on Houthi missiles
  Mr. Kirby reports on recent U.S. military strikes targeting Houthi missiles in the Southern Red Sea. He confirms strikes occurred the previous night and again that morning against missiles deemed prepared for imminent launch at shipping. Kirby stresses the U.S. goal is not broader conflict but protection of U.S. and international maritime traffic. He underscores that the strikes are degrading Houthi capabilities and will continue as needed under the President’s self-defense authorities, without specifying future tactical options.
- Mr. Kirby states that U.S. Central Command conducted strikes last night against a range of Houthi missiles that were prepared to fire into the Southern Red Sea.
- Mr. Kirby reports that additional strikes took place this morning against a couple of anti-ship missiles believed to be prepared for imminent launch into the Southern Red Sea.
- Mr. Kirby says Central Command will issue a formal statement on the latest strikes.
- Mr. Kirby explains that the United States is not seeking conflict with the Houthis or broader regional conflict but must act in self-defense to protect U.S. and merchant vessels and international shipping.
- Mr. Kirby states that, as President Biden said, strikes against the Houthis will continue as long as necessary to disrupt and degrade their ability to conduct attacks in the Red Sea.
- Mr. Kirby argues that each strike removes capabilities from the Houthis and makes it harder for them to continue attacks, while reiterating that they face a choice to stop.
- Mr. Kirby declines to detail or "telegraph" additional options or changes in tactics, including any potential strikes beyond current targets.
- Mr. Kirby later clarifies that these strikes are conducted under the President’s Article II Commander-in-Chief authorities and in line with U.N. Charter Article 51 on self-defense.
#### Pakistan-Iran missile exchanges
  Mr. Kirby addresses reports that Pakistan launched missile strikes into Iran following Iranian strikes in Pakistan. He expresses U.S. concern about possible escalation between two well-armed states and characterizes Iran’s earlier action as reckless and destabilizing. Kirby notes the U.S. is monitoring the situation closely and remains in contact with Pakistani counterparts but refrains from commenting on operational details, target legitimacy, or any specific U.S. support commitments. He also declines to evaluate external calls for peace, including those from the Taliban, beyond reiterating U.S. opposition to escalation and skepticism about Taliban credibility.
- Mr. Kirby confirms awareness of Pakistani missile strikes into Iran and says the United States is monitoring the situation very closely.
- Mr. Kirby notes that both Pakistan and Iran are well-armed nations and stresses that the United States does not want to see an escalation of armed conflict between them.
- Mr. Kirby describes Iran’s initial strike inside Pakistan as another reckless attack and an example of Iran’s destabilizing regional behavior.
- Mr. Kirby says he is not aware of any prenotification from Pakistan to the United States before Pakistan’s strikes.
- Mr. Kirby declines to provide an update on whether the United States intends to support Pakistan in response to these events.
- Mr. Kirby says he has no independent U.S. military assessment to share regarding whether Pakistan’s targets in Iran were legitimate.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that the United States is in touch with Pakistani counterparts but will let Pakistan speak to its own military operations.
- Mr. Kirby responds to a question about Taliban calls for peace between Iran and Pakistan by saying the United States will let the Taliban speak for themselves and emphasizing that the Taliban must meet their commitments on women’s rights and international law to be taken seriously.
#### Iran nuclear ambitions and U.S. policy
  In response to questions about Iran’s motivations and nuclear ambitions, Mr. Kirby restates U.S. policy that Iran must not obtain a nuclear weapon. He notes the administration initially preferred a diplomatic path to constrain Iran’s program but acknowledges that prospect is not viable in the near term. Kirby emphasizes that the President is prepared to use available capabilities to prevent Iran from reaching nuclear weapons capability if necessary. He explicitly declines to ascribe specific nuclear ambitions to Iran, leaving that to the regime to state.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that U.S. policy is to prevent Iran from acquiring nuclear weapons because a nuclear-armed Iran would be destabilizing for the region and the world.
- Mr. Kirby says the administration initially preferred to achieve the no-nuclear outcome through diplomacy but concedes that is not likely to happen anytime soon.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that the United States will ensure the Commander-in-Chief has the capabilities and options necessary to prevent Iran from achieving a nuclear weapon capability if it comes to that.
- Mr. Kirby declines to speculate on Iran’s motivations for pursuing nuclear capabilities and says the Iranian regime should speak to its own ambitions.

#### Two-state solution and Netanyahu’s stance
  Mr. Kirby responds to questions about Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu’s reported rejection of a Palestinian state in a post-war Gaza scenario. He reiterates that President Biden remains strongly committed to a two-state solution as the best outcome for Israelis, Palestinians, and regional stability. Kirby acknowledges Netanyahu’s opposing views but insists the U.S. position does not change and that discussions with Israel and regional partners continue. He also describes U.S. priorities for post-conflict Gaza, including no reoccupation, representative governance reflecting Palestinian aspirations, and continued humanitarian access, while maintaining support for Israel’s current military needs against Hamas.
- Mr. Kirby affirms that President Biden continues to view a two-state solution as in the best interest of Israelis, Palestinians, and the region as a whole.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Prime Minister Netanyahu’s rejection of a Palestinian state is not a new position and says the United States clearly sees the issue differently.
- Mr. Kirby states that the United States believes Palestinians have every right to live in an independent state with peace and security and that the administration will keep working toward that goal.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that current U.S. focus includes ensuring Israel has what it needs to defend itself against Hamas and that humanitarian aid continues to reach civilians in Gaza.
- Mr. Kirby outlines U.S. expectations for post-conflict Gaza, including governance that reflects Palestinian aspirations, a Palestinian vote and voice in their future, and no reoccupation of Gaza.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges skepticism about Netanyahu’s intentions but says the administration will keep pressing its position in discussions with Israeli and regional counterparts.
- Mr. Kirby insists the administration still considers a two-state solution viable and the best outcome for both peoples.

He indicates that any future decisions on the mission, posture, and resources will be handled through ongoing consultations with the Iraqi government rather than unilateral moves.

#### Israel strategy, civilian casualties, and conditioning aid
  Mr. Kirby responds to concerns from Democratic lawmakers about Israel’s conduct of the war in Gaza and suggestions to reconsider U.S. strategy or condition aid. He argues that the record shows Israel has not ignored U.S. advice, citing examples where U.S. urging influenced operational changes such as humanitarian corridors, border openings, and reduced airstrikes. Kirby acknowledges there have been too many civilian casualties and says the U.S. continues pressing Israel to be more discriminant and careful. He also notes that Israel has begun transitioning to lower-intensity operations, particularly in northern Gaza, in line with U.S. requests, while reaffirming Israel’s sovereignty over its own military decisions.
- Mr. Kirby disputes the notion that Israel has ignored U.S. guidance, asserting that the record does not support that claim.
- Mr. Kirby cites Israeli adjustments such as tailoring operations in northern Gaza, adding humanitarian corridors, opening the Kerem Shalom crossing, sharing operational information, and relying less on airstrikes as examples influenced by U.S. counsel.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that Israel is a sovereign country and ultimately decides its own military operations, but he underscores that the United States strongly influences "how" those operations are conducted.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges that one civilian casualty is one too many and says there have been too many civilian casualties in Gaza, which the United States has raised with Israel.
- Mr. Kirby explains that the United States has urged Israel to tailor its operations to be more discriminant, cautious, and careful in order to reduce harm to civilians.
- Mr. Kirby points out that Israel has recently removed a division of troops from northern Gaza, which he describes as the start of a transition to lower-intensity operations that the United States has been urging.
- Mr. Kirby does not directly address whether the administration will consider conditioning aid but emphasizes current engagement on operational conduct.

Charter. He does not indicate any immediate plan to seek additional authorization from Congress.
- Mr. Kirby says the strikes against Houthi targets are being conducted under the President’s Article II Commander-in-Chief authorities under the U.S. Constitution.

#### Uvalde DOJ report and gun violence prevention
  Ms. Dalton discusses the Department of Justice’s report on the Uvalde school shooting and the administration’s response. She says the report lays out heartbreaking details of what families have endured for two years and notes that the President will issue a more detailed statement committing to act on its recommendations. Dalton praises the Uvalde community for channeling grief into advocacy that helped secure passage of the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act, which she says is saving lives but remains insufficient. She underscores the role of the White House Office of Gun Violence Prevention in implementing lessons learned and supporting communities after mass shootings, while reiterating President Biden’s call for congressional action on measures like national red flag laws, universal background checks, and bans on assault weapons and high-capacity magazines.
- Ms. Dalton notes that Attorney General Garland’s press conference on the Uvalde DOJ report is ongoing as she speaks and says a detailed statement from President Biden will follow shortly.
- Ms. Dalton says the DOJ report presents, in heartbreaking detail, what Uvalde families have experienced over the past two years and acknowledges the additional pain the report may cause them.
- Ms. Dalton recalls that the President and First Lady visited Uvalde in May 2022 and praises the community for traveling to Washington to advocate for reforms that helped secure the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act.
- Ms. Dalton says the Uvalde community turned their pain into purpose by advocating for legislation that is now saving lives, while acknowledging that this is cold comfort to parents who lost children.
- Ms. Dalton previews that the President’s forthcoming statement will commit to implementing lessons learned from the report and strengthening the White House Office of Gun Violence Prevention’s support for communities after mass shootings.
- Ms. Dalton reiterates that the President believes only further congressional action—such as a national red flag law, universal background checks, and bans on assault weapons and high-capacity magazines—can truly prevent future tragedies like Uvalde.
