### Overview
The Education Enhancement Reserve Joint Powers Board convenes to approve prior minutes, review how Shoreline‑sourced funds currently support programs in the Mountain View–Los Altos Union High School District and Mountain View Whisman School District, and then focus on the expiring JPA agreement that governs these payments. Dr. Nellie Meyer and Dr. Ayindé Rudolph detail that the districts depend on roughly $3.2 million and $5.3 million respectively for specialized academies, STEM classes, technology infrastructure, hands‑on science, and future textbook adoptions, while Treasurer Jesse Takahashi reports that FY 2022–23 payments total about $8.7 million after a 6.82% rise in Shoreline property tax revenues and that cumulative payments since inception approach $82 million. Assistant City Manager Audrey Ramberg outlines that the current JPA expires June 30, 2023, describes major Shoreline obligations for sea‑level‑rise protection, landfill remediation, and transportation, and proposes a 12–18 month extension with continued payments at least at current levels while staff updates the Shoreline Area Plan and develops a successor agreement tied to a 20‑year development horizon and the forthcoming Google Master Plan. Dr. Rudolph, supported by Dr. Meyer, raises concerns that new North Bayshore housing such as the PAIR/Sobrato project will generate significant student enrollment without matching revenue, presses for clarity on the county’s tax share, and warns that MVWSD will otherwise have to plan for $2.8 million in cuts during its budget cycle. After public comments urging both stronger school funding and responsible local investment in sea‑level‑rise infrastructure, the Board, city staff, and district leaders coalesce around a two‑step path: negotiate a short‑term “bridge” agreement in early 2023 that contractually guarantees at least current‑year payment levels so districts can finalize budgets without cuts, and in parallel define by summer a clear process and timeline for negotiating a more comprehensive long‑term successor JPA. The meeting closes with consensus to hold a JPA Board meeting in the first quarter of 2023 to adopt the short‑term guarantee and to continue collaborative work on the long‑term framework, with all parties emphasizing their ongoing partnership despite differing institutional pressures.

#### Status of JPA agreement and need for successor
Assistant City Manager Audrey Ramberg explains that the Education Enhancement Reserve JPA, which governs SRPC payments to the districts, will expire on June 30, 2023. She outlines the Shoreline Regional Park Community’s statutory origins, mandated uses of tax increment, and substantial upcoming infrastructure obligations. She then describes the staff‑level negotiation process that began in 2019 and argues that more time is needed to align fiscal forecasts with the forthcoming Google Master Plan and to update the Shoreline Area Plan so that it can legally support expanded school capacity payments. Her presentation frames a proposed 12–18 month extension with continued payments as a bridge to a more robust long‑term agreement.
- Ms. Ramberg states that the SRPC has been providing payments to the districts since 2006 under the Education Enhancement Reserve JPA, which is scheduled to expire on June 30, 2023.
- Ms. Ramberg explains that the Shoreline Regional Park Community was created by state legislation in 1969 to fund a regional park, support housing and economic development in North Bayshore, and finance related public improvements via tax increment.
- Ms. Ramberg notes that the uses of Shoreline tax increment are constrained by the SRPC Act and a 1977 Area Plan adopted by the SRPC Board and the City, and that any funding to schools must be consistent with these documents.
- Ms. Ramberg reports that a staff working group, including city and district representatives, began meeting in mid‑2019 as required by the current agreement to share information on Shoreline obligations, North Bayshore implementation, and school funding mechanisms.
- Ms. Ramberg describes major planned improvements funded by SRPC tax increment, including $122 million for sea‑level‑rise protection, $19 million for the closed landfill, and $487 million in transportation improvements, of which roughly $267 million net of developer fees and community benefits is expected to be borne by the Shoreline community.
- Ms. Ramberg explains that she informed district staff in March that long‑range fiscal forecasts depend on assumptions in the forthcoming Google Master Plan for North Bayshore and that she therefore proposed a 12–18 month extension of the current agreement.
- Ms. Ramberg clarifies that during such an extension the city commits to continued payments at least equal to current levels—approximately $5.3 million to MVWSD and $3.4 million to MVLA—while a successor agreement is developed.
- Ms. Ramberg reports that legal analysis finds an update to the Shoreline Area Plan is necessary to allow expanded use of tax increment for school capacity and that staff proposes undertaking this update to explicitly recognize school needs arising from new housing.
- Ms. Ramberg outlines a conceptual 20‑year “development phase” during which Shoreline revenues must cover both major infrastructure obligations and increased payments to districts, followed by a steady‑state period when most remaining tax increment would flow to all taxing entities, including the districts.
- Ms. Ramberg concludes that a 12–18 month extension is needed to complete legal and fiscal analysis, update the Area Plan, and negotiate an interest‑based successor agreement acceptable to the Board, City Council, and both school boards.
#### District concerns about student growth and funding gap
Dr. Ayindé Rudolph raises substantive concerns about how new residential development in North Bayshore will impact district operating costs relative to the guaranteed minimum payment under the current JPA. He cites the PAIR/Sobrato project and anticipated student generation to argue that the district could face an unfunded mandate once those students enroll if funding does not increase commensurately. He also questions perceived differences between how the county and school districts are treated in Shoreline revenue sharing and warns that absent a firm successor agreement, MVWSD must begin planning multi‑million‑dollar cuts as it enters its budget cycle.
- Dr. Rudolph asks whether the Shoreline Act was amended in connection with the 2005 county–city agreement that provides the county with its tax share and questions why the county is treated differently from the school districts.
- Ms. Ramberg responds that the county agreement relates to a complex set of legal obligations and functions similarly to the EER JPA as a contractual obligation, but she does not agree that the county receives its full tax allocation.
- Dr. Rudolph explains that the PAIR/Sobrato project of 635 units is projected to generate approximately 120 students for MVWSD and that at the district’s current expenditure of about $23,000 per student, these students will require roughly $2.8 million in annual operating funds.
- Dr. Rudolph argues that because the guaranteed base payment to MVWSD is $2.8 million, the district could be educating students from Shoreline‑area housing without receiving sufficient incremental revenue, effectively creating an unfunded mandate borne by other taxpayers in the district.
- Dr. Rudolph notes that district modeling suggests per‑pupil expenditures may drop below $15,000 after full build‑out, potentially shifting MVWSD from basic‑aid (community‑funded) to LCFF status and altering its funding structure.
- Ms. Ramberg acknowledges the concern and says staff must align student timing with revenue, confirming that current payments include both general growth in assessed value and full tax‑rate allocation for residential within Shoreline, and she commits to analyzing timing and amounts relative to student enrollment impacts.
- Dr. Rudolph asks if all payments cease absent a successor agreement after June 2023, and Ms. Ramberg confirms that without some form of agreement payments would stop, although she emphasizes that the city does not intend that outcome.
- Dr. Rudolph states that he is only authorized to agree to a six‑month extension and warns that MVWSD’s budget cycle beginning in February will require planning for $2.8 million in cuts if there is no binding successor or extension by the time the board adopts its budget.
- Dr. Rudolph expresses concern that the county receives a more favorable revenue arrangement and that MVWSD has been criticized for considering a Mello‑Roos district even though such mechanisms are one of the few ways to cover operating costs for new students under the current Shoreline arrangement.
#### Clarification on county share and 20‑year phased concept
The board and staff seek clarification on the county’s Shoreline revenue share and on how a proposed 20‑year phased transition would function. Treasurer Takahashi corrects the impression that the county receives its full tax allocation, while Dr. Meyer and Dr. Rudolph probe how long school funding terms might last and how many renegotiations might be required. Ms. Ramberg explains that the 20‑year timeframe mirrors North Bayshore build‑out and is intended to capture the full development and infrastructure period before transitioning most tax increment back to local taxing entities, including the districts.
- Mr. Takahashi clarifies that the county currently receives closer to 40% of what it would receive under a full tax‑rate allocation rather than the full 20% share referenced in some documents.
- Dr. Rudolph requests backup documentation supporting the city’s interpretation of the county agreement, noting he has a copy of the 2005 agreement that appears to ramp the county to 20% of Shoreline tax revenues by FY 2010–11 and beyond.
- Mr. Takahashi agrees to provide the analytical backup showing how the county’s actual receipts compare to a full tax allocation.
- Dr. Meyer expresses concern about prolonged extensions leading to leadership turnover and emphasizes the district’s interest in completing a new agreement as soon as possible.
- Dr. Meyer asks whether the previously discussed phased transition was envisioned as 20 years and seeks clarity on what that phased transition entails.
- Ms. Ramberg states that the 20‑year horizon corresponds to the time needed to meet housing and infrastructure objectives in the Shoreline Act and North Bayshore Precise Plan and that during this period, growing assessed value should support both needed capital improvements and increased payments to the districts.
- Ms. Ramberg explains that once the 20‑year development period concludes, the Shoreline community’s role would narrow to regional obligations such as the park and landfill, with most remaining tax increment flowing to all taxing entities, including the school districts.
- Dr. Meyer asks how often the EER JPA might be renegotiated within that 20‑year window and whether a longer term agreement could provide more stability and reduce transaction costs.
- Ms. Ramberg replies that the renegotiation term is itself subject to negotiation and that staff intends to use a 20‑year forecast tied to development timing to guide decisions on payment levels and agreement duration.
#### Public comment on JPA and school funding
Two members of the public provide contrasting but related perspectives on how Shoreline funds should be allocated between infrastructure and schools. Former Shoreline advocate Laura Blakely urges prioritizing full school district shares and pressing Google to fund infrastructure, pointing to significant projected enrollment growth. Councilmember Pat Showalter stresses the city’s responsibility to fund sea‑level‑rise protections locally and supports an extension to allow careful negotiations that balance infrastructure and school needs. The comments highlight political and community expectations surrounding the successor JPA.
- Ms. Blakely identifies herself as one of the original “Shoreline Six” who advocated 13 years ago for redirecting SRPC funds to schools and describes the resulting redirections as rewarding.
- Ms. Blakely recalls that the 2010 deal was intended as an interim arrangement while the city determined how much Shoreline revenue it needed for obligations, including sea‑level‑rise and infrastructure, and she contends those studies framed sea‑level rise as a regional problem needing regional or higher‑level funding.
- Ms. Blakely argues that the city should negotiate a new JPA with the school districts before completing the North Bayshore Development Plan and Google development agreement, and she urges that Google be asked to pay for more of the desired infrastructure to avoid diverting school funds.
- Ms. Blakely cites Google’s $17.35 billion third‑quarter profit as evidence that the company can afford greater infrastructure contributions and asserts that districts now face serving a student population projected to grow by 50%, which requires both operating funding and capital for new schools and classrooms.
- Ms. Blakely calls for SRPC to begin allocating the districts’ full roughly 40% share of Shoreline tax revenues starting next fiscal year, arguing that the districts need these funds immediately to build reserves for future facilities.
- Councilmember Showalter, speaking in her personal capacity, affirms that the city is committed to negotiating in good faith with both school districts to meet infrastructure and school needs.
- Councilmember Showalter emphasizes that sea‑level rise is both a regional and a very local problem for Mountain View as a shoreline city and says the city has a responsibility to ensure timely construction of protections that enable continued development and community benefits.
- Councilmember Showalter cautions that it is naive to assume sufficient state and federal funding will materialize for sea‑level‑rise projects because many shoreline communities will compete for limited funds, making local financing via SRPC essential.
- Councilmember Showalter supports a reasonable extension of the JPA to allow proper planning that addresses sea‑level‑rise infrastructure, park and landfill maintenance, and school funding in a balanced way.
#### Developing a short-term payment guarantee and negotiation timeline
Following public comment, the Board and staff focus on practical next steps to avoid funding disruption while negotiating a more comprehensive successor agreement. Dr. Rudolph stresses the need for clarity on process and timing so the districts can complete their FY 2023–24 budgets without assuming cuts. Ms. Ramberg and Chair McCarthy support creating a short‑term agreement that guarantees at least current‑year payment levels while staff maps out a longer‑term process, and all parties commit to an accelerated timeline, including a Board meeting in Q1 2023 to formalize the bridge arrangement.
- Dr. Rudolph notes that the current JPA ends in 2023 and that the Board historically meets only once a year, and he asks what cadence and process will be used to ensure a successor or extension is approved before payments lapse.
- Ms. Ramberg proposes that city and district staff (herself, Mike, and Rebecca) continue meeting frequently to translate the interests and input shared at this meeting into concrete recommendations for an extension or short‑term agreement and a path toward a long‑term successor agreement.
- Dr. Rudolph explains that both school districts begin formal budget development months before adoption and that MVWSD needs a firm commitment by the time its budget is finalized in May to avoid planning for $2.8 million in cuts.
- Dr. Meyer adds that staffing decisions, including potential layoffs, would need to be made as early as February or March if cuts are anticipated, underscoring the need for timely clarity.
- Chair McCarthy responds that a February–March time frame for an interim agreement is reasonable and states that the city intends to keep the districts whole at least at current funding levels, with future increases reflecting assessed‑value growth.
- Ms. Ramberg supports the idea of a short, easily executed agreement that guarantees at least the current payment levels for the next payment cycle, alongside a more detailed plan for the longer‑term JPA renegotiation.
- Dr. Rudolph suggests a two‑track approach: first, a short‑term “bridge” agreement guaranteeing the next payment at current levels so districts can budget without cuts, and second, an agreed process and timeline by summer for negotiating the longer‑term arrangement, including any 6‑, 12‑, or 18‑month extensions.
- Dr. Rudolph clarifies that by summer he expects both an executed short‑term guarantee and a documented process to reach the comprehensive long‑term successor agreement, not necessarily the final long‑term terms themselves.
- Chair McCarthy agrees with this two‑step approach and confirms that the Board can schedule additional meetings as needed rather than waiting for the traditional annual session.
- Ms. Ramberg acknowledges the need for that clarity and states that staff will work toward an interim agreement that memorializes the city’s commitment to continue payments at least at current levels while the broader negotiation proceeds.
- Dr. Rudolph asks to schedule a Board meeting in February or early March to approve the short‑term guarantee, and Chair McCarthy proposes setting a Q1 2023 target and having staff coordinate the specific date.
- Both Dr. Meyer and Dr. Rudolph endorse the Q1 2023 meeting approach, and Chair McCarthy records consensus to meet in the first quarter to finalize the short‑term agreement and outline the negotiation process for the long‑term JPA.
