### Overview
The briefing features Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and Admiral John Kirby outlining both domestic economic measures and urgent national security issues. Jean-Pierre opens by promoting a new CFPB proposal to curb excessive overdraft “junk fees,” framing it as part of Bidenomics alongside strong retail sales, improved supply chains, and polling that shows Americans increasingly optimistic about their finances. Kirby then announces the designation of Yemen’s Houthis as a Specially Designated Global Terrorist group in response to their attacks on international shipping, stressing that the move is carefully structured to protect humanitarian aid flows and can be reversed if attacks stop, while he defends both the choice of SDGT over FTO and the 2021 delisting decision on humanitarian grounds. He links this designation to a broader pressure campaign on Iran and its proxies, including extensive sanctions, and insists that Houthi attacks are not credibly tied to the war in Gaza, even as U.S. forces continue targeted strikes to degrade Houthi capabilities. A large portion of the session previews President Biden’s closed-door meeting with congressional leaders, which Kirby and Jean-Pierre say will focus on the urgent need to restore U.S. security assistance to Ukraine after funding lapsed in late December; they describe Ukraine’s dwindling stocks of key munitions, warn that U.S. leadership is crucial to sustaining allied support, and explain that remaining drawdown authority cannot be used without replenishment funds. Against this backdrop, they navigate questions about Speaker Johnson’s insistence on resolving border security first and embracing H.R. 2, repeatedly pointing to ongoing bipartisan Senate talks on border measures, defending the White House’s criticism of House GOP positions on Border Patrol funding, and declining to hash out specific concessions from the podium. Jean-Pierre also addresses several political and rights-related issues, including Vice President Harris’s upcoming Roe v. Wade anniversary tour on reproductive rights, a White House apology to Asa Hutchinson for a DNC statement that Biden disavows, the administration’s defense of its rhetoric on a migrant drowning incident in Texas, and strong condemnation of the prosecution of Hong Kong pro-democracy publisher Jimmy Lai, while fielding questions on Red Sea shipping disruptions and inflation, outreach to Palestinian students shot in Vermont, and Biden’s small-business-focused trip where his joking remark about smoothie prices drew attention.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Action on hidden overdraft fees
Karine Jean-Pierre announces that the Biden-Harris administration is proposing a Consumer Financial Protection Bureau rule to curb what it calls hidden junk fees, specifically excessive bank overdraft charges. She emphasizes that these fees disproportionately affect vulnerable Americans and that reducing them is part of a broader effort to lower costs. The proposal is projected to halve the average overdraft fee and save millions of families about $150 per year, totaling an estimated $3.5 billion annually. She contrasts the administration’s position with Republicans in Congress, whom she says defend banks’ ability to impose such fees.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that the Biden-Harris administration is proposing a rule to end excessive overdraft fees often exceeding $30.

- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that the administration estimates total consumer savings of about $3.5 billion annually from the overdraft rule.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre asserts that some Republicans in Congress continue to defend the rights of big banks to exploit their customers through such fees.

Jean-Pierre highlights positive economic data to argue that Biden’s policies, dubbed Bidenomics, are working. #### Terrorist designation of the Houthis
Admiral John Kirby announces that the United States is designating the Houthis as a Specially Designated Global Terrorist (SDGT) group due to their attacks on shipping in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden. He stresses that the move targets the Houthis’ leadership and operations, not the Yemeni population, and reiterates U.S. commitment to humanitarian aid. The designation will take effect in 30 days to give time to coordinate with aid organizations and set up licenses so assistance can continue. Kirby underscores that the designation is reversible if the Houthis cease attacks, but warns that the U.S. will take further action to protect international commerce if necessary.

- Mr. Kirby says the designation is aimed at the Houthis and not the Yemeni people, noting that over 15 million Yemenis still urgently need food, water, and medicine.
- Mr. Kirby explains that the 30-day delay in implementation is to allow coordination with humanitarian organizations and to issue licenses enabling continued aid flows.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that if the Houthis stop their attacks, the U.S. can reconsider the designation, but if they do not, the U.S. will not hesitate to take further actions to protect people and global commerce.
#### Choice of SDGT vs Foreign Terrorist Organization
Kirby explains why the administration opted for an SDGT designation rather than re-listing the Houthis as a Foreign Terrorist Organization (FTO). He argues the SDGT label provides more flexibility and greater comfort for aid groups operating in Yemen because it allows for clearer licensing and carveouts under sanctions. He rejects the idea that the designation is a bargaining chip with the Houthis, framing it instead as one element in a broader mix of diplomatic, informational, military, and economic tools. He also defends the 2021 decision to delist the Houthis as an FTO, citing dire humanitarian conditions at that time and asserting that those humanitarian concerns remain.
- Mr. Kirby says designating the Houthis as SDGT rather than FTO gives the U.S. more flexibility and provides aid organizations with greater confidence about operating under sanctions.
- Mr. Kirby explains that the administration has already issued multiple licenses—carveouts within the sanctions regime—to allow certain goods to continue flowing to Yemen.
- Mr. Kirby insists the SDGT designation is not a bargaining chip but an accountability measure within a broader DIME toolkit of diplomacy, information, military, and economic levers.
- Mr. Kirby defends the 2021 decision to remove the Houthis from the FTO list as a necessary move to address a dire humanitarian situation in Yemen.

He underscores that the U.S. has significant national security interests in the region and has demonstrated its willingness to act militarily to defend them.
