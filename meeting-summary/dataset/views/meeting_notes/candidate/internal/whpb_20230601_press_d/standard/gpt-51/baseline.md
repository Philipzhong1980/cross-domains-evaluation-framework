### Overview
A senior administration official briefs reporters on a background call about the U.S. response to renewed conflict in Sudan between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces, explaining that the fighting has derailed a promised civilian transition and caused severe humanitarian suffering, including mass displacement, sexual violence, and looting. The official announces coordinated U.S. actions: Treasury sanctions on four SAF- and RSF-linked companies that supply arms, vehicles, and critical financing, visa restrictions on individuals from both forces and the former Bashir regime implicated in obstructing democracy, and an updated business advisory warning U.S. entities of heightened risks. In exchanges with multiple journalists, the official clarifies that these measures derive from President Biden’s earlier executive authority, aim primarily at accountability and behavior change by cutting off access to weapons and funds, and are designed with humanitarian carveouts so they do not impede aid, which already totals $245 million. They report that Jeddah ceasefire talks, hosted by Saudi Arabia, continue informally despite SAF’s announced suspension and stress that the process is narrow “emergency diplomacy” for guns-silencing and humanitarian access, while broader work with Sudanese civilians and partners such as the African Union, IGAD, the Arab League, the UN, and Congress focuses on restoring a civilian-led transition. Throughout the call, the official underscores that both SAF and RSF bear responsibility for grave violations, that sanctions on their affiliated companies are intended to have real operational impact rather than symbolic value, that visa designations remain confidential, and that future sanctions remain possible as part of a cumulative strategy that complements diplomacy to end the conflict and support Sudan’s democratic aspirations.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Conflict situation and humanitarian impact
The senior administration official outlines the outbreak of fighting on April 15 between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), noting it derails over a year of negotiations toward civilian rule and reverses the gains of the 2019 revolution. They emphasize President Biden’s view that the violence betrays Sudanese demands for democracy. The official details severe humanitarian consequences, including airstrikes, sexual violence, looting, and large-scale displacement. This context sets the stage for new U.S. measures aimed at accountability and conflict resolution.
- The Senior Administration Official states that fighting between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces erupted on April 15, ending more than a year of negotiations to restore civilian rule.
- The Senior Administration Official recalls that President Biden on May 4 called the violence "a betrayal of the Sudanese people’s clear demand for civilian government and a transition to democracy."
- The Senior Administration Official reports regular airstrikes, intense fighting, rape of young women and girls, and pillaging of humanitarian goods and hospital supplies.
- The Senior Administration Official cites that about 1 million people are internally displaced and 375,000 have fled to neighboring countries.
#### New U.S. sanctions and visa restrictions
The official announces a package of U.S. actions designed to hold conflict parties accountable and cut off resources fueling the war. Treasury sanctions target four companies linked to the SAF and RSF, while State imposes visa restrictions on individuals undermining Sudan’s democratic transition, including from SAF, RSF, and the former Bashir regime. An updated business advisory warns U.S. businesses about heightened risks tied to the conflict. These measures reflect a strategy of denying weapons and funding while signaling support for a civilian transition.
- The Senior Administration Official says the United States is taking steps to hold the parties accountable and deny them resources, funds, and weapons that enable the conflict.
- The Senior Administration Official announces that Treasury has sanctioned four companies, two affiliated with the SAF and two with the RSF.
- The Senior Administration Official explains that Defense Industries System is Sudan’s largest defense enterprise supplying arms, ammunition, and vehicles to the SAF.
- The Senior Administration Official notes that Sudan Master Technology produces weapons and vehicles for the SAF.
- The Senior Administration Official states that Al Junaid Multi Activities, controlled by RSF Commander Hemedti and his brother Abdul Rahim, runs 11 subsidiaries including in the gold sector, a key revenue source for the Dagalo family and the RSF.
- The Senior Administration Official describes Tradive General Trading as a front company controlled by another Dagalo brother that has produced vehicles, some retrofitted with machine guns for RSF patrols.
- The Senior Administration Official says the State Department has imposed visa restrictions on specific individuals from the SAF, RSF, and former Bashir regime officials implicated in undermining Sudan’s democratic transition.
- The Senior Administration Official adds that State, Treasury, Commerce, Labor, and USAID have updated the May 2022 business advisory to highlight increased risks for U.S. businesses and individuals due to the conflict.
#### U.S. objectives and conditions around sanctions
In response to questions, the official clarifies that the sanctions are rooted in President Biden’s earlier executive authority and were under consideration well before the latest turbulence in Jeddah talks. The core objectives are accountability for violence and behavior change by restricting access to funds and weapons. The official avoids speculating on when sanctions might be lifted, stressing focus on the current crisis rather than hypothetical truces. The U.S. frames sanctions as tools to shift the parties’ calculus toward silencing the guns and resuming civilian transition.

- The Senior Administration Official stresses that accountability and changing the parties’ calculus are central, because without that change they will continue to fight and destroy Sudan.
- The Senior Administration Official tells an unidentified questioner that the first purpose of sanctions is to hold people accountable for violence and destruction in Sudan.
- The Senior Administration Official emphasizes that sanctions aim at behavior change by targeting how the parties conduct the war and how they access funds and weapons, with the goal of silencing the guns and enabling a democratic transition.
- The Senior Administration Official declines to speculate on hypothetical scenarios for lifting sanctions, saying the focus remains on the current travesty and tragedy in Sudan.
#### Status and purpose of Jeddah talks
The official updates reporters on the state of the Jeddah negotiations, noting that while SAF has formally announced a suspension, both parties remain in Saudi Arabia and continue conversations around confidence-building measures. They characterize the Jeddah track as emergency diplomacy narrowly aimed at ceasefires and humanitarian access, which achieved limited impact. The official underscores that the ultimate goal is a civilian-led transition and that work with Sudanese civilians and regional/international partners will continue in parallel. This situates sanctions as complementary to ongoing diplomatic efforts rather than a replacement.

They emphasize that choking off weapons, funds, and resources for both SAF and RSF is currently the most impactful lever. The official leaves open the possibility of future measures, including additional sanctions, if needed.

#### Sanctions enforcement and role of Bashir-era elements
The official explains that enforcement of sanctions will involve Treasury and U.S. diplomats engaging countries where the designated companies operate or move funds to ensure compliance. They acknowledge that many figures from the former Bashir regime remain embedded in the SAF, have long histories of abuses, and were previously sanctioned for atrocities including genocide in Darfur. The official voices concern that these individuals, recently freed amid the violence, exert a negative influence on conflict resolution, which justifies including them in visa restrictions. This underscores how the current measures build on longstanding concerns about regime-remnant spoilers.

They affirm that key organizations—including the African Union, IGAD, and the Arab League—share the goal of ending the conflict and are engaged in productive coordination with the U.S. The official stresses that the U.S. designs sanctions to avoid impeding humanitarian aid, including through general licenses and carveouts, and notes the provision of $245 million in assistance so far. This balances pressure on warring parties with efforts to protect civilians and aid flows.

This reflects a whole-of-government and multilateral approach that recognizes sanctions alone cannot resolve the crisis.
