### Overview
  A senior administration official briefs reporters on U.S. steps to respond to the Sudan conflict, describing the April 15 outbreak between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces and the resulting humanitarian crisis (about 1 million internally displaced and 375,000 refugees). The administration announces Treasury sanctions on four companies tied to the SAF and RSF, Department of State visa restrictions on targeted individuals (including elements of the former Bashir regime), and an interagency update to a business advisory, while noting more than $245 million in U.S. humanitarian assistance. Officials frame the measures as designed to choke access to weapons and revenue, change the parties’ calculus, and complement diplomatic efforts centered in Jeddah and coordinated with partners such as Saudi Arabia, the African Union, IGAD, and the Arab League; they emphasize that diplomacy, not a military solution, remains the primary path forward. Reporters press on timing, enforcement, whether principal leaders will be sanctioned, humanitarian carveouts, and how effectiveness will be assessed; the administration says the sanctions predated the Jeddah pullout, that visa specifics are confidential to State, that Treasury and diplomats will work with partners to enforce measures and protect aid flows, and that assessments will be ongoing and tied to observable behavior and ceasefire compliance.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Outbreak and humanitarian impact
  The senior administration official summarizes that fighting between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) began on April 15, derailing progress toward civilian rule and producing severe humanitarian consequences. Reports include airstrikes, mass pillaging, sexual violence, and large-scale displacement, underlining the urgency of U.S. and international action. The current state is ongoing violence with significant internal and cross-border displacement.
- Senior administration official said fighting erupted on April 15 between the SAF and RSF and derailed over a year of negotiations for civilian rule.
- Senior administration official said there are regular reports of airstrikes, pillaging of humanitarian goods and hospitals, and sexual violence against women and girls.
- Senior administration official said the conflict has produced about 1 million internally displaced people and 375,000 refugees who fled to neighboring countries.
- Senior administration official said the Sudanese people deserve better and emphasized the urgency of international action.
  #### Treasury sanctions on companies
  The administration announces Treasury sanctions on four companies tied to the SAF and RSF to deny parties resources that enable the conflict. Officials describe each target and explain the sanctions aim to choke access to weapons, ammunition, vehicles, and revenue streams that sustain military operations. The measures are in effect immediately and are intended to be part of a broader pressure campaign.
- Senior administration official said the Department of Treasury sanctioned four companies, two affiliated with the SAF and two affiliated with the RSF.
- Senior administration official said the Defense Industries System is Sudan’s largest defense enterprise and manufactures small arms, ammunition, and military vehicles for the SAF.
- Senior administration official said Sudan Master Technology produces weapons and vehicles for the SAF.
- Senior administration official said Al Junaid Multi Activities is controlled by RSF Commander Hemedti and his brother and operates 11 subsidiaries including in the gold industry, and that Tradive General Trading is a front company controlled by another Dagalo brother that produced vehicles retrofitted with machine guns for RSF patrols.
  #### Visa restrictions on individuals
  The Department of State imposes visa restrictions on specific individuals tied to the SAF, RSF, and remnants of the former Bashir regime who are judged to be complicit in undermining Sudan’s democratic transition. The administration frames these measures as part of accountability efforts while noting visa actions are confidential and handled by State. Officials signal that additional, potentially public, measures could follow if behavior does not change.
- Senior administration official said the Department of State has imposed visa restrictions on officials from the SAF, RSF, and leaders from the former Omar al‑Bashir regime.
- Senior administration official said visa restrictions target individuals responsible for or complicit in undermining Sudan’s democratic transition.
- Senior administration official said questions on the specifics of visa processes and names should be referred to the State Department due to confidentiality.
  #### Interagency business advisory update
  Multiple U.S. agencies update a business advisory originally issued in May 2022 to warn U.S. businesses and individuals about growing risks tied to the conflict. The advisory signals elevated compliance and operational risks in Sudan and surrounding markets and is intended to reduce inadvertent support to sanctioned actors. The update reflects a whole‑of‑government effort to inform private-sector behavior amid instability.
- Senior administration official said the Department of State, Treasury, Commerce, Labor, and USAID issued an updated business advisory to highlight growing risks to U.S. businesses and individuals.
- Senior administration official said the advisory builds on prior guidance from May 2022 to inform commercial and humanitarian actors of evolving threats and compliance considerations.
  #### Diplomatic approach and humanitarian funding
  The administration emphasizes that diplomacy is the only viable solution and that there is no military answer to the Sudan conflict, while pledging continued support for civilian transition. The U.S. reports more than $245 million in humanitarian assistance to Sudan and neighboring countries and stresses that sanctions and diplomacy are complementary. Officials warn they will take additional steps if the parties continue to obstruct peace and the resumption of a civilian transition.
- Senior administration official said the United States believes only a diplomatic solution will resolve the conflict and declared there is no military solution.
- Senior administration official said the U.S. has marshaled over $245 million in humanitarian assistance for people inside Sudan and those who have fled to neighboring countries.
- Senior administration official said the sanctions and diplomatic efforts flow from President Biden’s commitment to end the fighting and that the U.S. will not hesitate to take additional steps if the parties continue to destroy their country.
  #### Sanctions timing and relation to Jeddah talks
  Reporters ask whether the sanctions are a reaction to the collapse of talks in Jeddah and about timing; the official responds that sanction planning predates the talks’ formal suspension and rests on presidential authority dated May 4. The administration frames sanctions as a tool to change party behavior rather than a direct reaction to the Jeddah status, and it refrains from hypotheticals about waiving measures tied to temporary truces.
- Jennifer asked whether the sanctions would have been imposed if Jeddah talks had not collapsed and about the timing of the decision.
- Senior administration official said the President authorized sanctions on May 4 and that the measures were being considered long before staff announced a pullout from negotiations.
- Senior administration official said the sanctions aim to change the calculus of the parties and are not solely a reaction to the Jeddah process.
  #### Objectives, conditions for relief, and enforcement
  The administration states the sanctions aim to hold perpetrators accountable and drive behavior change by restricting access to weapons and funding. Officials decline to set specific criteria for lifting measures, saying assessments will be cumulative and tied to ceasefire compliance and demonstrable shifts in conduct. Treasury and diplomats will coordinate enforcement with international partners and can issue carveouts or licenses to protect humanitarian flows.
- Senior administration official said the primary goals are holding people accountable and producing behavior change to silence the guns.
- Senior administration official said the sanctions are designed to restrict how the parties access funds and weapons and thus change their calculus.
- Senior administration official said there is not a fixed date to review sanctions and that effectiveness will be measured by whether parties show greater willingness and compliance with ceasefires.
- Senior administration official said Treasury and diplomats will engage other countries to ensure compliance and that carveouts and general licenses are used to protect humanitarian assistance.
  #### Jeddah negotiations status and next steps
  The official provides an update on Jeddah, saying the SAF announced a suspension but remains in Saudi Arabia and parties continue discussions focused on confidence‑building measures. Emergency diplomacy concentrates on silencing guns and expanding humanitarian assistance, but the broader objective is restoring a civilian‑led transition with regional and international partners. The administration underscores working closely with the African Union, Arab League, U.N., and IGAD to align efforts.
- Senior administration official said the SAF formally announced suspension from talks but remains in Saudi Arabia and that parties continue to talk about confidence‑building measures.
- Senior administration official said emergency diplomacy focused narrowly on silencing the guns and obtaining humanitarian assistance, and that the ceasefires were repeatedly violated.
- Senior administration official said the United States is working with the African Union, Arab League, U.N., and IGAD to advance a civilian transition in Sudan.
  #### Targeting senior leaders and potential expansions
  Reporters press why principal leaders like Burhan and Hemedti were not explicitly named for public sanctions; the official reiterates that visa restrictions are confidential and emphasizes targeting the parties’ resource networks first. The administration leaves open the possibility of future sanctions targeting additional individuals if needed to change behavior.
- Robbie asked why the administration did not sanction General Burhan and Hemedti directly given their central roles.
- Senior administration official said visa sanctions are private and referred further questions to State while emphasizing that stopping access to weapons and resources is the most important immediate step.
- Senior administration official said future opportunities to pursue additional sanctions exist if the parties continue their conduct.
  #### International coordination and partner roles
  The official acknowledges multiple regional initiatives and says urgency drives many actors to propose solutions, which complicates coordination but does not undermine the shared goal. The U.S. reports productive engagement with Saudi Arabia, the African Union, and IGAD and expects continued close coordination to align proposals with Sudanese priorities. The official declines to describe Saudi measures but affirms joint diplomatic efforts.
- Unidentified reporters asked whether uncoordinated regional initiatives risk undermining the broader effort and whether the Saudis will apply similar pressures.
- Senior administration official said urgency generates multiple regional initiatives but that the United States already coordinates closely with the African Union, IGAD, and the Arab League.
- Senior administration official said the Saudis hosted talks for weeks, worked closely with the U.S. to forge a ceasefire, and remain committed to ending the conflict.
  #### Sanctions impact, humanitarian protections, and assessments
  Questions about the sanctions’ practical impact prompt the official to stress that the targeting is substantive and intended to choke access to matériel and revenue while minimizing harm to humanitarian operations. The administration describes typical use of carveouts and general licenses to permit aid flows and says it will monitor the sanctions’ cumulative effect alongside diplomatic progress. There is no fixed review timeline, and continued engagement with Congress and partners is expected.
- Unidentified reporter asked whether the sanctions are symbolic or meaningful and how the administration will assess their impact.
- Senior administration official said the targeting of the four companies is far from symbolic and is intended to choke the parties’ access to weapons and resources and to chill other countries from engaging those firms.
- Senior administration official said sanctions generally include carveouts and general licenses to avoid impeding humanitarian assistance and that Treasury manages compliance details.
- Unidentified reporter asked about the timeline for assessing whether sanctions work and about potential roles for Congress or the African Union.
- Senior administration official said there is no date certain for reviewing sanctions and that assessment will depend on observed behavior and ceasefire compliance, and that the administration continues robust conversations with Congress and international partners.
  #### Accountability and continued engagement with parties
  Throughout the Q&A the official emphasizes that both SAF and RSF commit serious violations and that U.S. actions target actors on both sides while centering diplomacy to silence the guns. The administration reiterates willingness to pursue further measures and to keep working directly with Sudanese actors and international partners to restore a civilian transition. Ongoing engagement in Jeddah and other fora remains the primary avenue to secure humanitarian access and a political settlement.
- Senior administration official said both parties are responsible for serious violations including pillaging, attacks on hospitals, and sexual violence.
- Senior administration official said sanctions target companies affiliated with both the SAF and RSF to tighten the screws on the groups and limit their operational capacity.
- Senior administration official said diplomacy remains the main line of effort and that officials continue to work with parties to achieve the narrow objective of silencing the guns and restoring a civilian transition.
