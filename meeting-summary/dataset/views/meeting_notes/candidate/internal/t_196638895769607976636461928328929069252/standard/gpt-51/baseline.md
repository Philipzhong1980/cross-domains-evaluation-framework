### Overview
The Performing Arts Committee meeting focuses on formal approvals, financial oversight, partner organization performance, and future programming strategy for the Mountain View Center for the Performing Arts. Vice Chair Sukanya Kumar leads the approval of prior minutes with a correction to her title, while staff confirm no public comment and clarify procedure when no public is present. The committee receives a brief status report from Kumar on the Mountain View Film Festival subcommittee, which has met with Chair Carol Donahue and staff member Theresa Yvonne, developed an initial timeline for a potential 2023 festival, and plans to present full recommendations at the next meeting. For the Peninsula Youth Theatre (PYT) annual review, Committee Member John McAlister questions staff about the financial spreadsheet; staff members Noelle Magner and Theresa Yvonne explain venue codes, rent, facility use fees, and labor charges, note that broader overhead costs are tracked outside the current report, and Community Services Director John Marchant commits to redesigning the financial reports and providing more standard budget information in future sessions. PYT Director of Business Operations Connor Day then presents an in‑depth overview of PYT’s 30th season, detailing the gradual rebuilding of outreach programs such as School Play in a Box and Stories on Stage, strong ticket sales and educational initiatives for Mainstage musicals like Descendants, Fiddler on the Roof, and Frozen, a robust return of Theatre in the Park summer camps at about 80% of pre‑pandemic enrollment, and financial stability supported by grants, PPP forgiveness, and an unused SBA loan reserve. Day identifies audience rebuilding and technical staff recruitment as key challenges, reports generally positive parent responses to PYT’s safety policies, and invites committee members to attend upcoming performances, offering complimentary tickets through him or the box office. In closing business, Yvonne informs the committee that she will accept a National Arts and Humanities Month proclamation at an upcoming City Council meeting and will share schedule details, McAlister asks staff to explore national and regional arts booking conferences and agent rosters to inform future programming strategy, and Donahue thanks the city for a recent volunteer appreciation event before announcing the date and focus of the November 16, 2022 meeting and formally adjourning the session.

- Staff member Yvonne introduces the PYT annual review, noting that committee members have the annual review memo and the season events audit for Peninsula Youth Theatre.
- Committee member McAlister reports that he only sees one attachment and asks detailed questions about abbreviations like 'MS' and 'SSS,' and about the distinction between rent, total charges, and facility use fees.
- Staff member Magner explains that 'MS' stands for Mainstage, 'SSS' for Second Stage, 'APS' for Park Stage, and 'RS' for Rehearsal Stage.
- Staff member Magner clarifies that 'rent' represents stage rental and lease fees charged to the company, while the 'facility use fee' is a separate charge paid by patrons on ticket purchases.
- Staff member Magner notes that other spreadsheet columns (such as various receipt codes) represent different categories of charges billed to PYT for using the building.
- Committee member McAlister asks for a walkthrough of the grand total, including what Peninsula Youth Theatre actually pays versus what is paid by patrons and what the city recovers.
- Staff member Yvonne states that the total revenue associated with PYT’s activities is $73,816 and explains that $9,910 of that figure represents facility use fees paid by patrons rather than by PYT.
- Staff member Yvonne calculates that Peninsula Youth Theatre directly paid $63,906.88, which equals the total charges plus facility use fee minus the patron-paid facility use portion.
- Committee member McAlister asks about the 'house manager reception' line, questioning whether it represents a percentage-based fee or some overhead charge.
- Staff member Magner explains that the 'house manager' line is the receipt code for hourly house manager labor costs charged to the company.

- Staff member Yvonne clarifies that the current spreadsheet focuses strictly on revenues and charges tied to PYT’s use of the facility and does not include general overhead costs like utilities or janitorial services.

- Staff member Magner indicates that labor charges in this context use a cost‑plus‑20 percent structure as defined in the master fee schedule, but that those underlying expense details are not shown on the current spreadsheet.

#### Peninsula Youth Theatre annual operations and outreach review
PYT Director of Business Operations Connor Day presents a comprehensive annual review of Peninsula Youth Theatre’s 30th season, focusing on program delivery, outreach, attendance recovery, and pandemic-era adaptations. Day describes the restart and growth of school-based “School Play in a Box” programs, the Stories on Stage (SOS) outreach series including its virtual pivot and recent sold‑out school performances, and the return of in‑person Mainstage musicals with strong ticket sales for titles like Descendants, Fiddler on the Roof, and Frozen. Day also reviews the summer Theatre in the Park camps, donor and grant support, pandemic-related financial cushions, and current audience and staffing challenges, while inviting committee members to upcoming shows and offering to provide complimentary tickets.
- Chair Donahue confirms that Connor Day has not yet presented and invites Day to begin PYT’s annual review presentation.
- Connor Day introduces himself as PYT’s Director of Business Operations, noting that he presented last year after only six weeks on the job and is now reporting after a full season that coincided with PYT’s 30th year in operation.
- Connor Day explains that COVID-19 continued to affect PYT’s programs, especially outreach, even as in‑person classes and rehearsals resumed at the studio.
- Connor Day reports that many schools were initially unwilling to host outside providers, constraining after‑school programming, but that PYT still operated or restarted School Play in a Box programs at about eight schools including Bubb, Mariano Castro (likely 'My Virgos'), and Yew Chung International School in Mountain View.
- Connor Day states that for the current fall they have expanded School Play in a Box to 14 schools, with more anticipated for winter and spring.
- Connor Day describes the Stories on Stage (SOS) program as a core outreach vehicle in which actors aged 12 and up perform literature-based pieces for young audiences, traditionally including discounted school‑day shows at 50–70% off regular ticket prices.
- Connor Day outlines how SOS was adapted during the pandemic: actors rehearsed via Zoom, recorded their parts separately at home, and staff edited them together into unified performances for online field trips with live Zoom talkbacks and later archived access for schools.
- Connor Day notes that in late 2021 PYT returned to in‑person rehearsals but kept the first three SOS titles virtual, then returned to in‑person second-stage performances in spring 2022 while cancelling heavily discounted school‑day performances due to lack of school interest in field trips.
- Connor Day thanks MVCPA staff, specifically citing Bill McFarland and the ticketing team, for waiving cancellation fees when school‑day SOS shows were cancelled in spring 2022.
- Connor Day reports that in the current season, schools have resumed field trips and that the fall SOS production My Father’s Dragon sold out all three second stage performances to school groups.
- Connor Day states that all seven SOS titles for the current year are now fully sold out for school performances, with schools attending from Mountain View, Cupertino, Sunnyvale, Los Altos, and other nearby cities.
- Connor Day describes live talkbacks after each SOS performance and shares that roughly one‑third of children in the recent audiences indicated it was their first live theatre experience.
- Connor Day recounts that during talkbacks, children asked about on‑stage safety (such as whether an actor could breathe inside a box), technical elements like lights and sound effects, and stage fright, illustrating strong engagement.
- Connor Day explains that Mainstage musicals returned in a larger way, featuring fully designed productions with actors ages 5–18, and emphasizes that PYT’s philosophy is to cast every child who auditions.
- Connor Day notes that most Mainstage shows are double‑cast so that more children can have lead or featured roles and so that each role has a backup who can cover absences and help peers learn choreography.
- Connor Day highlights the All Together Now musical revue as PYT’s official post‑pandemic return to live performance at the center, staged simultaneously with other theaters worldwide under a Music Theatre International initiative.
- Connor Day reports that Disney’s Descendants: The Musical, based on the Disney Channel film series about the children of famous heroes and villains, drew the largest MVCPA crowds since before the pandemic, although Frozen later surpassed its ticket sales.
- Connor Day explains that while student group showings for All Together Now and Descendants were cancelled due to low school interest, PYT preserved student performances for Fiddler on the Roof, with two shows drawing audiences of 98 and 78.
- Connor Day notes that PYT has already sold 791 student group seats for the upcoming SpongeBob Musical, signaling strong school demand.
- Connor Day describes how the Fiddler on the Roof production coincided with the start of the war in Ukraine, prompting PYT to dedicate the show to Ukrainian refugees and to highlight partner organizations in the slideshow and program for audience donations.
- Connor Day highlights the Frozen production’s extensive costumes and puppetry, crediting Executive Artistic Director Karen Simpson for building the Olaf and Sven puppets, and describes post‑show photo opportunities with characters that were popular with young audience members and double‑cast performers alike.
- Connor Day reports that the Theatre in the Park summer camps fully returned in 2022 for the first time since 2019, with campers creating sets, props, and costumes for final outdoor performances at Pioneer Park and other locations.
- Connor Day states that PYT served about 600 campers in 2022—roughly 80% of the 2019 level and significantly above the 350–400 campers they had conservatively projected—despite strict vaccination and masking requirements.
- Connor Day explains that nearly all camp sessions required vaccinations and masks, with only a small number of optional‑mask sessions offered that saw low enrollment but were intended to expand access while maintaining safety.
- Connor Day reviews PYT’s funding sources, citing support from organizations such as Kiwanis, the Packard Foundation, the California Arts Council, and a state performing arts grant program, and notes that the committee has PYT’s 2019 and 2020 Form 990 filings while the 2021 taxes are still being finalized.
- Connor Day reports that PYT received and had both rounds of Paycheck Protection Program (PPP) loans forgiven and that the organization also secured a roughly $300,000 SBA loan during the pandemic, which remains untouched as a reserve while PYT begins repaying it.
- Connor Day invites committee members to upcoming performances, including the SOS show The Ugly Duckling on November 4–5 and The SpongeBob Musical, and notes that rehearsals are currently underway at the PYT studio.
- Staff member Yvonne asks how committee members should obtain tickets if they want to attend performances, and Day replies that members can either contact him directly or contact the box office, emphasizing that producer comps are available for both school and general admission shows.
- Chair Donahue asks about future masking requirements, and Day explains that audiences are currently asked to mask on a recommended but not mandatory basis, while rehearsals require masks and performers go unmasked only on stage.
- Connor Day indicates that for next summer’s camps PYT expects to keep a vaccination requirement but likely move masking to a recommended status rather than a mandate.
- Chair Donahue asks about parental response to safety protocols, and Day reports that the only significant pushback has been around the vaccination requirement for camps, with parents sometimes asking why PYT’s policies differ from public school standards.
- Connor Day states that despite some questions, parents overall support the safety measures and that PYT has avoided any COVID outbreaks traced to its programs, even though individual participants have occasionally contracted COVID elsewhere.
- Chair Donahue asks about new funders, and Day identifies the state performing arts grant program as a new source of support and notes renewed funding from the California Arts Council after a gap.
- Chair Donahue congratulates PYT on sustaining and growing funder relationships and acknowledges the effort required to maintain long‑term grant support.
- Chair Donahue asks Day about PYT’s biggest challenges for the coming year, and Day identifies rebuilding audiences—especially for SOS—and reestablishing the organization’s network of technical theatre staff as primary concerns.
- Connor Day observes that SOS general audiences were only 40–70 per performance last year and attributes this partly to families’ lingering safety concerns about in‑person events.
- Connor Day explains that many technical staff and directors moved out of the Bay Area or further away during the pandemic, and that wage pressures from inflation make it challenging to recruit and retain qualified seasonal theatre technicians and designers at sustainable pay levels.
- Connor Day notes that while PYT relies heavily on volunteer labor, the organization is working to better document the value and volume of volunteer work in its budget and to ensure that volunteer reliance remains sustainable.
- Chair Donahue points out that documenting volunteer labor is valuable for grant applications, and Day agrees that funders increasingly expect or appreciate this level of operational detail.
- Chair Donahue asks how the Performing Arts Committee can help PYT, and Day suggests that members attend shows, encourage the young performers, and help connect the organization with potential technical theatre staff who might be interested in part‑time or seasonal work.
- Chair Donahue thanks Day for the presentation, acknowledges his increased familiarity with the role after a year, and wishes PYT “break a leg” for upcoming productions.
- Connor Day offers to share his slides with the committee by e‑mail and invites further questions from members after the meeting.

- Committee member McAlister asks whether APAP publishes lists of attending acts and whether such catalogs could be obtained without attending in person so the committee can begin reviewing options.
