### Overview
The Human Relations Commission meeting, called to order by Vice Chair IdaRose Sylvester, focuses on year‑end business, subcommittee updates, governance requirements, and farewells to departing members. Early in the meeting, the Commission applies Council Policy K‑02 to classify Commissioner Rakestraw’s absence as excused due to personal illness and unanimously approves the November 3, 2022 minutes. Vice Chair Sylvester then reports substantial conceptual progress by the Housing and Security Subcommittee on a May in‑person event that will center the lived experiences of broadly defined unstably housed residents through mixed roundtable discussions, storytelling or a panel, and youth artwork, while Commissioner Nirvana Nwokidu shares that the Mental Health Subcommittee has held its first brainstorming session to define scope, explore partners such as NAMI, and plan a future community‑benefit program. Principal Management Analyst Laurel James delivers several operational updates, including upcoming virtual Brown Act trainings led by the City Attorney’s Office, Form 700 filing reminders, clarification of the attendance policy process that prompted questions from commissioners, and the planned February 2023 return to fully in‑person HRC meetings at City Hall, along with notice that Council will consider HRC appointment recommendations on December 13. Commissioner Julie Solomon provides a brief status report on ongoing work to finalize and secure sign‑off on interview transcripts for the Commission’s project on the history of discrimination and inclusion in housing. The meeting closes with an extended, heartfelt segment recognizing departing Commissioners Kevin Ball and Julie Solomon and former Chair Annette Lynn, who each reflect on how HRC service has deepened their sense of community and belonging in Mountain View, while remaining commissioners, especially Xavier Webb and Nirvana Nwokidu, express gratitude for their mentorship and commit to carrying forward the community‑focused culture they helped establish, before the Commission adjourns to its January 5, 2023 meeting.

#### Housing and Security Subcommittee event concept
Vice Chair Sylvester provides an update from the Housing and Security Subcommittee on planning an in‑person community event for May. The subcommittee, working with community partner Malia Perez of Reach Potential and former Chair Annette Lynn as a volunteer, is designing a dialogue‑driven event centered on residents who are unstably housed. The current concept includes mixed roundtable discussions with unstably housed participants, a reveal and storytelling or panel segment, youth artwork, and broad definitions of housing insecurity. Planning is still in progress, with key logistics like exact date, facilitation details, and program format to be finalized.
- Vice Chair Sylvester reports that the Housing and Security Subcommittee has met once since the last HRC meeting and is planning an in‑person event for May, with the exact date still to be determined.
- Vice Chair Sylvester explains that the event will use round tables where each table includes at least one unstably housed person who will not be identified as such during the initial discussions.
- Vice Chair Sylvester states that the subcommittee defines ‘unstably housed’ broadly to include homeless individuals, vehicle residents, couch surfers, residents in subsidized housing, and families significantly overcrowded in small apartments.
- Vice Chair Sylvester describes a proposed structure where participants first engage in facilitated table discussions to build community and explore concepts of housing security, followed by a moment when unstably housed participants stand, are recognized, and then share their lived experiences through storytelling or a panel.
- Vice Chair Sylvester notes that the subcommittee is considering incorporating artwork created by unstably housed children and young adults as a gallery in the event space.
- Commissioner Solomon praises the concept, especially the idea of integrating members of the “priority population” into the initial discussions without identifying them, and expresses interest in attending.
- Commissioner Nwokidu commends the subcommittee for the thought and progress already evident in the early planning and says she is excited to hear people’s stories.
- Vice Chair Sylvester highlights the key role of community partner Malia Perez from Reach Potential, describing her as passionate and idea‑rich, and shares that the subcommittee feels very positive about the event’s direction.

#### Staff updates: attendance policy clarification
Laurel James addresses concerns raised about the new process for classifying commissioner absences as excused or unexcused. She explains that the general consensus vote on absence status is required to align with Council Policy K‑02 and that the policy itself defines which reasons qualify as excused. She acknowledges prior confusion because the HRC had not been consistently following this procedure and offers to resend the policy. Commissioner Nirvana Nwokidu questions why a vote is needed if the policy already defines the categories, and Laurel commits to share that feedback with the City Clerk’s Office. This exchange is important for clarifying expectations and ensuring commissioners are comfortable with the implementation of attendance rules.
- Laurel James explains that concerns were raised about the November 3 attendance process, where the Commission used a general consensus vote to classify absences, and clarifies that this was done to align with Council Policy K‑02.
- Laurel James states that Council Policy K‑02 sets the expectation that advisory body members attend all scheduled meetings and specifies the justifications that qualify an absence as excused or unexcused.
- Laurel James stresses that classification of absences is based on the explicit criteria in the policy, not on commissioners’ personal opinions, and the vote process helps record the determination in the minutes.
- Laurel James acknowledges that the policy has existed for some time, but she had not previously implemented it at HRC meetings and did not realize commissioners would be unfamiliar with it, which contributed to confusion.
- Commissioner Nwokidu comments that because acceptable excuses are clearly defined in the code, it feels uncomfortable to vote on absences and questions the purpose of voting when the outcome is already dictated by policy.
- Laurel James thanks Commissioner Nwokidu for the feedback, indicates she does not have a specific explanation for the voting requirement, and commits to relay this concern to the City Clerk’s Office.
- Laurel James offers to resend Council Policy K‑02 and an attendance policy summary to any commissioners who cannot locate her prior e‑mail.
