### Overview
Senior White House officials, led by National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan and Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre, brief reporters on the U.S. response to Hamas’s October 7 attacks on Israel, emphasizing President Biden’s intense engagement, unwavering support for Israel’s self-defense, and ongoing efforts to surge ammunition, Iron Dome interceptors, and other military capabilities while repositioning U.S. forces to deter regional escalation. Sullivan confirms at least 14 Americans have been killed and some 20 or more remain unaccounted for, with U.S. citizens among the hostages, and describes hour-by-hour coordination with Israel, including intelligence sharing and deployment of hostage-recovery experts, while declining to consider U.S. combat troop deployments. He details a broader posture that includes warning Iran-backed militias, asserting Iran’s broad complicity through long-term support to Hamas but noting no confirmed evidence of Tehran’s direct role in the attack, defending the recent Iran prisoner swap, and arguing the U.S. can simultaneously support Israel, Ukraine, Indo-Pacific allies, and border security, with new aid requests for Israel and renewed funding for Ukraine forthcoming to Congress. Responding to questions on civilian harm and Gaza’s humanitarian conditions, Sullivan stresses that the U.S. and Israel must act consistent with the laws of war and are consulting Israel and Egypt on safe passage for civilians, while privately discussing operational and legal issues without public red lines, and he underscores heightened vigilance at home, including protection of synagogues and monitoring potential terrorist or hate-motivated threats. Jean-Pierre, for her part, defends Biden’s capacity to manage both the Israel crisis and his special counsel interview, urges rapid Senate confirmation of Jack Lew as ambassador to Israel, refuses to engage in the House speakership battle, strongly condemns members of Congress who equate Hamas’s terrorism with Israel’s actions as “repugnant and disgraceful,” and reiterates that the administration will denounce any violence against Jewish, Muslim, or other communities while continuing to back Israel’s right to defend itself and seek additional support from Congress.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### U.S. support to Israel’s defense
Jake Sullivan outlines President Biden’s intensive engagement since the Hamas attacks and the concrete steps the U.S. takes to support Israel’s defense. He emphasizes that the U.S. is surging ammunition and Iron Dome interceptors, coordinating additional military capabilities, and repositioning U.S. forces regionally to deter other actors. Sullivan repeatedly stresses that the U.S. commitment to Israel’s security is long-standing and unequivocal. The current state is that support is flowing, additional requests from Israel are under review, and broader contingency planning is underway.
- Mr. Sullivan states that President Biden and Vice President Harris have held more than three dozen meetings, briefings, and calls on the Israel crisis since the attacks began.
- Mr. Sullivan says the United States has surged ammunition and Iron Dome interceptors to Israel, including from U.S. stocks already in country.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that President Biden pledged to Prime Minister Netanyahu that American planes will land in Israel in the coming days with additional military capabilities.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that the U.S. military has moved a carrier strike group into the Eastern Mediterranean to deter other states or non-state actors from widening the war.
- Mr. Sullivan clarifies that the carrier strike group is not aimed at Hamas but is meant as a clear message of deterrence to others.
- Mr. Sullivan says his team is engaged in contingency planning for “any and all escalation scenarios” in close consultation with allies and partners.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates that the United States has always and will always “have Israel’s back” in its hour of need.
#### Status of American casualties and hostages
Sullivan provides updated figures on American casualties and missing persons resulting from the Hamas attacks and describes ongoing efforts to clarify the status of U.S. hostages. He confirms at least 14 Americans are dead and around 20 or more are currently unaccounted for, but cautions that not all unaccounted individuals are necessarily hostages. The administration is working closely with Israel, sharing intelligence and deploying U.S. experts on hostage recovery. The situation remains fluid, with numbers and information expected to change as more becomes known.
- Mr. Sullivan reports that more than 1,000 civilians in Israel have been killed, including at least 14 Americans as of his briefing.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. believes 20 or more American citizens are currently unaccounted for, while stressing this does not mean they are all hostages.
- Mr. Sullivan notes the number of American dead has risen during the day from 12 to 13 to 14 as more information comes in.
- Mr. Sullivan commits that the U.S. will work “hour by hour” to determine the status of missing Americans and will update the public when numbers are confirmed.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that U.S. citizens are among the hostages held by Hamas.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. is sharing intelligence and deploying experts from across the government to advise and coordinate with Israel on hostage recovery efforts.
- Mr. Sullivan emphasizes that there is no higher priority for President Biden than the safety and wellbeing of Americans held hostage overseas.
#### Potential deployment of U.S. forces
Sullivan addresses questions about whether the U.S. will deploy troops on the ground in Israel, drawing a clear distinction between sending military experts and sending combat forces. He explains that current planning focuses on intelligence, coordination, and advisory support rather than combat deployments. The administration stresses that putting U.S. forces into Israel is not under active planning at this time. The current policy is to avoid direct troop involvement while maintaining high-level support and readiness to adjust if circumstances change.
- Mr. Sullivan tells reporters that deploying American forces on the ground in Israel is “not something that is under planning” at this point.
- Mr. Sullivan clarifies that when the administration talks about sending experts, it refers to intelligence, planning, and coordination personnel rather than combat forces.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates that the U.S. is not currently sending forces to Israel and declines to speculate beyond that.
#### Outreach to families of victims and missing
Sullivan explains the administration’s engagement with families of Americans killed or missing in Israel, acknowledging the emotional toll of the crisis. He says President Biden has not yet spoken directly with the families of those killed but underscores that the State Department is in contact with them. He insists that outreach to families of the missing is ongoing and a top priority, and any gaps in contact will be immediately fixed. The administration frames these actions as part of a broader effort to honor victims, repatriate remains, and support relatives.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that President Biden has not yet spoken directly with the families of the 14 Americans killed, but says the State Department is in touch with them.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. government is in regular contact with every family that has someone missing or unaccounted for, and any missed families will be contacted immediately.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that the administration is working both to clarify the status of missing Americans and to secure and return the bodies of those who have died to their loved ones.
- Mr. Sullivan describes the situation as deeply emotional for U.S. officials, emphasizing that ties with Israeli counterparts make this crisis personal for the administration.
#### Iron Dome effectiveness and ongoing needs
Sullivan details the status of Iron Dome support and addresses questions about its performance in the conflict. He explains that interceptors have already been transferred from U.S. stocks in Israel and that more are being sent to sustain the system. While Iron Dome has intercepted many rockets and saved lives, he acknowledges some rockets have gotten through with tragic consequences. The U.S. commits to ensuring an ongoing supply of interceptors and exploring additional air defense support given uncertain conflict duration.
- Mr. Sullivan states that some Iron Dome interceptors transferred to Israel have come from U.S. stocks already in country, with more to be flown in.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. is exploring ways to augment Israel’s broader air defense capabilities beyond Iron Dome.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Iron Dome has been effective at intercepting many rockets and saving countless lives, even though some rockets have still gotten through.
- Mr. Sullivan highlights that Israel will have an ongoing need for interceptors and that the U.S. is committed to supplying enough to keep the system operating on an indefinite basis.
#### Iran’s role and the $6 billion funds
Sullivan distinguishes between Iran’s longstanding support for Hamas and any direct role in the October 7 attacks. He asserts that Iran is broadly complicit due to years of funding, training, and equipping Hamas, but says U.S. intelligence currently has no confirmation that Tehran planned or directed this specific operation. On questions about refreezing the $6 billion in Iranian funds unlocked in the recent prisoner swap, he repeatedly emphasizes that not a dollar has been spent and declines to discuss further steps. The administration continues to review intelligence with Israel and remains open to updating its assessment.
- Mr. Sullivan says Iran is “complicit in this attack in a broad sense” because it has provided the lion’s share of funding, training, and capabilities to Hamas over many years.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that, as of the time of the briefing, the U.S. has no confirmation that Iran knew of, planned, or directed the October 7 attacks.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that U.S. and Israeli officials are reviewing intelligence holdings and seeking additional information about Iran’s possible direct involvement, promising to share updates if they emerge.
- Mr. Sullivan describes Iran’s role as “sustained, deep, and dark” support for Hamas while reiterating the lack of specific evidence tying Tehran to the operation itself.
- Mr. Sullivan stresses, in response to questions on the $6 billion prisoner-swap funds, that “not a dollar” of that money has been spent and declines to say more or confirm whether refreezing is under consideration.
- Mr. Sullivan defends the U.S. decision to conduct a prisoner swap with Iran, later saying the administration does not regret bringing unjustly detained Americans home.
#### Intelligence failure and comparisons to ISIS
Sullivan responds to questions about how Israel and the U.S. missed warnings of the Hamas attack, directing accountability questions for Israeli intelligence back to the Israeli government. He says U.S. intelligence similarly did not detect indications of an attack of this scale. He and President Biden publicly compare Hamas to ISIS to underscore the barbarity of the attacks and to justify Israel’s robust military response. The administration uses this framing to reinforce that Hamas is treated as a terrorist organization to be confronted relentlessly alongside Israel.
- Mr. Sullivan tells reporters that why Israel missed warning signs is a question for the Israeli government, given its emphasis on intelligence against Hamas and other threats.
- Mr. Sullivan states that U.S. intelligence did not see anything indicating an attack of this type was coming, “any more than the Israelis did.”
- Mr. Sullivan says the atrocities committed by Hamas resemble the “evil and barbarity” of ISIS in Iraq and Syria, including attacks on women, children, the elderly, and babies.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates that Hamas has long been designated a terrorist organization by the United States and that Washington has consistently supported Israel’s efforts to counter it.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. will work with Israel so it can go after the Hamas threat in Gaza “effectively, relentlessly,” until Israel feels its security and deterrence are restored.
#### Scope and limits of U.S. support to Israeli operations
Sullivan outlines the administration’s view of Israel’s military actions in Gaza as self-defense rather than retaliation and avoids drawing explicit red lines on Israeli operations. He asserts that the U.S. will support Israel as long as needed to ensure its safety and security, while also reaffirming commitments to the rule of law and laws of war. He refuses to define explicit end-states or timetables publicly, but indicates U.S. goals include restoring Israeli deterrence and securing hostage releases. The U.S. conducts ongoing private conversations with Israel on operational issues and legal norms instead of issuing public warnings.
- Mr. Sullivan rejects framing Israel’s operations as “retaliation,” characterizing them instead as self-defense against an ongoing, imminent threat from Hamas rockets and terrorists.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. will support Israel “for as long as they need” to ensure Israel is safe and secure, declining to set a timetable.
- Mr. Sullivan refuses to draw red lines or publicly set operational objectives for Israel, stating his role is to implement the President’s direction to support Israel’s defense and hostage efforts.
- Mr. Sullivan underscores that the U.S. and Israel are strongest when committed to the rule of law and that U.S. policy is to ensure military operations are conducted consistent with the laws of war.
- Mr. Sullivan rejects the characterization that President Biden warned Prime Minister Netanyahu about conduct in Gaza, saying their conversations are collaborative between allies, not admonitions.
#### Civilian protection, Gaza conditions, and safe passage
Questions arise about the humanitarian situation in Gaza, including the lack of water, electricity, and medical supplies, and where civilians can go in light of Israeli warnings to leave. Sullivan emphasizes that the U.S. distinguishes between Hamas terrorists and innocent civilians and reiterates that democracies do not deliberately target civilians. He confirms that Washington is discussing safe passage and humanitarian considerations with Israel and Egypt but keeps operational details confidential. The U.S. signals concern for civilian protection while maintaining firm security support for Israel.
- Mr. Sullivan, echoing President Biden, says the United States and Israel do not deliberately target civilians and that both are committed to conducting military operations consistent with the law of war.
- Mr. Sullivan affirms that the U.S. is discussing issues of civilian safety and possible safe passage from Gaza with both Israeli and Egyptian counterparts.
- Mr. Sullivan declines to disclose specifics on safe passage plans, saying operational agencies are working on it and details will remain in diplomatic channels for now.
- Mr. Sullivan says his understanding is that a concept of a total “siege” of Gaza is not what the Israeli government intends to pursue, though consultations on their actions continue.
- Mr. Sullivan describes ongoing U.S.–Israeli conversations about distinguishing between going “full-bore” against Hamas and protecting innocent civilians in Gaza.
#### Regional escalation risk and deterrence posture
Sullivan addresses the risk that other Iran-backed militias or regional actors could exploit the conflict, stressing U.S. efforts to deter such escalation. He notes concerns about groups in Lebanon, Iraq, Yemen (including Houthis), and elsewhere, and says they pose urgent threats. The U.S. moves a carrier strike group and maintains advanced aircraft in the region to signal readiness to respond to any attacks on U.S. forces or attempts to widen the war. The administration communicates clear warnings that such actions would trigger firm U.S. responses.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that Iranian-backed militias across the region, including those in Iraq and the Houthis, pose urgent threats and may try to exploit the situation.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. has issued clear warnings that any attempt by these militias to take advantage of the crisis will result in firm consequences from the United States.
- Mr. Sullivan cites deployment of a carrier strike group to the Eastern Mediterranean and sustained presence of F-16s, F-35s, and A-10s in theater as key deterrent measures.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates that these military moves are intended to deter broader regional escalation rather than target Hamas directly.
#### China, Indo-Pacific commitments, and global bandwidth
Reporters ask whether the focus on Israel detracts from U.S. attention and resources in the Indo-Pacific, particularly regarding China. Sullivan responds that the U.S. is capable of supporting Ukraine in Europe, allies in the Indo-Pacific, and Israel simultaneously. He notes that U.S. officials are not surprised by Beijing’s restrained response to the Israel attacks, given past patterns. The administration presents its strategy as one of global parallel engagement rather than trade-offs between theaters.
- Mr. Sullivan says the United States has the resources, tools, and capacity to support Ukraine, its Indo-Pacific allies, and Israel at the same time.
- Mr. Sullivan argues that managing multiple theaters simultaneously is part of the national security team’s job and is precisely what they are doing daily.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that the administration is not surprised by the PRC’s relatively soft or cautious response, citing China’s historical commentary on similar crises.
#### Congressional aid packages for Israel and Ukraine
Sullivan and later Karine Jean-Pierre discuss plans to seek congressional approval for additional aid to Israel and renewed support for Ukraine. Sullivan confirms that the administration will submit requests to fund Israel’s defense needs and to continue Ukraine assistance, but he leaves the structure of the package to OMB. He pushes back on arguments that the U.S. cannot support “two wars,” framing current aid as cost-effective compared with the potential cost of broader Russian aggression. The White House also highlights the need for border resources, noting Congress previously did not fully fund such requests.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that President Biden will request funding from Congress that includes support for Israel and a renewed request for Ukraine aid.
- Mr. Sullivan says details of whether Israel, Ukraine, border security, and Taiwan assistance are bundled or separated will be worked out and presented by other officials, such as the OMB director.
- Mr. Sullivan argues that helping Ukraine now is far more cost-effective than facing the larger costs of Russian conquest and potential further aggression in Europe, including possible deployment of U.S. troops later.
- Mr. Sullivan firmly rejects claims that the U.S. cannot simultaneously support Ukraine and Israel, insisting America has the budget and capacity to do both.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that the administration previously sought more border funding in a supplemental request, but Congress did not provide the full amount.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reiterates that both Israel and Ukraine assistance are critical and that the administration will ask Congress for additional resources when it returns, without detailing the legislative strategy.
#### Border security and terrorism concerns at home
Sullivan responds to questions linking the southern border, watchlisted individuals, and the risk of terrorism inspired by events in Israel. He emphasizes that the administration remains vigilant about terrorist threats to the homeland from any direction and mobilizes all resources when credible threat streams appear. He notes that the White House has sought more border security funding from Congress and that a humane, orderly border policy remains a priority. He also underscores efforts to protect synagogues and other domestic targets from potential hate-motivated violence.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that the administration tracks terrorist threats to the homeland from all sources and mobilizes every asset when a threat stream emerges.
- Mr. Sullivan says the administration shares information and analysis with Congress about plots emanating from the Middle East and other regions.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that the administration has pursued a humane, orderly border policy and requested more border funding in a previous supplemental, which Congress did not fully grant.
- Mr. Sullivan says President Biden has directed heightened vigilance to protect places of worship, including synagogues, and to guard against antisemitic violence potentially sparked by the Israel crisis.
- Mr. Sullivan affirms that the administration is at a heightened state of vigilance regarding any terrorist threats inside or against the United States.
#### Ambassador to Israel nomination and diplomatic presence
Sullivan addresses the status of Jack Lew’s nomination as U.S. ambassador to Israel and the importance of having a confirmed envoy during the crisis. He notes that the Senate returns next week and that the administration will work with both parties and the Senate Foreign Relations Committee to secure a rapid confirmation. He frames Lew’s deployment as part of the broader response to Israel’s needs. The administration treats this as an urgent but parallel priority alongside military aid and other support.
- Mr. Sullivan says the Senate returns to session next week and that the administration will press Democratic and Republican leaders to confirm Jack Lew as ambassador to Israel as soon as possible.
- Mr. Sullivan identifies Lew’s confirmation and rapid deployment to the region as critical for high-level diplomatic representation during the crisis.
- Mr. Sullivan indicates that when Congress returns, the administration’s top priorities include the full spectrum of Israel’s needs, encompassing both aid packages and senior diplomatic appointments.
#### Normalization with Saudi Arabia and broader diplomacy
Sullivan briefly addresses the status of Israel–Saudi normalization talks amid the conflict. He says normalization is not formally on hold but acknowledges that the immediate focus of U.S.–Israel engagement is on the ongoing war and Israel’s defense. The answer suggests that longer-term regional diplomatic initiatives remain on the agenda but are deprioritized in the face of the crisis. No new milestones or timelines are offered.
- Mr. Sullivan states that the normalization process is “not on hold” but concedes that the last four days of U.S.–Israel discussions have focused squarely on the Hamas attacks and Israel’s defense.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that the administration’s current priority is responding to the brutal assault and helping Israel defend itself, rather than advancing the normalization track.
- Mr. Sullivan implies that normalization remains an objective but without immediate updates or procedural steps to share.
#### Domestic political debate over Israel response
Karine Jean-Pierre confronts questions about members of Congress calling for a ceasefire or equivocating in their condemnation of Hamas. She firmly criticizes such statements and reaffirms the administration’s unequivocal support for Israel and its right to defend itself. She stresses that the administration sees no moral equivalence between Hamas terrorism and Israel’s actions. The White House uses this moment to clearly define its political and moral stance on the conflict.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the White House views statements by some members of Congress equating Hamas’s attack with Israeli actions as “wrong,” “repugnant,” and “disgraceful.”
- Ms. Jean-Pierre asserts that condemnation should be directed squarely at terrorists who have murdered, raped, and kidnapped hundreds of Israelis.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre insists that “there are not two sides here,” emphasizing the administration’s clear moral framing of Hamas’s actions as terrorism.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre underscores that President Biden has repeatedly and clearly articulated U.S. support for Israel and is taking action to provide additional support for its defense.
#### Protection of Jewish, Muslim, and other communities in U.S.
Both Sullivan and Jean-Pierre address concerns about domestic tensions and safety for Jewish, Muslim, and other communities following the attacks. Sullivan highlights the administration’s focus on protecting synagogues and preventing antisemitic violence, while also guarding against terrorist threats more broadly. Jean-Pierre stresses that any violence against communities will be condemned and that the administration is monitoring clashes and demonstrations. Officials aim to assure communities they can express views and exercise civil liberties while staying safe, though details of operational measures are not disclosed.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that President Biden has directed heightened protection for places of worship, including synagogues, in light of the Israel–Hamas conflict.
- Mr. Sullivan says the administration is focused on preventing virulent antisemitism and on countering any terrorist threats in the U.S. that might be inspired by events abroad.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states the administration will always denounce any acts of violence against any community and will work to ensure the Jewish community is protected.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the administration is monitoring domestic demonstrations and clashes related to the Israel conflict and will continue to support communities at risk.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre, asked about Muslim diaspora and immigrant safety, affirms that the administration seeks to keep communities safe and will denounce any violence, though she offers no specifics on the President’s conversations.
#### Special counsel interview with President Biden
Jean-Pierre fields questions about President Biden’s interview with Special Counsel Robert Hur regarding classified documents, which occurred amid the Israel crisis. She declines to discuss details of the interview, deferring to the White House Counsel and Department of Justice. She emphasizes that the interview was scheduled in advance and argues that the President can manage multiple responsibilities simultaneously, including intensive engagement on Israel. The administration discloses the interview’s occurrence but provides no further procedural or substantive information.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre refuses to go beyond a prior White House Counsel statement on President Biden’s interview with Special Counsel Hur and refers detailed questions to DOJ and the Counsel’s Office.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre confirms that the interview was scheduled before the Israel crisis and notes the White House proactively disclosed that it occurred.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre asserts that the President can and does handle multiple tasks at once, highlighting his calls with foreign leaders and repeated national security meetings during the same period.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre rejects the idea that the special counsel interview impeded Biden’s public response to events in Israel, pointing to his remarks, statements, and diplomatic outreach since Saturday.
#### Congressional dynamics and House speakership
Jean-Pierre comments briefly on the leadership vacuum in the House of Representatives and its potential impact on moving Israel and Ukraine aid. She reiterates that choosing a Speaker is a Republican and House process and that the White House will not intervene. However, she signals the administration’s interest in a swift resolution so legislative business, including security assistance, can proceed. No specific outreach to accelerate the speakership race is acknowledged.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the White House will not get involved in the House speakership race, calling it a process for the House Republican majority to resolve.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that the administration wants the speakership issue resolved so Congress can move forward with the business of the American people, including aid packages.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre declines to confirm any White House conversations with lawmakers aimed at speeding up the Speaker election.
#### Public demonstrations and risk of domestic clashes
In response to reports of clashes among protesters across the U.S. after the Hamas attacks, Jean-Pierre outlines the administration’s concern and monitoring efforts. She reiterates general opposition to any violence and highlights the focus on protecting Jewish communities in particular. While she offers no specific new policies, she suggests federal authorities remain vigilant and engaged with community safety. The administration’s message to demonstrators is implicit: exercise rights peacefully and without violence.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre acknowledges reports of clashes among demonstrators in the U.S. related to events in Israel and says the administration is monitoring the situation.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reiterates that the White House will denounce any acts of violence against any community and will support protective measures for the Jewish community.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre signals that authorities are keeping “an eye on all of this” but does not outline new or specific enforcement actions or guidance for demonstrators.
