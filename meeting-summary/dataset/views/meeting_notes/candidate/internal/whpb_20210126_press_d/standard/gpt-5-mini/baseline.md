### Overview
  Ambassador Susan Rice and White House Press Secretary Jen Psaki brief reporters on the Biden administration’s early efforts to embed racial equity across the federal government, previewing an executive action package that includes HUD guidance on housing bias, a DOJ directive to end renewals of private prison contracts, strengthened tribal consultation, and measures to combat anti-Asian xenophobia; Rice emphasizes the personal and economic rationale for equity and cites independent estimates of poverty reductions from the American Rescue Plan. Reporters press on implementation and scope—Rice clarifies the private-prison action applies to DOJ facilities only and describes new intelligence and NSC capacity to assess domestic violent extremism—while Psaki focuses on operational priorities for the pandemic response, describing daily vaccine monitoring through systems like Tiberius, plans to ramp up production under the Defense Production Act, targeted outreach to communities of color, a governors’ call and a presidential update later that day, and the administration’s 100 million-in-100-days vaccination goal (which the President hopes to exceed) while deferring timing specifics to medical experts. The briefing also covers legislative strategy for durable policy changes, negotiation posture with Congress on the American Rescue Plan, climate priorities, foreign-policy matters including a Biden-Putin call (New START, Ukraine, SolarWinds, Navalny) and Nord Stream 2 concerns, and a range of follow-ups the administration will provide or defer to relevant agencies, with officials promising further readouts and technical briefings from CDC and other experts.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Racial equity initiative
  Ambassador Susan Rice outlines the Biden administration’s whole-of-government commitment to advance racial justice and embed equity across federal policies and institutions. She frames the effort as personal and urgent, citing longstanding disparities and the need for federal accountability. The administration positions equity as central to recovery from the converging health and economic crises.
- Ambassador Rice said the President has committed the whole of government to advancing racial justice and equity.
- Ambassador Rice said she is leading the effort from the Domestic Policy Council with a team to drive the agenda and hold agencies accountable.
- Ambassador Rice said equity will be embedded in public engagement, policy design, and program delivery across agencies.
  #### Economic impact and ARP
  Rice presents economic arguments for equity, citing estimates of historic losses from discrimination and projected gains from closing racial gaps. She highlights elements of the American Rescue Plan (ARP) and independent economist estimates that ARP would sharply reduce child and overall poverty and lift millions out of poverty. These projections are used to justify ambitious federal investments as both moral and growth-enhancing.
- Ambassador Rice said economists estimate the U.S. lost roughly $16 trillion over 20 years due to discrimination and that closing racial gaps could add about $5 trillion in five years.
- Ambassador Rice said independent economists estimate the American Rescue Plan would cut child poverty in half and reduce overall poverty by roughly 30 percent.
- Ambassador Rice said the ARP would disproportionately help Black, Latino, Native, and Asian American communities and lift over 8 million people out of poverty.
  #### Executive actions package
  Rice previews a set of executive actions the President will sign to advance equity, including directing HUD to mitigate racial bias in housing, directing DOJ not to renew contracts with private prisons, reinvigorating tribal consultations, and combating xenophobia targeting AAPI communities. She situates these actions as early steps that continue the administration’s initial executive measures on COVID relief, immigration, and the census. The package is presented as part of a broader agenda that will also include legislative engagement.
- Ambassador Rice said the President will sign a memorandum directing HUD to mitigate racial bias in housing and affirmatively advance fair housing laws.
- Ambassador Rice said the President will sign an executive order directing DOJ not to renew contracts with private prisons in order to reduce mass incarceration.
- Ambassador Rice said the President will sign an executive order to reinvigorate federal consultation with tribal governments and a memorandum to combat xenophobia against Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders.
  #### Private prisons scope
  Reporters ask whether the DOJ private-prisons directive applies to ICE facilities housing immigrants. Rice clarifies the action applies to Department of Justice–run prisons and does not extend to facilities run by other agencies, such as ICE, in this instance. She references a 2016 DOJ Inspector General report critical of private prisons and frames the action as reestablishing prior Obama-Biden policy reversed by the previous administration.
- Cecilia asked whether the private-prison action covers facilities housing immigrants, and Ambassador Rice said the order applies to Department of Justice–run prisons only.
- Ambassador Rice said the memorandum is silent on ICE-run facilities and does not change other agencies’ contracts in this action.
- Ambassador Rice referenced a 2016 DOJ Inspector General report finding DOJ-funded private prisons less safe and less secure.
  #### Domestic extremism assessment
  In response to questions about January 6 and rising white nationalism, Rice describes a formal intelligence and policy response. She says the President has tasked the intelligence community to compile a comprehensive assessment and the National Security Council has set up capacity to focus on domestic violent extremism and interagency coordination. The administration treats the threat as serious and intends to use the assessment to inform policy responses.
- Ambassador Rice said the President ordered the intelligence community to compile a comprehensive assessment of domestic violent extremism.
- Ambassador Rice said the National Security Council has created capacity to focus on domestic violent extremism and coordinate policy across the interagency.
- Ambassador Rice said white supremacists and nationalists have demonstrated a willingness in some instances to resort to violence and the administration is taking it seriously.
  #### Criminal justice and voting
  Reporters press on policing, mass incarceration, and efforts to protect voting access after January 6 and subsequent voting restriction proposals. Rice says criminal justice reform and policing measures are forthcoming in the coming weeks and emphasizes the administration’s intent to work with Congress on voting rights and potential legislation to restore and revitalize the Voting Rights Act. She frames access to the ballot as a priority tied to equity and democracy.
- Mario Parker asked about policing and mass incarceration, and Ambassador Rice said the administration will address criminal justice issues and has more to announce in the coming weeks.
- Mario Parker asked about state efforts to roll back voting procedures, and Ambassador Rice said the President will address voting access and work with Congress on democratic reforms.
- Ambassador Rice said restoring and revitalizing the Voting Rights Act is among legislative remedies the administration supports.
  #### Durability of executive actions
  Reporters ask how executive actions can be made permanent. Rice underscores that codifying policy requires congressional legislation and a signed law, and that executive action is sometimes an appropriate near-term step while pursuing durable legislative change. She reiterates legislative priorities including the American Rescue Plan and confirming nominees.
- Jonathan asked how executive actions could be made permanent, and Ambassador Rice said lasting change generally requires Congress to pass legislation and the President to sign it.
- Ambassador Rice said executive actions can be appropriate in advance of or alongside congressional efforts but are not a substitute for durable legislation.
- Ambassador Rice said the administration’s legislative agenda is full and that the American Rescue Plan and nominations are top priorities.
  #### Vaccine supply and rollout
  Jen Psaki addresses vaccine supply coordination, emphasizing the administration’s rapid focus on improving federal-state cooperation and operational bottlenecks. She cites daily monitoring through systems like Tiberius, identifies multifaceted obstacles (supply, vaccinators, sites, appointment access), and previews a governors’ call and a presidential update later that day. The Defense Production Act is invoked and production ramp-up is underway, while specific stockpile figures are deferred to forthcoming updates.
- Jen Psaki said the administration is coordinating with governors and local officials to address vaccine supply and distribution issues and will provide an update later that afternoon.
- Jen Psaki said the administration monitors vaccine distribution daily through systems like Tiberius and is diagnosing holdups such as shortages of vaccinators and vaccination sites.
- Jen Psaki said the Defense Production Act has been invoked and ramp-up of supplies like specialized syringes began within 24 hours of the President’s announcement.
  #### Vaccine outreach to communities
  Reporters ask how the administration will convince communities of color to get vaccinated and how access will be improved. Rice and Psaki point to the COVID task force’s equity component, targeted outreach campaigns, partnerships with community health centers and pharmacies, and efforts to make appointments and sites accessible. They acknowledge vaccine hesitancy rooted in historical reasons and emphasize tailored communication and local outreach.
- Peter asked how the administration will convince communities of color to take the vaccine, and Ambassador Rice said the COVID task force has an equity effort focused on targeted outreach and addressing skepticism.
- Ambassador Rice said the administration will ensure awareness, appointment access, and clear communication about vaccine safety in communities with limited online access.
- Jen Psaki said the administration will work with pharmacies, health centers, and community partners and pursue mobile and local outreach to reach underserved communities.
  #### Vaccination targets and timeline
  Reporters press on the administration’s vaccination goals and timing for herd immunity amid differing expert assessments. Psaki reiterates the goal of 100 million shots in 100 days as the administration’s benchmark while noting the President hopes to exceed it, and repeatedly defers timing specifics to medical experts like Dr. Fauci. Officials emphasize planning for contingencies and that broad population coverage will take months.
- Jen Psaki said the administration set a goal of 100 million vaccine doses in 100 days and that the President hopes to exceed that benchmark.
- Reporters asked about differing timelines for herd immunity, and Jen Psaki said the administration is guided by medical experts and Dr. Fauci on when herd immunity may occur.
- Jen Psaki said the administration is planning for contingencies and acknowledges it will take months for a broad swath of the population to be vaccinated.
  #### Congressional negotiations
  Psaki describes ongoing, often private, conversations with members of Congress—Republicans and Democrats—about the COVID relief package and emphasizes small-group engagement as productive. She says the administration hears a mix of support and concerns about size and targeting, expects the package will evolve during negotiations, and urges urgency to avoid expiration of key protections. The President’s top legislative priority remains passing the American Rescue Plan.
- Jen Psaki said the President and senior officials like Brian Deese are in active conversations with members of Congress and that many discussions happen privately and in small groups.
- Jen Psaki said the administration hears a range of feedback from Republicans and Democrats about package size and targeting but expects negotiations to refine the bill.
- Jen Psaki said the administration’s priority is preventing a March cliff that would end eviction moratoriums and pandemic supports for millions of people.
  #### Relief package rationale
  Psaki explains that the President’s requested relief size is based on expert recommendations and the cost of inaction, not a headline number. She frames the package as addressing immediate pandemic needs and providing a bridge to recovery while preventing urgent fiscal cliffs for families and small businesses. The administration emphasizes the package supports vaccine distribution, unemployment insurance, housing protections, and small business relief.
- Jen Psaki said the American Rescue Plan’s size and components are based on recommendations from economists and health experts and on preventing the cost of inaction.
- Jen Psaki said the package is designed to address immediate needs—including vaccine distribution, unemployment insurance, eviction moratoriums, and PPP support for small businesses.
- Jen Psaki said preventing the March cliff is critical to avoid mass economic harm to families and communities.
  #### Climate policy and FEMA funds
  Reporters ask whether FEMA funds might be reallocated to climate resilience projects and whether the President will declare a climate emergency. Psaki says she had not seen the FEMA report referenced and will follow up, and she emphasizes the President’s strong prioritization of climate as one of his central crises, with actions to follow both by executive orders and legislative outreach. The administration will provide more detail on climate actions in coming days.
- Peter asked about reports that FEMA might reroute funds to climate resilience, and Jen Psaki said she would check with the team about that specific report.
- Mario Parker asked whether the President is considering declaring a climate emergency, and Jen Psaki said the President considers climate a central crisis and will take additional steps in the days ahead.
- Jen Psaki said the administration has already taken executive actions on climate and will pursue further measures with Congress.
  #### Masking and travel testing
  Reporters ask about changing public guidance on double masking and proposals for testing domestic air passengers. Psaki defers technical guidance to the CDC and says the administration is constantly evaluating measures to keep Americans safe, noting travel restrictions announced previously and that no additional federal testing mandates were being previewed from her podium. She encourages journalists to ask CDC experts in their upcoming briefings.
- An unidentified reporter asked about mandatory testing for domestic air passengers, and Jen Psaki said the administration’s medical experts continually evaluate such measures and she had no new federal testing restrictions to announce.
- An unidentified reporter asked about double masking guidance, and Jen Psaki referred the question to the CDC for any official updates.
- Jen Psaki said yesterday’s travel restrictions remain and the administration will brief health experts approximately three times a week starting tomorrow.
  #### Russia and foreign policy calls
  Psaki confirms President Biden’s call with President Putin and previews the topics discussed, including willingness to extend New START, support for Ukraine sovereignty, SolarWinds, bounties reports, election interference, Navalny’s poisoning, and treatment of protestors. She also recounts the President’s call with Chancellor Merkel where Nord Stream 2 was discussed in the context of U.S. concerns, and defers details on other foreign policy questions (Palestine, Venezuela) to the State Department and national security teams. A readout will be issued.
- Jen Psaki confirmed President Biden spoke to President Putin to discuss extending New START, Ukraine sovereignty, SolarWinds, reported bounties, election interference, and the poisoning of Alexei Navalny.
- Jen Psaki said the President believes Nord Stream 2 is a bad deal for Europe and the administration will review prior restrictions and consult with partners.
- Jen Psaki said the administration will defer to the State Department and national security team for details on Palestine funding and temporary protected status for Venezuelans.
  #### Vaccination data and outreach metrics
  Reporters press the administration on racial demographic data for vaccinations and how success will be measured. Psaki acknowledges data gaps and refers specific data questions to CDC, while stating the administration will pursue targeted outreach, partnerships with community pharmacies and health centers, mobile operations, and culturally appropriate communication strategies. She and Rice emphasize the need for multi-faceted approaches and measurement through agency-driven equity assessments.
- Yamiche asked whether racial data on vaccinations is incomplete, and Jen Psaki said she would refer the question to CDC which is addressing data collection and will brief soon.
- Jen Psaki said the administration will work with pharmacies, community health centers, and mobile outreach to improve access in underserved and rural communities.
- Jen Psaki said Ambassador Rice’s executive order will infuse expertise and personnel across agencies to measure and address equity across health, housing, lending, and education.
  #### Other policy and legal questions
  Reporters raise a range of additional policy items—Guantanamo trials, Medicaid work-requirement waivers, PPP eligibility for people with records, the Arkansas gubernatorial race, and an unsolicited offer for Florida to host the Olympics. Psaki generally defers legal or specialized matters to DOJ, the Departments involved, or relevant agencies, and reiterates the administration’s focus on pandemic response over domestic political contests. Several questions are deferred for follow-up.
- An unidentified reporter asked about Guantanamo military trials and Jen Psaki said she had no new update and would follow up.
- Jonathan asked about Medicaid work-requirement waivers in court, and Jen Psaki directed legal questions to the Department of Justice while saying the President opposes additional restrictions to access health care.
- An unidentified reporter asked about PPP eligibility changes for people with criminal records and Jen Psaki said she would circle back with Ambassador Rice and the economic team.
  #### Press access and briefings format
  Reporters request remote participation options (Skype) for safety during the pandemic and ask how many governors will participate on the call. Psaki expresses openness to taking more remote questions from journalists and notes the administration will explore ways to broaden participation and take questions from the public via platforms like YouTube. She names Jeff Zients as a leader on the governors’ call and confirms bipartisan participation is expected.
- An unidentified reporter asked about bringing back Skype participation for briefings, and Jen Psaki said the administration is open to remote questions and will evaluate safe ways to increase participation.
- An unidentified reporter asked who is leading the governors’ call, and Jen Psaki said Jeff Zients would be one of the primary leaders.
- Jen Psaki said the governors’ call is expected to include bipartisan participation.
  #### Closing readout and next steps
  The briefing concludes with Psaki previewing a forthcoming presidential update on vaccines and a promised readout of the Biden-Putin call, while Ambassador Rice thanks participants and offers to return for follow-up. The administration signals continued daily focus on COVID-19 responses, equitable vaccine distribution, and rolling out executive actions on racial equity alongside legislative work. Reporters are directed to upcoming CDC and agency briefings for technical details.
- Jen Psaki said the President will provide a vaccine supply update later that afternoon and that the administration will brief governors on distribution plans.
- Jen Psaki said a readout of the President’s call with President Putin will be released early in the afternoon.
- Ambassador Rice said she was happy to take questions and indicated willingness to return for follow-up discussions.
