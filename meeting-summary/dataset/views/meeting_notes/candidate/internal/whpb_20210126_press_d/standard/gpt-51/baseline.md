### Overview
The meeting functions as an extended White House press briefing in which Ambassador Susan Rice and Press Secretary Jen Psaki jointly outline President Biden’s early agenda on racial equity, COVID-19, economic relief, and foreign policy, while fielding detailed questions from reporters including Peter Doocy, Cecilia Vega, Mario Parker, Jonathan Lemire, Yamiche Alcindor, and others. Rice explains that she leads a whole-of-government racial equity initiative, rooted in stark disparities in wealth, health, employment, and COVID outcomes, and describes both the American Rescue Plan’s projected anti-poverty impact and a new package of executive actions on fair housing, ending DOJ private prison contracts, strengthening tribal consultation, and combating anti-Asian xenophobia, while noting that durable change will also require legislation and future criminal justice and voting rights reforms. Psaki then pivots to COVID-19, previewing Biden’s upcoming announcement on vaccine supply and a bipartisan governors’ call led by Jeff Zients, emphasizing the administration’s efforts to fix inherited gaps in stockpile visibility, vaccinators, and sites, clarify goals like 100 million shots in 100 days, and defer technical guidance (e.g., double masking, variant response, domestic travel testing) to CDC and other health experts. Throughout, Psaki describes ongoing bipartisan outreach on the $1.9 trillion relief package framed around the cost of inaction and looming March program expirations, addresses questions on impeachment and unity by stressing Biden’s focus on pandemic and economic recovery rather than directing the Senate, and touches on a range of additional issues—from domestic extremism and Portland unrest, Medicaid work requirements, and PPP access for justice-involved individuals to Nord Stream 2, Venezuela, Israel–Palestine, Guantanamo, and Biden’s firm message to Vladimir Putin on New START and malign Russian activities—underscoring an administration that is simultaneously advancing an equity-centered domestic agenda and recalibrating U.S. foreign policy while committing to more transparent, science-driven communication with both the press and the public.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Administration-wide racial equity mandate
Ambassador Susan Rice outlines President Biden’s directive for a whole-of-government effort to advance racial justice and equity, led from the Domestic Policy Council. She connects her own family history to the broader American story of opportunity, while stressing that systemic racism continues to block the American Dream for many families. Rice highlights stark racial wealth, homeownership, health, and employment gaps, noting that COVID-19 has amplified these inequities. The administration positions racial equity as both a moral imperative and an economic necessity, committing every agency and White House office to this work.
- Ambassador Rice states that President Biden has committed the entire federal government to advancing racial justice and equity for all Americans and tasked her Domestic Policy Council team with driving this agenda.
- Ambassador Rice emphasizes that systemic racism in the economy, laws, and institutions still puts the American Dream far out of reach for many American families.
- Ambassador Rice cites data that the average Black family has one tenth the wealth of the average white family and that the Black–white homeownership gap is now larger than in 1960.
- Ambassador Rice notes that Americans of color are being infected with and dying from COVID-19 at higher rates, with one in ten Black Americans and one in eleven Latino workers unemployed and an estimated 40 percent of Black-owned businesses closed during the crisis.
- Ambassador Rice reports that Black and Latino families with children are twice as likely to experience food insecurity as white families and that Black and Latino Americans are 2.8 times more likely to die of COVID-19.
- Ambassador Rice stresses that these inequities, combined with COVID-19 and economic crises, make urgent federal action essential for all Americans.
#### Equity in economic recovery and American Rescue Plan
Rice details how President Biden’s economic and COVID-19 recovery agenda embeds equity as a central design principle. She argues that investing in underserved communities is not symbolic but empirically linked to faster growth and job creation. The American Rescue Plan is presented as a historic anti-poverty measure expected to dramatically reduce child and overall poverty, especially among communities of color and Native American communities. The administration frames equitable recovery as critical for long-term competitiveness and broad-based prosperity.
- Ambassador Rice says President Biden will deliver a national address outlining plans for an equitable and inclusive recovery that expands opportunity for communities that have been left behind, particularly communities of color.
- Ambassador Rice explains that Biden’s economic plans make historic investments in underserved communities and place equity at the heart of the recovery.
- Ambassador Rice cites economists’ estimates that the U.S. economy has lost $16 trillion over 20 years due to discrimination against families of color and could gain $5 trillion and 6 million jobs over five years by closing racial income and opportunity gaps.
- Ambassador Rice notes that on day one Biden signed an executive order launching a whole-of-government initiative to embed racial equity in federal policies, requiring agencies to review policies and institutions for systemic racism and equity gaps.
- Ambassador Rice states that every agency must put equity at the core of public engagement, policy design, and program delivery to ensure resources reach Americans of color and marginalized communities including rural, disabled, LGBTQ+, and religious minorities.
- Ambassador Rice highlights that Biden’s earlier executive actions on rent relief, student debt reprieve, and emergency food assistance help all Americans but especially Black and brown families hardest hit by the crisis.
- Ambassador Rice explains that the American Rescue Plan is estimated to cut child poverty in half and reduce overall U.S. poverty by 30 percent if Congress enacts it.
- Ambassador Rice reports that without the American Rescue Plan, Black families would face a 20 percent poverty rate, but passage would reduce Black poverty by over one third, Latino poverty by 40 percent, and Asian American and Pacific Islander poverty by one fifth.
- Ambassador Rice notes that the Plan provides critical relief to Native American communities and will lift over 8 million Black, Latino, and Asian Americans out of poverty, particularly in food security, healthcare, financial security, education, and childcare.
#### New racial equity executive actions package
Rice previews a new set of executive actions designed to operationalize racial equity across housing, criminal justice, tribal relations, and anti-xenophobia efforts. Key measures include directing HUD to advance fair housing, ending DOJ contracts with private prisons, reinstating robust tribal consultation, and combating xenophobic violence against Asian American and Pacific Islander communities. These actions build on earlier orders and are described as early steps, with more to come legislatively and administratively.
- Ambassador Rice announces that President Biden will sign a memorandum directing the Department of Housing and Urban Development to mitigate racial bias in housing and affirmatively advance fair housing laws.
- Ambassador Rice states that Biden will sign an executive order directing the Department of Justice not to renew any contracts with private prisons, ending the federal government’s reliance on private prisons for DOJ detainees.
- Ambassador Rice cites a 2016 DOJ Inspector General report finding private prisons funded by DOJ to be less safe and less humane for correctional officers and prisoners.
- Ambassador Rice links ending DOJ private prison contracts to Biden’s broader commitment to reduce mass incarceration while making communities safer.
- Ambassador Rice says Biden will sign an executive order reinvigorating regular, robust, and meaningful consultation between all federal agencies and tribal governments.
- Ambassador Rice notes that Biden will sign a memorandum directing all federal agencies to take steps to combat xenophobia and violence against Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders, who have been targeted in the context of COVID-19.
- Ambassador Rice characterizes these orders as a continuation of initial steps to advance racial justice and equity through early executive actions and says Biden will work with Congress to further advance equity in the economy, criminal justice, healthcare, and schools.

Rice describes a dedicated equity effort within the COVID-19 task force focused on health disparities and targeted outreach. Psaki later notes the need for mobile and community-based strategies and tailored communication to overcome distrust and logistical barriers. Both emphasize that equitable vaccine deployment is integral to the broader equity agenda.
- Ambassador Rice says the COVID-19 task force includes a specific equity effort addressing health disparities and the historic, valid reasons many Americans of color are skeptical of the federal response.
- Ambassador Rice explains that the COVID team is conducting targeted outreach campaigns to communities where vaccine skepticism is highest to inform them of vaccine availability, help them get appointments, and emphasize safety.
- Ambassador Rice stresses that communities of color often have less access to information and online appointment systems, so the administration must take additional steps to ensure equitable access.
- Press Secretary Psaki later adds that the administration plans mobile outreach, partnerships with pharmacies, and work with local health centers to bring vaccines directly to underserved and minority communities.
- Psaki acknowledges that vaccine hesitancy in communities of color is a serious challenge and says the administration will use trusted messengers and multifaceted communication to build confidence.
#### Scope of private prison order and ICE facilities
Reporters probe whether the private prison executive order extends to immigration detention. Rice clarifies that the directive applies only to DOJ-run prisons and not to ICE or facilities under other agencies. She links the order to Obama-era efforts reversed by the Trump administration and leaves open that the order does not predetermine future treatment of immigration facilities, which remain “silent” in the text.
- Ambassador Rice confirms that the new order on private prisons applies only to Department of Justice-run prisons and does not cover facilities run by other agencies such as ICE.
- Ambassador Rice reiterates that the executive order is silent on ICE detention facilities and does not specify what may happen with them in the future.
- Ambassador Rice recalls that the Obama–Biden administration had previously moved to end DOJ private prison contracts based on safety and humane treatment concerns, and that the Trump administration reversed those steps.
- Ambassador Rice frames the current order as reestablishing the prior Obama-era approach for DOJ facilities.

- Ambassador Rice explains that President Biden has ordered the intelligence community to compile a comprehensive assessment of the nature, origins, and roots of domestic violent extremism to inform policymaking.
- Ambassador Rice reports that the National Security Council has established a dedicated capacity to focus on domestic violent extremism and coordinate interagency efforts to address it.
- Ambassador Rice emphasizes that the administration is taking the threat of white nationalism and domestic violent extremism very seriously, including as the impeachment trial approaches.

- Ambassador Rice says the administration will work with Congress on legislative efforts to advance democratic reforms, including restoring and revitalizing the Voting Rights Act.
#### Durability of executive actions versus legislation
Rice addresses questions about the vulnerability of equity-related executive actions to reversal by future presidents. She explains that durable change requires congressional legislation, while executive actions can provide immediate progress and lay groundwork for statutory reforms. She highlights that the administration will pursue a robust legislative agenda alongside executive directives, with the American Rescue Plan and nominee confirmations as near-term priorities.
- Ambassador Rice explains that making these measures permanent requires both houses of Congress to pass legislation and the President to sign it, which is the most durable approach.
- Ambassador Rice notes that executive actions can appropriately precede or complement legislation and should not be seen as substitutes where statutes are also needed.
- Ambassador Rice stresses that the administration has a very full legislative agenda, with the American Rescue Plan and confirmation of nominees as the first priorities.
- Ambassador Rice suggests that in some areas, the administration will seek both executive action and legislation to solidify reforms such as those related to private prisons.
#### Vaccine supply, distribution, and federal–state coordination

Psaki underscores that the administration inherited a fragmented system and is still assessing supply, using tools like the Tiberius platform, while simultaneously trying to expand vaccinators, vaccination sites, and advance notice to states to stabilize planning.
- Press Secretary Psaki notes that President Biden and his team have worked around the clock in their first six days to increase vaccine supply and federal cooperation with state and local leaders.
- Psaki previews that Biden will speak later that day to update the public on efforts to bolster vaccine supply and put more shots in arms as quickly as possible.
- Psaki reports that the White House is holding calls with governors to brief them on vaccine plans and improve cooperation and efficiency.
- Psaki says the administration is using systems such as the Tiberius platform to track vaccine distribution and usage, though she acknowledges that data is not perfect and the team is still connecting the dots.
- Psaki emphasizes that supply is only one issue and that the administration is also focused on increasing the number of vaccinators and expanding vaccination sites so states can actually administer doses.
- Psaki explains that governors have complained about learning of shipment changes with little notice, and the administration is working to provide more predictable advance information on deliveries.

#### Impeachment trial, Biden’s stance, and unity
Psaki explains President Biden’s position on the Senate impeachment trial of former President Trump. Biden recognizes the Senate’s constitutional responsibility to conduct the trial and expects them to manage it alongside urgent legislative work, especially COVID relief. He avoids prescribing an outcome or publicly lobbying senators, focusing instead on his own mandate to deliver on pandemic control and economic recovery, while asserting that unity emerges from addressing shared crises rather than from avoiding accountability.
- Psaki explains that Biden believes the Senate has a constitutional duty to proceed with Trump’s impeachment trial and will allow Senate leaders to determine the pace and manner of the process.
- Psaki reiterates Biden’s hope that Senate leadership will handle impeachment while simultaneously addressing urgent national business, especially pandemic response and economic relief.
- Psaki says Biden will not engage in vote-counting or publicly state what outcome he believes the Senate should reach, reflecting his view that he is no longer a senator but the President with a different role.
- Psaki argues that Biden’s path to unity lies in delivering on his promises to get the pandemic under control and put people back to work, not in dictating impeachment outcomes.
- Psaki later responds to a question about Trump voters by saying Biden aims to serve all Americans, including the 74 million who did not vote for him, by focusing on nonpartisan goals such as health, safety, and reopening schools.
#### Bipartisan outreach and COVID relief negotiations
Psaki describes an active but mostly private process of congressional outreach on the $1.9 trillion American Rescue Plan. Senior officials, including Brian Deese, are consulting with Republicans and Democrats across ideological wings. She acknowledges concerns about the package’s size and targeting but stresses that the proposal is driven by the cost of inaction and future “cliffs” in unemployment, eviction moratoria, and small business support. The White House expects the final bill to evolve through negotiation, viewing this as democracy functioning, while maintaining urgency to avoid March policy expirations.
- Psaki says President Biden and senior officials such as Brian Deese are in close touch with members of both parties and different caucuses about the COVID relief package.
- Psaki explains that many Republican concerns expressed privately about the package’s size and targeting mirror their public comments, but that members also convey recognition of the urgency and expectation that Biden will stay focused on relief.
- Psaki notes that Biden prefers many conversations to happen in one-on-one or small-group settings rather than highly public Oval Office meetings, believing this is a more productive way to move the bill forward.
- Psaki argues that the $1.9 trillion proposal is based on expert advice about what is needed now and in coming months, not on an arbitrary headline figure.
- Psaki highlights looming March deadlines, including the end of eviction and foreclosure moratoria, $300 supplemental unemployment benefits, and PPP application windows, as reasons to act quickly.
- Psaki acknowledges that the final package will likely change from Biden’s opening proposal and characterizes this as democracy working properly through feedback and negotiation.

- Psaki clarifies that Biden’s stated hope of 150 million shots in 100 days did not replace the official goal of 100 million shots; rather, he wants to exceed the original target if possible.
- Psaki underscores that 100 million shots in 100 days is unprecedented and was set on health experts’ advice with contingencies, such as equipment failures or staffing shortages, factored in.
- Psaki acknowledges that Biden is pressuring his team to increase vaccine availability in the spring and improve access throughout the year but reiterates that it will still take months for a broad swath of the population to be vaccinated.
- Psaki defers to Dr. Fauci and other medical experts on precise herd immunity timelines and says the President will follow their guidance.
- Psaki notes that different officials’ projections reflect both current supply realities and the administration’s effort to accelerate capacity, which may adjust timelines over time.
