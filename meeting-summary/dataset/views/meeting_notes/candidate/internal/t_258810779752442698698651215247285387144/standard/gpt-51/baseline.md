### Overview
The Downtown Committee meeting, chaired by Pamela Brewer with staff support from John and other city staff, moves from routine business into a series of substantive updates and planning discussions affecting downtown Mountain View. After approving the March 7, 2023 minutes, the committee refines its near‑term agenda, with John shifting annual work plan development earlier to align with Council timelines, adding a May downtown discussion with the city manager, and agreeing with Brewer to bring back a previously postponed undercrossing and grade separation item. Planning staff member Mike Kasperszak then reports downtown development updates, including approval of façade modifications for the Vida restaurant at 110 Castro Street and plans for a grant‑supported Downtown Precise Plan update, while John provides a detailed Castro Street pedestrian mall update covering newly approved design standards, a three‑option patio railing demonstration, standardization of railings and compatible planters, upcoming live entertainment and social seating, and ongoing cleanup efforts, with members urging attention to side streets and long‑term restroom and parking considerations. Public Works engineers Tony Eagles and Tina brief the committee on a summer sewer main replacement project affecting multiple Castro Street segments and Wild Cherry Lane, outlining televised pipe defects, a mix of pipe bursting and open trench methods, night‑time work windows, lane closures, and Caltrans‑governed intersection work, as members press for clear communication with businesses and residents about noise, access, and operational impacts. John then presents a four‑part ARPA‑funded small business support package—façade matching grants, outdoor furnishings grants, an expanded Rethink Disposable program, and a Chamber‑run social media initiative—with robust committee feedback on program design and especially on the time and skills required for effective social media, generally endorsing the proposal ahead of its May 9 Council hearing. Finally, in response to earlier requests, the committee agrees that future staff reports should explicitly address business impacts and outreach plans rather than creating a separate “business issues” agenda slot, and closing comments highlight ongoing downtown walking tours, a potential downtown ambassador program, and a shared desire to keep meetings within their scheduled timeframe.

#### Castro Street sewer main replacement project
Public Works staff (assistant engineer Tony Eagles and colleague Tina) brief the committee on planned sewer main replacement work affecting multiple segments of Castro Street and Wild Cherry Lane. They explain the condition‑based need for replacement, describe construction methods (open trenching, spot repairs, and pipe bursting), and outline anticipated night‑time work windows, traffic detours, and noise impacts. Committee members press for clarity on business impacts, residential noise, communication plans, and coordination with events; staff emphasizes that detailed phasing and outreach will be refined once a contractor is selected, and commits to returning with updates and providing clear points of contact for concerns. This item is critical because it directly affects downtown access, operations, and quality of life during the summer construction season.
- Assistant engineer Tony Eagles explains that the city runs an annual water and sewer main replacement program citywide and that Castro Street and Wild Cherry Lane segments are included in the current cycle.
- Tony Eagles states that sewer mains are televised approximately once every eight years and that recent televising of Castro Street revealed multiple defects including cracks, offsets, and open joints.
- Tony Eagles identifies four project segments: Castro Street from City Hall to California Street, Castro Street from south of Fairmont Avenue to 1st Street, the El Camino Real and Castro Street intersection, and Wild Cherry Lane between Villa and Evelyn.
- Tony Eagles notes that staff primarily anticipates night‑time construction between 11:00 p.m. and 6:00 a.m., Sunday through Friday, with limited daytime work windows (7:30–8:30 a.m. and 1:30–4:00 p.m.) subject to city approval.
- In response to a question from Mary Nikki, Tony confirms that road closures will depend on location and phase, and that detours will be necessary when work affects travel lanes such as on Mercy Street or Fairmont.
- Tony describes open‑trench replacement, where a long trench (up to ~100 feet) is excavated to remove and replace the entire pipe run, noting that saw‑cutting asphalt and operating excavators will produce significant noise.
- Tony outlines spot and segment repairs as shorter open trenches (6 to 20 feet) that can be phased flexibly, potentially at night or during approved daytime windows, depending on contractor logistics.
- Tony explains pipe bursting as inserting a new high‑density polyethylene (HDPE) pipe from one pit and pulling it through to another pit while a bursting head breaks the existing vitrified clay pipe (VCP), leaving fragments in surrounding soil and placing the new pipe in the same alignment.
- In response to questions from Pamela Brewer and others, Tony clarifies that most Castro Street sewer segments are currently six inches in diameter and will be upsized to eight inches in line with city standards, and that HDPE and VCP have comparable lifespans but HDPE is more flexible.
- Tony notes that pipe bursting requires a minimum run length to be viable and that short segments and complex intersections must be handled with open trenching instead.
- Tony acknowledges that the hydraulic unit used for pipe bursting is noisy but emphasizes that short runs may have similar noise levels to open trench work.
- For Castro Street from City Hall to California Street, Tony proposes pipe bursting with four access pits at manholes and suggests primarily night‑time work to minimize impacts on daytime businesses.
- In response to a question from Pamela Brewer, Tony clarifies that this first segment is upstream of the 200‑block nightclubs, so their wastewater service should not be directly interrupted by that portion of the work.
- For Castro Street from south of Fairmont Avenue to Church Street, Tony proposes spot repairs mainly under the northbound lane, which will require lane closures and detours; an unidentified member suggests using cones to maintain two‑way traffic where street width allows, and Tony defers final decisions until the contractor develops a detailed traffic control plan.
- For the El Camino Real and Castro Street intersection, Tony explains that the sewer segment is too short for pipe bursting, forcing open trench work across the intersection within Caltrans right‑of‑way, and that Caltrans’ encroachment permit requires daytime work with a detailed phased lane‑closure plan.
- An unidentified member asks whether this intersection work will occur before or after Caltrans resurfaces El Camino, and Tony answers that it will be done beforehand, potentially improving the final pavement quality.
- For Wild Cherry Lane, Tony proposes a mix of open trenching and pipe bursting, noting that segments near Villa are too short for bursting and that the lane functions as a critical delivery access for rear‑serviced restaurants.
- Tony recommends that all Wild Cherry Lane work occur at night to avoid conflicts with daytime delivery trucks and states that Wild Cherry cannot accommodate both full sewer replacement and regular delivery operations simultaneously.
- In response to a question from an unidentified member, Tony explains that the project follows existing sewer main alignments on Wild Cherry and cannot simply be reconfigured into fewer longer runs for pipe bursting.
- John raises concerns about how night work on Wild Cherry Lane could affect restaurant back‑of‑house operations, such as late‑night dishwashing, and asks staff to define clear messaging for businesses about when sewer flows must be minimized; Tony agrees that this guidance will be developed once the contractor’s phasing plan is known.
- Mary Nikki notes that residential units across from the affected alleys (e.g., along Bryant) may experience amplified nighttime noise through garage structures and urges that residents be informed proactively.
- Tony states that contractors may start work at 11:00 p.m. but not earlier, in line with the city’s noise regulations and construction hour restrictions.
- Tony indicates that construction is expected to begin in the summer and acknowledges that the city will prohibit work during major downtown events, though precise schedule duration cannot be confirmed until a contractor is on board and has completed preceding El Camino Real work.
- Vice Chair Jamil Shaikh asks about points of contact for complaints or urgent questions, and Tony says he will be the pre‑award contact, after which a city construction manager will be the primary liaison once a contractor is hired.
- An unidentified member suggests ensuring that the city’s “Ask Mountain View” platform lists the sewer project as a hot topic so that noise or access complaints can be quickly correlated to known work and addressed with standard information.
- Tony outlines that street parking will be temporarily restricted in work zones to store equipment and materials, and that when daytime work occurs, sidewalks on at least one side of the street will remain open to maintain pedestrian access.
- Tony commits that once a contractor provides a detailed phasing schedule, staff will provide advanced notification to affected businesses and residents and will return to the committee with a more precise impact and traffic control update.
- Senior staff member Tina adds that daytime work in downtown is currently limited by a traffic construction moratorium and that any deviations require traffic engineer approval, including analysis of detours in the context of the existing Castro Street closure.
- Tina emphasizes that staff must balance noise concerns, business impacts, and construction efficiency, particularly in mixed‑use blocks where both residential and commercial frontages will be affected.
- Chair Pamela Brewer asks how long the work might last overall, and Tony reiterates that the timeline depends on contractor staffing, El Camino sequencing, and Caltrans coordination.
- Tony confirms that lateral connections will also be replaced as part of the sewer work, adding complexity near business frontages.
- Tina underscores that outreach will need to account for the diversity of downtown businesses and their different operating hours, and that the city will require contractors to support targeted notifications.
#### ARPA-funded small business support programs
John presents a package of four proposed programs using $500,000 in American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) funds already earmarked by City Council for small business support. The package includes a tiered façade matching grant, an outdoor furnishings grant, an expansion of the “Rethink Disposable” reusable and compostable foodware program, and a social media capacity‑building initiative in partnership with the Chamber of Commerce. Committee members provide feedback, especially on program design, communication, practical use cases, and the realities of social media work, and generally express support for the proposal ahead of its May 9 Council hearing. These programs aim to address aesthetic improvements, outdoor operations, waste reduction, and marketing capabilities across downtown and citywide businesses.
- John recounts that in June of the prior year, City Council set aside $250,000 in ARPA funds for small business support and subsequently increased the allocation to $500,000 during budget deliberations.
- John explains that Council’s discussion focused on three themes: direct cash assistance to small businesses, help with ADA‑related improvements in the context of widespread lawsuits, and ensuring benefits extend beyond downtown to other business districts.
- John states that staff has designed four programs for Council consideration: a façade matching grant program, an outdoor furnishings grant program, an expanded Rethink Disposable partnership, and a social media toolkit initiative with the Chamber of Commerce.
- John notes that two of the programs are direct grants, one is a contract amendment with a nonprofit (Clean Water Fund), and one is a service contract with the Chamber.
- John explains that the façade matching grant program is open to both property owners and businesses, and offers three tiers of one‑to‑one matching grants (Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3) for work such as awnings, signage, limited façade improvements, and potentially historic accents.
- John emphasizes that applicants must first meet with city staff to discuss their proposed improvements so staff can clarify likely costs, required permits, and timelines, thereby avoiding surprises after grant award.
- John clarifies that applicants must select only one tier per project and cannot stack tiers (e.g., a business may not combine Tier 1 and Tier 3 grants).
- John notes that the façade program is designed for “light” façade work rather than full‑building reconstructions, and that historically city code has required full‑facade upgrades once a façade project begins, so staff will need to administratively distinguish these smaller improvements.
- Chair Pamela Brewer asks how grant funds will be disbursed and verified, and John answers that the grants are matching reimbursements: businesses front the cost, complete permitted work, and then receive a city reimbursement after demonstrating completion.
- John introduces the outdoor furnishings program as a citywide grant of up to $5,000 per business for outdoor‑rated furniture, umbrellas, heaters, and similar items, funded at $200,000 total (up to about 40 grants).
- John notes that many businesses improvised outdoor setups during COVID and that this program gives them an opportunity to upgrade to safer, more durable, and aesthetically consistent fixtures.
- John states that if applications exceed available funding, the city will use a lottery to allocate outdoor furnishings grants rather than first‑come, first‑served, to improve fairness.
- Chair Pamela Brewer asks whether outdoor furniture designs must be pre‑approved, and John responds that this program will be more flexible, focusing primarily on suitability for outdoor use rather than specific design approval, though staff will offer guidance.
- John describes Rethink Disposable as an existing Clean Water Fund program that audits food service businesses, recommends reusable dine‑in foodware (e.g., ramekins instead of disposable sauce cups), and encourages more compostable options for take‑out, with the program purchasing some equipment for participating businesses.
- John says that the ARPA proposal includes a contract amendment with Clean Water Fund to expand Rethink Disposable, aiming for more business participation now that dine‑in has recovered and “everything disposable” COVID practices can be revisited.
- Chair Pamela Brewer asks how businesses are notified about Rethink Disposable, and John explains that outreach is primarily the vendor’s responsibility, supplemented by city support; he acknowledges that early uptake was limited due to pandemic conditions.
- Committee member Mary Nikki notes her experience with Rethink Disposable, explaining that the program bought compostable take‑out containers for her business, but she declined to switch her dine‑in glassware due to operational constraints, illustrating limits of adoption.
- John presents the final program as a $25,000 partnership with the Chamber of Commerce to develop social media toolkits and training that help small businesses use platforms such as Instagram, Facebook, Nextdoor, and Yelp more effectively.
- John observes that businesses with stronger social media engagement appear to show better sales tax performance and visitor traffic, and he hopes this program will widen that capability across smaller operators.
- Committee member Mary Nikki asks if the Chamber will host workshops, and Chamber representative Kira Pasca replies that the Chamber is exploring two approaches with consultants: workshops plus toolkits, or toolkits combined with more intensive one‑on‑one “hand‑holding” for selected businesses.
- Kira Pasca says the Chamber’s goal is to begin training in September so that businesses can apply new skills for Q4 holiday campaigns, when revenue is especially critical.
- Kira Pasca adds that a key design element will be requiring participating businesses to commit a defined amount of time to social media and to understand that consistent posting is necessary for meaningful results.
- Committee member Mary Yen shares her year‑long experience running social media for a local Catholic school, emphasizing that effective posting requires a dedicated person, weekly time investment, and experimentation with content types such as reels versus photo galleries.
- Mary Yen suggests that the city and Chamber could facilitate peer‑to‑peer sharing among businesses that have had social media success so they can exchange practical tips at low cost.
- Mary Yen warns that professional social media consultants and influencers can be expensive and that many small businesses may not be able to afford ongoing contracted services; she urges the program to emphasize affordable, owner‑driven approaches.
- Mary Yen highlights the importance of clearly defining business goals (e.g., more foot traffic, attracting different age segments, or promoting value‑driven campaigns) before designing social media content, noting that younger audiences often respond better to mission‑driven messages than pure discounts.
- Kira Pasca agrees and notes that the program will need to address perceived obstacles, including time, cost, and fear of lacking creativity, and that training should include low‑effort tools (e.g., simple smartphone apps) for quickly generating attractive content.
- Chair Pamela Brewer shares that the Historical Association uses national “theme days” (e.g., National Mom and Pop Business Day) to pre‑plan content and maintain posting discipline, suggesting that businesses adopt similar calendars to avoid “what do I say?” paralysis.
- Committee member Mary Nikki recommends tapping local high school and college students with strong social media skills for low‑cost part‑time support, observing that they often see $20 as meaningful income and can work remotely.
- Committee member April, a member of the public, briefly comments that building follower networks by following peer organizations and mutual engagement can help posts reach a larger audience organically.
- Vice Chair Jamil Shaikh suggests that the Chamber explore AI tools (e.g., GPT‑based systems) to generate content more efficiently, and Kira Pasca notes the idea.
- John clarifies to Chair Pamela Brewer that ARPA funds are one‑time and mostly allocated; this $500,000 represents a previously approved bucket dedicated to small businesses, and the current proposal simply specifies how to spend it before federal obligation and expenditure deadlines in 2024 and 2025.
- Committee members express general support for the ARPA program mix, viewing it as a balanced package that addresses physical improvements, operational needs, sustainability, and marketing capacity.
#### Framing business impacts within agenda items
The committee revisits a prior request to ensure that business impacts are systematically considered when staff presents downtown projects. John seeks guidance on whether a standing “business issues” agenda item is needed and how to describe it for the public, while several members clarify that their intent is to embed business‑impact analysis and communication plans into each relevant staff report rather than create a generic complaint segment. The discussion emphasizes the importance of clearly articulating how projects affect operations, what outreach will occur, and how the Downtown Business Association and Chamber can assist. This shapes how future agendas and memos will be structured to better serve downtown stakeholders.
- John reminds the committee that at a prior meeting members asked for a recurring way to raise business issues, and he explains that as staff he struggles with adding a standing agenda item without a clear description of what would be discussed for public noticing purposes.
- John notes that existing sections—future agenda topics and the “committee, staff comments, questions, committee reports” segment—already allow members to surface issues or refer specific business complaints to staff for follow‑up.
- Downtown Business Association representative Kira Pasca clarifies that her original request was not for a new standing item but to ensure that, when projects are discussed (e.g., sewers), the agenda and staff presentation explicitly address how those projects will impact businesses and what can be done to mitigate those impacts.
- Kira Pasca suggests that for each relevant project the committee should discuss signage, wayfinding, parking alternatives, delivery access, and other measures that could buffer negative effects on businesses during construction.
- Committee member Mary Nikki adds that the city should maintain ongoing communication with businesses during longer projects, like the sewer work, and not just before construction starts, so that evolving impacts can be monitored and addressed.
- An unidentified member notes that City Council staff reports often include standard sections such as recommendations, analysis, alternatives, and impacts, and suggests that staff could add a “business impact” section for downtown committee memos.
- John agrees that adding a dedicated business‑impact discussion to written memos would be a constructive approach and acknowledges that staff has already started providing more detailed written materials to this committee than in the past.
- Kira Pasca requests that memos also include a brief description of planned communication and outreach to businesses and residents, so the committee can understand whether and how information is being delivered and suggest improvements if needed.
- Mary Nikki proposes that the city leverage the Downtown Business Association’s network of volunteer block representatives to conduct in‑person outreach on key topics, noting that face‑to‑face visits often prompt questions and engagement that flyers and emails alone do not.
- An unidentified member stresses that businesses also need to take ownership of reading city communications and subscribing to relevant channels, because there are already many city resources that go unused due to low engagement.
- Kira Pasca observes that city emails can be dense and that businesses may gloss over key information; she recommends concise, bullet‑pointed messaging to improve readability and comprehension.
- John summarizes that he will treat the feedback as a directive to incorporate a business‑impact lens and outreach overview into future project memos and to use the existing agenda framework rather than creating a new standing item.
