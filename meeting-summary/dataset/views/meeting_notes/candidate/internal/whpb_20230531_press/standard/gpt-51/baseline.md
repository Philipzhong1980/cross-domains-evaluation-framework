### Overview
This briefing brings together National Security Council spokesman Admiral John Kirby and Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre to cover an unusually broad agenda spanning foreign policy crises and a high-stakes domestic budget deal. Kirby opens by detailing a new $300 million U.S. military aid package for Ukraine in response to intensified Russian airstrikes on Kyiv, reiterating that while Washington does not direct Ukrainian targeting it opposes the use of U.S.-supplied weapons for attacks inside Russia—a stance he links to avoiding escalation that could draw in NATO and trigger a wider war. He addresses multiple flashpoints, condemning an unsafe Chinese jet intercept over the South China Sea and pressing for restoration of U.S.–China military-to-military communications, explaining that the Iran nuclear deal (JCPOA) is currently deprioritized while Washington still seeks to prevent an Iranian bomb, criticizing Nicaragua’s deteriorating human rights record, and calling Sudan’s army decision to leave Jeddah talks “highly unfortunate” because it blocks humanitarian aid. Kirby also underscores U.S. backing for Zelenskyy’s 10-point “just peace” plan and for Sweden’s NATO accession (separate from F-16 sales to Turkey), welcomes potential mediation roles for actors from the Vatican to Brazil so long as Kyiv approves, and describes persistent U.S. efforts to free wrongfully detained Americans like Evan Gershkovich and Paul Whelan, even as he dismisses Tara Reade’s claims that her life was endangered by the U.S. government and denies corruption allegations against President Biden. In parallel, he acknowledges AI’s existential risks while calling for public–private cooperation, notes ongoing concern about North Korean missile and satellite tests and Chinese cyber and espionage activity, and repeatedly stresses that Russia shows no genuine interest in diplomacy. When Jean-Pierre resumes the podium, she pivots to domestic issues, framing the bipartisan debt ceiling and budget agreement as a “commonsense” deal that averts catastrophic default, adds an estimated $1.2 trillion in deficit reduction to the $1.7 trillion already achieved under Biden, and protects core programs like Medicaid, SNAP, veterans’ benefits, and recent climate and infrastructure investments—even while conceding that compromise on issues such as work requirements was necessary. She details extensive outreach to lawmakers across the Democratic caucus, expresses confidence the House and Senate will pass the bill before the June 5 X-date, and says Biden will issue a statement once the House votes. Throughout, she defends the administration’s economic record and multi-issue focus, addresses concerns about environmental justice and the Mountain Valley Pipeline by stressing preserved bedrock protections, and answers press-freedom complaints by pointing to expanded access post-COVID and promising NSC transcripts, resulting in a briefing that sketches both the administration’s global risk management and its domestic fiscal strategy in advance of a critical debt-limit deadline.

Kirby stresses that Ukraine has been using U.S.-supplied Patriot systems effectively and that G7 partners remain aligned with continued support.
