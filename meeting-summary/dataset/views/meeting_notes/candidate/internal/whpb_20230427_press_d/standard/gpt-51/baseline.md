### Overview
Senior administration officials brief reporters on the Biden-Harris administration’s first use of Executive Order 14078, grounded in the Robert Levinson Hostage Recovery and Hostage-Taking Accountability Act, to sanction Russia’s Federal Security Service (FSB), Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Intelligence Organization (IRGC-IO), and four senior IRGC-IO officials for patterns of wrongful detention and hostage-taking of U.S. nationals, including at Evin Prison. The lead official situates these actions within a four-part strategy of resolving existing cases (many inherited from the prior administration), warning U.S. travelers through enhanced advisories, strengthening global norms against wrongful detention, and punishing and deterring perpetrators, stressing that sanctions—freezing assets, restricting access to the financial system, and stigmatizing specific behaviors—are one tool in a broader, evolving toolkit and that more tranches are in development. In exchanges with reporters, the officials argue that layering these new designations on entities already sanctioned under other authorities sends a distinct message that wrongful detention itself is sanctionable, cite recent U.N. advocacy with Elizabeth Whelan to push back against a worrying global trend of using people as political pawns, and detail the individual roles of four IRGC-IO leaders being designated. They confirm that relevant families were briefed in advance; describe intensive, ongoing engagement by the White House, the State Department, the Special Presidential Envoy for Hostage Affairs, and the Secretary of State with families—including direct presidential contact with Evan Gershkovich’s parents and sustained work on Mark Fogel’s case—while emphasizing that presidential meetings and formal “wrongful detention” designations do not determine the level of effort. Finally, they clarify that the sanctions package predates Gershkovich’s detention but that his case illustrates the pattern the U.S. seeks to confront, and they note that sanctions may, consistent with law, be adjusted or lifted if they successfully drive behavioral change and contribute to bringing Americans home.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Administration’s four-part approach
The first senior administration official outlines the Biden-Harris administration’s overarching strategy for addressing hostage-taking and the wrongful detention of U.S. nationals abroad. They emphasize four pillars: resolving existing cases, warning U.S. travelers, strengthening international norms against wrongful detention, and punishing and deterring perpetrators. This framing matters because it situates the new sanctions as one component of a broader, long-term policy effort rather than a standalone action. The official underscores that a dedicated interagency team works daily on these issues and that the administration inherited many of the current cases.
- The first senior administration official states that from day one the administration prioritizes the safe return of Americans wrongfully held overseas.
- The first senior administration official explains there is a dedicated, skilled interagency group working daily to identify and pursue all available options to bring detainees home.
- The first senior administration official breaks the effort into four lines of effort: resolving current cases, warning U.S. persons through clearer travel advisories, strengthening global norms, and punishing and deterring perpetrators.
- The first senior administration official notes that many current cases were inherited from the prior administration but that the current team has achieved notable successes.
#### Legal basis and Executive Order 14078
The first senior administration official explains the legal framework underpinning the new sanctions, focusing on Executive Order 14078 and the Robert Levinson Hostage Recovery and Hostage-Taking Accountability Act. They highlight that EO 14078, signed in July 2022, expands U.S. tools to impose costs on those responsible for wrongful detention and hostage-taking. The Levinson Act is framed as both a substantive foundation and a legacy of the Levinson family’s advocacy after Bob Levinson’s abduction. This context is important because it shows that the sanctions rest on bipartisan statutory authority and a new presidential order designed specifically for hostage cases.
- The first senior administration official recalls that President Biden signed Executive Order 14078 in July 2022 to bolster efforts to bring hostages and wrongfully detained U.S. nationals home.
- The first senior administration official says EO 14078 provides expanded tools, including financial sanctions and visa restrictions, to deter and disrupt hostage-taking and wrongful detentions.
- The first senior administration official credits the 2020 Robert Levinson Hostage Recovery and Hostage-Taking Accountability Act as the core legislative basis for the executive order.
- The second senior administration official reiterates that the Levinson Act and Levinson family’s advocacy are central to today’s actions and that Bob Levinson’s legacy continues through these authorities.
#### Announcement of Russia and Iran sanctions
The first senior administration official announces the first sanctions issued under EO 14078, targeting Russia’s Federal Security Service (FSB) and Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Intelligence Organization (IRGC-IO). These entities are designated for involvement in the wrongful detention or hostage-taking of U.S. nationals, including using Americans as political leverage or bargaining chips. The official stresses that this behavior threatens international stability and the safety of U.S. and other nationals. This marks an initial tranche of sanctions meant to impose accountability and deter future abuses by these and other actors.
- The first senior administration official announces sanctions on Russia’s Federal Security Service (FSB) for responsibility or complicity in the wrongful detention of a U.S. national abroad under EO 14078.
- The first senior administration official notes that the FSB has repeatedly participated in the arrest, investigation, and detention of U.S. nationals wrongfully held in Russia, as even Russian state media has acknowledged.
- The first senior administration official announces sanctions on Iran’s IRGC Intelligence Organization (IRGC-IO) for responsibility or complicity in wrongful detention of a U.S. national abroad under the same authority.

- The first senior administration official emphasizes that actors in Russia and Iran have used Americans as political leverage or for concessions, threatening international system integrity and the safety of U.S. nationals abroad.
#### Purpose and deterrent effect of sanctions
Both senior officials outline how these sanctions fit into broader efforts to free hostages and deter future wrongful detentions. They argue that sanctions can freeze assets, cut off access to the global financial system, and impose reputational and operational costs, even when some targets are already sanctioned under other authorities. The officials present sanctions as a form of accountability that signals to current and potential offenders that using Americans as pawns carries consequences. They also stress that sanctions are one tool among many, will be issued in future tranches, and are intended to incentivize better behavior over time.

- The first senior administration official explains that having one’s assets frozen and being denied access to key parts of the global financial system constitutes a real detriment to sanctioned actors.
- The first senior administration official notes that some targets are already sanctioned under other authorities, while others are not, but all merit additional accountability for wrongful detention conduct.
- The first senior administration official indicates that today’s designations are the first tranche under EO 14078 and that additional sanctions packages are already in development.
- The first senior administration official says sanctions aim to show that governments and entities cannot use human beings as bargaining chips without paying consequences.
- The first senior administration official asserts that interagency experts assessed that these sanctions will not set back ongoing efforts to resolve hostage cases and may instead help change behavior.
- The second senior administration official adds that diplomacy usually relies on incentives but sometimes requires introducing negative consequences for bad actors who wrongfully detain Americans.
- The second senior administration official explains that continued wrongful detention activity over recent months demonstrated that these actors require consequences, motivating today’s actions.
#### Reinforcing global norms and messaging
The officials emphasize that sanctioning entities already under other designations still serves a distinct purpose: labeling wrongful detention as a specific, sanctionable behavior. They argue that stacking authorities underscores U.S. concern about this practice and reinforces international norms against using people as political pawns. The discussion highlights recent diplomatic efforts, including public advocacy at the United Nations, to galvanize global opposition to state-sponsored wrongful detention. This normative messaging is intended both to stigmatize the practice and to complement the practical impact of sanctions.
- In response to Vivian, the first senior administration official acknowledges many bad actors are sanctioned for multiple types of misconduct, including human rights abuses and corruption.
- The first senior administration official insists that being sanctioned under one authority does not confer immunity from sanctions under another authority, especially for wrongful detention of U.S. nationals.

- The first senior administration official warns that the practice of wrongful detention appears to be trending in the wrong direction and argues the international community must push it toward extinction.
#### Designation of specific IRGC-IO officials
Responding to a question about targeting individuals, the first senior administration official details four IRGC-IO leaders designated under EO 14078. Each official is tied to wrongful detention activities and other serious abuses, including suppressing civil society, cracking down on protests, arranging prison logistics, engaging in counterespionage operations, and planning assassination plots. This disclosure illustrates the administration’s willingness to pinpoint personal responsibility within organizations, not just sanction institutions. It also signals that additional individuals and entities may be designated in the future as evidence supports further cases.
- The first senior administration official clarifies to Zeke that, in addition to the FSB and IRGC-IO entities, four IRGC-IO individuals are sanctioned under EO 14078.
- The first senior administration official states that Mohammad Kazemi, IRGC-IO commander since June 2022, is responsible for repressing civil society, arresting dissidents, and overseeing brutal crackdowns on protests and is tied to wrongful detention of Americans.
- The first senior administration official explains that co-deputy chief Mohamad Sayyar is involved in wrongful detention activities and has arranged logistics for prisoners held in Iran.
- The first senior administration official notes that co-deputy chief and brigadier general Mohammad Mohagheghi is involved in wrongful detentions and counterespionage operations in Syria.
- The first senior administration official states that IRGC-IO counterintelligence official Ruhollah Bazghandi participates in wrongful detention of U.S. and other foreign prisoners and has been involved in assassination plots.
- The first senior administration official stresses that these four designations are not the end of potential targets and that more sanctions under EO 14078 are likely.
#### Victim patterns, leverage, and potential delisting
The first senior administration official addresses questions about which cases underpin the sanctions and whether sanctions could be lifted as part of negotiations. They explain that the designations are based on patterns of wrongful detention and hostage-taking, encompassing both resolved and unresolved cases in Russia and Iran that are already public. The official quotes Treasury’s guidance that sanctions aim to change behavior and can include removing designations when warranted. This suggests that, while the primary goal is accountability and deterrence, sanctions may also function as leverage or incentives in efforts to bring Americans home, within legal constraints.
- The first senior administration official tells Zeke that the sanctions rest on a pattern of wrongful detention and hostage-taking by the FSB and IRGC-IO rather than a single case.
- The first senior administration official notes there are both resolved and unresolved wrongful detention cases in Russia and Iran that illustrate the behavior justifying these sanctions.
- The first senior administration official quotes Treasury language that the integrity of OFAC sanctions depends on both designating and, when appropriate, removing persons consistent with the law.
- The first senior administration official underscores that sanctions aim not solely to punish but to foster positive changes in behavior by offending actors.
- The first senior administration official states the administration uses an all-tools approach to bring Americans home and does not rule out using sanctions flexibly if it could mean the difference between continued detention and release.
- The second senior administration official adds that EO 14078 and the Levinson Act provide flexibility both to impose additional consequences and to adjust them if governments change course on U.S. detainees.

Both officials describe regular, intensive engagement with families by senior White House and State Department officials, including the Special Presidential Envoy for Hostage Affairs and the Secretary of State. They stress that all cases receive sustained attention regardless of whether families have met the President personally.

The sanctions are positioned as one of several parallel efforts: warning, resolving cases, and punishing repeat offenders.
