### Overview
  Senior administration officials host an embargoed briefing to announce the first tranche of sanctions under Executive Order 14078 — grounded in the Robert Levinson Hostage Recovery and Hostage-Taking Accountability Act — to hold accountable actors that engage in wrongful detention and hostage-taking of U.S. nationals. Officials explain the administration’s four-part approach (resolving cases, warning travelers, strengthening norms, and punishing perpetrators) and say today’s designations target Russia’s FSB and Iran’s IRGC-IO, plus four IRGC-IO leaders, based on patterns of wrongful detention and human rights abuses at places like Evin Prison. Speakers emphasize that sanctions are one tool in a coordinated interagency toolbox intended to impose costs, change behavior, and deter future abuses while continuing diplomatic and case-specific efforts to secure releases; they confirm families were previewed on the action, outline institutional support mechanisms such as the Special Presidential Envoy for Hostage Affairs, and note the package was in development before recent high-profile detentions though new cases reinforce the rationale for action.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Sanctions policy overview
  Senior administration officials frame the announcement as part of a broader Biden-Harris administration effort to secure the safe return of Americans wrongfully held abroad. They describe four lines of effort — resolving cases, warning travelers, strengthening norms, and punishing perpetrators — and emphasize that today’s announcement focuses on the punitive/deterrent element. The officials position sanctions as one tool among many used by a dedicated interagency team to protect U.S. nationals and deter future abuses.
- Senior administration official says the administration prioritizes the safe return of Americans wrongfully held overseas.
- Senior administration official explains the government organizes its work into four categories: resolving cases, warning travelers, strengthening norms, and punishing perpetrators.
- Senior administration official says today’s actions focus on punishing and deterring hostage-taking and wrongful detention.
  #### Executive order authorities
  Officials describe Executive Order 14078 as the legal basis for today’s designations and trace it to the Robert Levinson Hostage Recovery and Hostage-Taking Accountability Act. They say the EO, issued in July 2022, expands tools available to impose financial sanctions and visa restrictions on those who wrongfully detain U.S. nationals. The administration presents this as the first tranche under the new authority after months of interagency review and coordination.
- Senior administration official notes President Biden issued Executive Order 14078 in July 2022 to expand tools against hostage-taking and wrongful detention.
- Senior administration official says the executive order builds on the Robert Levinson Hostage Recovery and Hostage-Taking Accountability Act.
- Senior administration official states today’s announcement is the first tranche of sanctions under the new authority after legal and evidentiary review.
  #### Designations announced today
  The administration announces sanctions on Russia’s Federal Security Service (FSB) and Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Intelligence Organization (IRGC-IO) for roles in wrongful detention and hostage-taking. Officials describe the FSB as repeatedly involved in the arrest and detention of U.S. nationals in Russia and say the IRGC-IO detains and interrogates detainees at Evin Prison, which has documented human rights abuses. In addition to the IRGC-IO entity designation, four IRGC-IO leaders are designated for their support of those activities.
- Senior administration official says the FSB is being sanctioned for being responsible for, complicit in, or otherwise directing wrongful detention of a U.S. national abroad.
- Senior administration official says the IRGC-IO is being sanctioned for being responsible for, complicit in, or otherwise directing wrongful detention of a U.S. national abroad.
- Senior administration official states that Evin Prison has a long history of human rights abuses and that the IRGC-IO interrogates detainees there.
- Senior administration official says four IRGC-IO leaders are being designated today for their support of the IRGC-IO’s wrongful detention activities.
  #### Sanctions impact and intent
  Officials characterize sanctions as a piece of a comprehensive toolbox intended to impose costs, change behavior, and deter future wrongful detentions. They explain practical effects include asset freezes and restrictions on access to parts of the global financial system, and they stress sanctions can be used alongside diplomacy and other approaches. The administration signals more designations are possible and frames sanctions as both punitive and potentially corrective incentives.
- Senior administration official says sanctions can freeze assets and deny access to key parts of the global financial system, which imposes real detriments on bad actors.
- Senior administration official says sanctions are one tool in a broader set of approaches and that additional tranches of sanctions are in development.
- Senior administration official says sanctions aim to hold actors accountable and to incentivize better behavior in the future.
  #### Levinson legacy and messaging
  Senior administration official 2 emphasizes the moral and legislative lineage of the effort, invoking Bob Levinson’s case and the Levinson Act as foundational to modern authorities. He quotes Secretary Blinken to underline the administration’s stated commitment to relentless efforts to reunite detainees with their families. Officials use this framing to signal continuity with congressional intent and to underline a principled basis for the actions.
- Senior administration official 2 connects today’s authorities to the Robert Levinson Hostage Recovery and Hostage-Taking Accountability Act and commemorates Bob Levinson’s legacy.
- Senior administration official 2 quotes Secretary Blinken saying the United States will continue relentless efforts to secure the release of U.S. nationals who are held hostage or wrongfully detained.
- Senior administration official 2 says the Levinson Act and the executive order together expand the government’s toolkit to deter and disrupt hostage-taking.
  #### Deterrence and negotiations (Q&A)
  Reporters ask whether the sanctions will deter future hostage-taking and whether timing could harm negotiations to free detainees. Officials answer that sanctions are part of comprehensive strategies and that the administration coordinated across government to avoid undermining ongoing resolution efforts. They say sanctions can change behavior and that the Treasury’s authority includes removing designations consistent with law if doing so advances U.S. objectives.
- Jennifer and Kylie ask what the practical implications of these sanctions are and whether they might harm negotiations to free detained Americans.
- Senior administration official responds that sanctions play a role in broader toolboxes and that the administration coordinated to avoid setting back efforts to resolve individual cases.
- Senior administration official says Treasury sanctions are designed to change behavior and that the administration hopes sanctions will incentivize better conduct.
  #### Overlap with existing sanctions
  Reporters question the incremental effect of new designations given existing sanctions on the same actors. Officials respond that being sanctioned under one authority does not preclude further designations under another and that labeling behavior specifically as wrongful detention sends an additional signal. They also note parallel diplomatic messaging at the United Nations to stigmatize the practice of using people as political pawns.
- Vivian asks whether additional sanctions matter when the same organizations already face other sanctions.
- Senior administration official answers that actors sanctioned for one wrongdoing are not immune from being sanctioned for wrongful detention, and that the new designations send a specific message about this conduct.
- Senior administration official notes that the U.S. Ambassador to the UN publicly condemned the use of detained people as bargaining chips to reinforce international norms.
  #### Individual targets and cases
  Officials identify four individual IRGC-IO leaders designated in this tranche and describe their roles in detention and other abusive activities. They also explain the sanctions are premised on patterns of wrongful detention that include both resolved and unresolved cases, without detailing specific detainees. Officials say sanctions can be lifted consistent with law as part of efforts to secure positive changes in behavior, and they underscore continued use of diplomatic and other tools.
- Senior administration official lists four designated IRGC-IO individuals: Mohammad Kazemi, Mohamad Sayyar (deputy/co-deputy), Mohammad Mohagheghi (co-deputy chief), and Ruhollah Bazghandi.
- Senior administration official says the sanctions are premised on patterns of wrongful detention that include both cases the administration has resolved and cases it continues to work to resolve.
- Senior administration official says OFAC and Treasury retain the ability to remove designations consistent with law and that sanctions aim to bring about positive changes in behavior.
  #### Family engagement and case handling
  Reporters ask whether families were notified and how the administration decides which families meet the President. Officials confirm they previewed the announcement with relevant families and explain that presidential meetings are limited but not determinative of government effort. They highlight institutional mechanisms — including the Special Presidential Envoy for Hostage Affairs (SPEHA) and the Hostage Recovery Fusion Cell — that ensure sustained, expert engagement on cases.
- Unidentified caller asks whether families were briefed before the announcement and how meeting access to the President is determined.
- Senior administration official confirms the administration previewed the sanctions for a number of relevant families ahead of the announcement.
- Senior administration official says presidential meetings are limited and not a determinant of government effort, and that career structures like the SPEHA and the Hostage Recovery Fusion Cell ensure sustained work on cases.
  #### Specific detainee cases discussed
  Reporters press on Mark Fogel and Evan Gershkovich and whether families have had contact with senior officials. Officials say the President spoke with the Gershkovich family by phone and that SPEHA and senior officials maintain frequent contact with many families. They decline to detail State Department wrongful detention designation reviews but state that the Fogel family has met with senior officials and that the embassy has sought his humanitarian release.
- Unidentified caller asks why Mark Fogel is not considered wrongfully detained and whether his family is in contact with the government.
- Senior administration official says the State Department conducts wrongful detention designation reviews and confirms the Fogel family has had contact with senior officials and that an Embassy Moscow humanitarian plea has been submitted.
- Senior administration official says the President spoke by phone with the Gershkovich family and that SPEHA staff have visited the Gershkovich family at home.
  #### Timing and recent detentions
  Reporters ask whether Evan Gershkovich’s detention prompted or accelerated the sanctions announcement. Officials say the sanctions package was already under development before Gershkovich’s detention but that new wrongful detentions reinforce the pattern of abuse the designations target. They reiterate that travel advisories and other warnings now explicitly highlight wrongful detention risks in high-risk countries.
- Mike asks whether the planning for today’s sanctions began before Evan Gershkovich’s detention and whether that case accelerated the announcement.
- Senior administration official says the sanctions package was well underway before Gershkovich’s detention but that new wrongful detentions reinforce the unacceptable pattern targeted by the designations.
- Senior administration official notes that State Department travel advisories now include a wrongful detention indicator to better warn Americans about specific risks.
