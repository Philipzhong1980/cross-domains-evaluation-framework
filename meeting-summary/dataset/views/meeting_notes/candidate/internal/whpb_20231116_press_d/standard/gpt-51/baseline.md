### Overview
Senior White House officials use this briefing to frame President Biden’s APEC Leaders’ Week agenda and the Indo-Pacific Economic Framework as evidence of robust U.S. economic leadership in the Asia-Pacific, with Deputy National Security Advisor Mike Pyle stressing strong two-way trade and investment flows, new IPEF supply chain, clean economy, and fair economy agreements, and high-profile events on green investment, “Invest in America,” and global labor rights as key deliverables that link regional engagement to U.S. job creation. In parallel, National Security Council spokesperson John Kirby addresses multiple national security issues, reaffirming U.S. support for Israel’s campaign to degrade Hamas’s operational capabilities, defending U.S. intelligence that Hamas uses Gaza’s Al‑Shifa Hospital for command-and-control, outlining Brett McGurk’s diplomatic efforts to coordinate humanitarian aid and hostage negotiations, and explaining that the United States will respond as needed to protect its forces from Iran-backed attacks while encouraging China to use its channels with Tehran to prevent regional escalation. Kirby also characterizes the Biden–Xi summit as productive, citing renewed military-to-military communications, a Chinese commitment to crack down on fentanyl precursor exports, ongoing work on AI-related security risks, and a steady U.S. line on Taiwan, while Pyle describes the broader U.S.–China economic approach as one of “de-risking, not decoupling,” allowing strong trade and investment but tightly restricting sensitive technology flows that could enhance Chinese military or surveillance capabilities.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Biden’s APEC engagement priorities
Deputy National Security Advisor Mike Pyle outlines President Biden’s objectives for APEC Leaders’ Week, emphasizing U.S. economic leadership in the Asia-Pacific and its benefits for American workers. He notes that Biden will underscore continued U.S. diplomatic and economic engagement, built on strengthened domestic foundations and alliances. The President plans to use the CEO Summit remarks to highlight deep economic ties with APEC economies and new two‑way investment figures. The current state is a confident U.S. administration framing APEC as a platform for inclusive, sustainable, and resilient regional growth.
- Mr. Pyle states that President Biden enters APEC Leaders’ Week as the Asia-Pacific looks to the United States as an engine of growth due to three years of domestic strengthening and alliance building.
- Mr. Pyle says Biden will make clear that the United States will remain deeply engaged economically and diplomatically in the region and explain why that matters for Americans.
- Mr. Pyle explains that at the APEC CEO Summit, Biden will stress strong economic ties between the United States and other APEC economies that are delivering for communities across the region.
- Mr. Pyle notes that Biden will frame the U.S. agenda around inclusive, sustainable, and resilient growth across the Asia-Pacific.
#### APEC trade, investment, and growth data
Pyle presents detailed metrics to demonstrate the scale and growth of U.S.–APEC economic relations. He cites increases in U.S. exports to APEC, massive inbound investment supporting U.S. jobs, and dominant U.S. foreign direct investment into APEC economies compared with China. These data points are used to argue that U.S. policies are driving significant cross-border investment flows and job creation. The relationship is portrayed as robust and expanding, providing the base for new regional initiatives.
- Mr. Pyle reports that companies based in other APEC economies have invested more than $200 billion in the United States since the start of the Biden administration.
- Mr. Pyle states that U.S. companies have invested at least $40 billion in other APEC economies in 2023 alone.
- Mr. Pyle notes that 60 percent of U.S. exports go to APEC economies and that U.S. exports to the region are up 12 percent in real terms since 2016.
- Mr. Pyle says APEC members have invested $1.7 trillion into the U.S. economy, supporting 2.3 million American jobs.
- Mr. Pyle emphasizes that the United States is the largest source of foreign direct investment into APEC, with U.S. FDI six times greater than that of the PRC and totaling about $1.4 trillion into APEC economies.
#### Climate, energy transition, and worker rights at APEC
Pyle describes Biden’s focus at APEC on sustainability, climate action, and labor standards. Biden will lead an APEC session on sustainability, climate change, and energy transition, using U.S. domestic progress as a model for combining decarbonization with job creation. Pyle also previews announcements on advancing workers’ rights globally, reflecting Biden’s self-described status as the most pro‑labor president. The administration positions labor empowerment as central to sustainable growth and a core U.S. contribution as APEC host.
- Mr. Pyle says Biden will lead the first APEC leaders’ session on sustainability, climate change, and the energy transition.
- Mr. Pyle explains that Biden will highlight U.S. progress on climate and call for collective action by APEC economies.
- Mr. Pyle states that Biden will underscore how the administration is modeling a clean energy transition that creates good jobs and grows the economy.
- Mr. Pyle notes that Biden will emphasize promoting workers’ rights internationally and argue that worker empowerment is key to sustainable growth.
- Mr. Pyle says the President is expected to take historic steps to advance workers’ rights globally, which senior officials including Secretary Blinken, Ambassador Tai, Acting Secretary Su, and NEC Director Brainard will showcase.
#### IPEF agreements and regional initiatives
Pyle details progress under the Indo-Pacific Economic Framework (IPEF), presenting it as a central tool of U.S. regional economic strategy. He notes that ministers will sign a Supply Chain Agreement and substantially conclude Clean Economy and Fair Economy agreements, calling them first-of-their-kind deals reached on an accelerated timeline. He also highlights high-level events showcasing IPEF and PGI initiatives to catalyze investment, including a new investment accelerator and a green transition credit fund focused on India. The trade pillar remains under negotiation, but the administration frames the concluded pillars as evidence of IPEF’s effectiveness.
- Mr. Pyle says IPEF is designed to tackle cutting-edge issues like supply chain resilience and the clean energy transition that will shape future economic success.
- Mr. Pyle announces that IPEF ministers will sign the Supply Chain Agreement and substantially conclude the Clean Economy and Fair Economy agreements during the week.
- Mr. Pyle describes these IPEF agreements as first-of-their-kind and concluded in record time over 18 months with all partners.
- Mr. Pyle confirms that negotiations on the trade pillar have made progress and will continue in the coming weeks and months toward a high-standard outcome.
- Mr. Pyle notes that an IPEF event with leaders of Japan, Vietnam, and Singapore will demonstrate U.S. commitment to deepening economic engagement through the framework.
- Mr. Pyle explains that Secretary Raimondo and Senior Advisor Amos Hochstein will co-host an investor forum to announce new Indo-Pacific investment opportunities, including an IPEF-PGI Investment Accelerator and a green transition credit fund for projects in India.
- Mr. Pyle says U.S. officials will also announce significant investments in critical minerals, digital infrastructure, and energy transition as part of PGI-related efforts.
#### Invest in America and job-creating FDI
Pyle previews an “Invest in America” event at APEC highlighting major Asia-Pacific investments in U.S. communities. The event, led by senior economic officials, will showcase high-quality, job-creating projects driven in part by Biden-era policies that make the United States an attractive investment destination. This is part of a broader narrative tying international economic engagement to domestic job creation. The current status is a communications push to connect foreign investment stories with policy achievements.
- Mr. Pyle says Secretary Raimondo, NEC Director Lael Brainard, and Export–Import Bank Chair Reta Jo Lewis will co-host an event spotlighting job-creating U.S. investments by Asia-Pacific companies.
- Mr. Pyle explains that the “Invest in America” event will feature high-quality investments in communities across the country that support high-quality jobs.
- Mr. Pyle attributes the strength of this inbound investment to President Biden’s policies, which he says have made America a powerful magnet for global investment.
#### Strategic focus of modern economic frameworks
In response to questions, Pyle frames IPEF-style arrangements as a model for modern economic engagement focused on cutting-edge issues rather than traditional trade deals alone. He notes that leaders and ministers worldwide prioritize supply chain resilience, clean energy, and governance issues like corruption and tax fairness. He points to similar frameworks, such as one agreed with the UK, to show this approach is spreading. The administration positions these issue-based frameworks as the preferred path for advancing global economic cooperation.
- Mr. Pyle tells Aamer from AP that leaders and ministers globally are focused on supply chain resilience, the clean energy transition, corruption, and tax fairness as central economic issues.
- Mr. Pyle argues that these priorities underpin the design of IPEF and explain the rapid progress on its pillars.
- Mr. Pyle cites the June framework with UK Prime Minister Sunak, covering supply chains and clean energy, as an example of similar issue-focused cooperation.
- Mr. Pyle concludes that targeting front-of-mind, cutting-edge issues is how President Biden and other leaders intend to advance global economic discussions.
#### U.S. support for Israel vs. Hamas
NSC spokesperson John Kirby responds to questions about President Biden’s comments on ending the war with Hamas by affirming Israel’s right to address what it sees as an existential threat. Kirby underscores that Hamas openly seeks Israel’s destruction and intends repeated attacks, so Israel is working to degrade Hamas’s capabilities. He argues that military and other tools can significantly reduce a terrorist group’s operational capacity, citing ISIS and al Qaeda. The United States commits to continue providing Israel the tools to pursue this objective while not promising the elimination of ideology itself.
- Mr. Kirby tells Aamer that Israel has the right and responsibility to eliminate what it views as an existential threat from Hamas.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Hamas leaders publicly state their intent to attack Israel repeatedly and remove the country from the face of the Earth.
- Mr. Kirby says experience shows states can significantly degrade terrorist groups’ ability to resource, recruit, train, and execute attacks through military and other means.
- Mr. Kirby cites ISIS and al Qaeda as examples of organizations reduced to a shadow of their former selves despite enduring ideologies.
- Mr. Kirby states that the United States will continue helping Israel by ensuring it has the tools and capabilities to go after Hamas.
#### IPEF trade pillar timing and credibility
Justin from Bloomberg raises concerns that delaying an expected early harvest on the IPEF trade pillar could undermine confidence in U.S. reliability, especially given domestic political pressures. Pyle responds that, relative to typical trade timelines, IPEF has already produced substantial outcomes in 18 months on key pillars. He acknowledges continued work is needed on the trade pillar and stresses intensive consultations with Congress and stakeholders, including labor. The administration presents the partial conclusion as a success rather than a failure, while deferring specific timing on further agreements.
- Justin from Bloomberg notes that delegations expected an early harvest on the IPEF trade pillar but that the U.S. pulled back amid pressure from congressional Democrats.
- Mr. Pyle replies that most trade negotiations take years, whereas IPEF was always intended to move on an accelerated timeline.
- Mr. Pyle stresses that concluding three pillars in 18 months on supply chains, clean energy, and good governance is a significant achievement.
- Mr. Pyle says the administration will keep working on the trade pillar in the coming weeks and months, consulting intensively with Capitol Hill, stakeholders, and labor.
- Mr. Pyle argues that the rapid conclusion of three pillars demonstrates the importance of IPEF and the United States’ ability to deliver outcomes through it.
#### Deterring attacks on U.S. forces and China’s role with Iran
Mary from ABC asks about using China’s leverage over Iran to reduce attacks by Iranian‑backed groups on U.S. forces. Kirby emphasizes that U.S. responses are driven by the need to protect troops and facilities, promising continued and appropriately calibrated retaliation if attacks persist. He acknowledges that China has communication channels and some influence with Iran that the United States lacks, and says Washington encourages Beijing to use those channels to discourage escalation. Kirby declines to detail what Xi and Biden discussed but confirms the U.S. position on preventing regional conflict deepening.
- Mr. Kirby tells Mary that U.S. actions are not just about messaging but about protecting American troops and facilities from ongoing attacks.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that if attacks continue, the United States will respond at a time and in a manner of its choosing, targeting support structures linked to the IRGC.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges that China has communication channels and influence with Iran that the United States does not have.
- Mr. Kirby says the United States encourages China to use those channels to press Iran against escalation or deepening the regional conflict.
- Mr. Kirby declines to provide detailed readouts of Biden–Xi conversations but affirms U.S. interest in PRC leveraging its ties with Iran.
#### Biden–Xi meeting: Taiwan, fentanyl, and rhetoric
David from Reuters asks about Xi’s comments on Taiwan, specifics of fentanyl cooperation, and whether Biden’s “dictator” remark undercuts diplomacy. Kirby reports that Biden sees the summit as productive, citing progress on fentanyl, restoring military-to-military communications, and starting work on AI-related security issues. He reiterates U.S. positions on the Taiwan Strait, including no support for Taiwan independence, continued provision of self‑defense capabilities, and opposition to unilateral status quo changes. On fentanyl, he explains that China now commits to crack down on precursor chemicals exported to Latin America, with Xi personally stating he does not want any more Americans to die from fentanyl. Kirby does not address the “dictator” remark’s impact directly but suggests substantive progress stands on its own.
- David from Reuters asks about Xi’s conditions for intervention in Taiwan, specifics of the fentanyl agreement, and reactions to Biden calling Xi a dictator.
- Mr. Kirby says Biden feels very good about the four-hour meeting and the progress achieved, especially on fentanyl and restoring military-to-military communication channels.
- Mr. Kirby notes that teams will begin working together on cooperation around artificial intelligence, particularly where national security is involved.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that the United States does not seek conflict, wants peace and stability across the Taiwan Strait, maintains its One China policy, does not support Taiwan independence, and will continue providing Taiwan self-defense capabilities under the Taiwan Relations Act.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that the United States opposes unilateral changes to the status quo in the Taiwan Strait, especially by force.
- Mr. Kirby explains that many fentanyl precursor chemicals are produced in China and shipped to Latin America, where cartels convert them into fentanyl for export to the United States.
- Mr. Kirby says that after years of reluctance, China now agrees to crack down on production and export of these chemicals through law enforcement measures, focusing on limiting exports to Latin America.
- Mr. Kirby relays that Xi told Biden he does not want another American to die because of fentanyl and made a personal commitment to progress on the issue.
#### Hamas use of Al-Shifa Hospital
Yasmeen from the Washington Post presses for U.S. evidence about Hamas’s use of Gaza’s Al‑Shifa Hospital amid Israeli raids and international concern. Kirby reiterates that U.S. intelligence independently supports the assessment that Hamas uses Al‑Shifa as a command-and-control node and likely a storage site, effectively using patients and staff as human shields. He declines to comment on the pace of Israeli operations or demand for public evidence, deferring operational details to the Israeli Defense Forces. On a separate note, he lightly addresses Xi’s comments about possibly returning pandas to the United States, saying Washington would welcome such a decision but it remains China’s choice.
- Yasmeen from the Washington Post asks what the U.S. knows about Hamas’s use of Al-Shifa Hospital and whether Washington is pressing Israel for more evidence or faster operations.
- Mr. Kirby says U.S. intelligence convinces Washington that Hamas is using Al-Shifa as a command-and-control node and likely as a storage facility.
- Mr. Kirby asserts that Hamas is sheltering in the hospital and using it as a shield, thereby placing patients and medical staff at greater risk.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that the United States remains confident in its own intelligence assessment regarding Al-Shifa.
- Mr. Kirby declines to discuss operational specifics of the Israeli raid and defers to the Israeli Defense Forces to describe their progress and findings.
- Mr. Kirby notes separately that the United States would welcome the return of Chinese pandas if Beijing chooses to send them back, but that this is a sovereign decision for President Xi.

Kirby says McGurk’s trip began in Brussels and then moved into the region, combining briefings to NATO and allies with direct talks on support for Israel, humanitarian assistance into Gaza, and hostage negotiations. He highlights incremental progress such as continued truck flows, expanded humanitarian pauses, and a new corridor out of North Gaza. Kirby promises to provide a more detailed itinerary later but stresses that securing humanitarian access and hostage releases remains a high-priority, hour-by-hour effort.
- Deepa from NPR asks for specifics on Brett McGurk’s travel, meetings, and deliverables in the Middle East and Europe.
- Mr. Kirby says McGurk started his trip in Brussels before heading into the region and has been moving around extensively.
- Mr. Kirby explains that McGurk is updating NATO and allies on U.S. support for Israel, humanitarian efforts, and hostage-release work while coordinating on the ground with regional counterparts.
- Mr. Kirby reports that more aid trucks entered Gaza overnight, noting that while numbers are still below U.S. goals, flows continue.
- Mr. Kirby says humanitarian pauses continue and in some cases expand in hours, and he notes that the Israeli Defense Forces opened another corridor out of North Gaza that people are using.

#### Business engagement with China and de-risking
M.J. Lee from CNN asks whether it is safe for U.S. firms to invest in China as Xi courts American business, and also presses Kirby again on intelligence from Al‑Shifa. Pyle responds that U.S. companies must make their own choices but notes that bilateral trade remains strong, with last year a record for U.S.–China trade. He explains that U.S. policy is not broad decoupling but targeted de‑risking, using export controls and outbound investment restrictions only in narrow national security–sensitive areas such as technologies that could aid Chinese military or surveillance. Kirby reiterates U.S. confidence in its intelligence on Hamas’s use of Al‑Shifa while declining to discuss specific intelligence exchanges with Israel.
- M.J. Lee from CNN asks whether the administration considers it safe for American businesses to invest in China as Xi pitches that China is open for business.
- Mr. Pyle responds that U.S. businesses must decide for themselves but notes that last year was a record year for U.S.–China bilateral trade, which the United States welcomes.
- Mr. Pyle explains that there are narrow areas of concern, such as certain technology sectors that could support Chinese military or surveillance capabilities, where export controls and outbound investment restrictions apply.
- Mr. Pyle stresses that U.S. policy is not decoupling from China but de-risking, allowing strong trade and investment while narrowly targeting flows that could compromise national security.
- Mr. Pyle reiterates that robust trade and investment with China continues broadly, with only specific high-risk sectors constrained.
- Mr. Kirby, answering M.J. Lee’s follow-up, confirms that the United States remains confident in its own intelligence that Hamas uses Al-Shifa as a command-and-control and storage facility.
- Mr. Kirby says the United States stays in daily touch with Israeli counterparts about their operations but declines to detail any new intelligence shared since the raid began, leaving operational disclosures to Israel.
