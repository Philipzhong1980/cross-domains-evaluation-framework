### Overview
  Senior White House officials provide an APEC-focused briefing outlining President Biden’s agenda in San Francisco and tangible outcomes the administration expects to highlight. Mike Pyle emphasizes strong U.S. economic ties with the Asia-Pacific—citing substantial bilateral investment and trade figures—and previews IPEF achievements including a Supply Chain Agreement to be signed and near-complete Clean Economy and Fair Economy Agreements, alongside new public‑private investment initiatives and a green transition credit fund. John Kirby addresses Middle East and China matters raised in the press pool, stating U.S. confidence in intelligence that Hamas uses Al-Shifa Hospital as a command-and-control node, reaffirming support for Israel’s efforts to degrade Hamas’s operational capacity, and describing progress from Biden–Xi talks on fentanyl precursor controls, military-to-military communications, and AI cooperation while declining to disclose private diplomatic details. On IPEF’s trade pillar, the administration says negotiations will continue with intensive domestic consultation, and officials stress a de‑risking approach to China that preserves broad economic ties while restricting narrow, security‑sensitive technology flows.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### APEC Leaders' Week goals
  Mike Pyle frames President Biden’s participation in APEC Leaders’ Week as an effort to demonstrate continued U.S. diplomatic and economic engagement in the Asia-Pacific and to show how that engagement benefits American communities. He emphasizes the administration’s multi-year work to strengthen economic foundations and alliances. The day’s events are presented as a platform to showcase U.S. leadership and announce concrete outcomes.
- Mike Pyle says President Biden will make clear that the United States will continue to engage diplomatically and economically across the Asia-Pacific.
- Mike Pyle attributes the administration’s ability to lead in the region to nearly three years of work strengthening economic foundations at home and relationships abroad.
- Mike Pyle frames the APEC events as opportunities to explain to the American people why engagement in the region matters.
#### Economic ties and statistics
  Pyle supplies detailed trade and investment figures to quantify U.S. economic integration with APEC economies and to argue that engagement delivers jobs and growth. He cites multi-hundred-billion-dollar investment flows in both directions and emphasizes U.S. leadership in foreign direct investment. These data points are used to support the administration’s narrative that U.S. engagement produces high-quality jobs and strengthens the economy.
- Mike Pyle states that companies from other APEC economies have invested more than $200 billion into the United States since the start of the administration.
- Mike Pyle reports that U.S. companies invested at least $40 billion in other APEC economies in 2023 alone.
- Mike Pyle says 60 percent of U.S. exports are sent to fellow APEC economies and that U.S. exports to the region are up 12 percent in real terms since 2016.
- Mike Pyle notes that APEC members have invested $1.7 trillion into the U.S. economy, supporting 2.3 million American jobs.
#### Sustainability and climate session
  Pyle previews President Biden leading an APEC session focused on sustainability, climate change, and the energy transition, stressing collective action among APEC economies. He plans to highlight U.S. progress on combating climate change and position the U.S. transition as a model that can create good jobs. The session is presented as central to advancing inclusive, resilient regional growth.
- Mike Pyle says President Biden will lead the first APEC session focused on sustainability, climate change, and the energy transition.
- Mike Pyle says the President will highlight U.S. progress on climate change and call for collective action from APEC economies.
- Mike Pyle frames the clean energy transition as an opportunity to model how to create good jobs while growing the economy.
#### IPEF outcomes and progress
  Pyle outlines concrete IPEF accomplishments, saying ministers will sign the Supply Chain Agreement and will substantially conclude the Clean Economy and Fair Economy Agreements, describing them as first-of-their-kind and completed in record time. He stresses that IPEF targets cutting-edge issues like supply chains, clean energy, and high-standard governance. The administration presents IPEF as a vehicle for practical, accelerated multilateral economic cooperation while continuing work on the trade pillar.
- Mike Pyle says ministers will sign the IPEF Supply Chain Agreement this week and will substantially conclude the Clean Economy and Fair Economy Agreements.
- Mike Pyle calls those IPEF outcomes first-of-their-kind agreements achieved in record time.
- Mike Pyle says the trade pillar negotiations have made progress and that the administration will continue work in the weeks and months ahead.
#### Investment events and initiatives
  Pyle summarizes administration-led investment events at APEC that aim to deepen U.S. economic engagement through targeted public-private initiatives. He describes an investor forum hosted by Secretary Raimondo and Amos Hochstein to launch an IPEF–PGI Investment Accelerator and a green transition credit fund for projects including solar and storage. These announcements are positioned as efforts to mobilize public capital and catalyze private investment in critical areas across the region.
- Mike Pyle says Secretary of Commerce Gina Raimondo and Amos Hochstein will co-host an investor forum to announce new Indo-Pacific investment opportunities.
- Mike Pyle says the event will launch two new IPEF and PGI initiatives, including an IPEF–PGI Investment Accelerator.
- Mike Pyle says the administration will announce a green transition credit fund to invest in solar, wind, hybrid storage, e-mobility, and other projects in India.
#### Global workers' rights initiative
  Pyle says President Biden will spotlight international workers’ rights as part of the administration’s APEC agenda, framing labor standards as essential to sustainable growth. He describes a major initiative to advance workers’ rights globally, to be highlighted by senior officials including Secretary Blinken and NEC Director Lael Brainard. The administration positions these steps as a continuation of its pro-labor domestic agenda extended to international economic engagement.
- Mike Pyle says President Biden will emphasize promoting workers’ rights internationally as part of his APEC agenda.
- Mike Pyle says Secretary Blinken, Ambassador Tai, Acting Secretary Su, and NEC Director Lael Brainard will highlight a bold initiative to advance workers’ rights.
- Mike Pyle describes the initiative as part of concluding the United States’ APEC host year.
#### IPEF model versus trade deals
  Reporters ask whether IPEF represents a new normal compared with traditional trade deals and whether domestic politics undermines U.S. credibility; Pyle responds that leaders are prioritizing cutting-edge issues where frameworks can deliver accelerated outcomes. He cites other partner frameworks (e.g., with the UK) as evidence that this approach is becoming a model for addressing supply chains, clean energy, and governance. On the trade pillar, Pyle says progress continues and that the administration will consult intensively with Capitol Hill and stakeholders including labor as work proceeds.
- Mike Pyle says global leaders are focused on cutting-edge issues like supply chain resilience and the clean energy transition and that frameworks like IPEF can deliver results on those topics.
- Mike Pyle points to a June U.K. framework as an example of partners using similar approaches to tackle modern economic challenges.
- Mike Pyle says the administration expects to continue negotiations on the IPEF trade pillar and will consult intensively with Congress and stakeholders, including labor.
#### Hamas threat and Israeli operations
  John Kirby addresses questions about President Biden’s comment that the war will end when Hamas no longer maintains the capacity to harm Israelis, stressing Israel’s right to remove what it sees as an existential threat. He explains that military and other measures can significantly degrade a terrorist group’s operational capacity while noting that ideological threats may persist. Kirby reiterates U.S. support for Israel’s efforts and says the U.S. will continue to help ensure Israel has the tools and capabilities it needs.
- John Kirby says Israel has the right and responsibility to eliminate what it views as an existential threat posed by Hamas.
- John Kirby says military and other measures can have a significant practical effect on a terrorist group’s ability to resource, train, recruit, plan, and execute attacks.
- John Kirby says the United States will continue supporting Israel by ensuring it has the tools and capabilities to address the threat.
#### U.S.–China meeting outcomes
  Reporters press for details from the Biden–Xi meetings; Kirby and Pyle describe progress on several fronts including reopening military-to-military communications, cooperation on AI security topics, and Chinese commitments on fentanyl precursor controls. Kirby says President Biden feels good about the meeting’s substantive outcomes but declines to discuss specific private assurances. Pyle reiterates that bilateral trade and investment remain strong while identifying targeted national-security-related restrictions on certain technology areas.
- John Kirby says the meetings produced progress on reopening a military-to-military communications channel and on cooperation between U.S. and Chinese teams on AI where it involves national security.
- John Kirby says President Xi expressed that he does not want to see another American die because of fentanyl and signaled willingness to crack down on precursor chemical production and export.
- Mike Pyle says last year was a record year of bilateral trade between the United States and China while noting targeted concerns in technology areas that could affect national security.
#### Al-Shifa hospital intelligence
  Multiple reporters question U.S. knowledge about Hamas use of Al-Shifa Hospital; Kirby asserts the U.S. has its own intelligence indicating Hamas uses the hospital as a command-and-control node and likely as storage, and that the U.S. remains confident in that assessment. He declines to provide operational details or to recount new Israeli intelligence publicly but says the U.S. stays in daily touch with Israeli counterparts. Kirby emphasizes that the Israeli Defense Forces should speak to the progress of their own operations.
- John Kirby says U.S. intelligence convinces the administration that Hamas is using Al-Shifa Hospital as a command-and-control node and likely as a storage facility.
- John Kirby says the U.S. remains confident in its intelligence assessment and will let the Israeli Defense Forces speak to operational progress.
- John Kirby says the U.S. stays in daily touch with Israeli counterparts but will not discuss specific intelligence shared between partners.
#### Humanitarian assistance and McGurk travel
  Kirby summarizes Brett McGurk’s recent travel to Europe and the Middle East to coordinate allied and partner responses, humanitarian assistance, and efforts to recover hostages. He says McGurk started in Brussels and moved into the region to update NATO, support humanitarian convoys, and coordinate with partners including Qatar. Kirby reports additional trucks have arrived, humanitarian pauses and corridors have been used, and hostage negotiations remain a high priority.
- John Kirby says Brett McGurk’s trip began in Brussels and moved into the region to brief NATO and engage regional partners on support for Israel and humanitarian assistance.
- John Kirby says more trucks have arrived overnight and that humanitarian pauses and newly opened corridors in North Gaza are enabling movement.
- John Kirby says McGurk is heavily engaged in negotiations to secure the release of hostages and is coordinating with partners such as Qatar.
#### Investor risk and China policy
  When asked whether it is safe for American businesses to invest in China, Pyle emphasizes that U.S.–China trade and investment remain robust but that the administration is using targeted tools to 'de-risk' narrow areas of concern. He cites export controls and an outbound investment screening program to prevent investment and exports that could erode U.S. national security. The administration frames this approach as avoiding decoupling while protecting critical technologies.
- Mike Pyle says U.S.–China bilateral trade flows are strong and that last year was a record year for bilateral trade between the two countries.
- Mike Pyle says the administration has specific concerns about narrow technology sectors that could contribute to military modernization or surveillance and is using export controls and an outbound investment program to address them.
- Mike Pyle frames the effort as 'de-risking' rather than decoupling, which preserves broad economic ties while protecting national-security-sensitive areas.
