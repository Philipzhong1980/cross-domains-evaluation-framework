### Overview
This press call features White House Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and NSC spokesperson Admiral John Kirby briefing reporters on a wide range of national security and domestic political issues. Kirby outlines intensive U.S. diplomatic and security engagement abroad: President Biden and senior advisers coordinate closely with allies and President Zelenskyy on sustaining aid to Ukraine as existing U.S. funds run out; the administration pushes for a new Gaza hostage deal through talks in Doha while urging Israel’s shift to lower-intensity operations that can reduce civilian casualties and expand humanitarian aid; and U.S. forces conduct targeted strikes to degrade Houthi capabilities threatening Red Sea shipping while insisting Washington does not seek a wider war and continues to monitor Iran’s regional activities and the destabilizing Ethiopia–Somaliland MOU that could benefit al‑Shabaab. Jean-Pierre then focuses on Biden’s upcoming meeting with congressional leaders to press for passage of his national security supplemental—linking Ukraine aid, border security, and broader U.S. interests—while stressing that Senate negotiations on a bipartisan border and immigration package are ongoing and that the immigration system has been broken for decades. She sharply criticizes Texas Governor Greg Abbott’s border actions as extreme political stunts that block Border Patrol, endanger migrants, and force legal confrontation, contrasts them with federal support for migrant-receiving cities via work-permit clinics and grants, and repeatedly frames Biden as defending democracy against election denial and January 6 conspiracy theories even as she avoids direct 2024 campaign commentary. Additional updates cover John Kerry’s impending departure as climate envoy, Secretary Austin’s recovery and continued engagement, a concerning swatting incident handled by protective agencies, and Biden’s continued emphasis on economic and social initiatives at home, such as his North Carolina trip and MLK Day service visit, underscoring an administration narrative of active global engagement paired with a domestic agenda focused on security, climate, and support for working families.

Biden will host congressional and committee leaders to discuss his national security supplemental request, and he will travel to North Carolina to highlight the impact of his economic and infrastructure agenda. These events underscore the administration’s push for additional security funding, including Ukraine and border resources, while showcasing perceived economic successes. The current state is that planning is underway, with further details promised after the meetings and trip occur.

#### Middle East: Gaza operations, hostages, and humanitarian aid
Kirby provides a status update on the Gaza conflict, emphasizing U.S. efforts to secure hostage releases and promote a transition to lower-intensity Israeli operations. He notes that Brett McGurk recently met counterparts in Doha to pursue a new hostage deal, amid Hamas’s release of hostage videos that the U.S. characterizes as evidence of cruelty. Kirby says Israel has begun shifting to low-intensity operations in northern Gaza, withdrawing a division and planning similar changes in the south, which Washington hopes will reduce civilian casualties and improve aid flows. U.S. envoys are working with Israel to increase humanitarian assistance and prepare for eventual civilian returns to northern Gaza, even as the administration reiterates Israel’s right to self-defense and urges more precision in operations.

- John Kirby reports that Israel has shifted to a low-intensity phase in northern Gaza and has withdrawn an army division from Gaza, with plans to extend low-intensity operations to the south.
- John Kirby states that the U.S. is preparing to increase humanitarian assistance into Gaza and to help set conditions for civilians to return to north Gaza, in coordination with U.N. assessment missions.
- John Kirby emphasizes that the U.S. wants to see no more civilian casualties, urges Israel to conduct operations as surgically and precisely as possible, and notes that Hamas remains an active and vowed threat to Israel.
- John Kirby notes that U.S. envoy David Satterfield and Ambassador Jack Lew are meeting Israeli senior civilian and military leaders at Southern Command to discuss humanitarian and operational issues in Gaza.
#### Red Sea and Houthi attacks on shipping
Kirby addresses ongoing Houthi attacks against commercial shipping in the Red Sea and the U.S.-U.K. military response. He maintains that initial coalition strikes significantly degraded Houthi capabilities but acknowledges that the group has continued lower-scale, largely ineffective attacks. Kirby argues retaliatory U.S. strikes are necessary to defend international shipping and deter a catastrophic attack, even as the administration insists it does not seek a broader war and frames its actions as de-escalatory by reducing Houthi capabilities. He confirms the U.S. is reviewing a possible terrorist designation for the Houthis, is monitoring reported Iranian strikes in the region, and remains committed to protecting ships and sailors with further action if required.
- John Kirby tells Justin (Bloomberg) that the Houthis have conducted some additional smaller-scale retaliatory attacks in recent days, none of which have been effective.
- John Kirby says U.S. and U.K. strikes on Houthi targets Friday night were expected to prompt some retaliation but are assessed to have had good effect in disrupting and degrading Houthi offensive capabilities.
- John Kirby confirms that U.S. forces conducted additional strikes to destroy four ballistic missiles in Yemen that were believed to be prepped and ready for launch, neutralizing them before they could be fired.
- John Kirby stresses that the United States is not seeking war or conflict expansion and says the Houthis have a choice to stop their reckless attacks.
- John Kirby explains to Weijia Jiang that most Houthi missile and drone attacks have been knocked out of the sky or have missed, contributing to the relatively low number of reported casualties on commercial vessels; he commits to seek casualty data from U.S. Central Command.
- John Kirby argues that U.S.-led retaliatory strikes are worth the risk because degrading Houthi capabilities reduces the chance of a catastrophically successful attack and signals that the U.S. will not ignore ongoing threats to international shipping.
- John Kirby tells Zeke Miller that the administration is still reviewing whether to redesignate the Houthis as a Foreign Terrorist Organization and has made no decision yet.
- John Kirby rejects the idea that U.S. strikes are part of a wider Gaza-related war, insisting the operations are about defending global shipping lanes and noting Houthis have struck vessels with no connection to Israel.

This underscores U.S. concern about regional spillover even as officials aim to prevent escalation.

#### Horn of Africa: Ethiopia–Somaliland MOU and al-Shabaab

He reiterates U.S. support for Somalia’s sovereignty and territorial integrity and says Washington is concerned the deal could disrupt counterterrorism operations against al‑Shabaab. Kirby notes the U.S. is engaging regional organizations such as the African Union and IGAD to reduce tensions and warns that undermining coordination against al‑Shabaab could pose a direct threat to U.S. national security interests. The administration views the MOU as a destabilizing step at a time when the region cannot afford more conflict.
- John Kirby says the U.S. is troubled by the reported MOU between Ethiopia and Somaliland and reiterates support for Somalia’s sovereignty and territorial integrity.
- John Kirby warns that the Ethiopia–Somaliland MOU threatens to disrupt joint efforts by Somalis, Africans, and international partners, including the U.S., to fight al‑Shabaab.
- John Kirby emphasizes that al‑Shabaab remains a viable terrorist threat in the region and is designated as a foreign terrorist organization by the United States.
- John Kirby tells Anita Powell that the MOU could create a U.S. national security threat because anything that strengthens al‑Shabaab or undermines counterterrorism cooperation directly affects U.S. interests.
- John Kirby notes that the U.S. is engaging with partners in the Horn of Africa and welcomes African Union and IGAD efforts to calm the situation.

He argues that by preemptively degrading Houthi capabilities, the U.S. actually reduces the risk of escalation. The administration maintains that its overarching objective remains preventing the Gaza conflict from sparking a broader regional war.

The administration has issued a cease-and-desist letter and says it will refer the matter to the Justice Department if Texas does not comply, while maintaining that Border Patrol must have access to the border to enforce federal law. Jean-Pierre underscores that legal action is the main immediate federal tool, while calling on Abbott to stop obstructing federal operations.

#### Support for migrant‑receiving cities and work authorization
In response to questions about migrant busing to cities like Chicago, Jean-Pierre explains federal efforts to support municipalities coping with new arrivals. She notes that the administration has collaborated with states and cities to set up one-stop clinics helping eligible noncitizens obtain work permits and relieve shelter systems, serving over 10,000 people so far. She adds that the administration has provided more than $1 billion in grants to jurisdictions hosting migrants, but emphasizes that more funding requires congressional action. The White House contrasts its coordination and resource efforts with Abbott’s unilateral busing decisions, which it portrays as uncoordinated political moves that burden cities.

Jean-Pierre calls Trump’s characterization of January 6 defendants as “hostages” grotesque and offensive, especially when contrasted with hostages abducted by Hamas, and praises journalists who risked their safety covering the attack. The White House frames Biden as standing against revenge politics, conspiracy theories, and efforts to undermine democratic institutions.

The White House does not yet announce a successor or Kerry’s future plans, characterizing his exit as a personal decision.
