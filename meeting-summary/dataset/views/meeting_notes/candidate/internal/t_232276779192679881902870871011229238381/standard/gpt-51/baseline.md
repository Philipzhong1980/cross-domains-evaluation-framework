### Overview
The Visual Arts Committee (VAC) meeting focuses on three main areas: future agenda planning and process improvements, selection of artists for the Center for the Performing Arts (CPA) gallery, and an in‑depth briefing on the Transit Center Grade Separation and Access Project, with cross‑cutting themes about artist engagement and public safety. Early in the meeting, members including Susi Merhar and Regina Sakols request future discussions on clearer artist “touch points,” better bundling of call‑for‑artists materials, and possible alternatives or complements to the CaFÉ platform, while Tööt Too Thomson and others propose new youth art opportunities and expanded venues for displaying student work; John Lang records these for later agendas and reminds members to route public art policy inquiries through staff. For ongoing capital projects, John Lang reports no major changes but confirms insurance issues are resolved for Rankstorff Park maintenance murals, enabling contracts to proceed. The committee then reviews scoring from a 63‑entry CPA call and, after clarifications on scoring and logistics, unanimously votes on a motion by Regina Sakols and seconded by Cliff Bryant to expand from six to eight exhibiting artists with three alternates, directing John Lang to coordinate shorter show windows and enhanced promotion with CPA staff. Public Works staff Joy Houghton, Jim Lightbody, and Robert Gonzales present the Transit Center grade separation design and urban design guidelines, emphasizing safe, well‑lit undercrossings, a new concourse, and multiple art‑integration opportunities; VAC members respond enthusiastically with ideas for lighting, living walls, and potential rotating gallery elements, while staff outline a K5‑funded artist‑selection path that likely includes stipends for finalists and must align with the project’s mid‑2024 design schedule. In a related policy discussion, John Lang summarizes past use of stipends on high‑value projects and hears broad committee support, particularly from Cliff Bryant and Don Whitebread, for using a flexible threshold (around $60,000 per piece) as a trigger for considering a two‑phase selection with paid concept development, subject to further refinement with Public Works. The meeting closes with mutual thanks between staff and committee members and confirmation that VAC will reconvene after the January recess.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Future agenda topics and process improvements
The committee reviews potential future agenda items, focusing on improving how public art integrates with city initiatives and how the Visual Arts Committee (VAC) works with artists. Members raise the need for clearer expectations about artist “touch points,” better documentation of calls for artists, and potential alternatives or complements to the CaFÉ submission platform. They also flag new program ideas, including youth art opportunities and additional venues or formats for displaying community artwork.
- Susi Merhar notes that recent projects revealed inconsistent strategies for when and how VAC interacts with artists, and asks for a future discussion to clarify expected touch points, requirements, and when the committee should or should not go back to artists during a project.
- Susi Merhar states that many CaFÉ submissions did not answer application questions as intended, making it difficult for VAC to find needed information, and suggests exploring process improvements or alternatives.
- John Lang points VAC to agenda item 7.3 as an upcoming discussion of selection thresholds and stipends, and records Susi’s additional process suggestions as future topics.
- Regina Sakols reports that she could not locate the original call for artists in the recent review package and requests that future packets bundle the call text together with artist statements and CaFÉ materials for easier comparison.
- Regina Sakols observes that some applicants appeared to be outside the intended geographic scope and wants to compare the call’s local-artist wording to actual submissions.
- Jesse Cupp relays feedback from an artist who finds CaFÉ difficult and repetitive, and notes that some artists avoid applying because of the platform.
- Don Whitebread remarks that CaFÉ is effectively the industry standard, suggesting that alternative platforms may have smaller reach even if they are more usable.
- Susi Merhar mentions seeing another call that used SlideRoom, typically a portfolio tool for schools, and suggests it as a possible alternative or complement.
- Jesse Cupp proposes piloting acceptance of submissions through two platforms to compare participation, while acknowledging unknown cost and administrative impacts.
- John Lang summarizes that staff will return with a future agenda item focused specifically on submission platforms and process.
- Toot Too Thomson proposes a future program to issue calls specifically for young artists to display work, based on interest expressed by a middle school art teacher.
- Regina Sakols supports the youth art idea and suggests shop windows or other locations as possible venues.
- Jesse Cupp and others recall that CSMA has previously managed rotating children’s art displays at City Hall and the library, clarifying that those are non‑city‑run programs.
- John Lang reminds members not to deeply deliberate on items not agendized, and confirms the youth art concept will be added as a future topic.

#### Placemaking, landscaping, and art integration
Staff and committee discuss how biodiversity and landscaping strategies intersect with public art, especially in streetscapes and parks. A member of the public later urges that art and landscaping opportunities not be limited strictly to biodiversity objectives. The conversation underscores VAC’s interest in using infrastructure and planting projects as art opportunities and in learning from regional examples.
- John Lang explains that staff has coordinated with Community Services on the city’s biodiversity strategy, which examines trees, landscaping, and related habitat issues, and notes that this creates opportunities for art to be embedded in landscape design.
- John Lang recalls a prior K5 park project involving an artistic butterfly habitat as an example of combining biodiversity goals with public art.
- John Lang notes that the biodiversity strategy is still in a research phase, that the K5 policy has been given to the consultants, and that staff will bring the topic back when the strategy is further along.
- Clifford Bryant describes a median on Charleston Road in Palo Alto with integrated planting and a colorful three‑piece sculpture, citing it as a strong example of art and landscaping working together to calm traffic and enhance a streetscape.
- Jesse Cupp and Clifford Bryant discuss how similar approaches could be applied to medians or protected bike lanes in Mountain View for both safety and aesthetic benefits.
- Bruce England, speaking as a member of the public, supports linking art to the biodiversity strategy but cautions that art and landscaping should also address broader ‘bread and butter’ Community Services needs and not be constrained exclusively by biodiversity goals.

#### Youth and student art display opportunities
Committee members briefly revisit historical and current youth art display practices and debate whether VAC should create a new city‑run program for young artists. The discussion surfaces questions about venues, roles of existing partners like CSMA, and program ownership. Given agenda constraints and Brown Act concerns, staff capture the idea for more structured future discussion rather than shaping a program in this meeting.
- Toot Too Thomson reports that a middle school art teacher expressed interest in a city‑supported ‘call for young artists’ to display student work in public venues.
- Jesse Cupp notes that CSMA, a nonprofit, has historically curated rotating children’s art exhibits at City Hall and the library, often in partnership with public schools, but clarifies that these displays are not VAC‑run.
- Regina Sakols recalls that City Hall used to host children’s artwork and suggests that store windows or other public spaces could be candidates for youth exhibitions.
- Don Whitebread notes that any new youth art program would first need well‑defined venues and logistics before VAC could develop a call for artists.
- Toot Too Thomson asks how public‑school art teachers could access opportunities similar to CSMA’s programs and advocates adding this as a formal agenda item.
- John Lang acknowledges the interest and reiterates that, because the topic is not on the current agenda, staff will bring it back for focused discussion at a future meeting.

#### Public comment on landscaping and restroom design
A public commenter returns to two emerging themes: ensuring art and landscaping work beyond the biodiversity mandate, and leveraging design to make public restrooms both functional and visually acceptable. The remarks connect VAC’s art role with broader urban design and amenity considerations in city projects.
- Bruce England reiterates that art integrated into landscaping should support, but not be constrained by, the biodiversity strategy and should also fit within standard Community Services landscaping functions.
- Bruce England emphasizes that public restrooms, which advocacy groups already support, also have artistic and urban design dimensions, and urges that their visual design be addressed alongside utility.
- Bruce England cites San Francisco’s recent restroom installations as precedents where form, anti‑graffiti surface treatments, and potential advertising space are consciously designed.
- Bruce England indicates he would advocate from the public side for VAC to consider the design quality of public restrooms where relevant.

#### Center for the Performing Arts (CPA) gallery artist selection
The committee reviews scoring results from a CaFÉ juried call to select artists for the Mountain View Center for the Performing Arts gallery displays. Staff clarify how scores were calculated and address a concern about unscored entries. Members decide to expand from six to eight exhibiting artists, add three alternates, and direct staff to work with CPA on a compressed exhibition schedule and enhanced promotion, while also raising questions about platform usability and exhibition formats for future cycles.
- John Lang explains that the CPA call reused a 2019 framework, intended for Bay Area (nine‑county) artists, and notes that staff turnover led to minor misses in the new call’s detail.
- John Lang reports that 63 artist responses were received and that CaFÉ provided average scores; he additionally calculated medians and score ranges to show consensus or divergence among VAC members.
- John Lang clarifies that blank scores from one committee member were treated as null entries rather than zeros and thus did not drag down averages.
- John Lang notes that one top‑scoring artist is already contracted as a Rankstorff Park muralist and that another artist had previously been selected as an alternate but never exhibited, which staff flagged for context.
- Susi Merhar requests assurance about the weight of certain tile works, and John Lang confirms that the artist supplied weights compatible with CPA’s hanging system.
- Jesse Cupp observes that the summary table clarifying averages, medians, and score ranges is more transparent than past practice and useful for decision‑making.
- John Lang points out that, between the two lowest‑ranked finalists (Bot and Bruckner), Bot’s scores show a narrower range, suggesting more consistent committee support, though both average equally.
- VAC members consider whether to choose between the last two candidates or to carry both as alternates; John Lang notes they could carry up to four alternates if desired.
- Susi Merhar and other members express strong satisfaction with the overall quality of selected artists and request quick image reviews of Bot and Bruckner to inform alternate decisions.
- John Lang explains that historically the CPA program has featured six artists per year, each exhibiting for roughly six to seven weeks, with two to three alternates used as needed for cancellations or conflicts.
- Committee members discuss potentially shortening exhibition windows to five weeks to accommodate more artists without exceeding budget, and John Lang confirms existing CPA art funds can support additional exhibitors.
- Clifford Bryant questions why the program has always featured one artist at a time and asks whether multiple artists could exhibit simultaneously; John Lang cites hanging‑system limits, operational complexity, and staff workload as likely historical reasons, but commits to further exploration.
- Don Whitebread notes that CPA’s large walls favor big works and that even single artists must curate down their portfolio due to limited high‑impact wall space, complicating multi‑artist shows.
- Susi Merhar argues that the main value is giving more artists a platform and recommends expanding the roster to eight exhibitors if the city can also promote shows through bulletins, online channels, and other city outlets.
- Regina Sakols reads CPA’s posted gallery hours (noon–1 pm on select weekdays plus pre‑performance openings) and emphasizes that shorter runs still mirror typical gallery show lengths when adjusting for limited public hours.
- Toot Too Thomson suggests leveraging the library and citywide banners (similar to summer events and Monster Bash promotion) as additional marketing channels for CPA artists.
- Clifford Bryant notes that CPA’s electronic signboard on Castro Street already lists the current exhibiting artist, and members discuss additional digital outreach (e.g., Instagram).
- VAC members agree that, in practice, most CPA visitors focus on performances rather than art, but that the exhibit still provides a valuable résumé and promotional asset for artists, especially if artists stage receptions or staff tables during events.
- Jesse Cupp encourages exploring receptions and further collaboration with CPA staff to support artist‑led events, referencing previous successful evening receptions.
- After discussion, Regina Sakols moves that VAC select eight artists for the upcoming CPA visual arts season and name three alternates; Clifford Bryant seconds the motion, and it passes unanimously after roll call.
- John Lang commits to coordinating with CPA staff to rework the calendar for eight artists plus three alternates and to report back on logistics and scheduling.

#### Transit Center Grade Separation & Access project briefing
Public Works staff present a detailed update on the Transit Center Grade Separation and Access Project, including schedule, design elements, and specific undercrossing and concourse configurations. They emphasize that urban design guidelines call for safe, well‑lit, attractive underpasses and that there are multiple opportunities to integrate public art. VAC members respond enthusiastically, proposing themes such as lighting, living walls, and potential rotating galleries, and staff outline a path toward selecting an artist under K5 funding in coordination with the design timeline.
- Joy Houghton presents the project context, explaining that the current Castro/Moffett/Central/Caltrain at‑grade rail crossing experiences about 104 trains per day, with gate downtime expected to increase significantly with Caltrain electrification and future high‑speed rail integration.
- Joy Houghton notes that the Transit Master Plan (2017) led Council in 2018 to split implementation into two elements: the current grade separation/access project and a later redevelopment of the transit center itself, potentially including mixed‑use development and underground parking in partnership with Caltrain.
- Joy Houghton reviews milestones: completion of a mitigated negative declaration in 2019, a cooperative agreement with Caltrain and VTA for final design in 2020–2021, and the start of final design in July 2022 after Caltrain procured design services.
- Joy Houghton states that the project’s goals are to improve safety for all travel modes, reduce congestion, and support downtown pedestrianization.
- Joy Houghton outlines major elements: a new vehicle ramp from West Evelyn to Shoreline; a shared‑use path under Evelyn/Shoreline connecting to the existing multi‑use path; closure of the at‑grade Castro rail crossing with new pedestrian and bicycle undercrossings; and new pickup/drop‑off areas along Central Expressway.
- Joy Houghton presents plans for the north‑side undercrossings at Moffett (Moffett Plaza) and Adobe (Adobe Plaza), including stairs, ramps, and elevator access, and shows visualizations of the tunnels and entry areas.
- Joy Houghton shows the south‑side concourse between Central and the tracks, with ramps and stairs linking Castro Street and the undercrossings, emphasizing wide, open circulation spaces.
- Joy Houghton explains that with Council’s decision to permanently close the first 300 block of Castro to vehicles, Evelyn Avenue will be realigned into an S‑curve to improve safety and create better bike and pedestrian crossings.
- Jim Lightbody emphasizes that, due to long‑standing community concerns about underpasses, the city embedded urban design guidelines and a dedicated budget to ensure that undercrossings are safe, comfortable, and aesthetically pleasing, not just functional.
- Joy Houghton describes the urban design guidelines as supporting art and architectural integration through surface treatments, lighting, landscaping, signage/wayfinding, fixtures, and railings, with the undercrossings and concourse especially suitable for public art.
- Joy Houghton shows precedent images of art‑enhanced tunnels using tile, ceiling treatment, patterned paving, and integrated lighting to illustrate possible directions for Mountain View’s undercrossings.
- Joy Houghton presents conceptual underpass models with non‑rectangular interior forms, noting that the city is not locked into generic rectangular tunnels and can use structure geometry as part of an artistic approach.
- Joy Houghton highlights the concourse as a large area with multiple opportunities for surface treatments, landscaping, and artful railings and lighting to create a strong sense of place and inviting approaches to the tunnels.
- Joy Houghton explains that staff will hold a community meeting in January to present updates and gather feedback on architectural and design elements, then return to VAC in early 2023 with specific public art location recommendations and a proposed call for artists.
- Jim Lightbody clarifies that project design is moving toward 65% completion by mid‑year, and notes that some art options may narrow as structural decisions are finalized, so art decisions should be coordinated with that timeline.
- Jesse Cupp praises the design as bicycle‑friendly and spacious, and immediately connects undercrossing art to lighting, citing the Community Center’s backlit forest‑path glass artwork as a successful precedent for making a deep corridor feel safe and inviting.
- Regina Sakols stresses that underpass lighting is critical from a safety perspective, especially for women, and praises example images where bright, attractive lighting makes tunnels feel safer.
- Committee members discuss future development potential on the north side near Moffett, and Jim Lightbody notes the recent 100 Moffett housing development while acknowledging no other active projects yet.
- Joy Houghton confirms that this briefing is primarily informational and that specific VAC actions will be requested at a future meeting once art locations and parameters are clearer.
- Clifford Bryant asks whether VAC should begin considering themes now; staff welcome early ideas but suggest focusing more deeply once precise art locations and a call for artists are defined.
- Robert Gonzales notes that the project budget, via K5, can support a substantial art allocation (capped at $400,000) and suggests that artist stipends for concept models would be most efficient after VAC narrows finalists to a short list (e.g., two or three).
- John Lang flags that projects of this magnitude have historically used a two‑phase selection with stipends and that such an approach typically adds about two months to the selection timeline, which must be reconciled with the public works schedule.
- Joy Houghton asks whether stipends require individual contracts; John Lang confirms but notes they can use short‑form agreements appropriate to smaller dollar amounts.
- Jim Lightbody indicates that the city will likely be responsible for long‑term maintenance of the undercrossings and related art, which influences material choices and design durability.
- Jesse Cupp proposes integrating living walls or plant‑based art in the tunnels both for aesthetics and to mitigate odors common in rail underpasses, suggesting coordinated grow lighting to support plant health and enhance nighttime safety.
- Clifford Bryant references Parisian living‑wall precedents that transform building facades and endorses exploring similar treatments in Mountain View’s undercrossings or concourse areas.
- Regina Sakols and Jesse Cupp also highlight the need for high‑quality, artfully designed public restrooms and water access near the transit center as part of a comprehensive, humane design, though staff clarify that restrooms are more likely to be addressed in later transit center phases.
- Regina Sakols suggests reserving some protected, lit niches or display cases within the tunnels or concourse as a city‑run rotating gallery, while Jesse Cupp cautions that such a program would require ongoing funding and administrative capacity beyond this one‑time CIP art budget.
- Robert Gonzales reminds VAC that future steps will include a more detailed map of recommended art locations and a call for artists in which the committee may embed thematic guidance (e.g., safety, nature, light).
- Bruce England publicly supports the concept of a living wall in the undercrossing and indicates that Green Spaces Mountain View would likely advocate for it, offering to coordinate with staff offline on ideas.

#### Artist stipend thresholds and two-phase selection
Building on prior discussions, staff present a short memo summarizing historical use of artist stipends in large public art commissions and ask whether VAC wants to formalize a trigger or default policy. Committee members generally favor using stipends on higher‑budget projects to obtain more detailed proposals, but they also want flexibility and closer integration with how calls are written and with project schedules. The item concludes with direction for staff to refine an approach in coordination with Public Works and return with a more holistic proposal.
- John Lang reviews a memo showing that past VACs used stipends for three major projects: Fire Station #5 (2009), the Community Center remodel, and the Rengstorff Aquatic Center, all under the former 1% K5 policy.
- John Lang notes that at Fire Station #5, with a $67,000 art budget for one location, VAC provided a $500 stipend for refined design; for the Community Center, with a $140,000 art budget across four locations, VAC offered $5,000; and for the Rengstorff Aquatic Center, with a $180,000 art budget, VAC offered $1,000.
- John Lang observes that historically stipends were used when a single art component exceeded roughly $60,000 in value, suggesting a rough threshold where more detailed proposals were deemed necessary.
- John Lang explains that a stipend phase typically adds about two months to a project’s selection timeline, including time to short‑list artists, execute small contracts, and have them develop refined concepts for VAC review.
- Susi Merhar expresses discomfort deciding on a stipend trigger in isolation from broader process changes, arguing that stipend use is intertwined with what is asked in the initial call for artists and the type of portfolios received.
- Susi Merhar points out that some artists’ prior work (e.g., Fayette Park’s metal animal sculptor) makes their proposed approach obvious, while others (e.g., painters applying to park projects) require more information to understand what they would do, and that this affects whether a stipend is necessary.
- Don Whitebread suggests that a dollar threshold, such as $60,000, could serve as a trigger for discussion rather than an automatic requirement, preserving VAC’s flexibility to decide when a stipend is appropriate.
- Clifford Bryant argues that by default VAC should expect to use stipends for larger projects, both because K5 now provides 2% for art and because past experience shows stipends improve the quality of final proposals, but he agrees there should always be an option to waive stipends in clear‑cut cases.
- Regina Sakols and others emphasize that artists should not be asked to do substantial additional design work without compensation, aligning with best practices for fair treatment of creative labor.
- Susi Merhar and Regina Sakols recommend that future calls for artists explicitly ask applicants to describe, in words, what they would do at a site (e.g., mural, bench, painted pavement), to help VAC decide who to invite into a stipend‑funded concept phase.
- Don Whitebread notes that a stipend phase offers newer or less established artists a chance to compete with experienced public artists by demonstrating strong ideas through paid concept development, not just past portfolios.
- John Lang cautions that making stipends the default on all projects above a given value would standardize a three‑month selection process that might not align with all capital project schedules, and he commits to reconciling VAC’s preferences with Public Works’ timing constraints.
- Jesse Cupp proposes using $60,000 as a guideline threshold where VAC will normally consider a stipend phase, with the understanding that a majority of members can still decide not to use stipends when prior work or project clarity makes them unnecessary.
- John Lang agrees to consult with Public Works on scheduling implications and to return with a refined proposal that respects both timing and fair‑compensation objectives, possibly alongside broader discussions about artist engagement and call design.
- VAC members agree informally that any future stipend thresholds should be inflation‑adjusted in parallel with K5 policy updates, though they do not take a formal vote at this meeting.
- John Lang indicates he will draft an approach incorporating these principles and bring it back at a later meeting for possible formal adoption.
