### Overview
  The Visual Arts Committee meeting covered planning and process items for upcoming public art work and project-specific updates. Staff clarified that the City’s biodiversity strategy is exploring how art can integrate with native plantings and habitat restoration and agreed to return with more detailed opportunities; committee members asked for clearer artist touchpoints and original call materials and raised platform issues with the CAFÉ submission system, requesting a future agenda item to evaluate alternatives. Staff reported that insurance documents for the Reinstorff Park maintenance project now permit execution of artist agreements, and the committee reviewed the Center for the Performing Arts call (63 submissions), accepted staff scoring methods, and unanimously voted to advance the top eight artists with three alternates while directing staff to work with the CPA on a revised schedule and increased promotion. Public Works presented the Transit Center Grade Separation project (final design target July 2024; construction expected July 2024–December 2026), highlighted multiple undercrossing and concourse opportunities for integrated public art, and committed to return early next year with site-specific recommendations and a call for artists; committee members emphasized lighting, living walls, rotating display concepts, and maintenance considerations. The committee discussed a stipend policy memo and historical precedent and endorsed a trigger-based approach (discussion trigger near $60,000, with stipends typically $2,000–$5,000) while asking staff to coordinate timing and contracting with Public Works to avoid schedule delays. Staff will follow up on CPA scheduling, Transit Center art locations and calls, the stipend policy wording and logistics, evaluation of submission platforms, and a youth art display agenda item.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Placemaking and biodiversity
  Committee members reviewed upcoming agenda items around placemaking and the City’s biodiversity strategy and discussed how art could be incorporated into landscaping and habitat projects. Staff clarifies the biodiversity strategy focuses on native plantings and habitat restoration and that art could be integrated into those efforts as the strategy moves from research into implementation. The committee asked staff to return with clearer proposals and to fold this into future agenda items as the biodiversity strategy develops.
- John Lang: The biodiversity strategy is focused on native plantings and habitat restoration and staff will bring placemaking/art integration back to the VAC as the strategy advances.
- Susi Merhar: Committee members need clearer expectations and scheduled touchpoints with artists so submissions, calls, and follow-up reviews are consistent.
- Regina Sakols: Committee members should receive the original call-for-art materials, artist statements, and the CAFÉ platform package for future juries.
- Clifford Bryant: I recommend reviewing a Palo Alto example on Charleston between Middlefield and Central as a model of successful art-landscaping integration.
  #### Artist application platform
  Members evaluated the CAFÉ submission platform and discussed alternatives to improve artist response rates and clarity in applications. Several members reported artist complaints about CAFÉ’s repetitiveness and accessibility and suggested exploring SlideRoom, Google Forms, or accepting multiple platforms as a pilot. Staff agreed to schedule a dedicated future discussion about application platforms and process improvements.
- Susi Merhar: Many artists find the CAFÉ platform difficult and inconsistent in how it captures required information and we should discuss ways to improve the call-for-art process.
- Jesse Cupp: Artists report CAFÉ is the dominant platform but other platforms such as Google Forms or SlideRoom may be usable and worth piloting.
- John Lang: Staff will bring a future agenda item to discuss the submission platform and potential alternatives or pilots.
  #### Youth artist displays
  A committee member proposed piloting a program to showcase work by young artists in public venues. Members discussed existing options (City Hall, library, CSMA rotating displays) and the need for a venue and coordination with schools or CSMA to restart youth exhibits. Staff agreed to add the item to a future agenda for formal consideration and next steps.
- Toot Too Thomson: I propose piloting a call for young artists so schools and students can exhibit in city venues.
- Regina Sakols: City Hall and the library historically hosted youth displays and this could be reinstated or extended to shop windows and other public sites.
- John Lang: Staff will add the youth artist display idea to a future agenda for further discussion.

John Lang reported that updated insurance documents have arrived for the Reinstorff Park maintenance building, enabling staff to execute artist agreements and advance planned mural work.   #### CPA artist selection
  Staff presented results of the Center for the Performing Arts (CPA) call for artists—63 submissions—and explained scoring, median/average computations, and score ranges. John Lang clarified that blank juror scores are treated as null (excluded from averages) rather than zeros, then reviewed the top candidates and logistics (hanging system weight constraints and schedule). The VAC voted to advance the top eight artists as primary selections and designate three alternates to accommodate scheduling conflicts; staff will work with the CPA to finalize scheduling and contracts.
- John Lang: The CPA call received 63 responses and staff calculated medians and averages to show score ranges and reviewer consistency.
- John Lang: Blank scores are treated as null fields and do not lower an artwork’s average score.
- Toot Too Thomson: I only scored some artists and wanted clarification that leaving entries blank does not penalize those entries.
- John Lang: The CPA hanging system has weight limits and staff confirmed several top artists’ pieces meet those limits.
- Regina Sakols: Committee members requested that staff provide original call text and artist statements to better compare submissions with the call.
- Regina Sakols: I move to select eight primary artists and three alternates for the 2023–2024 CPA season.
- Clifford Bryant: I second the motion.
- John Lang: The motion to select eight artists with three alternates passed unanimously by roll call.
- Committee: Members discussed shortening exhibition windows from the historical six-to-seven weeks to accommodate more artists and recommended increased promotion and reception support to boost visibility.
  #### Transit center art opportunities
  Public Works presented the Transit Center Grade Separation and Access project, described major elements (vehicle ramp, shared-use path, undercrossings, concourse), and outlined project schedule and design responsibility. Staff stressed urban design guidelines and multiple opportunities to integrate art (walls, floors, ceilings, lighting, railings, wayfinding, concourse treatments) and committed to return early next year with specific public art location recommendations and to initiate a call for artists. Committee members and public commenters raised ideas for lighting, living walls, rotating display cases, public restrooms, and long-term maintenance responsibilities.
- Joy Houghton: The project is in final design with final design targeted for July 2024 and construction anticipated July 2024 through December 2026.
- Joy Houghton: Major project elements include a vehicle ramp from West Evelyn to Shoreline, a shared-use path, undercrossings with vertical circulation, a concourse area, and new pick-up/drop-off locations.
- Jim Lightbody: The project developed Urban Design Guidelines to guide architecture and public-art integration and staff will seek community input as the design progresses.
- Joy Houghton: Staff will finalize public art location recommendations and return to the VAC early next year to initiate the call for artists.
- Robert Gonzales: Policy K5 applies and the visual arts component could be sizable; the policy cap is approximately $400,000 for this project.
- Robert Gonzales: If the VAC wants model proposals the typical stipend historically ranges from $2,000 to $5,000 per shortlisted artist.
- Jim Lightbody: As design advances to 65% some options may be constrained, so coordination timing matters for proposals that require structural or electrical accommodations.

- Regina Sakols: Lighting and safe design are essential because undercrossings can feel unsafe, especially for women.
- Bruce England (public): Green Spaces Mountain View supports living walls and attractive restroom design, and I will follow up offline about those features.
- John Lang: Staff will coordinate timing and stipend logistics with Public Works to avoid delaying the project while still enabling meaningful artist design work.
  #### Public inquiries guidance

Members agreed to follow that direction.

  #### Stipend threshold policy
  Staff presented a one-page memo summarizing historical use of artist stipends and options for a two-phase RFP that pays shortlisted artists to develop refined concepts. The memo documents prior stipend examples (Fire Station #5, Community Center, and Reinstorff Aquatic Center) and indicates stipends historically ranged from $500 to $5,000 depending on project scale; committee members favored a policy trigger around $60,000 that prompts a stipend discussion rather than an automatic rule. Staff will consult Public Works about timing and contract logistics and return with a proposed approach for VAC review.
- John Lang: Historical precedent shows the VAC has provided stipends when individual art pieces exceeded roughly $60,000, with past stipend amounts ranging from $500 to $5,000.
- John Lang: Typical stipends to develop refined concepts are in the $2,000–$5,000 range and the two-phase approach generally adds approximately two months to the selection timeline.
- Susi Merhar: Stipend need depends on how clear a submission already is, and a more holistic RFP approach should be considered before deciding fixed rules.
- Clifford Bryant: I prefer making stipend the default for larger projects and allowing the VAC to waive it when unnecessary.
- Jesse Cupp: Committee members generally support triggering a stipend discussion at $60,000 and applying an inflation adjustment consistent with the K5 policy schedule.
- John Lang: Staff will consult Public Works about contracting timelines and return with recommended procedures to avoid delaying large CIP projects.
