### Overview
The briefing, led by Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and National Security Council spokesperson Admiral John Kirby, covers a broad mix of foreign and domestic issues, framed by an opening condemnation of the mass shooting at Charles University in Prague and a year-end review of Biden administration achievements. Jean-Pierre highlights progress in lowering prescription drug and insulin costs through the Inflation Reduction Act, expanding ACA coverage, defending reproductive rights via executive actions, strengthening the labor market with over 14 million new jobs and record business formation, cracking down on junk fees, and advancing a historically ambitious climate agenda that has attracted more than $600 billion in clean energy and manufacturing investment. Kirby focuses heavily on foreign policy, detailing Biden’s call with Mexico’s President López Obrador on managing unprecedented migration flows and announcing an upcoming visit by senior U.S. officials to Mexico, while also updating reporters on humanitarian aid flows into Gaza, acknowledging severe hunger and logistical bottlenecks, and explaining U.S. efforts to encourage Israel’s eventual transition to lower-intensity operations without dictating timelines. He addresses criticism from Democratic lawmakers by stressing Biden’s value-based approach that balances Israel’s security with Palestinian humanitarian needs and hostages’ release, confirms ongoing but sensitive negotiations for another Gaza pause, and defends U.S. diplomacy at the U.N. over a forthcoming Security Council resolution. Kirby also outlines the administration’s rationale for a recent detainee swap with Venezuela and denied torture claims, underscores the need for new congressional funding for both Ukraine and border security, and cites revitalized alliances—from AUKUS and the Quad to coalitions backing Ukraine and Israel—as Biden’s signature foreign policy achievement. Throughout, Jean-Pierre fields domestic questions on border surges and immigration negotiations, insisting Biden has maximized his unilateral authority and now needs bipartisan congressional action on both funding and policy, while also defending his economic record amid lingering public skepticism, commenting on issues from RSV vaccine supply to China tariffs and Angola’s exit from OPEC, and closing by promising further scheduling details and wishing the press corps happy holidays.

#### Humanitarian aid and conditions in Gaza
Kirby provides updates on aid deliveries to Gaza and fields questions on the humanitarian crisis. He notes a recent 24-hour period with nearly 200 trucks entering via Kerem Shalom and Rafah but acknowledges these flows remain insufficient given severe hunger and health needs, including winter hardships. He does not dispute U.N. assessments that one in four civilians is starving and describes multiple daily bottlenecks tied to conflict conditions, inspections, UN distribution capacity, and aid availability. He emphasizes U.S. leadership in pushing humanitarian access while maintaining Israel’s central role in the inspection regime and reiterates that the administration seeks to alleviate suffering without dictating operational details in public.
- Mr. Kirby reports that in the previous 24 hours about 120 trucks entered Gaza through Kerem Shalom and over 71 through Rafah, nearly 200 total.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. works daily to increase the flow of aid and ensure it reaches those most in need inside Gaza.
- Mr. Kirby states the U.S. is not in a position to dispute U.N. estimates that one in four civilians in Gaza is starving and agrees there is immense suffering, including hunger, health needs, and exposure to harsh winter weather.
- Mr. Kirby explains there is no single bottleneck for aid; obstacles vary daily and include active fighting, inspection capacity and processes, UN distribution challenges, and aid and truck availability.
- Mr. Kirby notes Israel has a pivotal and understandable role in the inspection regime for goods entering Gaza and declines to negotiate U.N. resolution language from the podium.
- Mr. Kirby says Americans should recognize how hard President Biden and his team work to lead global efforts to alleviate suffering in Gaza and notes the U.S. leads the world in humanitarian assistance there.

He says Israeli officials acknowledge the need to transition and will decide on timing and tactics themselves. The administration believes a near-term move to lower-intensity operations is the best outcome but insists it will not dictate terms or timelines and instead focuses on advising and posing tough questions to Israeli counterparts.
- Mr. Kirby corrects a reporter and says the U.S. has not said it is pushing Israel to end high-intensity operations, though it discusses transitions.
- Mr. Kirby states that Israeli leaders themselves recognize they must transition from higher-intensity operations to lower-intensity operations at some point.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that Israel will decide when to transition and what lower intensity looks like, and the U.S. is not dictating terms or timelines.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Secretary Austin, Secretary Blinken, and National Security Advisor Sullivan have all consulted Israelis on lessons from past U.S. transitions and have asked them tough questions.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. believes a transition in the near future is the best possible outcome, provided Israel can do it safely and effectively.
