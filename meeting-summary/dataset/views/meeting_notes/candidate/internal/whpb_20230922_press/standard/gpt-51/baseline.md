### Overview
The briefing centers on President Biden’s decision to create the first White House Office of Gun Violence Prevention, which Karine Jean-Pierre says will be overseen by Vice President Harris and led by Stefanie Feldman to accelerate implementation of existing gun laws and executive actions; Representative Lucy McBath grounds the announcement in her experience losing her son to gun violence and argues the office will save lives across all communities. Jean-Pierre also highlights a bipartisan law Biden is signing to break up the organ transplant system monopoly and modernize services for more than 100,000 people on waiting lists, and previews an upcoming Pacific Islands leaders summit focused on climate, growth, and development. Throughout the briefing she repeatedly blames House Republicans for steering toward a “Republican shutdown” despite a bipartisan budget deal, outlines early OMB contingency planning for potential impacts on federal pay, food safety, and student loan servicing, and resists calls for Biden to renegotiate, insisting it is up to Speaker McCarthy’s conference to honor their agreement. Reporters press her on several active issues—Senator Menendez’s indictment (on which she declines comment), the expanding UAW strike (where she stresses Biden’s pro-union stance, ongoing talks led by Julie Su and Gene Sperling, and encouragement that negotiations continue), high border crossings (where she cites removals, surging CBP resources, and Republican opposition to funding), and foreign policy questions including Ukraine aid and ATACMS, allegations against India in a Canadian killing, and prospects for Israel–Saudi normalization and a possible Biden–Xi meeting—with Jean-Pierre emphasizing confidentiality of diplomacy while reaffirming strong U.S. support for Ukraine and continued engagement with allies and partners.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Creation of gun violence prevention office
Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre announces that President Biden will establish the first-ever White House Office of Gun Violence Prevention, building on the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act and prior executive actions. She explains that Vice President Harris will oversee the office, with Stefanie Feldman as director and advocates Greg Jackson and Rob Wilcox as deputy directors. The administration frames this as a response to the pleas of gun violence survivors who ask leaders to “do something.” The office’s mandate is to coordinate and accelerate implementation of existing gun safety laws and policies and to signal elevated, sustained White House focus on the gun violence epidemic.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that President Biden will announce the establishment of the first-ever White House Office of Gun Violence Prevention to build on prior legislative and executive actions.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre explains that Vice President Harris will oversee the office, with Stefanie Feldman as director and Greg Jackson and Rob Wilcox as deputy directors.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre characterizes the new office as a response to survivors’ repeated message to the President to “do something” about gun violence.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre later adds that placing this work in a standalone office, separate from the Domestic Policy Council, is meant to accelerate implementation of gun-related executive actions and laws and demonstrate heightened commitment to saving lives.
#### Personal impact of gun violence and office goals
Representative Lucy McBath speaks as a mother whose son Jordan was murdered by gunfire in 2012, describing the lifelong losses that followed. She underscores that over 100 Americans are shot and killed daily and that gun violence now affects suburban, urban, and rural communities alike, including traumatized schoolchildren. McBath argues that President Biden understands grief and is taking decisive action by creating the new office, which will focus on coordination and proper implementation of existing gun safety laws. She asserts that this structural step marks a new era in efforts to keep Americans safe and will save lives if effectively executed.
- Representative McBath recounts that her son Jordan was killed when a man fired 10 rounds into a car at a gas station, ending all the milestones she anticipated for him.
- Representative McBath notes that more than 100 people are shot and killed every day in the United States and that gun violence has no geographic boundaries.
- Representative McBath stresses that children are traumatized by lockdown drills designed to shield them from potential gunfire at school.
- Representative McBath says the new Office of Gun Violence Prevention will increase coordination between states and ensure proper implementation of gun safety legislation Congress has already passed.
- Representative McBath concludes that President Biden’s actions creating the office will save lives.

The White House position is that Republicans should honor the bipartisan budget agreement reached in May rather than pursue what it calls a “Republican shutdown.” The administration frames the situation as unnecessary chaos that risks economic harm and public safety and insists responsibility lies with House Republicans, not the President or Democrats.

#### Organ transplant system reform legislation
Jean-Pierre notes that President Biden will sign a bipartisan law to reform the U.S. organ transplant system, which currently has a longstanding monopoly arrangement and is widely criticized as broken. She explains that the law will introduce competition for organ procurement and allocation contracts, remove funding caps, and modernize operations. The administration argues this will create a more transparent, accountable, and efficient system for more than 100,000 people on transplant waiting lists. The measure is presented as another example of bipartisan action aimed at saving lives by fixing a critical health infrastructure function.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the President is signing a bipartisan law to make the organ transplant system work better for over 100,000 people on waiting lists.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre describes the current organ transplant system as broken for years with heartbreaking consequences.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre explains that the new law will break up the current monopoly and allow HHS to contract with the best entities through competition.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that the law eliminates a funding cap so that more resources can be used to modernize the system and improve transparency and accountability.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that these reforms will save lives by expanding access to needed organ transplants.

#### Border crossings and immigration policy debate
Fox News’s Peter Doocy presses Jean-Pierre on high daily migrant crossings and challenges previous White House claims about “stopping the flow” at the border, citing reports of 10,000 crossings in a single day. Jean-Pierre responds by emphasizing that Biden proposed comprehensive immigration reform on day one and has implemented new enforcement policies, including removing more than 250,000 individuals lacking legal basis to remain since May 12. She argues the system is long broken, blames the prior administration for gutting immigration processes, and criticizes House Republicans for opposing additional border resources and trying to cut DHS and CBP funding. The administration maintains that it is imposing consequences and surging resources, including at Eagle Pass, but insists durable solutions require congressional action that Republicans have thus far blocked.
- Mr. Doocy asks what the administration calls a day when 10,000 people cross the border illegally and questions whether this constitutes “stopping the flow.”
- Ms. Jean-Pierre replies that Biden proposed comprehensive immigration reform on his first day in office and that Congress has failed to act on a broken system.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that since May 12 the administration has removed more than 250,000 individuals who lacked a legal basis to remain in the U.S.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre argues that House Republicans opposed record border funding sought by Biden, which would have enabled hiring about 25,000 CBP agents and enhanced interdiction of drugs including cocaine, fentanyl, heroin, and methamphetamine.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that in Eagle Pass CBP surged resources, vetted and processed more than 2,500 individuals into custody, and cleared areas where migrants had gathered, while Republicans are simultaneously trying to cut DHS resources.

The administration sees the current moment as ripe to prioritize centralized coordination to reduce gun violence, while acknowledging that further congressional action is still needed. The office is presented as a structural tool to deepen and speed work already underway rather than a substitute for legislative reform.
