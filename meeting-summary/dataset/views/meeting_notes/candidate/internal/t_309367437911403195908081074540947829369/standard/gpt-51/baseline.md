### Overview
The Council Policy and Procedures Committee convenes remotely under emergency provisions to approve prior minutes, hear public comments, and consider policy changes related to advisory body governance and the committee work plan. After approving the September 28, 2022 minutes, the committee hears from Mr. England, who seeks clearer paths for resolving issues he previously raised and is advised by Chair Matichak to submit them by email to the City Council for staff follow‑up. The core agenda item is a set of amendments to the Council Code of Conduct and Council Policy K‑2, presented by City Clerk Glaser, creating a consequence for advisory body members who fail to file required Form 700 disclosures by suspending their participation and counting missed meetings as unexcused, along with minor policy clean‑up edits; Committee Members Ramirez and Matichak support the changes and additional flexibility in application timelines, while Committee Member Hicks voices concern about potential impacts on the Development Review Committee but ultimately supports the amendments after City Manager McCarthy clarifies that Form 700 obligations come from state law and already apply to the DRC. The committee then reviews its work plan, with staff noting completed items and upcoming work on Council Policies A‑2 and A‑15, and hears Ramirez’s request for a more transparent process for developers seeking to use public land, which McCarthy agrees to address initially via an off‑agenda memo describing current practices. Mr. England uses the work plan discussion to highlight inconsistencies between the Advisory Body Handbook and Council policy on residency requirements for advisory body members and is again directed to email Council so the City Clerk can respond, after which the committee concludes with no further comments and adjourns at 4:17 p.m.

#### Form 700 non‑filing consequences and policy cleanup
The committee considers amendments to the Council Code of Conduct and Council Policy K-2 to create explicit consequences when advisory body members fail to file required Form 700 financial disclosures, along with several minor clean‑up edits. City Clerk Glaser explains that the referral dates back to August 2021 and that staff propose suspending members from participating in their advisory body until they file, with missed meetings counted as unexcused absences. Committee members explore broader implications, including application deadlines for advisory bodies, how absences are classified as excused or unexcused, and potential impacts on the Development Review Committee (DRC), which faces recruitment challenges due to Form 700 requirements. After discussion and clarification that Form 700 obligations flow from state law rather than city policy, the committee supports the staff recommendation and incorporates additional procedural flexibility on application timelines.
- City Clerk Glaser explains that the item was referred in August 2021 to establish recourse when an advisory body member fails to file a Form 700, and staff propose suspending participation until a proper filing is made and treating missed meetings as unexcused absences.
- City Clerk Glaser notes that beyond the Form 700 enforcement language, the item also includes a variety of minor, non‑substantive changes to clean up the policy text.
- Committee Member Ramirez states that he finds nothing controversial in the proposed changes but wants to examine advisory body application deadlines and the handling of excused versus unexcused absences.
- City Clerk Glaser recommends against tying application deadlines to agenda publication because staff need lead time to compile and publish materials.
- City Clerk Glaser proposes adding language to Council Policy K‑2 stating that application deadlines will be set as near as practicable to the advisory body interview date while preserving enough time for staff to assemble packets.
- City Clerk Glaser notes that historically, application deadlines have coincided with the initial advertisement of vacancies, but staff have already adjusted upcoming advertisements for the Public Safety Advisory Board and Rental Housing Committee to allow applications later in the cycle.
- City Clerk Glaser emphasizes that a clear application deadline is needed to trigger the existing policy that allows incumbents in their second term to reapply if there are insufficient qualified applicants.
- City Clerk Glaser clarifies that current policy assigns advisory bodies the authority, by general consent or majority vote, to determine whether an absence is excused or unexcused, based on a recommendation from the chair in consultation with the staff liaison.
- City Clerk Glaser confirms that the city charter, not the policy, governs councilmember absences and vests the decision to excuse absences in the mayor.
- Committee Member Hicks asks whether staff are experiencing difficulty obtaining Form 700 filings from advisory body members and whether this enforcement mechanism is needed for compliance leverage.
- City Clerk Glaser responds that there have been some difficulties in securing compliance but not a pervasive problem, although staff have had to emphasize the importance of filing to some members.
- Chair Matichak notes her understanding that the City Clerk’s office does extensive outreach to educate filers and resolve issues before they become enforcement problems with the Fair Political Practices Commission.
- City Manager McCarthy identifies the Development Review Committee as a body where Form 700 requirements may create future challenges, because its architect members function more like consultants and may be reluctant to file.
- City Manager McCarthy states that staff are working with the Community Development Director on options for changing how the DRC operates, and flags that Form 700 requirements could affect the city’s ability to recruit DRC members.
- Committee Member Hicks expresses concern that stringent disclosure requirements may make it harder to staff the DRC, which she views as an important body for project review, and asks whether alternatives such as non‑architect members or consultant structures are being considered.
- City Manager McCarthy indicates that shifting the DRC to a consultant model is under discussion but cautions that consultants may still have to file Form 700s under state law.
- Committee Member Hicks initially signals she may not support the proposed policy change until the DRC implications are resolved, citing fears of weakening or losing the DRC.
- City Clerk Glaser confirms that the DRC is an ordinance‑created advisory body subject to the Brown Act and financial disclosure requirements due to the significance of its decisions and recommendations.
- Committee Member Ramirez argues that Form 700 compliance is critical to reveal financial conflicts of interest, especially for DRC members whose recommendations the Council often accepts, and says he has little sympathy for people unwilling to file required disclosures.
- Committee Member Ramirez states that if he refused to file a Form 700 he should not be on the City Council, and applies the same standard to advisory body members.
- Chair Matichak expresses support for suspending non‑filing advisory body members from participation until they file, and thanks staff for the additional minor policy clean‑up edits.
- Chair Matichak outlines proposed edits to Council Policy K‑2 procedures to remove specific month counts (five, four, and two‑to‑three months) and replace them with a more flexible timeline.
- City Clerk Glaser recommends removing explicit month references in Procedures 1A, 1B, and 1C and adding language in 1B that application deadlines be set as near as practicable to the interview date, while preserving time to prepare packets and apply the incumbent‑reapplication rule.
- Committee Member Ramirez restates his understanding that the revisions are intended to give the City Clerk’s office flexibility to set reasonable deadlines that align closely with interview dates, and indicates he is comfortable with this approach.
- Committee Member Ramirez moves the staff recommendation, including the new language on application deadlines and removal of fixed month references, to amend the Council Code of Conduct and Council Policy K‑2.
- Chair Matichak seconds the motion to adopt the Form 700 enforcement mechanism and related policy changes.
- City Manager McCarthy confirms that Form 700 filing requirements are mandated by state law and City Attorney guidance, not by the local policy, and that DRC members must file regardless of whether the committee adopts the proposed amendments.
- City Manager McCarthy explains that some potential DRC members may resist Form 700 filing for personal privacy reasons rather than any improper conduct.
- Upon learning that the Form 700 obligation exists independent of the local policy, Committee Member Hicks changes her position and states she will vote for the amendments despite remaining concerns about DRC recruitment.
- The committee approves the motion by roll‑call vote, with Committee Member Hicks, Committee Member Ramirez, and Chair Matichak all voting yes.
#### Work plan status and future policy topics
Staff provide a brief update on the Council Policy and Procedures Committee work plan, noting that several items are already completed and two policy updates—Council Policy A‑2 (finance‑related cleanup) and Council Policy A‑15 (Shoreline ticket policy modifications)—will carry into 2023. Committee Member Ramirez uses the discussion to raise a new concern about the lack of a transparent process for private developers seeking to incorporate public land into their projects. The committee and City Manager discuss whether this topic belongs with CPPC or in another forum, agree that an initial informational memo outlining current practice would help, and hear additional public comment urging clearer guidance on advisory body residency rules and handbook updates.
- Assistant City Clerk Thomas reports that many 2022 work plan items have been completed and that upcoming work includes cleanup to Council Policy A‑2 in coordination with the finance team and further modifications to Council Policy A‑15 on the Shoreline ticket policy in collaboration with Community Services.
- Committee Member Ramirez asks if this is an appropriate time to propose additional work plan items and then raises the absence of clear policy governing private developers’ use of public land within their development proposals.
- Committee Member Ramirez cites recent projects, such as 333 Team Way and 70 Castro, where developers sought to use public property and notes that some matters went to the Council in closed session while others followed different paths, creating uncertainty about process.
- Committee Member Ramirez suggests that having a public, transparent framework would help the public, council, and development community understand when and how developers can request use of city land and when such requests must go to the Council.
- City Manager McCarthy cautions that CPPC typically handles administrative and procedural council policies, while land use and development rules may be better addressed through other vehicles or processes.
- City Manager McCarthy proposes that staff first prepare an off‑agenda memo explaining current internal practices for evaluating developer requests to use public land, including when matters are taken to Council, so that the Council can decide later whether to formalize a policy or assign further work.
- Chair Matichak agrees that the topic is important for transparency and supports receiving a memo as a first step before deciding if it belongs on the CPPC work plan or elsewhere.
- Committee Member Hicks supports examining the issue but expresses concern about overloading the Council’s broader work plan and notes that assigning some mid‑scale topics to committees can help manage Council’s workload.
- Member of the public Mr. England uses this item to highlight the Advisory Body Handbook and notes that while the current version from late 2021 includes the Public Safety Advisory Board, it is unclear and inconsistent about whether non‑residents can serve on various advisory bodies.
- Mr. England explains that the Advisory Body Handbook allows one non‑resident member on the Performing Arts Committee, Senior Advisory Committee, and Visual Arts Committee while other bodies are silent, and contrasts this with Council Policy K‑2, which generally requires Mountain View residency.
- Mr. England describes a prior situation in which a PSAB member who is a student living outside Mountain View but maintaining residence with parents in the city raised questions about eligibility, and notes the outcome was resolved but underscored the need for clearer codification.
- Chair Matichak reiterates her earlier advice that Mr. England should email his concerns about the Advisory Body Handbook and residency rules to the City Council so that the City Clerk can respond and determine appropriate follow‑up.
- City Manager McCarthy confirms that staff will prepare an off‑agenda memo describing how staff currently handle developer requests to use public property, including decision criteria and when issues are brought to Council.
