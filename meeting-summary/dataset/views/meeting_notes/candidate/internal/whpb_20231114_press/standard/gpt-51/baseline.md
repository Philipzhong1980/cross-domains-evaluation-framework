### Overview
The briefing, led by Karine Jean-Pierre with extensive remarks from National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan, covers the administration’s domestic infrastructure progress, high-stakes foreign policy agenda, and urgent budget debates. Jean-Pierre opens by highlighting two years of implementation of the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law as a pillar of Bidenomics, citing tens of thousands of projects, expanded affordable internet, clean transit investments, and over $600 billion in related private-sector commitments. Sullivan then sets the stage for President Biden’s week of diplomacy, previewing the elevation of U.S.–Indonesia ties to a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership, a consequential summit with China’s Xi Jinping focused on restoring communication channels, managing strategic competition, and exploring cooperation on fentanyl and AI, as well as broader APEC economic initiatives to advance IPEF, mobilize infrastructure investment, and attract high-tech jobs to the United States. On the Middle East, Sullivan stresses that securing the release of hostages held by Hamas—amid limited visibility into their condition—is a top priority that drives intensive talks via Qatar, while the U.S. simultaneously pushes Israel for humanitarian pauses, protection of hospitals, and civilian safety in Gaza, yet maintains strong support for Israel’s right to defend itself against Hamas. He fields pointed questions on hospital combat, laws-of-war compliance, Iranian proxy attacks and U.S. deterrence strikes, humanitarian pauses, and China’s role in restraining Iran, while reaffirming the need for supplemental funding for Ukraine, Israel, the Indo-Pacific, and border security. Jean-Pierre later addresses domestic issues, including rising crime in Washington, D.C., the Supreme Court’s new Code of Ethics, Meta’s AI political ad policy, and internal Democratic disagreements over Gaza, consistently pointing to prior legislation like the American Rescue Plan and the Safer America Plan as evidence of Biden’s efforts while criticizing House Republicans’ ‘unserious’ laddered continuing resolution and warning of shutdown risks. Throughout, both officials emphasize leader-to-leader diplomacy (with Xi and Widodo), careful handling of sensitive intelligence, and a commitment to uphold U.S. national security and economic leadership amidst overlapping global crises and contentious domestic politics.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Bipartisan Infrastructure Law progress
Karine Jean-Pierre marks the two-year anniversary of President Biden signing the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law and outlines its implementation. She stresses that the law underpins the administration’s domestic economic agenda, particularly Bidenomics, by driving large-scale public works and private investment. The current state is that thousands of projects are underway and major connectivity, clean energy, and resilience goals are in progress but ongoing.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law has enabled the launch of 40,000 infrastructure projects across 4,500 communities nationwide.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that more than 21 million low-income households now have access to free or discounted high-speed internet through the Affordable Connectivity Program.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre explains that the law has supported improvements on hundreds of thousands of miles of roads, thousands of bridge repairs, and the purchase of over 3,000 clean transit buses and 2,400 clean school buses.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reports that 2,300 resilience projects have been launched to help communities face climate impacts and cyberattacks.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre highlights that the broader Investing in America agenda has catalyzed more than $614 billion in private-sector manufacturing and clean energy investments, contributing to record construction-sector employment.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre frames these efforts as central to Bidenomics, which she defines as investing in America and workers to grow the economy from the middle out and bottom up.
#### Elevating U.S.-Indonesia partnership
Jake Sullivan previews President Biden’s bilateral meeting with Indonesian President Joko Widodo and the decision to upgrade the relationship. He emphasizes that moving to a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership signals deeper economic, energy, and security ties at a time of regional flux. The status is that leaders will formally announce the elevation and pursue cooperation on clean energy, critical minerals, and defense while acknowledging differing views on Gaza.
- Mr. Sullivan announces that the United States and Indonesia will elevate their bilateral relationship to a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership as they approach 75 years of diplomatic relations.
- Mr. Sullivan says Presidents Biden and Widodo will discuss the clean energy transition, critical minerals, and deepening security and defense cooperation.
- Mr. Sullivan later reiterates that Biden and Widodo will also exchange views on the Gaza conflict, where their positions differ, while advancing tangible cooperation on critical minerals, clean energy, and investment.
#### Biden–Xi summit objectives and context
Sullivan lays out the goals and framing for President Biden’s upcoming summit with Chinese President Xi Jinping in the Bay Area. He stresses the need to manage a competitive but potentially volatile U.S.-China relationship through leader-level engagement, focusing on communication channels, guardrails, and selective cooperation. The administration positions Biden as entering the meeting from a point of economic strength and improved alliances, seeking durable mechanisms rather than a one-off encounter.
- Mr. Sullivan says President Biden and President Xi will discuss fundamental elements of the U.S.–PRC relationship, including strengthening open lines of communication and managing competition responsibly so it does not veer into conflict.
- Mr. Sullivan describes recent high-level exchanges—including his three meetings with Wang Yi and cabinet-level visits—to show that both sides are investing in ‘intense diplomacy.’
- Mr. Sullivan argues that Biden comes into the summit on solid footing, citing strong U.S. economic recovery, low inflation relative to peers, creation of 14 million jobs, and 21 straight months of sub-4 percent unemployment.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Biden has deepened alliances through AUKUS, a trilateral summit with Japan and South Korea at Camp David, upgraded relations with Vietnam, and multiple Pacific Islands and U.S.-ASEAN summits.
- Mr. Sullivan identifies specific potential outcomes, including cooperation on illicit fentanyl and reestablishing military-to-military communications, as well as discussions on Ukraine and the Middle East.
- Mr. Sullivan underscores that Biden sees no substitute for face-to-face diplomacy with Xi to manage a complex relationship and that the summit is meant to set parameters for responsible competition over time, not just a tactical reset.
- Mr. Sullivan declines to preview specific lines Biden will use with Xi on topics like Taiwan elections and the spy balloon, emphasizing that Biden will address them directly in private and then brief afterward.
- Mr. Sullivan says the United States has long been ready to restore military-to-military communications and believes China has recently been constructive on this issue, but any final outcome will be known only after the summit.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates that managing U.S-China competition effectively is the single most important responsibility for the President and his team on this file, highlighting sustained dialogue on Ukraine and Taiwan since the last leaders’ meeting.
#### APEC, IPEF, and regional economic agenda
Sullivan outlines Biden’s agenda for APEC Economic Leaders’ Week in San Francisco, where the U.S. will showcase its economic leadership in the Asia-Pacific. He describes plans to advance the Indo-Pacific Economic Framework (IPEF), mobilize infrastructure investment with private-sector partners, and attract foreign CEOs to invest in U.S. technologies and jobs. The U.S. aims to position itself as the preeminent driver of inclusive and sustainable regional growth that also benefits workers at home.
- Mr. Sullivan says Biden will use APEC week to present his economic vision, portraying the United States as the preeminent driver of inclusive, sustainable growth in the Asia-Pacific and emphasizing the region’s importance to U.S. prosperity.
- Mr. Sullivan previewed that Biden will join regional leaders to announce clear outcomes ‘in record time’ on several pillars of the Indo-Pacific Economic Framework.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that Biden will host a Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment event with major CEOs and IPEF governments to mobilize high-standard infrastructure and innovation investments in the Indo-Pacific.
- Mr. Sullivan adds that the White House will also host an event with major foreign CEOs to highlight how Biden’s economic policies and close Asia-Pacific partnerships are drawing historic investments in critical and emerging technologies into the United States and creating good-paying U.S. jobs.
#### Hostage negotiations and humanitarian situation in Gaza
Sullivan provides an update on U.S. efforts to secure the release of hostages held by Hamas and to increase humanitarian aid to Gaza. He underscores the personal priority President Biden and senior officials place on the hostages, including American children, and describes ongoing negotiations via Qatar and Israel. Simultaneously, he highlights U.S. pressure on Israel for humanitarian pauses, protection of hospitals, and civilian safety, while affirming Israel’s right to defend itself against Hamas.
- Mr. Sullivan says the United States is working around the clock to rescue and reunite hostages held by Hamas, including a three-year-old American whose parents were killed on October 7.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Biden, Harris, Blinken, and he himself have all engaged directly with families of American hostages and raised the issue with foreign counterparts, calling it a top priority.
- Mr. Sullivan reports that Biden spoke with the Emir of Qatar the previous day to support ongoing negotiations involving Qatar, Israel, and the United States aimed at securing hostage releases, though he stresses that ‘we’re not there yet.’
- Mr. Sullivan confirms he will meet at the White House with families of Americans held hostage to update them and hear their concerns.
- Mr. Sullivan states that the United States is leading efforts to increase flows of food, water, and medicine into Gaza and acknowledges that, while some progress has been made, far more aid is urgently needed.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. remains in active discussions with Israel about tactical humanitarian pauses to let civilians leave active combat areas, increase aid, and enable hostage releases.
- Mr. Sullivan emphasizes that Biden urges Israel at all levels, including in conversations with Netanyahu, to take every possible measure to protect civilians, and he calls any innocent death—Palestinian or Israeli—a tragedy.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates U.S. support for Israel’s right to defend itself, arguing that Hamas openly seeks repeated October 7–style attacks and a permanent state of war with Israel.
#### Protection of hospitals and civilian infrastructure in Gaza
In response to questions, Sullivan elaborates on U.S. concerns about fighting near hospitals in Gaza, particularly Al-Shifa, and Israel’s obligations under the laws of war. He acknowledges public reports of Hamas using civilian infrastructure, including hospitals, for military purposes, while refusing to disclose U.S. intelligence. The U.S. position is that hospitals must be protected, evacuation routes must be safe, and Israel must grapple with the complexity of fighting Hamas while minimizing harm to civilians.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates that the U.S. position is that hospitals and patients must be protected and that the U.S. does not want to see firefights in hospitals.
- Mr. Sullivan says the Israeli government has told Washington that it shares the view that hospitals should not be sites of firefights and that Israel is prepared to provide fuel to hospitals to keep them operating.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Israel has informed the U.S. about difficulties communicating with some hospital administrators and has committed to maintaining evacuation routes for patients and staff moving between hospitals.
- Mr. Sullivan stresses that the U.S. will keep stating its clear position on hospital protection and continue consulting Israel to help ensure their stated policies are implemented on the ground.
- Mr. Sullivan states that public open-source reporting shows Hamas using hospitals and civilian infrastructure to store weapons and house fighters, but he declines to comment on specific intelligence regarding Al-Shifa or underground bunkers.
- Mr. Sullivan calls Israel’s situation highly complex because Hamas uses civilians as human shields and embeds in civilian infrastructure, yet he insists this ‘added burden’ does not lessen Israel’s responsibility to comply with the laws of war.
- Mr. Sullivan says operational decisions about specific tunnels or bunkers rest with the Israeli military, while the United States focuses on insisting that the laws of war be followed.
#### Government shutdown risk during multiple crises
Sullivan addresses the implications of a potential U.S. government shutdown while Washington manages wars in Ukraine and the Middle East. He warns that a shutdown would harm service members and signal political dysfunction abroad at a critical strategic moment. The administration is actively urging Congress to agree on a funding measure and is framing the issue as a test of bipartisan responsibility.
- Mr. Sullivan warns that a government shutdown would be a ‘devastating blow’ to U.S. service members by disrupting benefits and support for troops and their families.
- Mr. Sullivan argues that a shutdown would telegraph to the world that the United States cannot sustain bipartisan government funding at a time of global turbulence.
- Mr. Sullivan says the administration will do everything in its power to support Congress in reaching an agreement that funds the government and avoids a shutdown.
#### U.S. messaging to China on Iran and regional escalation
Reporters ask how Biden will use the Xi meeting to address Iran’s role in Middle East tensions. Sullivan indicates Biden will urge Xi to use China’s relationship with Iran to discourage destabilizing behavior. The U.S. frames de-escalation as a shared interest of all responsible powers, including China.
- Mr. Sullivan says Biden will tell Xi that de-escalation, not escalation, should be the priority in the broader Middle East for all responsible countries.
- Mr. Sullivan states that Biden will argue Iran’s escalatory, destabilizing actions are not in China’s interests or those of any responsible country.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that China has a relationship with Iran and is capable, if it chooses, of conveying these points directly to Tehran.
#### Hostage status and proof of life
Sullivan answers questions about what the U.S. knows regarding the condition of American hostages held by Hamas. He concedes significant uncertainty, noting limited visibility into hostages’ locations and health. While indicating that some information suggests a substantial number of hostages are alive and might be included in a possible release, he refuses to offer precise figures and ties clarity to actual outcomes of negotiations.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. has limited visibility into both the whereabouts and condition of hostages in Gaza.
- Mr. Sullivan repeats that there are nine missing American citizens and one missing green card holder, and he cannot say how many are still alive.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that the U.S. has information indicating a substantial number of hostages are alive and could be included in a release, but he cannot quantify how many Americans this might cover.
- Mr. Sullivan cautions that the U.S. will only know the exact number of surviving hostages once releases occur and individuals are safely returned to their families.
#### Supplemental funding for Ukraine, Israel, and other priorities
Sullivan defends the administration’s supplemental funding request covering Ukraine, Israel, the Indo-Pacific, and border security. He emphasizes that bipartisan majorities still exist in both chambers to support Ukraine, even as legislative vehicles remain uncertain. He warns that delays are already complicating U.S. military aid planning and that failure to fund these priorities would harm U.S. national interests.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that the administration has submitted a detailed funding request for Israel, Ukraine, the Indo-Pacific, and the U.S. border and insists all components are still needed urgently.
- Mr. Sullivan says he is personally calling members of Congress from both parties daily to make the case for Ukraine and other priorities.
- Mr. Sullivan expresses confidence that a strong bipartisan majority exists in both chambers to approve additional Ukraine funding, though the legislative path remains a ‘winding road.’
- Mr. Sullivan warns that each week without new Ukraine funding makes it harder to fully supply what Kyiv needs, and he says the lack of funding is already affecting U.S. ability to provide assistance consistently.
- Mr. Sullivan clarifies that while the U.S. can still supply requested military assistance to Ukraine, full funding would allow more certainty and stability in support.
#### China’s engagement motives and durability of diplomacy
Sullivan is asked why China has stepped up diplomatic engagement and whether outcomes will be durable. He declines to speculate on Beijing’s motives but acknowledges that global conditions change quickly and can introduce new crises. The U.S. aim is to build communication channels and guardrails that endure shocks rather than rely on short-term tactical shifts by China.
- Mr. Sullivan refuses to attribute specific motives to China’s recent openness to engagement, suggesting reporters ask Beijing directly.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that world events such as the October 7 attacks demonstrate that unexpected turbulence can arise, underscoring the need for robust U.S.-China communication channels.
- Mr. Sullivan says Biden sees the Xi summit as a chance to build a basis for responsibly managing competition through future crises, not as a one-off event.
- Mr. Sullivan underscores that the U.S. wants to set broad parameters so that competition does not escalate into conflict regardless of shifting circumstances.
#### Spy balloon incident and accountability
Reporters press Sullivan on whether Biden will confront Xi about the Chinese surveillance balloon incident and seek an apology. Sullivan avoids previewing specific demands but recounts U.S. actions to safely shoot down and exploit the balloon. He asserts that the U.S. prevented problematic information gains and says Biden will focus on broader U.S. interests going forward rather than public demands from the podium.
- Mr. Sullivan declines to say whether Biden will directly confront Xi over the spy balloon, insisting that Biden will address sensitive topics privately in the meeting.
- Mr. Sullivan recounts that Biden ordered the balloon shot down once it was safe for civilians and that the U.S. recovered and exploited the debris.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. does not believe the balloon yielded problematic intelligence gains for China due to mitigation measures taken during its transit.
- Mr. Sullivan refuses to state whether the U.S. wants an apology, arguing that publicly making demands would not be the most effective way to secure diplomatic outcomes.
#### U.S. strikes on Iranian proxies and deterrence
Sullivan comments on recent U.S. airstrikes against facilities linked to Iran in Syria, carried out in response to attacks on U.S. forces. He frames the strikes as part of a longer-term deterrence strategy rather than a one-day metric of success. The administration pledges continued responses if attacks persist, with the priority of protecting U.S. personnel in Iraq and Syria.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that the U.S. has carried out multiple rounds of strikes against facilities tied to Iran in Syria in response to attacks on U.S. forces.
- Mr. Sullivan argues that the effectiveness of deterrence should be judged over time, not merely by attacks in the 24 hours after a strike.
- Mr. Sullivan states that if Iranian proxies continue to attack U.S. forces, the U.S. will continue to respond, as protecting U.S. troops is Biden’s responsibility.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates that Biden is committed to safeguarding the security and physical safety of U.S. forces in Iraq and Syria.
#### AI, autonomous weapons, and China
Sullivan is asked about reports that Biden and Xi will agree to ban AI use in autonomous weapons. He confirms AI will be on the agenda but does not validate the specific report. This indicates that AI governance and security implications are emerging topics in U.S.-China strategic discussions but details are still evolving and classified.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that artificial intelligence will be part of the Biden–Xi discussions.
- Mr. Sullivan says he cannot confirm reports of an agreement to ban AI in autonomous weapons and is unsure of the report’s origin.
- Mr. Sullivan suggests that any concrete outcomes on AI-related issues will be communicated after the summit if appropriate.
#### China’s support to Russia in Ukraine war
Sullivan provides an updated assessment of Chinese support to Russia’s war in Ukraine. He states that, to U.S. knowledge, China has not provided weapons to Russia, while promising to verify that this assessment remains current. This underscores ongoing U.S. monitoring of Chinese assistance and the linkage between China policy and the Ukraine conflict.
- Mr. Sullivan says the latest U.S. assessment is that China has not supplied weapons to Russia for use in the Ukraine war.
- Mr. Sullivan commits to rechecking this assessment because his information is a few days old, indicating active, ongoing monitoring.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Biden has previously had candid discussions with Xi about avoiding Chinese military support to Russia, tying this issue to broader U.S.-China crisis management.
#### Humanitarian pauses and Gaza ceasefire debates
Sullivan responds to questions about the adequacy of four-hour humanitarian pauses in Gaza and broader calls for ceasefires. He expresses support for flexible, needs-based pauses and notes that Israel has already extended some pauses to longer periods. The U.S. favors potentially longer, days-long pauses in the context of a hostage agreement rather than an unconditional ceasefire, and continues to negotiate such arrangements.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Israel has implemented four-hour daily humanitarian pauses but has also extended some to seven hours to facilitate safe civilian movement.
- Mr. Sullivan argues that the length of pauses should be driven by operational needs to achieve safe passage and aid delivery, not by a rigid timetable.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. would like to see longer pauses, measured in days rather than hours, as part of a hostage release agreement and confirms this is being actively pursued in negotiations.
- Mr. Sullivan distinguishes between tactical pauses and broader ceasefire demands, reaffirming the U.S. focus on hostage-linked pauses and continued Israeli operations against Hamas.
#### U.S. casualties in training accident near Israel
Sullivan addresses a fatal helicopter crash involving five U.S. Army special operations soldiers deployed in the Mediterranean. He clarifies that the deaths occurred during a training mission, not in combat, and therefore he does not classify them as casualties of the Israel–Hamas war. The administration expresses sorrow but keeps the incident analytically separate from active hostilities.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that five Army special operations forces died in a helicopter crash in the Mediterranean during a training mission.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that the forces were part of the broader regional deployment but states the crash did not occur in combat.
- Mr. Sullivan says he would not consider these deaths casualties of the current war, while acknowledging the tragedy of training accidents in global deployments and referring formal classification questions to the Defense Department.
#### Accuracy of casualty information and credibility
Sullivan is confronted with questions about U.S. reliance on Israeli figures and skepticism toward Palestinian sources, amid reports revising Israeli casualty numbers from October 7. He firmly rejects accusations that the White House is spreading misinformation and pledges to update statements as new data emerge. He emphasizes a commitment to honesty about what is known, unknown, or changing, including on sensitive topics like the number of American hostages.
- Mr. Sullivan says that when he knows information he will share it, and when he does not or when it is uncertain, he will say so explicitly, citing his comments on the unknown status of American hostages.
- Mr. Sullivan commits to updating prior statements if new information supersedes older data, pointing to the administration’s track record of doing so.
- Mr. Sullivan categorically rejects the notion that the White House is ‘peddling any misinformation’ regarding Israeli and Palestinian casualties.
- Mr. Sullivan underscores that the administration’s public messaging seeks to maintain credibility while navigating incomplete and evolving information from a conflict zone.
#### Compliance with laws of war in Gaza
Asked if Israel is abiding by international humanitarian law given high Palestinian casualties, Sullivan refuses to render a legal judgment from the podium. Instead, he reiterates the U.S. policy line that Israel must separate terrorists from civilians and minimize harm, even though Hamas’s tactics complicate this. The administration thus maintains public pressure on Israel’s conduct while avoiding definitive legal conclusions in public remarks.
- Mr. Sullivan says he is not in a position to act as ‘judge and jury’ on whether Israel is complying with the laws of war, calling it a legal determination he cannot make from the podium.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates the U.S. stance that Hamas’s use of civilians as human shields increases the burden on Israel but does not lessen its responsibility to protect civilians and follow the laws of war.
- Mr. Sullivan states that the U.S. has consistently communicated, both publicly and privately, that Israeli operations must be conducted in ways that separate terrorists from civilians and minimize civilian harm.
- Mr. Sullivan acknowledges the scale of Palestinian casualties and the dire situation in hospitals while declining to formally conclude that Israel is or is not in compliance with international law.
#### Newsom and Schumer China trips feeding into Xi summit
Sullivan confirms that Governor Gavin Newsom’s and Senator Chuck Schumer’s recent visits to China are coordinated with the administration and inform Biden’s agenda. Ambassador Nick Burns joined Newsom’s meeting with Xi, and Sullivan has consulted Schumer about his bipartisan delegation’s trip. These engagements help build context and identify potential opportunities ahead of the leaders’ summit.
- Mr. Sullivan says the White House had advance notice of Governor Newsom’s meeting with Xi and that Ambassador Nick Burns accompanied the delegation, indicating coordination with the State Department.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that the administration received a readout from Newsom’s team but declines to share details publicly because they are part of private diplomatic conversations.
- Mr. Sullivan notes he spoke directly with Senator Schumer about his bipartisan delegation’s China visit and is using those insights to help shape Biden’s work with Xi and potential tangible outcomes.
#### Disclosure of U.S. military aid to Israel
Sullivan is asked whether the administration will provide more detail on the type of military support the U.S. is sending to Israel. He says the Defense and State Departments will follow normal processes to disclose sales and transfers, and briefly lists key categories of assistance. This indicates a commitment to some transparency while operational and political debates continue.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that the Defense Department will lay out what the U.S. is providing to Israel, consistent with normal protocols on arms sales and transfers.

#### Indonesia, OIC resolution, and ‘wrong side of history’ concern
Sullivan is asked whether Biden worries about being on the ‘wrong side of history’ given global calls, including from the OIC, for a ceasefire in Gaza. He responds by emphasizing issue-based engagement with Indonesian President Widodo, acknowledging differences on Gaza while stressing the strong trajectory of the overall relationship. The administration frames divergent views on the conflict as one part of a broader strategic partnership.
- Mr. Sullivan states that Biden and Widodo will discuss the Gaza conflict along with many other bilateral, regional, and global issues, some of which they will agree on and others where they will differ.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Widodo’s position on Gaza and Biden’s are well known and that the two leaders will respectfully exchange views rather than seek immediate alignment.
- Mr. Sullivan emphasizes that, despite differences over the crisis, the U.S.-Indonesia relationship is robust and advancing, with concrete progress expected on critical minerals, clean energy cooperation, investment, and elevation to a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership.
#### Multiple fronts in Middle East tensions
Sullivan is asked whether the Middle East conflict now spans three fronts: Gaza, the Israel–Hezbollah border, and attacks on U.S. forces by Iranian proxies. He acknowledges all three strands of violence but resists describing them as a single three-front war. The U.S. is tracking each line of effort while seeking to prevent broader regional escalation.
- Mr. Sullivan agrees that there is an ongoing Israeli military operation in Gaza and an exchange of fire between Israel and Hezbollah in the north.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that U.S. forces in Iraq and Syria have been repeatedly attacked by IRGC-aligned proxy groups and that the U.S. has responded with strikes.
- Mr. Sullivan says he would not describe the situation as a unified ‘three-front conflict’ but acknowledges the multiple, interconnected flashpoints.
#### Supreme Court Code of Ethics
Karine Jean-Pierre responds to questions about the Supreme Court’s newly announced Code of Ethics for Justices. She does not assess whether it goes far enough but restates Biden’s general position that high officials should adhere to strong ethics and transparency standards. She points back to Biden’s earlier ProPublica interview for detailed views, indicating the White House will not immediately re-litigate the issue.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre declines to add to Biden’s prior comments in a ProPublica interview on Supreme Court ethics when asked about the new Code of Ethics announced by the Court.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that ethics rules already apply to lower federal courts and says Americans have a right to expect high ethics and transparency standards from officials in high office.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre does not directly evaluate the sufficiency of the Court’s new code, indicating the White House is not providing a fresh, detailed reaction at this time.

#### Crime in Washington, D.C. and federal response

She highlights billions in funding for local law enforcement provided through the American Rescue Plan and calls on Congress, especially Republicans, to support additional investments like the Safer America Plan. The White House portrays Biden as taking crime seriously while criticizing Republicans for using the issue politically rather than legislating solutions.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre acknowledges concern about increased crime in D.C. and nationally, including auto thefts and carjackings affecting government workers and officials.

#### House laddered continuing resolution and shutdown politics

She labels the approach ‘unserious’ and ‘untested,’ arguing that Republicans wasted weeks in internal turmoil and are now risking a shutdown. The White House is consulting Democratic leaders before taking a definitive position, emphasizing the need to keep government open and fund national security supplements.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre calls the latest House GOP laddered CR ‘unserious’ and ‘untested,’ suggesting it creates ongoing uncertainty and future shutdown risks.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says Biden will consult with Democratic leaders in both the House and Senate before deciding on the path forward or taking a public position on the CR proposal.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre criticizes House Republicans for threatening a government shutdown that would harm the economy and national security while engaging in weeks of internal chaos over the speakership.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre insists that the administration’s priority is to keep the government open and pass the national security supplemental, including funding for Ukraine, Israel, the Indo-Pacific, and domestic needs.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that some Democrats see the CR as preferable to a shutdown but reiterates that Democratic leadership will decide on the best course after internal discussions.
#### AI-generated political ads and platform responsibilities

- Ms. Jean-Pierre says she has no specific comment on Meta’s new policy requiring disclosure of AI or digital tools in political and social-issue ads.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre points to Biden’s comprehensive AI executive order as evidence that the administration is taking AI-related risks, including misinformation, very seriously.
