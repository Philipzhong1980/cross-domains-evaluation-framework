### Overview
The briefing, led by Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and NSC spokesman John Kirby, centers on national security, foreign policy, and domestic political and economic issues. Kirby condemns Russia’s suspension of the Black Sea Grain Initiative as a reckless de facto blockade that is already raising global food prices, outlines constrained overland alternatives, and rejects Russian propaganda that Western sanctions target agriculture. He also details President Biden’s call with Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu and the upcoming visit by President Herzog, reaffirming an ironclad U.S. commitment to Israel’s security while voicing concern over settlement expansion, West Bank violence, and contentious judicial reforms, and he underscores continued U.S. pressure on Iran, new deployments to deter Iranian maritime threats, and support for Israeli democracy and protesters’ aspirations. Kirby addresses a Chinese-linked hack of unclassified Microsoft cloud email accounts affecting officials such as Commerce Secretary Raimondo, saying U.S. teams detected and contained it, that attribution to a PRC group appears consistent with Microsoft’s findings, and that cyber defense is a continuous effort that must coexist with responsible U.S.-China engagement. He backs new Afghan adjustment legislation, defends Afghan refugees in Pakistan, signals readiness to further sanction Iran’s morality police, and discusses the Wagner mutiny’s limited observed impact on the Ukraine war, while taking seriously growing congressional scrutiny of unidentified aerial phenomena and noting a Pentagon office now tracks UAP reports. Jean-Pierre opens by sharply criticizing Senator Tommy Tuberville’s unprecedented hold on hundreds of military promotions over Pentagon abortion travel policy, arguing it harms readiness and military families and calling on Republican senators to intervene, a theme Kirby later reinforces with a detailed justification of reproductive healthcare as central to recruiting and retention in an all-volunteer force. She embraces Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene’s comparison of Bidenomics to Social Security and Medicare as validation that Biden’s agenda reduces rural poverty, touts falling inflation, strong job gains, rising wages, and record EV sales as evidence of economic strength, and disputes social-media fact-checks of the President’s wage claims. Jean-Pierre also denounces Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s COVID-19 remarks as racist and antisemitic, supports Hollywood writers and actors’ strikes and UPS-Teamsters bargaining as legitimate expressions of worker power, reiterates that the House’s culture-war–laden NDAA will not reach Biden’s desk, and reaffirms backing for Labor nominee Julie Su. On several emerging issues—including the cocaine found in the White House, misdirected .mil emails to Mali, Tunisia’s treatment of migrants, requests to declassify documents on Brazil’s 1964 coup, Farm Bill nutrition priorities, and the Secret Service’s inconclusive cocaine probe—she or Kirby offer limited detail, emphasize confidence in relevant agencies, and often promise follow-up, while maintaining the overarching message that the administration is focused on protecting national security, defending democratic values at home and abroad, and advancing a worker-centered economic agenda.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Tuberville hold on military promotions
Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre criticizes Senator Tommy Tuberville for blocking hundreds of military promotions and tying them to Pentagon reproductive health policy. She frames this as an unprecedented partisan move that harms national security, military readiness, and military families. The White House urges other Republican senators to pressure Tuberville to drop his hold rather than contemplating changes to Senate procedures. President Biden’s comments in Helsinki are reiterated to underscore administration concern and the call for GOP intervention.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says Senator Tuberville is hijacking what has traditionally been a bipartisan process and delaying the confirmation of hundreds of qualified military nominees, depriving the armed forces of needed leadership.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that, for the first time in over a century, the Marine Corps lacks a Senate-confirmed commandant because of Tuberville’s actions.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre cites Tuberville’s past statements praising military spouses and condemning use of the military as a political pawn to argue he is contradicting his own prior positions.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the President and his team will keep speaking out and calls on Republican senators to speak up against Tuberville’s hold in order to protect national security and military families.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre rejects the idea that the White House is the problem, stating that Senator Tuberville has made a historically bipartisan process “recklessly partisan” and is solely responsible for the current impasse.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the President will sit down with the senator only if there is any indication he would change his position, and she emphasizes that this is fundamentally a question for Tuberville to resolve.
#### Response to Marjorie Taylor Greene on Bidenomics
Jean-Pierre highlights comments by Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene criticizing “Bidenomics” for echoing FDR’s Social Security and LBJ’s Medicare and for reducing rural poverty. The White House embraces these comparisons, saying it agrees with Greene’s description of Bidenomics’ goals while criticizing her and the Republican Study Committee for seeking cuts to Social Security, Medicare, and the Inflation Reduction Act. The administration plans to use these attacks as proof that Biden policies are lowering rural poverty and creating jobs, including in Greene’s own district.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the White House ‘agrees with Marjorie Taylor Greene’ that Bidenomics is in line with FDR’s Social Security and LBJ’s Medicare and that it is reducing rural poverty.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes Biden is opposed to rural poverty and is committed to protecting Medicare and Social Security, contrasting that with the Republican Study Committee’s proposals to cut those benefits.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says Greene is seeking to repeal the Inflation Reduction Act even though it is driving high-paying manufacturing jobs into her own district.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre states that President Biden looks forward to visiting Greene’s district to highlight the good-paying jobs created by his policies and the real-world impact on Georgians.
#### Russia’s suspension of Black Sea Grain Initiative
National Security Council spokesman John Kirby condemns Russia’s decision to suspend participation in the Black Sea Grain Initiative, describing it as a dangerous move that will worsen global food insecurity and raise prices. He explains that the deal has moved 33 million metric tons of grain, mostly to developing countries, helping stabilize global prices, and that its suspension effectively reinstates a naval blockade on Ukrainian ports. The U.S. is exploring overland alternatives with partners but acknowledges these are less efficient and cannot fully replace Black Sea exports, and it rejects Russian claims that Western sanctions target agricultural exports.
- Mr. Kirby calls Russia’s suspension of the Black Sea Grain Initiative an irresponsible and dangerous decision that will exacerbate food scarcity and harm millions of vulnerable people worldwide.
- Mr. Kirby notes that more than half of the 33 million metric tons shipped under the initiative went to developing countries, and that the deal helped drive down and stabilize global food prices.
- Mr. Kirby states that Russia will be fully and solely responsible for the consequences of resuming its effective blockade of Ukrainian ports.
- Mr. Kirby says global wheat, corn, and soybean prices have already spiked on the day of Russia’s announcement, demonstrating immediate impact.
- Mr. Kirby explains that without the grain deal, Ukraine must rely on hazardous and cumbersome overland routes through EU countries, which are less efficient and politically complex.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that U.S. sanctions do not target Russian food or fertilizer and characterizes Moscow’s claims to the contrary as propaganda.
- Mr. Kirby says the United States will keep working with allies and partners to find new ways to get Ukrainian grain to markets but concedes there is no way, mathematically, to replace the volume moved under the Black Sea deal.
#### Questions on blockading grain shipments and Crimean bridge attack
Reporters ask whether the U.S. or partners could escort grain ships through the Black Sea and challenge the Russian blockade, and whether the Crimean bridge is a legitimate target. Kirby rules out running the blockade, saying a military challenge to Russia’s naval posture is not being actively pursued. He declines to attribute the bridge attack, rejects Russian claims of causation between the bridge strike and the grain suspension, and reiterates the U.S. view that Crimea is Ukrainian territory, meaning Ukraine determines its own lawful targets while the U.S. does not support strikes inside Russia proper.
- Mr. Kirby rejects the idea of U.S.-backed escorted convoys running Russia’s Black Sea blockade, saying that is not an option being actively pursued.
- Mr. Kirby says he has seen nothing proving that the strike on the Crimean bridge caused Putin’s decision not to extend the grain deal, noting that Putin had already been leaning against renewal.
- Mr. Kirby states that the United States does not decide what is a legitimate target for Ukraine and underscores that Crimea is Ukrainian soil.
- Mr. Kirby distinguishes between targets in Ukraine and inside Russia, repeating that the U.S. does not encourage or enable Ukrainian strikes inside Russia.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. is not in a position to attribute responsibility for the Crimean bridge attack and has seen no impact from the strike on Russia’s military posture or defensive capabilities in Crimea or southern Ukraine so far.
#### Israeli security, judicial reform, and U.S.-Israel relations
Kirby provides a readout of President Biden’s call with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and previews Biden’s upcoming meeting with Israeli President Isaac Herzog. The President reaffirms his ironclad commitment to Israel’s security, cooperation against Iran, and prevention of an Iranian nuclear weapon, while also raising concerns about West Bank violence, settlement growth, and the Israeli government’s judicial reform agenda. He urges measures to maintain a two-state solution, support Palestinian livelihoods, and seeks regional consultations in the Aqaba/Sharm format, while activists and some U.S. lawmakers press for more vocal U.S. support for Israeli protesters resisting judicial changes.
- Mr. Kirby says President Biden underscored his ironclad and unwavering commitment to Israel’s security and condemned recent acts of terror against Israeli citizens in his call with Prime Minister Netanyahu.
- Mr. Kirby reports that Biden and Netanyahu discussed close coordination to counter Iran, including regular joint military exercises, and agreed that the U.S.-Israel partnership is central to preventing Iran from acquiring a nuclear weapon.
- Mr. Kirby says Biden stressed the need to maintain the viability of a two-state solution, improve security in the West Bank, and welcomed Israeli willingness to consider new steps to support Palestinian livelihoods.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Biden expressed concern about continued settlement growth and called on all parties to refrain from unilateral measures.
- Mr. Kirby says the two leaders agreed to consult regional partners with the aim of convening another Aqaba/Sharm-style meeting soon.
- Mr. Kirby confirms that Biden and Netanyahu will meet in the United States in the fall, with details on timing and venue still being worked out.
- Mr. Kirby explains that agreeing to a meeting does not mean U.S. concerns about judicial reform or extremist cabinet behavior have diminished, and he says Biden reiterates those concerns candidly in discussions with Israeli leaders.
- Mr. Kirby states that President Herzog’s visit will include discussion of judicial reform but also focus on Iran’s regional activities, potential new protests in Iran, climate change, and further integration and normalization of Israel in the Middle East.
- Mr. Kirby says the White House is glad Representative Jayapal apologized for calling Israel a “racist state” and uses that moment to restate that the U.S. commitment to Israel is ironclad.
- Mr. Kirby says Biden’s message to Israeli protesters is consistent with his public stance: the U.S. stands for Israel, its people, and its democracy, and supports reforms built on compromise and the broadest possible consensus.
#### Chinese cloud hacking of U.S. officials’ emails
Kirby addresses a Chinese-linked cyber intrusion into Microsoft-hosted, unclassified U.S. government email accounts, including Commerce Secretary Gina Raimondo’s. He explains that U.S. security teams detected the activity, alerted Microsoft, and moved quickly to mitigate vulnerabilities. While the investigation continues and formal U.S. attribution has not been made, the administration does not dispute Microsoft’s assessment that a PRC-based group is responsible, and stresses that cyber resilience remains an ongoing priority, not a static fix.
- Mr. Kirby describes the episode as an intrusion into unclassified Microsoft cloud services that allowed access to some government email traffic, including that of officials such as Secretary Raimondo.
- Mr. Kirby says U.S. authorities discovered the intrusion and alerted Microsoft, then took immediate steps to limit further impact and close vulnerabilities.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that U.S. government networks face attempted intrusions daily from various state and non-state actors, making cybersecurity a constant, evolving challenge.
- Mr. Kirby notes that the administration recently released a new national cybersecurity strategy to strengthen government defenses in cyberspace.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. is not yet in a position to independently verify attribution but has seen nothing that would cause it to dispute Microsoft’s conclusion that a PRC-linked group was responsible.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges that not every cyberattack can be prevented but insists that the government works continuously to improve proactive defenses.
- Mr. Kirby later reiterates that if investigation confirms PRC responsibility, there will be accountability, but he argues this should not derail broader U.S.-China engagement on managing competition responsibly.
#### Afghan Adjustment Act and Afghan refugees
Kirby expresses support for new bipartisan legislation to provide a more permanent status for Afghan allies evacuated to or seeking to enter the United States. He signals Biden’s general backing for the concept of an Afghan Adjustment Act, while stopping short of a specific signing commitment before the bill progresses in Congress. On related regional issues, he defends Afghan refugees in Pakistan against generalized accusations of terrorism and praises Pakistan for hosting displaced Afghans, while reiterating U.S. willingness to cooperate with Pakistan on genuine counterterrorism threats.
- Mr. Kirby says the administration supports legislative efforts on an Afghan Adjustment Act and is grateful for congressional work on the issue.
- Mr. Kirby states that President Biden feels strongly about doing everything possible to welcome Afghan allies, wherever they choose to resettle, and help them start new lives and become citizens.
- Mr. Kirby declines to pre-judge the specific bill language but signals continued engagement with Congress and support for the legislation’s aims.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. has seen no indication that Afghan refugees in Pakistan or along the border are guilty of terrorism, pushing back on Pakistani claims.
- Mr. Kirby praises Pakistan’s generosity in hosting Afghan refugees and says the U.S. will continue working with Pakistan on legitimate terrorism threats and counterterrorism challenges without speculating on specific over-the-horizon operations.
#### UAPs and Pentagon transparency
Kirby responds to growing congressional interest in unidentified aerial phenomena (UAPs), including an upcoming House hearing and proposed declassification measures. He underscores that the Pentagon now has a dedicated organization to collect and analyze UAP reports, driven in part by safety concerns for military training. While he avoids commenting on individual whistleblowers, he says the administration takes UAPs seriously and will share information with Congress and the public as much as national security allows.
- Mr. Kirby notes that the Pentagon has established an organization specifically to coordinate reporting and analysis of UAP sightings across the military, indicating the issue is taken seriously.
- Mr. Kirby says the administration aims to be as transparent as possible with Congress and the public on UAPs, subject to national security constraints.
- Mr. Kirby explains that UAPs have already affected military training ranges by creating uncertainty for pilots and impacting their ability to hone their skills.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that the government is not asserting what UAPs are or are not, but acknowledges pilots are seeing phenomena that need to be better understood.
- Mr. Kirby declines to comment on the credibility of individual UAP whistleblowers, keeping the focus on institutional processes.
#### Iran morality police and protests
Kirby answers questions about Iran’s reported redeployment of the morality police and renewed enforcement of compulsory hijab laws. He reiterates U.S. condemnation of the morality police’s role in the killing of Mahsa Amini and injuries to other protesters, and insists Iranian women should decide for themselves how to dress and practice their faith. The administration has already sanctioned morality police members and related entities and signals readiness to take additional pressure measures if abuses continue, while also watching for potential new protests triggered by the renewed enforcement.
- Mr. Kirby recalls that the U.S. has already sanctioned members of Iran’s morality police and supporting entities for their role in killing a young woman and injuring others.
- Mr. Kirby says Iranian women should be able to decide for themselves how to observe their faith and how to dress in public, and that nothing has changed in the U.S. stance on this issue.
- Mr. Kirby states that the administration will watch developments closely and is prepared to take additional action to increase accountability and pressure on the regime if warranted.
- Mr. Kirby later notes that the White House expects possible new protests in Iran now that the morality police are back on the streets enforcing dress codes.
#### Afghanistan-Pakistan tensions over refugees and militants
In response to questions about Pakistani claims that Afghan refugees contribute to instability and harbor anti-Pakistan militants, Kirby draws a distinction between refugees and terrorist threats. He says the U.S. sees no evidence refugees are responsible for terrorism and reiterates appreciation for Pakistan’s role in sheltering Afghans. While not detailing specific operations, he confirms the United States remains committed to maintaining and improving over-the-horizon counterterrorism capability to address real threats as needed.
- Mr. Kirby says the U.S. has seen no indication that Afghan refugees in Pakistan or along the border are guilty of acts of terrorism.
- Mr. Kirby thanks Pakistan for its “incredible generosity” in hosting large numbers of Afghans seeking safety and stability.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that the U.S. will continue to coordinate with Pakistan on legitimate terrorism threats and shared counterterrorism challenges.
- Mr. Kirby refrains from commenting on specific potential over-the-horizon strikes but affirms that President Biden is committed to maintaining effective over-the-horizon counterterrorism capabilities.
#### U.S.-China relations and hacking versus engagement
Kirby is pressed on whether continued engagement with China, despite cyber intrusions, signals weakness or acceptance. He argues that accountability and engagement are compatible, saying that even if investigations confirm PRC responsibility, the U.S. should respond proportionally while preserving a functioning, responsible bilateral relationship. He stresses that the U.S.-China relationship is the most consequential in the world and cannot be “torpedoed” over a single incident, while reaffirming that hacking against U.S. officials would warrant consequences.
- Mr. Kirby says the appropriate response to tensions and disagreements, including cyber incidents, is continued engagement that allows for candid conversations and mutual accountability, not a rupture of relations.
- Mr. Kirby underscores that the U.S.-China relationship is the most consequential in the world militarily, economically, and politically, and should not be derailed by one act.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that if the FBI investigation confirms PRC responsibility for the email hack, the U.S. will pursue accountability.
- Mr. Kirby insists that taking cyber intrusions seriously does not mean abandoning efforts to manage U.S.-China competition in a responsible manner.
#### Iran maritime threats and U.S. military deployments
Kirby confirms increased Iranian attacks and harassment against commercial shipping in the Gulf and explains why the Pentagon is deploying additional air assets, including F-16s and F-35s, to the region. He frames the move as a necessary response to more frequent and more dangerous Iranian maritime activity, designed to protect peaceful shipping and ensure adequate U.S. readiness. He defers detailed operational questions to the Defense Department.
- Mr. Kirby cites a recent Pentagon announcement of new military deployments to the Middle East in response to Iranian attacks on maritime shipping.
- Mr. Kirby says Iran’s maritime behavior has become more bellicose, more active, and more dangerous to peaceful shipping in the Gulf, requiring a stronger U.S. posture.
- Mr. Kirby explains that Secretary of Defense Austin wants to ensure the U.S. has proper capabilities and readiness in place to address this increased threat.
- Mr. Kirby defers specifics on which units or platforms are deploying to the Department of Defense.
#### Tunisia migrant expulsions (limited response)
Asked about reports that Tunisian authorities are expelling sub-Saharan migrants into the desert without food or water, Kirby does not offer a substantive position. He refers reporters to the State Department for more detailed comment and leaves open whether the administration will respond diplomatically or through human rights channels. The exchange reflects awareness of emerging concerns about Tunisia’s treatment of migrants but no announced U.S. policy response during this briefing.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges reports that Tunisian authorities may be expelling migrants but says he does not have a good sense of the situation to comment in detail.
- Mr. Kirby directs questions about U.S. messaging to Tunisia and any diplomatic actions to the State Department.
#### Misdirected .mil emails to Mali domain
Kirby addresses a report that U.S. military emails have been misdirected for years due to users typing “.ml” instead of “.mil,” potentially exposing sensitive information to Mali, a Russian-aligned government. He explains that the Pentagon has implemented a technical tool to block outbound emails lacking the proper “.mil” suffix but acknowledges it cannot control incorrectly addressed inbound messages from external users. He notes that DOD has conducted employee training to reduce such errors and stresses that the department has been mindful of this risk for some time.
- Mr. Kirby says the Defense Department has implemented a tool to stop outbound emails that lack the correct .mil domain from leaving U.S. military systems.
- Mr. Kirby notes there is little the Pentagon can do to block inbound emails misaddressed to non-.mil domains, such as .ml, originating from outside networks.
- Mr. Kirby says DOD has been training its employees to avoid sending official documents from home devices and to be careful when addressing emails.
- Mr. Kirby emphasizes that DOD has been aware of this issue for some time and has taken steps to mitigate it but that some residual risk remains.
#### Papal envoy visit on Ukraine humanitarian issues
Kirby previews a visit by Cardinal Matteo Zuppi, sent by Pope Francis to meet President Biden about the war in Ukraine, with a focus on humanitarian concerns. He notes particular attention to Ukrainian children forcibly taken to Russia or Russian-occupied territories, describing them as being in conditions akin to concentration camps. The administration welcomes the dialogue and views it as part of broader efforts to address the human cost of the conflict, including child deportations and other abuses.
- Mr. Kirby says Cardinal Zuppi is coming as an envoy of Pope Francis specifically to discuss the war in Ukraine and humanitarian issues.
- Mr. Kirby highlights the plight of thousands of Ukrainian children deported to Russia or Russian-occupied areas, calling some of their destinations essentially concentration camps.
- Mr. Kirby says the White House looks forward to the discussions with the papal envoy on these humanitarian concerns.
#### Wagner Group aftermath and Russian military strength
Responding to questions tying Russia’s grain blockade to its battlefield performance and the mutiny by Wagner Group, Kirby avoids direct psychological analysis of Putin’s motives but reiterates that Russia’s grain decision is reckless. He acknowledges Ukraine’s counteroffensive is progressing slower than Kyiv would like due to entrenched Russian defenses and heavy minefields. He indicates that Wagner elements remain active in various locations but that the mutiny has not yet produced a major observable impact on Russian forces’ defensive posture against the Ukrainian counteroffensive.
- Mr. Kirby declines to speculate on whether Russia’s grain blockade is driven by battlefield weakness but repeats that the decision is reckless and irresponsible.
- Mr. Kirby says the Ukrainian counteroffensive is harder and slower than hoped because Russia has had months to build defense in depth, including extensive minefields, and because defense is the stronger form of warfare.
- Mr. Kirby underscores that Ukrainian forces are being shot at and shelled while breaching minefields, making progress particularly difficult.
- Mr. Kirby says Wagner Group fighters appear dispersed between Russian-occupied Ukraine, Belarus, and Africa and remain capable of malign activity.
- Mr. Kirby states that it is too soon to assess a significant impact of the Prigozhin mutiny on the broader war effort and that there has not been a major effect on the Ukrainian counteroffensive so far.
#### Defense of Pentagon abortion travel policy and readiness
Kirby delivers an extended justification of the Defense Department policy that supports service members’ access to reproductive healthcare, including travel for abortion services, arguing it is crucial to morale, retention, and readiness. He notes that one in five U.S. service members is a woman and emphasizes the obligation of the military to care for members and families who have volunteered to serve and cannot choose their duty stations. He cites conversations with military spouses who say restrictive state abortion laws are affecting their willingness to serve or stay in uniform, warning that rescinding the policy would worsen recruiting and retention challenges in an all-volunteer force.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that women constitute one in five members of the U.S. military and that the institution has a sacred obligation to provide them and their families with comprehensive healthcare, including reproductive care.
- Mr. Kirby argues that policies on diversity, inclusion, transgender service, and reproductive care are foundational to treating service members with dignity and respect, regardless of who they are or whom they love.
- Mr. Kirby recounts meeting military spouses who told him that restrictive abortion laws are already affecting their willingness to serve or to support their spouses’ continued service.
- Mr. Kirby warns that without reproductive healthcare access, assignments to restrictive states such as Alabama could prompt some service members to leave, causing a loss of critical talent in an already stressed all-volunteer force.
- Mr. Kirby links the policy directly to recruiting and retention, stating that it is not only morally right but also essential to maintaining readiness and morale.

#### Inflation, grain prices, and Bidenomics performance
Jean-Pierre is asked whether grain price spikes from the Black Sea deal collapse could reverse recent progress on inflation. She emphasizes that inflation has fallen for 12 consecutive months to 3 percent and credits Bidenomics for strong growth, rising wages, and historically low unemployment. While acknowledging that grain prices have risen and saying the administration will monitor the situation, she does not offer a quantitative forecast of impacts, instead reinforcing the narrative that U.S. economic fundamentals remain strong under Biden’s policies.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre highlights that inflation has fallen for 12 straight months to 3 percent, the lowest in two years, and attributes this progress to Bidenomics.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre notes that wages are rising and unemployment has stayed below 4 percent, which she says has not happened in 50 years.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre acknowledges that grain prices have gone up following Russia’s suspension of the Black Sea deal but says she does not yet have a full assessment of how this will affect inflation trends.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says the administration will monitor the grain situation closely while continuing to point to broader positive economic indicators.
#### RFK Jr.’s COVID-19 remarks and antisemitism

#### Real wage tweet and social media fact-check
