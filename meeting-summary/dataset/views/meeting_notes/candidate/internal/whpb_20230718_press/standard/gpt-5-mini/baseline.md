### Overview
  The briefing covers a broad set of domestic and foreign policy topics with Press Secretary Karine Jean‑Pierre and Admiral John Kirby leading discussions. Jean‑Pierre opens by welcoming new WHCA leadership and then focuses sharply on a standoff in the Senate, saying Senator Tommy Tuberville’s holds on NDAA‑related military confirmations threaten leadership, retention, and military family stability and urging Republican senators to act. Kirby addresses major foreign policy issues: he condemns Russia’s suspension of the Black Sea Grain Initiative as a humanitarian and market‑disrupting act and urges reversal, provides a readout of President Biden’s call with Prime Minister Netanyahu emphasizing security cooperation and concern about judicial reform, and describes U.S. responses and constraints on alternatives for moving Ukrainian grain. Kirby also discusses a Microsoft cloud intrusion that affected unclassified government email, saying the U.S. alerted Microsoft, continues investigation, and sees no evidence to dispute Microsoft’s attribution to a PRC‑linked group. The briefing covers a range of additional items: support for an Afghan adjustment bill; Pentagon efforts to analyze unidentified aerial phenomena; sanctions and human‑rights responses related to Iran’s morality police; concerns about Ukraine’s counteroffensive and the Wagner Group; DOD policy links between reproductive‑care access and military readiness; and practical issues from a Secret Service investigation into an illicit substance found in the West Wing to DOT‑domain email misrouting risks. On the domestic agenda, Jean‑Pierre defends the administration’s economic record—highlighting easing inflation and job gains under ‘Bidenomics’—voices support for striking workers and collective bargaining, previews a White House meeting with labor organizers, and reiterates active efforts to secure Julie Su’s Labor Secretary confirmation. Throughout, the White House emphasizes monitoring near‑term economic implications (including possible commodity‑driven price effects), continued diplomatic engagement, transparency where possible, and administrative readiness to pursue legislative and bilateral solutions.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### WHCA leadership transition
  Press secretary Karine Jean‑Pierre formally welcomes Kelly O’Donnell as the new president of the White House Correspondents’ Association and thanks outgoing partner Tamara Keith. She emphasizes the WHCA’s role coordinating press coverage and travel and expresses the White House’s eagerness to work with the new leadership. This sets the administrative tone before policy discussions begin.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE welcomes Kelly O’Donnell as the new WHCA president and thanks Tamara Keith for her partnership.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE states the WHCA and White House work closely to ensure press coverage and travel run smoothly.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says the White House looks forward to cooperating with the WHCA in the next chapter.
  #### Senator Tuberville holds
  Jean‑Pierre condemns Senator Tommy Tuberville’s use of holds on military promotions tied to social policy, arguing it hijacks the traditionally bipartisan NDAA process and harms national security. She and other administration officials warn the holds create leadership gaps, relocation uncertainty for military families, and retention risks. The White House calls on Republican senators to speak out and resolve the impasse.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says Senator Tuberville is hijacking the bipartisan NDAA process by delaying confirmations.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE warns the holds deprive the armed forces of leadership, risk a brain drain, and unsettle military families.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE reiterates President Biden’s call, quoted from his Helsinki remarks, for Republican senators to speak up against the holds.
  #### Response to Greene praise
  Jean‑Pierre highlights an unusual point of agreement with Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene: opposition to rural poverty and support for preserving Medicare and Social Security. She frames Greene’s criticism of Bidenomics as incoherent given Greene’s association with efforts to cut those same programs. The White House signals intent to continue promoting policies that reduce rural poverty and create local jobs.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says the administration agrees with Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene on opposing rural poverty and protecting Medicare and Social Security.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE notes Greene’s membership in the Republican Study Committee, which has proposed cuts to those programs.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE states President Biden looks forward to visiting Greene’s district to highlight high‑paying jobs created by administration policy.
  #### Black Sea grain suspension
  Admiral John Kirby condemns Russia’s decision to suspend participation in the Black Sea Grain Initiative and says it will worsen global food scarcity and raise prices. He stresses the initiative’s role in shipping over 33 million metric tons of grain—much of it to food‑insecure countries—and assigns full responsibility for the humanitarian consequences to Russia. The administration urges Moscow to reverse course and says it will cooperate with partners to keep grain flowing by other means where feasible.
- MR. KIRBY calls Russia’s suspension of the Black Sea Grain Initiative irresponsible and dangerous and says it will harm vulnerable populations worldwide.
- MR. KIRBY notes more than half of the 33 million metric tons shipped under the initiative went to developing countries and helped stabilize global food prices.
- MR. KIRBY urges Russia to immediately reverse its decision and says the U.S. will work with partners to enable grain to reach markets through alternative routes.
  #### Biden‑Netanyahu call readout
  Kirby provides a readout of President Biden’s call with Prime Minister Netanyahu, saying Biden reaffirms an ironclad commitment to Israel’s security and condemns recent terrorist acts. The leaders consult on countering Iran, joint exercises, and preserving the two‑state solution while expressing concern about settlement growth. They agree to consult regional partners and aim to convene an Aqaba/Sharm‑format meeting, and Kirby says Biden stresses broad consensus amid Israel’s judicial reform debate.
- MR. KIRBY says President Biden underscores an unwavering commitment to Israel’s security during his call with Prime Minister Netanyahu.
- MR. KIRBY reports the leaders discussed coordination to counter Iran and noted the U.S.‑Israel partnership as essential to preventing a nuclear Iran.
- MR. KIRBY says the two leaders agreed to consult regional partners and aim to convene an Aqaba/Sharm‑format meeting soon.
  #### Grain logistics and bridge attack
  Reporters ask about alternatives if Russia maintains its maritime blockade and whether escorting ships or striking the bridge are options; Kirby says overland routes (rail, truck) are possible but costly and hazardous, and explicitly rejects running a military blockade or escorting ships as an actively pursued option. On the Kerch bridge attack, Kirby says the administration sees no proven causation linking the strike to Russia’s decision and declines to adjudicate legality of Ukrainian target choices while reaffirming U.S. policy against enabling strikes inside Russia.
- MR. KIRBY says overland routes can move grain but are more cumbersome, hazardous, and less efficient than the maritime initiative.
- MR. KIRBY states the U.S. is not actively pursuing a military escort to run a Russian blockade of the Black Sea.
- MR. KIRBY says the administration sees no evidence proving that the bridge strike caused Russia’s suspension and will not make legal determinations about Ukrainian targeting.
  #### Israel judicial reform and protests
  Kirby says judicial reform in Israel is a topic of concern discussed with Israeli leaders and expects it will be on the agenda when President Biden meets President Herzog. He emphasizes the U.S. preference for reforms achieved through broad consensus and notes U.S. support for Israel’s democracy while also recognizing other regional security priorities. The White House welcomes steps to support Palestinian livelihoods and stresses restraint on unilateral measures like settlement expansion.
- MR. KIRBY says judicial reform will be part of the conversation when President Biden meets President Herzog but will not be the only topic discussed.
- MR. KIRBY emphasizes the U.S. preference that reforms proceed via compromise and the broadest possible consensus.
- MR. KIRBY reiterates U.S. support for Israel’s democracy while also welcoming steps to support Palestinian livelihoods and security.
  #### Chinese cloud intrusion
  Kirby describes a Microsoft cloud intrusion affecting unclassified email traffic that the U.S. discovered and alerted Microsoft to, enabling mitigations; investigation continues but Microsoft attributes the intrusion to a PRC‑linked hacking group and the administration has seen nothing to contradict that finding. The White House emphasizes ongoing cyber resilience work, recent national cybersecurity strategy release, and that prevention is continuous though not foolproof.
- MR. KIRBY explains the intrusion targeted Microsoft cloud services at the unclassified level and that the U.S. alerted Microsoft after detecting it.
- MR. KIRBY says the administration is still evaluating the impact and has not independently attributed the intrusion but sees no evidence to dispute Microsoft’s finding linking it to a PRC group.
- MR. KIRBY emphasizes cyber resilience as a top priority and cites a recently released national cybersecurity strategy.
  #### Afghan adjustment legislation
  Kirby expresses broad White House support for congressional efforts on an Afghan adjustment bill to ease pathways for Afghan allies to resettle and integrate. He stops short of pre‑committing presidential signature and says the administration will continue legislative engagement and support. The comment reiterates executive sympathy for facilitating durable solutions for Afghan partners.
- MR. KIRBY says the administration supports legislative efforts on an Afghan adjustment bill to help Afghan allies resettle and integrate.
- MR. KIRBY declines to pre‑judge whether the President will sign a particular bill but says the administration will stay in touch with Congress and support efforts.
  #### Unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP)
  Kirby confirms the Pentagon takes UAP sightings seriously and has established an organization to collate, analyze, and coordinate reporting across the military. He says UAPs have affected training ranges and pilot operations and that the administration seeks to be as transparent as possible consistent with national security. No conclusions are offered about the phenomena’s nature and analysis continues.
- MR. KIRBY says the Pentagon has stood up an organization to coordinate reporting and analysis of UAP sightings.
- MR. KIRBY notes UAP sightings have affected training operations and pilot readiness, which motivates further study.
- MR. KIRBY says the administration aims for transparency with Congress and the public consistent with national security constraints.
  #### Iran enforcement and morality police
  Kirby reiterates existing sanctions on members and entities tied to Iran’s morality police and condemns enforcement that restricts women’s dress, saying Iranian women should decide how to observe their faith. He says the U.S. will monitor developments and impose additional accountability if needed. The administration also links regional instability to Iran’s destabilizing activities and new deployments in the region.
- MR. KIRBY says the U.S. has sanctioned members and supporters of Iran’s morality police over abuses that have led to deaths and injuries.
- MR. KIRBY states Iranian women should be free to decide how to observe faith and dress in public and the U.S. will watch developments closely.
- MR. KIRBY links recent regional security steps to responses to destabilizing Iranian activity, including attacks on maritime shipping.
  #### Afghanistan‑Pakistan tensions
  In response to Pakistan’s complaints about militants and refugee‑linked instability, Kirby says the U.S. sees no indication Afghan refugees are committing terrorism and thanks Pakistan for hosting displaced Afghans. He declines to confirm any specific over‑the‑horizon strikes but reiterates the administration’s commitment to maintaining effective over‑the‑horizon counterterrorism capabilities.
- MR. KIRBY says the U.S. sees no indication that Afghan refugees in Pakistan are responsible for acts of terrorism.
- MR. KIRBY thanks Pakistan for hosting large numbers of Afghan refugees and says the U.S. will continue counterterrorism cooperation with Islamabad.
- MR. KIRBY declines to speculate on potential over‑the‑horizon actions but affirms U.S. capability and intent to use those tools as necessary.
  #### Mali domain email issue
  Reporters raise a Financial Times report about U.S. military emails misdirected to the Mali country domain due to a dot‑ml typo; Kirby defers to the Department of Defense and notes DOD uses outbound tools to block misaddressed emails and provides training to mitigate the risk. He stresses DOD awareness and mitigation actions but refers technical specifics to defense channels.
- MR. KIRBY defers technical details about the dot‑ml/dot‑mil email issue to the Department of Defense.
- MR. KIRBY says DOD has implemented outbound tools that block misaddressed outbound emails and has provided employee training to reduce the risk.
- MR. KIRBY notes the Pentagon has been mindful of the issue for some time and is working on fixes and training.
  #### Papal envoy on Ukraine humanitarianism
  Kirby previews Cardinal Zuppi’s visit as a papal envoy to discuss urgent humanitarian concerns in Ukraine, including the reported transfer of Ukrainian children to Russian‑controlled territory. The White House welcomes the engagement and plans to discuss humanitarian assistance and related issues. The meeting is framed as a diplomatic, humanitarian exchange.
- MR. KIRBY says Cardinal Zuppi is visiting as an envoy of the Pope to discuss humanitarian issues related to the war in Ukraine.
- MR. KIRBY highlights concerns such as Ukrainian children moved to Russian‑controlled areas as a topic of the discussions.
- MR. KIRBY says the administration looks forward to engaging on humanitarian steps and assistance.
  #### Ukraine counteroffensive and Wagner
  Kirby characterizes Ukrainian offensive progress as slower and harder than anticipated, noting entrenched Russian defenses, minefields, and strong Russian resistance. He says the administration cannot read Putin’s motives for decisions like the grain suspension and that it is too soon to assess the Wagner Group’s long‑term operational impact on the war. The U.S. remains focused on ensuring Ukraine has the tools it needs.
- MR. KIRBY says the Ukrainian counteroffensive is encountering tough, well‑entrenched Russian defenses and significant minefields.
- MR. KIRBY says he cannot attribute Russian decisions like the grain suspension to specific battlefield dynamics and that those motives remain uncertain.
- MR. KIRBY says it is too soon to judge lasting effects of the Wagner Group’s internal events on Russia’s military performance.
  #### Abortion policy and military readiness
  In answering a question about DOD policy and recruitment/retention, Kirby argues reproductive‑care access affects military readiness because servicemembers expect the military to take care of them and their families. He reports conversations with military spouses who say restrictive laws are influencing service decisions, and he warns that changes could harm retention in an all‑volunteer force. The administration frames supportive health policy as necessary to preserve force readiness and diversity.
- MR. KIRBY says access to reproductive care affects military recruiting, retention, and morale because service members expect their needs to be supported.
- MR. KIRBY reports military spouses told him restrictive abortion laws are affecting willingness to continue service or encourage family members to serve.
- MR. KIRBY frames reproductive‑care support as a foundational obligation of military leadership to maintain a diverse, volunteer force.
  #### Secret Service cocaine probe
  Jean‑Pierre says the White House has been briefed on the Secret Service investigation into a baggie of cocaine found in the West Wing and that the agency conducted a thorough probe which it publicly summarized. She defers operational and protocol questions to the Secret Service and expresses confidence in their investigation while declining to comment on investigative specifics. Any potential changes to security protocols are the Secret Service’s responsibility.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says the White House is briefed on the Secret Service’s public statement and believes the agency conducted a thorough investigation.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE defers questions about security protocol changes to the Secret Service, citing their role in campus security.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says the Secret Service noted hundreds of visitors pass through the area where the substance was found during the relevant weekend.
  #### Grain price inflation and U.S. economy
  Jean‑Pierre acknowledges the administration is monitoring the grain‑price spike triggered by Russia’s move and its potential implications for U.S. inflation trends. She reiterates recent positive U.S. inflation data—CPI down to 3 percent and twelve months of easing—and credits ‘Bidenomics’ for strong labor markets and wage gains while saying the administration will watch for any reversal. The White House does not offer a specific inflation forecast tied to the grain development but stresses close monitoring.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says the administration is monitoring recent commodity price increases after Russia’s grain suspension and their potential inflationary effects.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE highlights positive trendlines such as 12 consecutive months of easing CPI to 3 percent and strong labor market indicators.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE attributes recent economic gains to the administration’s policies, which she describes as ‘Bidenomics.’
  #### Labor actions and union meetings
  Jean‑Pierre affirms presidential support for workers’ rights to strike, collective bargaining, and fair pay, noting the President is the most pro‑union president in history. She previews a White House meeting with Senator Sanders and young union organizers from sectors such as Starbucks and Minor League Baseball and says the administration will be constructive on Teamsters–UPS negotiations while respecting requests not to intervene. The White House urges good‑faith negotiations and supports workers’ demands for fair compensation.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says President Biden supports workers’ rights to strike and collective bargaining and believes employees deserve fair pay and benefits.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE previews a White House meeting where the President and Senator Sanders meet young workers organizing unions across multiple industries.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says the administration will play a constructive role in labor negotiations and will respect parties’ requests regarding intervention.
  #### Julie Su confirmation effort
  Jean‑Pierre reiterates the administration’s active, ongoing commitment to securing Julie Su’s confirmation as Secretary of Labor, calling her qualified based on her record as Deputy Secretary. She says the White House is conducting coordinated efforts to support her nomination and will continue to fight for confirmation while declining to comment on private conversations with specific senators. The administration expresses confidence in Su and intends continued engagement with lawmakers.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says the White House remains committed to getting Acting Secretary Julie Su confirmed as Labor Secretary and considers her qualified.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says the administration is actively working to secure votes and will continue to engage with senators to advance the nomination.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE declines to provide details about private conversations with individual senators and refers to senators to speak for themselves.
  #### Electric vehicle market dynamics
  Jean‑Pierre responds to questions about EV inventory and pricing by noting EV sales hit a record high last quarter, rising nearly 50 percent year‑over‑year, while average EV prices declined about 20 percent. She highlights that EV models eligible for Inflation Reduction Act credits are seeing strong demand and argues increased availability and lower prices reflect successful policy implementation. The administration frames these trends as progress toward climate and jobs goals enabled by the IRA.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE says EV sales reached a record high last quarter and increased nearly 50 percent from the same period a year earlier.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE notes average EV prices are down about 20 percent year‑over‑year and that models eligible for Inflation Reduction Act credits see high demand.
- MS. JEAN‑PIERRE frames improved availability and falling prices as outcomes of the administration’s Inflation Reduction Act policies supporting climate goals and job creation.
