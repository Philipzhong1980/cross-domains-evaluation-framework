### Overview
Senior White House COVID-19 advisor Andy Slavitt, CDC Director Dr. Rochelle Walensky, and NIAID Director Dr. Anthony Fauci brief reporters on the state of the pandemic, emphasizing strong early progress vaccinating high-risk groups—especially long-term care residents and seniors—while acknowledging equity gaps and the need to scale supply, sites, and staffing through tools like the Defense Production Act and potential support from the American Rescue Plan. Walensky reports four weeks of declining cases and hospitalizations but warns that overall levels remain extremely high, deaths are affected by reporting lags, and nearly 700 confirmed variant cases across 34 states, predominantly B.1.1.7, pose a serious threat that makes continued masking, distancing, and avoidance of travel essential, including in the context of potential domestic travel testing. Fauci explains why the administration continues to follow two-dose regimens for Pfizer and Moderna, citing superior antibody levels, broader variant coverage, and theoretical risks of driving mutations with suboptimal immunity, and he underscores that current vaccines remain highly effective against the dominant strain and B.1.1.7 even as questions about long-term efficacy and virulence are studied. In Q&A, officials describe rapid expansion of genomic sequencing to better track variants, clarify available data on long-term care vaccinations and the initial reliance on pharmacy-specific registration systems, and reiterate that any relaxation of state mitigation measures is ill-advised while cases remain high and variants spread, stressing that community control and consistent in-school masking and distancing are key to reopening schools safely. Walensky notes ongoing, frequent engagement with governors to advocate for strong public health measures, and Slavitt frames the overall effort as a collaborative, whole-of-government and whole-of-country response aimed at reducing mortality now while building toward broader vaccination access for all.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Vaccination progress in high‑risk groups
Andy Slavitt outlines current vaccination progress, emphasizing prioritization of elderly people, long-term care residents, healthcare workers, and essential workers because they face the highest risk of hospitalization and death. He cites CDC data showing 4.8 million doses administered to 3.7 million long-term care residents and staff and notes that facilities with at least one clinic have a median of 80 percent resident coverage. Slavitt stresses that as these facilities complete vaccinations, doses will free up for subsequent priority groups, with over 17 million doses already administered to people 65 and older. He frames this as an expression of national solidarity and underscores that reducing mortality among the most vulnerable is the current focus while many others remain in the queue.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt reports that the Long-Term Care Program has administered over 4.8 million doses to more than 3.7 million of the most vulnerable Americans.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt notes that in skilled nursing facilities with at least one vaccination clinic, a median of almost 80 percent of residents have received at least one vaccine dose.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt states that of more than 40 million doses administered nationally, over 17 million have gone to people aged 65 or older.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt emphasizes that prioritizing seniors, long-term care residents, and frontline healthcare workers reflects a societal commitment to protect those at highest risk first.

#### Equity and scaling vaccination capacity
Slavitt stresses that vaccine rollout must address racial and ethnic disparities, noting that health equity is central to the federal response, with more detail promised in future briefings. He acknowledges that the pace of vaccination is slower than desired but outlines multiple strategies to accelerate it, including boosting supply via the Defense Production Act, expanding vaccination sites, and increasing the vaccinator workforce. He describes a whole-of-government and whole-of-country approach and links further scaling to congressional passage of the American Rescue Plan. The administration commits to using every available federal tool and to continually reducing death risk for vulnerable populations.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt states that vaccination rates in racial and ethnic communities that suffered disproportionately must increase and that health equity is a cornerstone of the federal effort.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt explains that the administration is using the Defense Production Act to increase vaccine supply.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt says the government is creating more vaccination locations, including large community sites and retail pharmacies, to improve access.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt notes that more vaccinators are being mobilized by authorizing retired clinicians and deploying the military.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt links further expansion of vaccination efforts to Congress delivering the American Rescue Plan to the President’s desk.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt reiterates that the administration treats COVID-19 as a national emergency and will leave no stone unturned in response.

#### Current pandemic trends and hospitalization data
Dr. Rochelle Walensky provides a data-driven update on the pandemic, noting four consecutive weeks of declining cases and hospital admissions but warning that overall levels remain very high. She reports an average of about 120,000 daily cases in early February, roughly 20 percent below the prior week yet still above last summer’s peak, and nearly 10,000 new hospital admissions per day with over 83,000 people currently hospitalized. Walensky also notes a reported 2.4 percent increase in average daily deaths, explaining that reporting delays from one state artificially elevated recent death figures. She underscores that CDC continues to monitor trends closely and that the situation is still serious despite improvements.
- Dr. Walensky reports that average daily cases between January 31 and February 6 are 119,900, a nearly 20 percent decline from the prior week but still higher than last summer’s peak.
- Dr. Walensky states that new COVID-19 hospital admissions average 9,977 per day, down about 17 percent from the previous week.
- Dr. Walensky notes that more than 83,000 Americans are currently hospitalized with COVID-19, a level she describes as much higher than in the summer and fall.
- Dr. Walensky explains that average reported deaths rose 2.4 percent to 3,221 per day due partly to a delayed report of 1,570 deaths from one state spanning several months.
- Dr. Walensky cautions that reporting delays can cause fluctuations in daily death statistics and that CDC is watching the data closely.

#### Variant spread and continued mitigation measures
Walensky highlights the emergence and spread of SARS-CoV-2 variants as a major ongoing threat that could reverse recent progress. As of February 7, she reports 699 confirmed variant cases across 34 states, including 690 of the B.1.1.7 lineage first identified in the UK. She urges the public to maintain strict mitigation behaviors—masking, distancing, avoiding travel and crowded or poorly ventilated spaces, and accepting vaccination when eligible. Walensky acknowledges pandemic fatigue but argues that collective adherence to these measures is necessary to turn the tide.
- Dr. Walensky reports 699 confirmed variant cases across 34 states, including 690 cases of the B.1.1.7 variant first identified in the UK.
- Dr. Walensky warns that variant proliferation is a serious concern that could reverse recent declines in cases and hospitalizations.
- Dr. Walensky urges people to wear masks, stay six feet apart from non-household members, avoid travel and crowds, and shun poorly ventilated spaces.
- Dr. Walensky encourages individuals to get vaccinated when it is available to them as part of a comprehensive prevention strategy.
- Dr. Walensky acknowledges the pandemic’s toll but maintains that sustained prevention behaviors are crucial to finally turning the tide.

#### One-dose versus two-dose vaccination strategy
Dr. Anthony Fauci addresses debate over whether to prioritize first doses at the expense of timely second doses given constrained supply. He explains that phase 3 trial data for Pfizer and Moderna show maximal 94–95 percent efficacy with the standard two-dose prime-boost regimen, whereas a single dose produces protective but significantly lower neutralizing antibody levels. Fauci argues that conducting a large randomized trial to compare one versus two doses would take months and likely become moot as supply grows. He further notes that higher antibody titers from the second dose increase breadth of protection against variants like B.1.1.7 and B.1.351 and warns that suboptimal immunity from one dose could theoretically encourage the selection of escape mutants.
- Dr. Fauci reiterates that Pfizer and Moderna vaccines achieve about 94 to 95 percent efficacy with the full prime-boost regimen used in phase 3 trials.
- Dr. Fauci says a large study comparing one versus two doses would take several months and might be moot by the time sufficient data are available because vaccine supply will have expanded.
- Dr. Fauci explains that a single mRNA dose produces neutralizing antibodies above a protective threshold but that the second dose increases titers roughly tenfold.
- Dr. Fauci emphasizes that higher antibody titers from a second dose broaden protection to cover both wild-type virus and key variants such as B.1.1.7 and B.1.351.
- Dr. Fauci warns that widespread suboptimal immunity from only one dose could theoretically exert selective pressure favoring the emergence of more mutant strains.
- Dr. Fauci concludes that the optimal strategy is to vaccinate as many people as possible with a first dose while ensuring on-time administration of second doses.

#### Vaccine effectiveness against B.1.1.7 and other variants
Fauci discusses how current vaccines perform against the B.1.1.7 variant and the implications of its expected spread. He notes that modeling suggests B.1.1.7 may become the dominant U.S. lineage by the end of March, which he characterizes as sobering. However, he stresses that existing mRNA vaccines remain quite effective against B.1.1.7, though they show reduced efficacy against the B.1.351 variant first identified in South Africa. Fauci concludes that adherence to public health measures and rapid vaccination are the best tools to limit variant evolution and maintain control over the epidemic.
- Dr. Fauci cites modeling that projects the B.1.1.7 lineage could become dominant in the United States by the end of March.
- Dr. Fauci states that current mRNA vaccines are quite effective against the B.1.1.7 variant.
- Dr. Fauci notes that vaccine efficacy is lower against the B.1.351 South African variant, heightening the need for control before it spreads widely.
- Dr. Fauci underscores that the best defense against variant evolution is to vaccinate people as quickly as possible and maintain strict public health measures.

#### Virulence of emerging variants and vaccine durability
In response to questions from Brian Karem, Fauci addresses whether SARS-CoV-2 is becoming more or less virulent and how long vaccines will remain effective. He notes that there is currently no evidence the virus is mutating into a less virulent form and that early UK data suggesting increased virulence of B.1.1.7 remains under evaluation. He explains that highly transmissible viruses can cause more hospitalizations and deaths overall even if individual-level virulence is unchanged. Fauci adds that vaccine trials continue for two years, and ongoing follow-up and regulatory reporting will clarify over time how long high efficacy persists and whether changes in virulence or escape affect performance.
- Dr. Fauci says there is no current data indicating that SARS‑CoV‑2 is mutating into a less virulent strain.
- Dr. Fauci notes that initial UK reports of increased virulence of B.1.1.7 require further confirmation and remain under evaluation.
- Dr. Fauci explains that higher transmissibility alone can increase total hospitalizations and deaths even without a change in per-case virulence.
- Dr. Fauci states that vaccine efficacy data will continue to accrue because the phase 3 trials are designed to run for two years with ongoing follow-up and FDA reporting.
- Dr. Fauci emphasizes that current vaccines remain highly efficacious—94 to 95 percent—against the dominant wild-type virus circulating in the United States.

#### Variant surveillance and genomic sequencing expansion
Kaitlan Collins asks about the true extent of variant spread and the limitations of sequencing capacity. Walensky responds that rising variant counts partly reflect a significant recent increase in sequencing and that CDC is rapidly scaling efforts through partnerships with state, commercial, and academic laboratories. She reports a roughly tenfold increase in sequencing over the last three weeks and plans to triple or quadruple that volume again, along with ensuring samples from every state. Walensky declines to estimate the total number of variant cases without more robust data but commits to improving surveillance to better define prevalence and proportions.
- Dr. Walensky explains that reported variant case numbers are increasing in part because sequencing volume has risen roughly tenfold in the past three weeks.
- Dr. Walensky says CDC is expanding collaborations with state public health labs, commercial labs, and academic centers to enhance genomic surveillance.
- Dr. Walensky anticipates that sequencing volume will increase to three to four times the current enhanced level in the coming weeks.
- Dr. Walensky notes that comprehensive sampling from every state will improve estimates of total variant burden and variant proportions nationwide.
- Dr. Walensky declines to give a ballpark estimate of actual variant numbers beyond detected cases but offers to follow up when data are stronger.

#### Potential COVID testing for domestic air travel
Collins also asks about Transportation Secretary Buttigieg’s comments regarding possible testing requirements for domestic flights. Slavitt, who has not seen the specific remarks, defers to CDC’s broader stance that people should avoid travel at this stage of the pandemic. Walensky adds that increased screening in high-gathering settings like airports would likely detect more asymptomatic infections and could serve as an additional mitigation layer. She reiterates that CDC’s primary recommendation is to avoid travel, but if travel occurs, testing could help reduce spread.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt reiterates CDC’s message that now is not the time to travel domestically if it can be avoided.
- Dr. Walensky states that more screening in settings where people gather, such as airports, would likely identify more asymptomatic cases.
- Dr. Walensky explains that, resources permitting, testing travelers would be an extra mitigation measure to decrease spread in addition to existing protections.
- Dr. Walensky emphasizes that her first recommendation is to avoid travel altogether, with testing considered a secondary protective step for those who must travel.

#### Relaxation of state mitigation measures
Kristen Welker asks for an assessment of states rolling back public health restrictions, such as Iowa easing masking and distancing rules. Walensky firmly discourages relaxation, emphasizing that the U.S. still reports over 100,000 daily cases and faces the added threat of variants. She argues that maintaining masking, distancing, and other mitigation measures is essential to controlling community spread, which in turn underpins safe school reopening. Walensky connects community transmission levels directly to school risk, noting that most school infections originate from the community rather than in-school spread when mitigation is followed.
- Dr. Walensky says she discourages states from relaxing mitigation measures while the country still records over 100,000 new cases per day.
- Dr. Walensky stresses that the U.S. has not yet controlled the pandemic and continues to face emerging variant threats.
- Dr. Walensky states that maintaining masking, distancing, and related measures is necessary to reduce community spread and hospitalizations.
- Dr. Walensky notes that most infections in schools are introduced from the community and that in-school transmission is low when masking and distancing are consistently applied.
- Dr. Walensky links safe school reopening to reducing community spread through sustained mitigation measures.

#### CDC school reopening guidance and teacher vaccination

Walensky responds that the guidance will come in the days ahead but does not detail its content or explicitly answer the teacher vaccination question in this exchange. She reiterates that data suggest limited in-school transmission when mitigation measures are in place and that outbreaks arise mainly from community introduction and lapses in masking or distancing. Her remarks imply that controlling community spread and maintaining school-based mitigations are central to safe operations while formal guidance is finalized.
- Dr. Walensky says new CDC guidance on school reopening will be released in the coming days.
- Dr. Walensky reiterates that data show little in-school COVID-19 transmission when masking and distancing are followed.
- Dr. Walensky explains that when schools do experience transmission, it often stems from community-introduced cases and breaches in mitigation measures.
- Dr. Walensky underscores that reducing community spread is the best strategy for enabling schools to open and remain open safely.

#### Federal engagement with governors on restrictions
Isaac Stanley-Becker asks how the administration communicates its opposition to easing restrictions to governors and whether federal influence is limited because these decisions are local. Slavitt and Walensky describe a high-touch, collaborative approach that relies on data and CDC guidance rather than directives. Walensky notes that she participates in at least weekly calls with governors and has had multiple one-on-one conversations to advocate for strong mitigation policies. Slavitt acknowledges the political and economic pressures governors face but emphasizes that the administration consistently shares the rationale for maintaining precautions, positions CDC as the authoritative voice, and avoids detailing private state-level discussions publicly.
- Dr. Walensky reports that she joins at least one weekly governors call and has spoken individually with numerous governors about mitigation policies.
- Dr. Walensky says she encourages states to reduce community spread to limit variants, lower hospitalizations, and bring the pandemic under control.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt acknowledges that governors operate under significant pressures but says the federal approach is collaborative and data-driven.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt emphasizes that the administration promotes adherence to CDC’s public health recommendations and seeks to persuade and partner with states rather than dictate actions.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt declines to characterize specific conversations with individual governors, arguing that doing so could be counterproductive.
