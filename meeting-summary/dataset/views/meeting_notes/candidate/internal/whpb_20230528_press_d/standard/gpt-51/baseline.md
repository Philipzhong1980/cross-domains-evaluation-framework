### Overview
White House officials brief reporters on a new bipartisan budget agreement between President Biden and Speaker McCarthy that couples a suspension of the debt limit through January 1, 2025 with a two‑year appropriations framework, which they say averts default risk, preserves key Biden legislative achievements, and delivers roughly $1 trillion in discretionary savings. They explain that the deal holds non‑defense discretionary spending roughly flat in 2024, raises both defense and non‑defense by 1 percent in 2025, fully funds veterans’ medical care and defense at the President’s requested levels, and includes a backstop that slightly cuts funding below 2023 levels if Congress fails to pass all 12 appropriations bills on time. Officials stress that Medicaid is untouched; SNAP work requirements are extended to ages 50–54 but offset by broad new exemptions for homeless individuals, veterans, and foster youth; and TANF work participation rules are tightened far less aggressively than in the House GOP bill to preserve state flexibility to support vulnerable children. They highlight that the agreement blocks Republican attempts to roll back the Inflation Reduction Act and core protections in major environmental statutes while enacting targeted NEPA and permitting reforms—such as single lead‑agency reviews and one‑ to two‑year review deadlines—that aim to accelerate clean energy and infrastructure projects without weakening substantive safeguards, and they clarify related provisions on IRS funding repurposing, administrative PAYGO with an OMB waiver, and regulatory studies on transmission and grid resilience.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Overall budget agreement context
White House officials explain that President Biden has reached a bipartisan budget agreement with Speaker McCarthy that pairs a debt limit increase with a two‑year spending framework. They stress that the deal prevents default until 2025, thereby protecting the ongoing economic recovery and historically low unemployment. Officials emphasize that it preserves major Biden legislative achievements like the Infrastructure Law, CHIPS and Science Act, and Inflation Reduction Act, while fending off what they describe as extreme cuts proposed in the House Republicans’ Limit, Save, Grow Act. The administration frames the outcome as consistent with past bipartisan budget deals that accompanied debt limit increases.
- A White House official says the agreement removes the threat of default until 2025, which they present as critical to sustaining the economic recovery and avoiding recession risk.
- A White House official states that the deal protects core Biden legislative accomplishments, including the Infrastructure Law, CHIPS and Science Act, and key climate and prescription drug provisions of the Inflation Reduction Act.
- A White House official notes that the President long insisted Congress has an obligation to prevent default but was open to parallel budget negotiations similar to bipartisan deals in 2015, 2018, and 2019.
- A White House official characterizes the outcome as beating back the deep spending cuts and policy changes sought in the House Republicans’ Limit, Save, Grow Act.
#### Two-year appropriations framework
Officials outline that the agreement sets a two‑year appropriations deal that keeps non‑defense discretionary spending roughly flat at 2023 levels in 2024, when agreed adjustments are included, and then raises both defense and non‑defense by 1 percent in 2025. They explain that some of the adjustments are financed by repurposing unobligated COVID relief and a portion of IRS mandatory funds, among other technical changes. Beyond 2025, the bill contains only non‑binding spending targets rather than enforceable caps. The administration portrays this structure as avoiding the far larger and longer‑term cuts Republicans initially sought while still reflecting the reality of divided government.
- A White House official explains that 2024 non‑defense spending is held roughly at 2023 levels, with both defense and non‑defense growing by 1 percent in 2025.
- A White House official notes that the agreement repurposes unobligated emergency COVID relief and some mandatory IRS funding as part of the appropriations adjustments.
- A White House official specifies that beyond 2025 there are no binding caps, only non‑enforceable appropriations targets in the legislation.
- A White House official emphasizes that the agreed levels are close to what a full‑year continuing resolution would have produced and follow roughly 16 percent non‑defense spending growth over the prior two years.
#### Protection of domestic programs and veterans
The administration stresses that the deal protects non‑defense spending that supports a wide range of domestic programs, such as research, nutrition services, and education. Officials contrast this with the approximately 22 percent non‑defense discretionary cut and decade‑long caps that House Republicans initially proposed. They also highlight that the agreement fully funds veterans’ medical care, including the PACT Act Toxic Exposure Fund, and funds the Defense Department at the President’s requested level. They present this as evidence that core domestic and national security priorities are preserved despite overall spending restraint.
- A White House official says the deal protects non‑defense spending for programs like scientific research, Meals on Wheels, and education from large proposed cuts.
- A White House official contrasts the outcome with the House Republicans’ earlier push for a 22 percent cut to non‑defense discretionary spending and 10 years of caps.
- A White House official states that veterans’ medical care, including PACT Act Toxic Exposure Fund levels, is fully funded consistent with the President’s 2024 budget.
- A White House official confirms that Defense Department funding in the agreement aligns with the levels requested in the President’s budget.
#### Treatment of Medicaid, SNAP, and TANF
Officials review how the agreement handles safety net programs relative to the House GOP proposal. They underscore that Medicaid is untouched and that they fought to prevent healthcare loss and increases in poverty. On SNAP, they acknowledge expanded work requirements to ages 50–54 but stress newly added, broad exemptions for homeless individuals, veterans, and foster youth that they expect will leave the overall number of people subject to work requirements roughly unchanged, with all changes sunsetting in 2030. On TANF, they describe more moderate adjustments to participation rules than in the McCarthy bill, designed to preserve state flexibility to continue providing cash assistance to vulnerable children and families.
- A White House official notes that the Limit, Save, Grow Act would have cut Medicaid, SNAP, and TANF in ways that risked millions losing health care and support, but the final agreement avoids those outcomes.
- A White House official states plainly that there are no changes to Medicaid in the agreement.
- A White House official explains that SNAP work requirements will be phased in for ages 50 to 54, but new exemptions for homeless people, veterans, and foster youth across ages 18–54 should keep the number of individuals subject to work requirements roughly constant.
- A White House official highlights that the SNAP policy changes sunset in 2030, giving Congress a chance to reconsider them.
- A White House official describes that, instead of resetting TANF work participation calculations to 2022 as in the McCarthy bill, the agreement resets the caseload reduction base year to 2015 and gives states two years to adjust, which they argue will significantly reduce the risk of states cutting cash assistance to vulnerable children.
#### Climate protections and blocking GOP rollbacks
The second White House official details how the agreement rejects multiple House Republican attempts to weaken environmental and climate protections. They say provisions that would have allowed hazardous mining waste storage without permits, weakened Clean Air Act reviews, and increased chemical exposure from refineries are not included in the final deal. The official further notes that efforts to roll back core elements of the Inflation Reduction Act’s clean energy investments, especially in disadvantaged communities, are blocked. They present the result as preserving major climate legislation and environmental statutes while still enabling process reforms.
- A White House official states that House Republicans sought permitting changes that would have allowed mining companies to store hazardous waste without permits and polluters to evade Clean Air Act review, but none of these measures are in the agreement.
- A White House official notes that Republican attempts to claw back tens of billions of dollars in Inflation Reduction Act clean energy investments, including in disadvantaged communities and for home energy rebates, are excluded from the deal.
- A White House official asserts that the agreement protects the substantive environmental safeguards in the Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, Toxic Substances Control Act, and the key provisions of the National Environmental Policy Act.
- A White House official characterizes the preservation of the IRA climate law as a direct outcome of President Biden’s leadership and commitment to environmental protection.
#### NEPA and federal permitting reforms
Officials explain that, alongside preserving core environmental laws, the agreement includes bipartisan permitting reforms designed to make federal project approvals more efficient. Key changes include designating a single lead agency responsible for one consolidated NEPA environmental review document per project and setting clear timelines: one year for less complex projects and two years for more complex ones. They emphasize that these process changes do not narrow NEPA’s substantive scope, shorten statutes of limitations, or restrict judicial remedies, distinguishing them from proposals like H.R. 1. The administration argues these reforms will accelerate deployment of clean energy, transmission, EV infrastructure, and related projects while maintaining environmental protections.
- A White House official says the agreement introduces measures to improve coordination, predictability, and certainty in federal environmental reviews by designating a single lead agency and requiring a single NEPA document per project.
- A White House official stresses that the NEPA changes do not curtail the statute’s substantive scope, do not reduce the statute of limitations, and do not limit standing or injunctive relief, in contrast to H.R. 1 proposals.
- A White House official argues that the reforms will help accelerate construction of solar, wind, EV chargers, transmission lines, and other infrastructure needed for a clean energy economy.
- A White House official describes the changes as harnessing government efficiency to better utilize the “tailwind” created by the Inflation Reduction Act and Infrastructure Law for clean energy deployment.
#### Deficit reduction and IRS funding repurposing
In response to questions, officials clarify how the agreement repurposes some IRS funding from the Inflation Reduction Act and what that means for enforcement and modernization. They note that the IRA provided a single $80 billion, 10‑year appropriation, and that the agreement redirects $10 billion in each of FY24 and FY25—about $20 billion total—without eliminating those specific years of funding, leaving roughly $60 billion to be spent over time. They argue this will not materially affect IRS operations in the near term but may require additional appropriations later in the decade. On deficit reduction, they estimate discretionary savings will be on the order of $1 trillion or more but defer to the forthcoming CBO score for precise numbers.
- A White House official clarifies that the IRS received a 10‑year $80 billion appropriation under the Inflation Reduction Act, not annual $10 billion tranches, so the $20 billion repurposed over FY24–25 reduces the overall pool to about $60 billion.
- A White House official says they do not expect the IRS to change its near‑term enforcement and modernization plans but acknowledge that additional funding might be needed from Congress in six to eight years.
- A White House official underscores that IRS enforcement dollars provide a strong return to taxpayers by allowing the agency to crack down on tax evasion, particularly among high‑income filers.
- A White House official estimates that discretionary spending savings from the agreement are likely to be in the $1 trillion or higher range, pending a formal CBO score.
#### Appropriations backstop and CR incentive
Officials describe an enforcement mechanism designed to encourage Congress to pass annual appropriations bills on time. If, by the end of the calendar year, all 12 regular appropriations bills are not enacted, both defense and non‑defense funding levels automatically revert to slightly below 2023 levels. The administration suggests this creates a strong incentive, particularly for lawmakers who oppose reductions in defense spending, to avoid extended continuing resolutions. This backstop is presented as a discipline mechanism to support timely budgeting within the new spending caps.
- A White House official explains that the agreement includes a backstop under which, if all 12 appropriations bills are not passed by year‑end, defense and non‑defense toplines drop slightly below 2023 levels.
- A White House official says the possibility of defense falling below 2023 levels is intended as an incentive for Congress to complete appropriations work.
- A White House official frames this mechanism as a way to motivate timely passage of spending bills instead of relying on long continuing resolutions.
#### NEPA timelines, FAST-41 comparison, and transmission
Responding to a question about FAST‑41, the White House clarifies that the agreement’s NEPA reforms are distinct from the existing FAST‑41 process, even though both seek efficiency. The official explains that FAST‑41 applies to a narrow set of large infrastructure projects with specific dashboarding and record‑of‑decision timelines, whereas the new provisions apply government‑wide to environmental review development, with one‑ and two‑year deadlines depending on project complexity. They argue these timelines will significantly speed up approvals for transmission and clean energy projects, citing prior multi‑year review timelines, and highlight provisions enabling agencies to share categorical exclusions for EV chargers and similar infrastructure. The agreement also directs FERC to study how transmission expansion supports clean, affordable, reliable, and resilient power.
- A White House official clarifies that the new permitting provisions are not a direct extension of FAST‑41 but a separate framework that focuses broadly on environmental review efficiency across agencies.
- A White House official notes that the agreement imposes deadlines of one year for less environmentally complex projects and two years for more complex ones to complete NEPA environmental reviews.
- A White House official cites specific examples of transmission projects like TransWest Express and Energy Gateway South that took four to six years to clear environmental review, arguing the new deadlines will accelerate similar projects.
- A White House official explains that agencies will be able to borrow each other’s categorical exclusions for EV chargers and related infrastructure, drawing on concepts from a Carper‑Schatz Senate bill.
- A White House official states that the agreement instructs the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission to study how transmission buildout advances clean energy deployment and grid resilience, reflecting the President’s priority on transmission.
#### Administrative PAYGO and OMB waiver
An official describes the administrative PAYGO provision, under which new administrative actions with budgetary impacts generally must be offset. They confirm that the Office of Management and Budget director receives broad authority to waive these requirements and that such waiver decisions are shielded from judicial review. The provision is temporary and sunsets after two years, meaning it will apply only during the remainder of the Biden administration’s current term. The administration presents this as a constrained, time‑limited framework that preserves executive flexibility over implementation of policies.
- A White House official confirms that the administrative PAYGO provision includes broad waiver authority for the OMB director, whose waiver decisions are protected from judicial review.
- A White House official notes that this administrative PAYGO framework, including the waiver authority, sunsets after two years and thus applies only for the remainder of the current administration’s term.
- A White House official indicates that the specifics of how the waiver will function are set out in the legislative text that will be released.
#### Non-defense discretionary (NDD) spending levels
In response to concerns about non‑defense discretionary cuts, officials argue that the agreement effectively holds NDD funding flat in nominal terms for 2024, with only a minimal change. They state that non‑defense funding outside of veterans’ programs will be about $637 billion versus $638 billion in 2023. They acknowledge that with only 1 percent growth in 2025, inflation will likely erode purchasing power, but they frame this as a compromise outcome in a divided government following real increases in prior years. The administration signals it will continue to push for higher NDD funding going forward.
- A White House official says 2024 non‑defense discretionary funding, excluding veterans, is effectively flat at about $637 billion compared with $638 billion in 2023.
- A White House official acknowledges that a 1 percent increase in 2025 implies a real cut after inflation but notes that NDD saw a roughly 4–5 percent real increase over the past two years.
- A White House official underscores that the modest NDD path reflects the constraints of divided government and says the administration will keep pressing for higher non‑defense funding in future negotiations.
#### Debt limit suspension and economic impact
Officials confirm that the agreement suspends the statutory debt limit through January 1, 2025, after which Treasury can again use extraordinary measures. They argue that pushing the debt limit beyond 2024 removes a major source of economic uncertainty and risk of default during this period. The White House links this to the broader context of a strong labor market and recovery, portraying the removal of default risk as stabilizing for markets, businesses, and households. They describe this as a central achievement of the deal alongside the spending framework.
- A White House official specifies that the bill suspends the debt ceiling until January 1, 2025, after which the Treasury Secretary can deploy extraordinary measures again.
- A White House official argues that taking the threat of default off the table into 2025 is a significant economic positive, given the current strong recovery and low unemployment rate.
- A White House official notes that business leaders and economists viewed a potential default as one of the biggest threats to the economic outlook, and the President is pleased the agreement removes that risk for the next two years.
#### Detailed TANF policy changes
In closing, an official provides a technical explanation of the TANF changes. Under current law, states must meet a 50 percent work participation rate for TANF cash assistance recipients, but they can reduce this target based on caseload declines since 2005, giving them flexibility to serve very vulnerable families. The McCarthy bill would have reset the base year to 2022, effectively forcing most states back to a 50 percent standard and potentially driving them to shift TANF funds away from cash benefits. The agreement instead resets the base year to 2015, provides states two years to implement the change, and preserves mechanisms that allow states to lower required participation by spending more TANF dollars, which the administration believes will significantly mitigate the risk of cuts to assistance for vulnerable children.
- A White House official explains that current TANF rules require a 50 percent work participation rate, but states reduce this requirement using caseload declines since 2005, which gives them flexibility to serve low‑income children whose parents are not working.
- A White House official says the McCarthy bill would have reset the caseload reduction base year to 2022, which experts warned would push states to divert TANF funding away from cash assistance because meeting a 50 percent work rate would be very difficult.
- A White House official states that the final agreement instead resets the base year to 2015, allows two years for implementation, and maintains options for states to lower their required work participation by increasing TANF spending.
- A White House official reiterates that, taken together, these TANF changes are far less severe than the McCarthy proposal and are expected to let states continue supporting vulnerable children and families.
#### SNAP and TANF impact estimates
Officials briefly address expected participation impacts from the SNAP and TANF changes but emphasize that formal scoring is pending. For SNAP, they reiterate their internal estimate that the expansion of work requirements to ages 50–54 will be roughly offset by the new exemptions for homeless individuals, veterans, and foster youth, leading to a similar number of people being subject to work rules before and after the deal. For TANF, they underscore the complexity of projecting caseload effects and rely on qualitative assessments that the agreed‑upon base year shift and transition period will avoid the worst‑case scenarios modeled under the House GOP bill. They note that CBO and other entities will provide more detailed quantitative estimates later.
- A White House official says their estimate is that the number of SNAP participants subject to work requirements will be roughly unchanged once both the age expansion and new exemptions are accounted for.
- A White House official notes that more precise estimates of SNAP and TANF impacts will come from CBO and other outside scorekeepers.
- A White House official characterizes TANF as more complex to model but reiterates that the negotiated changes should have far milder consequences than the McCarthy bill for vulnerable children receiving support.
