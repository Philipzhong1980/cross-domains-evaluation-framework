### Overview
  White House officials hold an embargoed background call to explain a bipartisan two-year budget agreement that suspends the debt limit through January 1, 2025, and aims to avert default while protecting recent legislative priorities. Officials say the deal keeps non-defense discretionary spending roughly flat in 2024 with a 1 percent increase in 2025, repurposes certain emergency and IRS funds, and fully funds veterans’ medical care and Defense at the President’s requested levels, while leaving post-2025 targets as non-binding. On safety-net programs, Medicaid remains unchanged, SNAP work requirements phase in up to age 54 but include broad exemptions and sunset in 2030, and TANF’s base-year change is moderated to 2015 with a two-year implementation window to preserve state flexibilities. The agreement also secures climate and environmental protections—preserving the Inflation Reduction Act and key environmental statutes—while introducing NEPA coordination and review deadlines (one year for less complex and two years for more complex projects), provisions to accelerate transmission and EV charger deployment, and an administrative PAYGO waiver for OMB that sunsets in two years; officials note CBO scoring is pending, discretionary savings could be large (potentially $1 trillion+), and IRA IRS funding is a 10-year appropriation the administration expects will not cause near-term operational disruption. The White House attributes these outcomes to presidential leadership and presents the package as a negotiated compromise that averts immediate economic risk while preserving core programmatic protections. 
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Deal overview and goals
  White House officials frame the bipartisan budget agreement as a measure that averts default, preserves recent legislative achievements, and rejects extreme proposals from the Limit, Save, and Grow Act. They say the deal sustains the economic recovery by taking the threat of default off the table into 2025 and protects laws such as the infrastructure law, CHIPS and Science Act, and key parts of the Inflation Reduction Act. The administration positions the agreement as a compromise that preserves priorities while enabling future budget negotiations with Republicans.
- White House official said the deal takes the threat of default off the table into 2025 and helps continue a strong economic recovery.
- White House official said the agreement protects the infrastructure law, the CHIPS and Science Act, and key climate and prescription drug elements of the Inflation Reduction Act.
- White House official said the deal beats back extreme demands from the Limit, Save, and Grow Act while leaving room for separate budget negotiations with Republican leadership.
#### Two-year appropriations structure
  Officials describe the agreement as a two-year appropriations deal that keeps non-defense discretionary (NDD) spending roughly flat in 2024 relative to 2023, and increases both non-defense and defense levels by 1 percent in 2025. They note several technical adjustments, including repurposing obligated emergency COVID relief and some mandatory IRS funding. Beyond 2025, the agreement contains non-enforceable targets rather than statutory caps.
- White House official said the agreement is a two-year appropriations deal with NDD roughly flat in 2024 and a 1 percent increase in 2025 for both non-defense and defense levels.
- White House official said the deal repurposes obligated emergency COVID relief and some mandatory IRS funding among other agreed adjustments.
- White House official said that beyond 2025 the legislation sets non-enforceable appropriations targets rather than binding caps.
#### Comparison to Republican proposal
  The administration contrasts the agreement with the Limit, Save, and Grow Act, saying it avoids the deep 22 percent non-defense cuts and 10-year caps Republicans initially proposed. Officials argue the outcome is similar to what would have occurred under a full-year continuing resolution and reflects recent bipartisan appropriations increases over the prior two years. They present the deal as retaining much of the administration's negotiated increases while recognizing the constraints of divided government.
- White House official said the agreement is much different than the 22 percent non-defense cut and 10-year cap originally proposed by House Republicans.
- White House official said the agreed levels reflect negotiations last calendar year and two years of bipartisan appropriations increases that raised non-defense spending about 16 percent over that period.
- White House official said the result is roughly equivalent to what would have happened under a full-year continuing resolution absent an agreement.
#### Veterans and defense funding
  Officials emphasize the deal fully funds veterans medical care, including mandatory PACT Act Toxic Exposure Fund levels in line with the President’s 2024 budget, and funds the Department of Defense at the President’s budget level. They present these as critical protections secured through negotiations. These funding commitments are described as central to the administration’s priorities within the agreement.
- White House official said the agreement fully funds veterans medical care, including PACT Act Toxic Exposure Fund levels from the President’s 2024 budget.
- White House official said the deal includes funding for the Defense Department at the President’s budget level.
#### Safety net framing
  The White House frames the safety net conversation by contrasting what was proposed in the Limit, Save, and Grow Act—large Medicaid, SNAP, and TANF cuts—with the administration’s goal of preventing increases in poverty or loss of healthcare. Officials say the President insisted protections to ensure vulnerable populations do not lose benefits and that the final agreement reflects those principles. They then walk through program-specific outcomes for Medicaid, SNAP, and TANF.
- White House official said the Limit, Save, and Grow Act would have resulted in Medicaid, SNAP, and TANF cuts that risked healthcare and assistance for millions.
- White House official said the President directed negotiators to ensure no one loses healthcare and that poverty does not increase as a result of the agreement.
- White House official said the administration walked through the three programs—Medicaid, SNAP, and TANF—to explain specific protections and reforms included in the deal.
#### Medicaid outcome
  White House officials state that the agreement makes no changes to Medicaid, which they present as a clear protection for health coverage. They emphasize that preserving Medicaid aligns with the administration’s principle that people should not lose healthcare as a condition of a debt-limit deal. The statement leaves Medicaid unchanged from current law under the agreement.
- White House official said there are no changes to Medicaid in this bill.
- White House official said preserving Medicaid was central to the President’s negotiating objectives so people do not lose healthcare.
#### SNAP changes and exemptions
  The agreement phases in SNAP work requirements up to age 54 (from 49) but includes exemptions for veterans, people who are homeless (broadly defined), and foster youth, and preserves state waiver flexibility. Officials estimate that, when exemptions are factored in, the number of people subject to work requirements will remain roughly the same and the SNAP changes sunset in 2030. The administration portrays these as temporary, limited changes made at the President’s insistence to protect vulnerable populations.
- White House official said the deal phases in SNAP work requirements to people up to age 54 while preserving state waiver flexibility.
- White House official said the agreement includes exemptions from SNAP work requirements for veterans, people who are homeless, and foster youth, and noted the statutory definition of homeless includes housing instability.
- White House official said they expect the number of people subject to SNAP work requirements to remain roughly the same after accounting for the age shift and the new exemptions.
- White House official said the SNAP changes are temporary and will sunset in 2030 to allow Congress to reevaluate them.
#### TANF reforms and flexibilities
  Officials explain that the Limit, Save, and Grow Act would have tightened TANF work participation rules by updating the base year to 2022, putting cash assistance for vulnerable children at risk. The final agreement instead updates the base year to 2015, gives states two additional years to implement changes, and preserves state flexibilities tied to spending choices that allow lower work participation rates. The administration contends these changes will be far less disruptive and preserve states’ ability to support vulnerable children.
- White House official said the McCarthy bill would have amended the TANF base year to 2022 and effectively pushed states toward a near-50 percent work participation requirement.
- White House official said the agreement amends the base year to 2015, gives states two years to implement the policy, and preserves flexibilities that allow states to continue supporting vulnerable children.
- White House official said experts expect the agreement’s TANF changes to have much less severe consequences than the McCarthy bill and to maintain states’ abilities to provide assistance.
#### Climate and environmental protections
  The White House stresses that the agreement protects the Inflation Reduction Act and substantive safeguards in the Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, Toxic Substances Control Act, and NEPA, rejecting Republican proposals to roll back environmental protections. Officials frame this as the President’s leadership preserving historic climate investments and preventing deregulation that would expose communities to pollution. They also highlight that the agreement includes bipartisan measures to improve government decision-making for project permitting without narrowing statutory environmental protections.
- White House official said the agreement protects the Inflation Reduction Act and its clean energy investments in disadvantaged communities.
- White House official said the deal preserves substantive environmental safeguards in the Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, Toxic Substances Control Act, and NEPA.
- White House official said the agreement rejects House Republican proposals that would have allowed hazardous-waste storage near communities or weakened Clean Air Act reviews.
#### Permitting reforms and NEPA coordination
  Officials describe provisions to accelerate federal environmental reviews by better coordinating NEPA processes, designating a single lead agency for single projects, and producing a single environmental review document on a clear timeline. They emphasize these changes do not reduce NEPA’s substantive scope, limit statutes of limitations, or curtail judicial remedies, and include specific deadlines—one year for less complex reviews and two years for more complex reviews. The administration presents these process reforms as accelerants for transmission, solar, wind, EV chargers, and other clean-energy infrastructure.
- White House official said the agreement designates a single lead agency to produce a single environmental review document on a clear timeline.
- White House official said the changes impose deadlines for environmental reviews: one year for less complex projects and two years for more complex projects.
- White House official said the permitting improvements do not curtail NEPA’s substantive scope, shorten statutes of limitations, or remove judicial remedies.
- White House official said streamlined reviews will help accelerate transmission, solar, wind, EV chargers, and other clean-energy projects.
#### Permitting specifics and transmission focus
  Officials argue that accelerated reviews will materially shorten timelines for transmission and renewable projects that historically took multiple years to clear environmental review, citing examples like TransWest Express and Energy Gateway South. The agreement also allows agencies to borrow categorical exclusions (for example, for EV chargers) and directs FERC to study and advance transmission build-out to improve grid reliability and resilience. The administration positions transmission build-out as a priority for both clean energy deployment and grid affordability/resilience.
- White House official said projects like TransWest Express and Energy Gateway South took five to six years for environmental review and that the agreement will accelerate such timelines.
- White House official said the agreement lets agencies borrow categorical exclusions—such as for EV chargers—so that one agency’s exclusion can be applied by others.
- White House official said the agreement directs FERC to study and advance transmission build-out to accelerate clean energy and improve grid resilience and reliability.
#### CBO scoring, deficit impact, and IRS funding
  When asked about the net deficit reduction, White House officials say they do not have a CBO score yet but expect discretionary savings likely in the $1 trillion or higher range and will await the formal score. Regarding IRS funding, officials explain the IRA provided $80 billion over ten years and that the $10 billion-per-year framing in some materials does not represent one-year appropriations; they argue the IRS can spend remaining funds over the coming years and do not expect near-term operational disruptions. Officials acknowledge the possibility of returning to Congress for additional IRS funds later in the decade.
- White House official said the administration does not yet have a CBO score and will wait for the official scoring to determine net deficit reduction.
- White House official said discretionary savings are likely in the $1 trillion or higher range but emphasized that CBO scoring is pending.
- White House official said the IRA’s $80 billion for the IRS was a 10-year appropriation and that the agency will continue to spend available funds over multiple years without immediate operational changes.
- White House official said the administration may need to return to Congress for additional IRS funding later in the 10-year window.
#### Backstop for appropriations and administrative PAYGO waiver
  Officials explain the agreement includes a backstop to incentivize Congress to pass annual appropriations: if the 12 spending bills are not enacted by year-end, defense and non-defense levels drop to slightly below 2023 levels. They also confirm the agreement grants the OMB director temporary waiver authority under an administrative PAYGO provision with protection from judicial review, and that this waiver authority sunsets after two years. The administration frames these as procedural tools tied to implementation of the deal.
- White House official said the agreement contains a backstop that reduces defense and non-defense levels to slightly below 2023 if the 12 appropriations bills are not passed by year-end.
- White House official said the agreement provides the OMB director broad waiver authority under an administrative PAYGO provision and that the waiver is protected from judicial review.
- White House official said the administrative PAYGO waiver authority sunsets after two years.
#### Timing of debt limit suspension
  Officials state the debt limit component of the deal is a suspension through January 1, 2025, and note that the Treasury Secretary can use extraordinary measures after that date. They emphasize that passing this legislation would remove the imminent threat of default for the next two years and that the administration views that as an important economic safeguard. The administration highlights this timing as a principal accomplishment of the agreement.
- White House official said the debt limit is suspended through January 1, 2025.
- White House official said the Secretary of the Treasury can employ extraordinary measures after the suspension expires.
- White House official said taking the threat of default off the table into 2025 is a significant upside for the economy and a major objective of the agreement.
