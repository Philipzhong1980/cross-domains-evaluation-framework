### Overview
The briefing centers on the Biden Administration’s warning that House Republicans are driving the government toward a preventable shutdown and the concrete consequences that would follow. Agriculture Secretary Tom Vilsack, drawing on his 2013 experience, details how a lapse in funding would almost immediately halt WIC benefits for nearly 7 million women and children, strain SNAP if prolonged, shut Farm Service Agency offices and rural housing lending, close national forests and associated tourism, furlough more than 50,000 USDA employees, and complicate food safety lab work, farm bill negotiations, and even foreign land monitoring, all while he criticizes House GOP budget proposals as “pathetic, punitive, and petty.” Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre reinforces that message by repeatedly labeling the potential lapse a “Republican shutdown,” arguing that Republicans are reneging on a bipartisan budget deal the President already negotiated, and warning of broader impacts on programs from education and housing to student loan servicing, while declining to detail any direct Biden outreach to House leaders. She also addresses other issues: announcing new deputies for the Office of Gun Violence Prevention and extending Yom Kippur greetings; defending the President’s non-public COVID booster as a scheduling matter amid a broader vaccination push; responding cautiously to Senator Menendez’s indictment by calling it serious but deferring decisions to him and Senate leaders; and fielding questions on border management, Nagorno-Karabakh, and Northeast Asian diplomacy, often referring specifics to relevant agencies. A major additional focus is President Biden’s historic decision to join UAW workers on a Detroit picket line, which Jean-Pierre frames as consistent with his identity as the most pro-union president and his belief that record automaker profits should support a “record UAW contract,” even as she declines to endorse specific wage or hour demands and insists the details remain for the union and companies to negotiate, portraying his role as one of solidarity with workers rather than direct interference in collective bargaining.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Impacts of potential Republican-led shutdown
Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre opens by framing a looming government funding lapse as an “extreme Republican shutdown” and signals that the administration will highlight concrete impacts on communities, the economy, and national security. She emphasizes that House Republicans are demanding “reckless cuts” as a condition of keeping the government open, and that the White House intends to hold them accountable. The remarks set the political and policy backdrop for Secretary Vilsack’s detailed briefing. The administration’s position is that the shutdown is preventable and entirely within Congress’s control to avoid.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE states that House Republicans’ “chaos” is marching the country toward an “extreme Republican shutdown.”
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says the White House will call out how a shutdown would damage communities, the economy, and national security.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE asserts the administration will hold “extreme House Republicans” accountable for demanding reckless cuts as a condition of keeping government open.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE previews that Secretary Vilsack will explain how a shutdown would affect families, including WIC recipients, farmers, and rural homeowners.
#### USDA mission and shutdown context
Secretary Tom Vilsack outlines USDA’s core responsibilities—supporting farmers, ensuring a stable food supply, and providing nutrition assistance—before explaining why a shutdown is particularly disruptive. Drawing on his experience during the 2013 shutdown, he characterizes the current scenario as an “extreme House Republican effort” to steer the government toward a preventable closure. He positions his briefing as an overview of how rural Americans, farmers, and families in need would be affected. The thrust is that USDA’s broad mission makes it a critical agency whose operations cannot easily pause without real harm.
- SECRETARY VILSACK notes USDA supports farmers, ensures a stable food supply, and provides vital nutrition assistance to millions of Americans.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says an extreme House Republican effort is recklessly steering the government toward a preventable shutdown that puts USDA services at risk.
- SECRETARY VILSACK recalls being Agriculture Secretary during the 2013 shutdown and witnessing needless disruption firsthand.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says he will highlight impacts on rural Americans, farmers, and families in need if a shutdown occurs.
#### WIC and SNAP impacts under shutdown
Vilsack details how a shutdown would almost immediately disrupt the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC), which serves nearly 7 million pregnant women, new mothers, and young children and covers over half of U.S. newborns. He explains that WIC funding effectively stops when the shutdown begins, with only a small USDA contingency fund and any unspent state benefits potentially extending services by days, not weeks. By contrast, SNAP (food stamps) can continue at least through October, but a prolonged shutdown would create serious consequences for SNAP as well. The administration stresses there is no legal authority to extend WIC absent congressional action, underscoring the severity and immediacy of the risk.
- SECRETARY VILSACK explains that WIC serves nearly 7 million participants and covers over 50 percent of U.S. newborns.
- SECRETARY VILSACK warns that during a shutdown, WIC benefits would be denied within days or weeks depending on the state, leading to millions of mothers and children losing nutrition assistance.
- SECRETARY VILSACK clarifies that SNAP will continue at least through October but would face serious consequences if the shutdown lasts longer.
- SECRETARY VILSACK states there is no authority to extend WIC funding beyond a day or two of USDA contingency funds and limited unspent state benefits.
- SECRETARY VILSACK emphasizes that the vast majority of WIC participants would see an immediate reduction and elimination of benefits once a shutdown begins.
#### Farm loans, rural housing, and rural economy
Vilsack highlights that a shutdown would halt critical financial services for farmers and rural families at a time of year when those services are most needed. Farm Service Agency (FSA) offices in nearly every county would close, blocking access to marketing assistance loans that many farms rely on for cash flow, harvest operations, and survival. He also notes that USDA-guaranteed or direct rural housing loans for first-time homebuyers would stop, potentially causing borrowers to lose home purchase deals. The overarching message is that a shutdown creates acute financial stress for small and mid-sized farmers and rural households with no policy justification.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says that during harvest season farmers depend on marketing loans to secure decent prices, but FSA offices in virtually every county would shut down in a shutdown.
- SECRETARY VILSACK warns that with FSA offices closed, farm loans are not available, jeopardizing the ability of small and mid-sized farms to get credit and pay bills.
- SECRETARY VILSACK explains that newlyweds and rural families seeking USDA-backed or direct home loans would be unable to close, and some could lose their deals entirely.
- SECRETARY VILSACK stresses that the resulting financial stress could mean unharvested crops, no profits, and ultimately, the loss of farms.
#### National forests, tourism, and natural resources
The Secretary describes how USDA’s stewardship of 195 million acres of national forests and grasslands is another area vulnerable to shutdown. Recreational areas that draw visitors and tourism dollars to rural communities would be closed, eliminating family trips and the local economic activity they generate. He also connects this to firefighting capacity, pointing out that proposed House cuts and the failure to fund a “firefighter fix” could undermine the staffing needed to address tens of thousands of wildfires. The administration argues that the shutdown would ripple through both recreation economies and natural resource management.
- SECRETARY VILSACK notes USDA manages 195 million acres of national forests and grasslands that support recreation and tourism-dependent communities.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says that in a shutdown, national forests close, canceling family trips and halting tourism spending that supports local jobs.
- SECRETARY VILSACK warns that House Republican cuts and failure to fund the firefighter pay fix put at risk the workforce combating roughly 44,000 Western U.S. fires this year.
- SECRETARY VILSACK frames these impacts as real consequences for real people tied to a preventable shutdown.
#### USDA workforce furloughs and service disruption
Vilsack estimates that more than 50,000 USDA employees would be furloughed in a shutdown, meaning they would go temporarily unpaid and their communities would experience reduced economic activity. He stresses that the furloughs would span FSA, Rural Development, Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS), Forest Service, Agricultural Research Service (ARS), and critical administrative staff across every county in the country. This broad workforce disruption would hinder everything from conservation assistance to research and day-to-day operations. He characterizes the anticipated disruption as “incredibly disruptive” to both internal functioning and the public.
- SECRETARY VILSACK anticipates that more than 50,000 USDA employees would be furloughed in a shutdown and would not receive paychecks.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says furloughs would impact FSA offices, rural development offices, NRCS conservation staff, some Forest Service personnel, ARS researchers, and administrative support.
- SECRETARY VILSACK emphasizes that USDA has a presence in every U.S. county and that the shutdown’s effects would literally touch every county.
- SECRETARY VILSACK notes that even if some frontline staff remain, the lack of administrative support can prevent work from getting done, making the shutdown “incredibly disruptive.”
#### Continuing resolution and criticism of House GOP cuts
Responding to questions about a potential continuing resolution (CR), Vilsack criticizes the House Agriculture appropriations bill and any CR that simply carries forward its cuts. He reiterates his earlier description of that budget as “pathetic, punitive, and petty,” arguing that it fails to fund key priorities like WIC and firefighting pay. He underscores that the broader point is not only the shutdown itself but also the underlying spending proposals that would weaken USDA’s ability to serve the public. The administration’s stance is that Republicans are reneging on an earlier bipartisan budget deal without substantive justification.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says the Republican-proposed CR would extend extreme cuts from the House Agriculture appropriations bill.
- SECRETARY VILSACK repeats his characterization of the House agriculture budget as “pathetic, punitive, and petty.”
- SECRETARY VILSACK notes that the proposed funding levels fail to support WIC and a necessary firefighter pay fix.
- SECRETARY VILSACK reiterates that there are real-world consequences for Americans when Congress allows a shutdown, especially one that “ought not to happen.”
#### Food safety and inspection during shutdown
Vilsack addresses concerns about food safety under a shutdown by clarifying the division of responsibilities between USDA and FDA. USDA will maintain inspection of meat, poultry, and processed eggs, and those inspectors are considered essential and will remain on the job. However, he notes that FDA’s broader food oversight may be more limited and suggests reporters direct detailed questions to FDA. He also warns that if lab analysis is required—for example, to detect animal diseases—reduced lab capacity in a shutdown could slow detection and indirectly threaten the food supply.
- SECRETARY VILSACK explains that USDA is responsible for meat, poultry, and processed eggs, while FDA oversees all other foods.
- SECRETARY VILSACK assures that USDA food inspectors for meat, poultry, and processed eggs will remain onsite during a shutdown.
- SECRETARY VILSACK notes he cannot speak for FDA and encourages questions to be directed to that agency regarding their shutdown plans.
- SECRETARY VILSACK cautions that lab work may be curtailed in a shutdown, potentially slowing the detection of animal diseases and affecting the food supply.
- SECRETARY VILSACK illustrates the split oversight with a joke that a pepperoni pizza’s safety is guaranteed by USDA, while a cheese pizza falls under FDA.
#### Farm bill timeline and shutdown’s effect on legislation
Reporters ask about prospects for passing a new farm bill and how a shutdown might interfere. Vilsack says committee leaders are working hard but warns that deep budget cuts—up to 20–30 percent in some proposals—would undermine USDA’s ability to implement any farm bill. He explains that USDA provides technical assistance to lawmakers during farm bill drafting, and that a shutdown would leave legislative calls unanswered, delaying negotiations. He argues this underscores the irrationality of risking a shutdown when rural communities and the farm economy need certainty and consistency.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says farm bill leaders in both chambers are working to complete a new bill but must use all budget tools to address their challenges.
- SECRETARY VILSACK warns that proposed cuts of 20–30 percent to USDA’s budget would undermine the implementation of any farm bill, regardless of its content.
- SECRETARY VILSACK explains that in a shutdown, when lawmakers call USDA for technical assistance on farm bill language, no one will answer because staff are furloughed.
- SECRETARY VILSACK stresses that the farm community and rural areas need certainty, and a shutdown makes it “pretty tough” to advance the farm bill.
#### Chinese purchases of U.S. farmland and CFIUS
Switching topics, a reporter asks about Chinese entities buying U.S. farmland and potential national security or food security risks. Vilsack cites a North Dakota case where a Chinese interest sought land near a military installation as an example of legitimate concern. He says USDA has advocated for a bigger role in the CFIUS review process and additional tools to better track foreign land transactions, noting that county-level deed recording makes comprehensive, real-time tracking difficult. He expresses confidence in current efforts to identify problematic cases but acknowledges the system is not foolproof and could be improved.
- SECRETARY VILSACK references a North Dakota case where a Chinese interest attempted to purchase land near a military installation as a legitimate national security concern.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says USDA has pushed to be more engaged in the CFIUS process to review foreign acquisitions that might pose risks.
- SECRETARY VILSACK notes that real estate deeds are filed at county recorder offices, making it difficult to systematically identify foreign, including Chinese, purchasers in real time.
- SECRETARY VILSACK states USDA is confident in its current work identifying cases like North Dakota but believes processes could be improved to make the system closer to foolproof.
- SECRETARY VILSACK suggests better tools to collect and analyze land transaction information more quickly would help determine if threats exist.
#### Crop insurance, drought relief, and shutdown interactions
Vilsack clarifies that crop insurance will not be directly affected by a government shutdown, providing some reassurance for farmers. However, he warns that failure to pass or extend the farm bill could eventually impact crop insurance authorities. He also notes that certain specialty or region-specific crops, particularly in Florida and Georgia, may need additional assistance beyond existing safety net programs due to severe drought and other challenges. He folds this into the broader argument that Congress must avoid a shutdown so it can focus on legislation like the farm bill and avoid disruptions to WIC, firefighting, farm loans, and rural home loans.
- SECRETARY VILSACK says the shutdown will not impact crop insurance operations directly.
- SECRETARY VILSACK warns that failure to extend or reauthorize the farm bill could affect crop insurance authorities over time.
- SECRETARY VILSACK identifies Florida and Georgia as states where certain crops may need additional help beyond current safety net programs due to significant challenges.
- SECRETARY VILSACK argues that avoiding a shutdown is necessary so Congress can work on the farm bill and prevent disruption to WIC, firefighter funding, farm loans, and rural home loans.
- SECRETARY VILSACK reiterates that there are “real impacts to real people” when Congress and House Republicans do not complete their work on time.

#### Biden’s COVID booster and vaccination messaging
A reporter asks why President Biden received his latest COVID booster out of public view at a time when the administration is promoting updated vaccines. Jean-Pierre responds that the President’s schedule dictated the timing and format of the vaccination and that he received it at the earliest feasible moment. She argues that a presidential “photo op” is not required for effective public messaging and notes that a physician’s memo documenting the shot has been released. She stresses that the White House remains engaged in a robust campaign to encourage uptake of the updated COVID vaccine, RSV shots, and flu vaccines, independent of whether the President’s booster is televised.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says President Biden received his updated COVID vaccination at the earliest time that fit his very busy schedule.

#### White House stance on clean CR and shutdown blame
On questions about a clean continuing resolution in the Senate and overall shutdown dynamics, Jean-Pierre insists the burden rests on House Republicans to honor the bipartisan budget deal reached in May. She repeatedly frames any lapse in funding as a “Republican shutdown” and says it is Congress’s basic job to keep the government open. She highlights potential impacts—including on WIC, food safety, education, housing, law enforcement, and programs like Meals on Wheels and Head Start—to underscore the stakes for the public. She maintains that the President already negotiated the deal and that House Republicans must now “stick to it” rather than seek leverage for what she calls “extreme” policy demands.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE says it is up to House Republicans to fix the funding situation because a bipartisan budget deal was already made in May.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE repeatedly labels a potential funding lapse as a “Republican shutdown.”
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE argues that a deal is a deal and that Republicans should adhere to the agreement negotiated with the President and passed by both chambers.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE warns that a shutdown would hurt WIC participants, impact food safety, disrupt education and housing programs, and affect services like Meals on Wheels and Head Start.
#### White House response to Senator Menendez indictment
Asked about Senator Bob Menendez’s refusal to resign following bribery charges, Jean-Pierre calls the case a “serious matter” and says the White House believes his decision to step down from his committee chairmanship was “the right thing to do.” She declines to say whether President Biden believes Menendez should remain in the Senate or in the Democratic Party, stating that any further decisions are up to the senator and Senate leadership. The administration keeps a cautious distance from intraparty calls for resignation, while acknowledging the gravity of the allegations.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE describes the Menendez case as a serious matter.

She frames Biden as supportive of workers’ right to collective bargaining and a “win-win” outcome while staying out of the detailed terms of the negotiations. Under questioning, she explicitly confirms that Biden is standing with the workers rather than remaining neutral, though the companies’ interests must still be resolved at the bargaining table.
