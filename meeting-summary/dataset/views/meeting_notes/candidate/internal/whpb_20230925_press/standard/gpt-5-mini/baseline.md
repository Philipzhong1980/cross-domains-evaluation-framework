### Overview
  Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and Agriculture Secretary Tom Vilsack brief reporters on the real-world consequences of a potential House-driven government shutdown, repeatedly emphasizing that an existing bipartisan deal should be honored to prevent immediate harm. Secretary Vilsack details how a shutdown would cut WIC benefits for nearly 7 million women and children, constrict SNAP over time, halt farm service offices and marketing loans at harvest, close national forests, and furlough roughly 50,000 USDA employees, while noting that USDA meat and poultry inspections would continue even as FDA-covered inspections and lab analysis could be disrupted. The briefing also covers related risks: delayed farm-bill work and technical assistance, limitations monitoring foreign farmland purchases, crop insurance resiliency but regional drought needs, and student-loan servicing vulnerabilities if a shutdown endures. Jean-Pierre addresses additional topics raised by reporters, confirming the President received his COVID booster privately but pointing to public vaccine promotion, announcing the President will join a UAW picket line in Detroit in solidarity with workers while declining to litigate negotiation specifics, deferring operational questions on the border, Nagorno-Karabakh, and trilateral diplomacy to relevant agencies, and reiterating that the administration holds House Republicans responsible for preventing a shutdown and will continue to press the practical impacts on families and communities.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Republican shutdown impacts
  Press secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and Secretary of Agriculture Tom Vilsack frame an imminent House-led shutdown as an avoidable crisis that would disrupt USDA services. They emphasize potential harms to nutrition programs, rural economies, public lands, and USDA employees. The brief establishes the administration's position that House Republicans must adhere to an existing deal to prevent those harms.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration is calling out how a shutdown would damage communities, the economy, and national security.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said an extreme House Republican shutdown would put many critical USDA services at risk.
- SECRETARY VILSACK recalled his experience during the 2013 shutdown to underscore the needless disruption a shutdown causes.
  #### WIC and SNAP continuity
  Secretary Vilsack explains that the WIC program would cease almost immediately in a shutdown while SNAP would continue only for a limited time. He emphasizes the scale of the potential loss by noting WIC serves nearly 7 million pregnant women, new mothers, and young children. The administration warns of immediate nutrition gaps for vulnerable families if a shutdown occurs.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said WIC supports nearly 7 million pregnant moms, new mothers, and young children and would stop immediately when a shutdown occurs.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said SNAP will continue at least for the month of October but could face serious consequences if a shutdown extends beyond that period.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said USDA contingency funds might extend WIC for a day or two and some states may have residual benefits to stretch for a week.
  #### Authority to extend WIC
  Reporters asked whether the administration has legal authority to extend WIC funding beyond short contingency periods during a shutdown. Secretary Vilsack states there is no authority analogous to SNAP that would permit a sustained extension of WIC once a shutdown begins. The implication is that only congressional action can prevent an immediate cutoff of WIC benefits.
- An unidentified reporter asked whether the administration could exercise authority to extend WIC funding past one or two days during a shutdown.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said there is no authority to extend WIC in the way SNAP can be sustained for a limited period.
  #### Farm loans and stress
  The secretary outlines how farm service operations and marketing assistance loans are time-sensitive during harvest and would be disrupted by a shutdown. He warns that inability to access loans risks forcing small- and mid-sized farms into financial distress or losing their operations. Vilsack frames the disruption as avoidable because a bipartisan deal already exists in Congress.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said farm service agency offices would shut during a shutdown, preventing farmers from accessing marketing loans at a critical time.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said loss of loan access creates stress that can jeopardize small- and mid-sized farming operations and risk farms being lost.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said a deal passed by the Senate and a majority of House members exists and that there is no reason to face this shutdown.
  #### Food safety inspections
  Reporters asked how a shutdown would affect food inspections and public health. Vilsack clarifies that USDA inspectors for meat, poultry, and processed eggs would remain onsite, while FDA-managed inspections (for other foods) may be affected, and lab analysis capabilities could be constrained. The answer signals a partial continuity of critical food inspections but raises concerns about broader testing and detection capacity.
- Weijia asked about previous shutdown impacts on food and environmental inspections and public health.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said USDA will continue inspections for meat, poultry, and processed eggs but noted FDA responsibilities for other foods may be affected.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said lab analysis needs could be impacted, potentially slowing detection of animal diseases and affecting food supply.
  #### Furloughs and staffing impacts
  Vilsack estimates more than 50,000 USDA employees could be furloughed in a shutdown, affecting every county through closure of various USDA offices. He lists affected offices including FSA, rural development, NRCS, Forest Service, ARS researchers, and administrative staff. The administration highlights the cascading local economic impacts when employees do not receive paychecks.
- An unidentified reporter asked which programs and workers would be impacted by the potential 50,000 furloughs.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said furloughs would be across the board in every county, affecting FSA offices, rural development, NRCS conservation staff, some Forest Service employees, ARS researchers, and administrative staff.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said furloughs mean paychecks stop and local economies get impacted.
  #### Farm bill timeline risks
  Reporters probed whether a shutdown or funding cuts would jeopardize passage or implementation of the farm bill. Vilsack said committee leaders are working but warned that severe budget cuts would undercut any farm bill's implementation and that a shutdown would complicate congressional and USDA support. He commits USDA to provide technical assistance and to seek certainty for rural communities.
- An unidentified reporter asked about confidence that a new farm bill will pass by year-end or whether an extension is more likely.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said committee leaders are working to get the farm bill done and that USDA will provide technical assistance to help reach a 'yes.'
- SECRETARY VILSACK warned that underfunding the budget by large percentages would undermine a farm bill's implementation and that a shutdown makes timely passage much harder.
  #### Legislative affairs disruptions
  Reporters asked whether a shutdown would impede USDA staff from providing on-the-spot technical assistance to members of Congress negotiating the farm bill. Vilsack explained that during a shutdown phones will not be answered and staff will not be present to respond to congressional requests, directly slowing negotiation and problem-solving. He uses this to argue the practical absurdity of a shutdown while farm bill work remains unfinished.
- A reporter asked whether a shutdown would affect USDA legislative affairs and negotiations for the farm bill.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said staff would be unavailable to answer calls for technical assistance during a shutdown, hampering members' ability to refine farm bill language.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said this operational gap illustrates why a shutdown is 'ridiculous' and undermines Congress doing its job.
  #### Foreign farmland purchases
  On questions about Chinese purchases of U.S. farmland and national security, Vilsack acknowledged legitimate concerns in cases near sensitive sites and urged stronger engagement with the CFIUS process. He said current systems (e.g., county recorder filings) make real-time monitoring difficult and recommended improving information collection and analysis. The administration signals interest in additional tools and faster screening to assess potential threats.
- Phil asked whether Chinese purchases of farmland present a national-security or food-security threat.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said there are legitimate concerns in specific circumstances and advocated for greater USDA engagement in the CFIUS process.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said the current monitoring setup (county recorder filings) makes it hard to quickly identify foreign purchasers and recommended improving information collection and analysis.
  #### Crop insurance and drought aid
  Reporters asked whether a shutdown would affect crop insurance and whether supplemental drought funding is needed. Vilsack said crop insurance is not affected by a shutdown but reiterated that failure to pass or implement a farm bill could create risks. He noted certain regions, like Florida and Georgia, may need additional targeted help despite an overall safety net.
- An unidentified reporter asked whether crop insurance would be affected and whether supplemental drought funding is needed.
- SECRETARY VILSACK said crop insurance is not impacted by a shutdown but stressed that a delayed or underfunded farm bill could jeopardize longer-term supports.
- SECRETARY VILSACK identified Florida and Georgia as states with significant needs that may require additional help.
  #### White House personnel update
  Karine Jean-Pierre briefly welcomed two new deputy directors for the White House Office of Gun Violence Prevention and noted recent administration announcements. She also offered Yom Kippur greetings on behalf of the President and First Lady. These items serve as internal administrative and ceremonial updates unrelated to the shutdown discussion.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE welcomed Greg Jackson and Rob Wilcox as new deputy directors of the Office of Gun Violence Prevention.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE extended best wishes from the President and First Lady for an easy fast and meaningful Yom Kippur to Jewish communities observing the holiday.
  #### President's COVID booster timing
  Reporters asked why the President received his updated COVID booster privately rather than in public. Jean-Pierre and her team said the President got the booster at the earliest workable time in his schedule and pointed to the physician's memo. They stressed that a photograph or public demonstration was not required for the administration's broader vaccine-promotion efforts.
- Will asked why the White House chose for the President to receive his latest COVID booster out of public view.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President received the booster at the earliest available time in his busy schedule and directed reporters to the physician's memo.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration will continue robust public engagement to encourage vaccinations and that a photo op was not necessary.
  #### Continuing resolution posture
  Reporters asked whether the White House is working with the Senate on a clean continuing resolution (CR) and whether there is reason for optimism. Jean-Pierre reiterated the administration's position that this is a problem for House Republicans to fix and that a bipartisan deal previously negotiated by the President is being disregarded. She emphasized the human consequences of a shutdown and called on House members to adhere to the deal.
- A reporter asked whether the White House is working with the Senate on a clean CR.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration's position is that House Republicans must fix the problem and honor the bipartisan deal the President brokered.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE warned that a shutdown would jeopardize programs such as WIC and other services that support families and communities.
  #### Senator Menendez reaction
  Reporters asked the President's view after Senator Menendez stepped down from his chairmanship amid serious allegations. Jean-Pierre described the matter as serious and said stepping down from the chairmanship was the right decision, while deferring other determinations to the senator and Senate leadership. The White House positions itself as acknowledging the gravity of the situation without pronouncing further judgment.
- MJ asked whether the President believes someone facing allegations like Senator Menendez should remain in the Democratic Party.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the situation is a serious matter and that the senator's decision to step down from his chairmanship was the right thing to do.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said further decisions are for the senator and Senate leadership to make.
  #### President joining UAW picket
  Reporters extensively questioned why President Biden will join the UAW picket line in Detroit and whether the visit signals he is taking the union's side over automakers. Jean-Pierre emphasized the President's longstanding pro-union stance, said he accepted the UAW invitation, and affirmed he will join the picket line in solidarity while declining to negotiate or comment on specific contract terms. The White House frames the trip as a historic show of support for workers without attempting to mediate details of ongoing contract negotiations.
- Several reporters asked why the President is going to Detroit to stand on the UAW picket line and whether that signals siding with the union over companies.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President is the most pro-union president in modern times and accepted UAW President Shawn Fain's invitation to join the picket line.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the White House will not get into the specifics of negotiations and emphasized the President supports workers getting a fair share.
  #### Presidential visit specifics and political risk
  Reporters pressed for details about whether the President will actually picket, speak, or risk political backlash, and whether prior actions by former President Trump influenced the decision. Jean-Pierre said he will join the picket line, denied Trump's visit influenced the decision, and reiterated the President's desire to stand with workers while deferring any operational trip details. The White House acknowledges political judgment but prioritizes showing solidarity with labor.
- Reporters asked whether Trump's visit to UAW workers influenced the President's decision to attend.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the decision was the President's and not influenced by former President Trump.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the President will 'join the picket line' and that trip details are not being read out at that time.
  #### Polling and economic approval
  Reporters cited low approval numbers on the economy and concerns about the President's age and fitness and asked whether the White House finds these trends troubling. Jean-Pierre acknowledged the polls but stressed the administration's focus on delivering policy outcomes like lowering costs and highlighted recent economic gains. She framed upcoming state visits as opportunities to speak directly to Americans about the administration's record.
- A reporter cited NBC polling showing 37 percent approval on the President's handling of the economy and 74 percent expressing concerns about his age or fitness.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said polls vary but the administration focuses on delivering results for American families through policies aimed at lowering costs.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE pointed to job growth and low unemployment as evidence of economic progress under the President.
  #### Vaccine access and insurance issues
  Reporters raised concerns about consumer difficulties getting updated COVID vaccines and insurance coverage denials after the federal procurement phase shifted to commercial distribution. Jean-Pierre acknowledged reports of coverage problems and said HHS is working with insurers and pharmacies to resolve issues and ensure broad access. The White House treats access to the updated vaccine as a top priority and defers operational details to HHS.
- A reporter asked whether shifting vaccine distribution to the commercial market created access and insurance-coverage problems.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration is aware of coverage denials and that HHS is working with insurance plans and pharmacies to resolve the issues.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said ensuring everyone can access the updated vaccine is a top priority for the administration.
  #### Credit rating and economic risk
  A question about Moody's warning that a shutdown could hurt the U.S. credit rating prompted Jean-Pierre to reiterate that a shutdown is unnecessary and would harm everyday Americans. She declined to provide independent economic analysis and left technical commentary to economists, while emphasizing job creation and low unemployment under the current administration. The White House frames the shutdown risk as a self-inflicted political choice by House Republicans.
- A reporter noted Moody's warning that a shutdown could negatively impact the U.S. credit rating and asked whether the President is concerned.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said she would leave economic analysis to experts but reiterated that an extreme Republican shutdown would hurt American people.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE cited job creation and low unemployment as reasons Republicans should not put the economy at risk.
  #### Student loan repayment restart
  Reporters asked about the Oct. 1 restart of student loan payments and whether a shutdown could disrupt Department of Education support. Jean-Pierre said critical Federal Student Aid activities should continue for a couple of weeks but a prolonged shutdown could substantially disrupt the return to repayment and servicing. The administration warns borrowers to expect uncertainty if a lengthy shutdown occurs and underscores the importance of avoiding one.
- A reporter asked whether a shutdown could disrupt the Department of Education's ability to help borrowers when payments restart on Oct. 1.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said key Federal Student Aid activities will continue for a couple of weeks but a prolonged shutdown could substantially disrupt return-to-repayment efforts.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said more than 28 million federal student loan borrowers are affected by the restart and a shutdown could create disruption.
  #### Nagorno-Karabakh developments
  A reporter asked about thousands of refugees leaving Nagorno-Karabakh and whether the U.S. describes the situation as ethnic cleansing. Jean-Pierre said the administration is watching the reports closely but deferred specific policy responses and terminology to the National Security Council. The White House signals concern and points reporters to NSC for detailed briefings.
- A reporter asked whether the President is concerned about reports of refugees leaving Nagorno-Karabakh and whether this amounts to 'ethnic cleansing.'
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said the administration is watching the reports closely and referred detailed questions to the National Security Council.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE described the situation as concerning and deferred to NSC for characterization and response.

She framed U.S. policy as respecting sovereign decisions while continuing allied coordination.
- A reporter asked about Xi Jinping's reported support for resuming China-Japan-South Korea trilateral summits and Japan's ministerial changes.

Jean-Pierre said OMB Director Shalanda Young and Legislative Affairs staff remain in regular contact with members but had no direct presidential engagement to read out. She reiterated the administration's position that the President brokered a bipartisan deal and that it's now Congress's responsibility to implement it and keep the government open.
- A reporter asked whether President Biden has had engagement with House Republicans about the shutdown.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE said OMB Director Shalanda Young and Legislative Affairs staff are in regular contact with members but had no presidential calls to read out on the issue.
