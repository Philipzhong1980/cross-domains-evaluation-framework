### Overview
The meeting featured a detailed briefing by Secretary Vilsack on the severe impacts a potential government shutdown, driven by extreme House Republicans, would have on USDA programs and rural communities. Key issues included the immediate cessation of WIC nutrition assistance for nearly 7 million mothers and children, disruption of farm loans critical during harvest, closure of national forests affecting tourism, and furloughs of over 50,000 USDA employees nationwide. The Secretary underscored the avoidable nature of the shutdown given a bipartisan deal already passed. The White House Press Secretary, MS. JEAN-PIERRE, reinforced that responsibility for preventing the shutdown lies with House Republicans, emphasizing the broad harm to American families if it occurs. The discussion also covered President Biden's planned visit to the UAW picket line in Michigan, highlighting his longstanding pro-union stance and support for fair worker contracts without intervening in negotiations. Additional topics included the President's private COVID booster vaccination, student loan repayment risks amid a shutdown, concerns over foreign ownership of farmland and national security, and international relations developments. The administration maintained a consistent message urging Congress to uphold agreements and avoid disruptions to essential services and the economy.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Impacts of Government Shutdown
The discussion focused on the potential consequences of a government shutdown driven by extreme House Republicans. Secretary Vilsack detailed how such a shutdown would disrupt vital USDA services, including nutrition assistance programs like WIC, farm loans, rural housing loans, and national forest access, affecting millions of Americans and rural economies. The shutdown would also furlough over 50,000 USDA employees, further impacting local economies and service delivery.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE emphasized the damage a shutdown would cause to communities, economy, and national security.
- Secretary Vilsack explained that nearly 7 million mothers and children rely on WIC, which would be immediately affected by a shutdown.
- Secretary Vilsack noted that farm service agency offices would close, preventing farmers from accessing marketing loans during harvest season.
- Secretary Vilsack highlighted that national forests and grasslands would close, reducing tourism revenue and risking jobs.
- Secretary Vilsack stated that over 50,000 USDA workers would be furloughed, impacting local economies.
#### Nutrition Assistance Programs During Shutdown
Secretary Vilsack clarified the operational differences between SNAP and WIC during a shutdown. SNAP benefits would continue for at least a month, but WIC benefits would cease almost immediately, with only minimal contingency funds or leftover state benefits possibly extending support briefly. There is no authority to extend WIC funding beyond this short period during a shutdown.
- Secretary Vilsack stated SNAP benefits continue for at least October during a shutdown.
- Secretary Vilsack explained WIC benefits stop immediately upon shutdown, with only limited contingency funds available.
- Secretary Vilsack confirmed no authority exists to extend WIC funding beyond a day or two during a shutdown.
#### Effects on Farmers and Food Prices
The shutdown would cause significant stress for farm families by blocking access to critical marketing assistance loans needed to maintain operations and harvest crops. This disruption risks farm profitability and could lead to farm losses. Secretary Vilsack emphasized that the shutdown is unnecessary given the bipartisan deal already passed by the Senate and House.
- Secretary Vilsack described how marketing assistance loans are vital for farm families to maintain operations.
- Secretary Vilsack warned that inability to access loans could lead to farm losses.
- Secretary Vilsack stressed that a bipartisan deal exists, making the shutdown avoidable.
#### Food Safety and Inspection During Shutdown
Secretary Vilsack addressed concerns about food safety inspections, clarifying that USDA inspectors for meat, poultry, and processed eggs would continue working during a shutdown. However, FDA inspections, which cover other foods, may be affected. Laboratory analyses critical for detecting animal diseases could be disrupted, potentially impacting food supply safety.
- Secretary Vilsack confirmed USDA food inspectors remain on duty during a shutdown.
- Secretary Vilsack noted FDA inspections might be disrupted, affecting non-meat food safety.
- Secretary Vilsack highlighted potential delays in lab analyses for animal disease detection during a shutdown.
#### USDA Workforce Impact
The shutdown would furlough approximately 50,000 USDA employees across all counties, including those in farm service, rural development, conservation, forest service, research, and administrative roles. This widespread furlough would severely disrupt USDA operations and services nationwide.
- Secretary Vilsack stated furloughs would affect USDA employees in every county.
- Secretary Vilsack listed impacted roles including FSA, rural development, NRCS, Forest Service, ARS, and administrative staff.
- Secretary Vilsack emphasized the disruption caused by furloughing essential administrative personnel.
#### Farm Bill Passage and Shutdown Effects
Secretary Vilsack expressed hope that the farm bill would be passed by year-end but noted that a shutdown would hinder legislative affairs and technical assistance necessary for negotiations. He stressed that underfunding the USDA budget undermines farm bill implementation and that certainty is crucial for rural communities.
- Secretary Vilsack acknowledged ongoing efforts by committee leaders to pass the farm bill.
- Secretary Vilsack warned that a shutdown would delay farm bill negotiations and technical support.
- Secretary Vilsack highlighted that budget cuts would undermine farm bill effectiveness.
#### Foreign Ownership of Farmland and National Security
Concerns about Chinese purchases of U.S. farmland near sensitive sites were discussed. Secretary Vilsack acknowledged legitimate national security concerns and the need for improved monitoring and engagement in the CFIUS process. He noted challenges in tracking foreign ownership due to decentralized deed recording systems.
- Secretary Vilsack cited the North Dakota case as an example of legitimate security concerns.
- Secretary Vilsack advocated for enhanced USDA involvement in CFIUS to monitor foreign land purchases.
- Secretary Vilsack explained difficulties in identifying foreign buyers due to county-level deed recording.
#### President Biden's Support for UAW Workers
The White House confirmed President Biden's planned visit to the UAW picket line in Michigan to show solidarity with union workers. The administration emphasized the President's long-standing pro-union stance and support for fair contracts reflecting corporate profits. While not intervening in negotiations, the President stands firmly with workers' rights and collective bargaining.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE stated the President accepted the UAW invitation to join the picket line.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE described President Biden as the most pro-union president in modern history.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE emphasized the President supports workers receiving a fair share of corporate profits.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE clarified the administration does not negotiate contract specifics but supports collective bargaining.
#### White House Messaging on Shutdown Responsibility
The White House repeatedly attributed responsibility for the potential government shutdown to extreme House Republicans, urging them to honor the bipartisan deal made earlier in the year. The administration stressed that keeping the government open is a fundamental Congressional duty and highlighted the negative impacts a shutdown would have on American families.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE called the potential shutdown a Republican shutdown caused by extreme House Republicans.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE emphasized that a deal was made and Republicans must stick to it.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE highlighted the broad negative effects of a shutdown on programs like education, housing, and nutrition assistance.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE stated it is Congress's job to keep the government open.
#### COVID-19 Booster and Vaccination Efforts
The White House explained that President Biden received his latest COVID booster privately due to scheduling constraints, not to diminish the importance of vaccination. The administration continues a robust public campaign encouraging vaccinations, including COVID, RSV, and flu shots, emphasizing accessibility and public health priorities.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE noted the President's vaccination was scheduled at the earliest available time.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE affirmed ongoing efforts to promote vaccination to the public.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE clarified that a public photo op was not necessary to demonstrate the President's vaccination.
#### Student Loan Repayment and Shutdown Risks
The administration acknowledged that key activities at Federal Student Aid would continue for a few weeks during a shutdown but warned that a prolonged shutdown could disrupt repayment efforts for over 28 million federal student loan borrowers. The SAVE Plan was highlighted as the most affordable repayment plan launched by the President.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE stated Federal Student Aid operations would continue briefly during a shutdown.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE warned of potential disruptions to student loan servicing if the shutdown is prolonged.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE emphasized the importance of the SAVE Plan for borrowers.
#### International Relations and Regional Developments
Questions about recent developments in trilateral summits involving China, Japan, and South Korea, as well as personnel changes in Japan's government, were addressed. The White House deferred to respective governments for details but reaffirmed commitment to strong partnerships and respect for sovereign decisions.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE referred inquiries about trilateral summits to Japan and South Korea.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE emphasized the U.S. commitment to deepening partnerships with Japan and South Korea.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE respected sovereign decisions regarding Japanese government personnel changes.
#### Border Humanitarian Situation
The White House directed questions about the humanitarian crisis at the Southwest border and recent agreements with Mexico to Customs and Border Protection (CBP) for detailed information. The administration acknowledged the situation but deferred to agencies directly involved for updates.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE referred border crisis questions to CBP.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE noted CBP's recent engagement with the Mexican government.
- MS. JEAN-PIERRE acknowledged concerns but deferred detailed updates to relevant agencies.
