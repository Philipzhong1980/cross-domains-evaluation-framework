### Overview
The Downtown Committee of Mountain View convenes remotely to approve its October minutes and then moves quickly into substantive discussions on downtown operations, economics, and events. Economic Development staff outline future agenda topics—including busking policy, general cleanliness, and a Castro Street bikeway study—while member Marina Keith raises an urgent cleanliness and environmental issue regarding mobile hot-dog vendors dumping grease into storm drains, prompting offers of follow-up from staff and recognition by Chair Mike Kasperzak that the city treats stormwater contamination very seriously. Staff member Edgar Maravilla provides development updates on new restaurant and facade projects and previews a proposed temporary cap on downtown office space to give the city time to respond to state parking law changes, and colleague John Lang delivers a detailed block-by-block vacancy analysis that shows 19 ground-floor vacancies with many already in transition; this leads members such as Vice Chair Pamela Baird, Eric Cormier, and others to voice concern about declining retail, long-term vacancies, and the need for potential incentives or vacancy-related policies in the upcoming Precise Plan update.

The committee then receives a comprehensive Castro Street update from Lang, who confirms that council has formally adopted a pedestrian mall for the 100–300 blocks with no filed objections and is extending the temporary Castro StrEATS program through 2023 while an ad hoc group develops permanent design and functional guidelines; he also describes efforts to declutter the corridor and to manage pedestrian safety as traffic signals on Villa are reactivated in phases. Recreation staff member Maureen Grzn-Pieracci presents plans for major city events, notably a December 5 downtown tree lighting with the stage moved into Castro Street, expanded family activities, and coordinated promotions with the Downtown Business Association (represented by Kira Pascoe), as well as the return of Arbor Day in Pioneer Park, Kids Stock, Concerts on the Plaza, outdoor movies, and National Night Out. Pascoe reports that Taste of Mountain View ticket sales are slower than in summer and asks for help spreading the word, and she outlines holiday promotions such as a “Santa-thon” and an Elf on the Shelf scavenger hunt to keep customers coming downtown before and after the holidays.

Lieutenant Matthew Atkins of MVPD briefs the committee on downtown public safety, highlighting the success of the earlier Downtown Ambassador program and lighting/landscaping changes in sharply reducing auto burglaries and overall crime, and he describes a holistic approach to homelessness centered on a dedicated outreach officer, a Behavioral Services Unit, and service-first engagement; in response to questions from Chair Kasperzak and member Maria Lange, he explains how residents should use the non-emergency line or online reporting for issues ranging from problematic street performances to public defecation, and he notes legal limits on loitering enforcement and the recent end of jaywalking enforcement. The committee unanimously re-adopts an AB 361 resolution to continue virtual meetings while staff confirms with the City Attorney how long that will remain lawful, and it approves a 2023 schedule with 10 AM meetings on selected first Tuesdays, reflecting member preferences for mid-morning timing and acknowledging likely future shifts to in-person or hybrid formats. The meeting closes with brief announcements on a downtown history walking tour, upcoming DBA events, council interviews for committee reappointments and a likely resignation due to attendance, and reminders about Election Day and Thanksgiving, before Chair Kasperzak adjourns the session until December.

#### Hot dog vendors dumping grease in storm drains
Committee member Marina Keith raises a significant cleanliness and environmental concern about mobile hot dog vendors on Castro Street. She reports that vendors have been dumping cooking oil directly into storm drains near the former Mervyn’s site, which may already be clogged, and states that her business has documented the behavior on video. The committee underscores that storm drains lead directly to the Bay and notes the city’s strict rules on what can enter the stormwater system, treating this as a serious violation requiring follow-up by city departments and safety staff.
- Member Marina Keith reports that hot dog stands on Castro Street have long been an issue for her business and that she recently observed them dumping used oil directly into the storm drain in front of the former Mervyn’s.
- Keith says the gutter in that location is now very dirty and possibly clogged, and notes that all brick‑and‑mortar food businesses must maintain grease traps, which makes the vendors’ behavior particularly shocking.
- Keith states that she has the dumping captured on camera, has already contacted the Fire Department, and will forward the video to them for investigation.
- Member Cormier asks Keith to contact him offline and share the video so he can obtain details and assist, and Keith agrees to send him the footage.
- Chair Kasperzak reminds the committee that city storm drains flow directly to the Bay and notes that even clean ice water from the Art and Wine Festival must be handled as wastewater and hauled away, illustrating how serious stormwater protections are.

#### Completion and transition of Castro Street pedestrian mall
Staff provides a comprehensive update on the formal establishment of the Castro Street pedestrian mall and the transition from the temporary Castro StrEATS program. Council has adopted the pedestrian mall ordinance for the 100–300 blocks and is preparing to extend the temporary street dining program through 2023 to allow time for ad hoc committee work on functional and design guidelines. This transition phase aims to preserve current outdoor operations while moving toward a long‑term, regulated pedestrian mall framework and continued decluttering of the corridor.
- Economic Development staff member Lang reports that City Council adopted an ordinance on October 25 to formally enact the Castro Street pedestrian mall covering the 100–300 blocks, with the ordinance taking effect 30 days after adoption.
- Lang notes that because the pedestrian mall’s functional plan and design guidelines are not yet approved, council will consider an item on consent to extend the Castro StrEATS program—which currently authorizes business operations in the street—through the end of 2023.
- Lang explains that extending Castro StrEATS creates a transition period, allowing an ad hoc committee to develop the pedestrian mall’s design guidelines and functional plan while keeping existing outdoor dining and layouts in place.

- Chair Kasperzak asks whether any formal objections or claims were filed against the pedestrian mall during the statutory process, and Lang confirms there were none, enabling council to act on October 25.
- Lang mentions that staff is concurrently “decluttering” Castro Street by removing tents, broken items, and worn infrastructure in coordination with businesses.
#### Downtown vacancy data and retail versus restaurant mix
Lang presents a detailed, block‑by‑block inventory of downtown Castro Street vacancies, distinguishing between ground‑floor street‑facing spaces and upper‑floor spaces. The data show 19 ground‑floor vacant units, of which many already have permits pending, ownership changes, or business leads, and at least 10 upper‑floor vacancies with some undercounting. Committee members express concern about declining retail presence and the conversion of large retail footprints to food uses, while staff explains market dynamics, parking in‑lieu fees, zoning tools, and potential policy options such as a vacancy tax or incentives to encourage retail and reduce long‑term vacancies.
- Lang shares a summary table of vacancies across nine Castro Street blocks, breaking out ground‑floor (retail/restaurant) vacancies, non‑ground‑floor vacancies, active permits, and change‑of‑ownership situations for each block.
- Lang reports that the 400 block has the highest overall number of vacancies, while the 300 block has the most prominent ground‑floor street‑facing vacancies.
- Lang states that there are currently 19 ground‑floor vacant units, three of which have permits in process, two are undergoing ownership changes, and two have letters of interest, meaning true open ground‑floor vacancies will likely settle around 12 once these are resolved.
- Lang notes that there are at least 10 non‑ground‑floor vacancies but cautions this is an undercount because staff cannot access multi‑story buildings to determine the exact number of suites vacant; each tower was conservatively counted as one vacant unit if any upper floor was empty.
- Vice Chair Baird voices concern that downtown is losing retail, citing the Jennings Building where a large former kitchen store space is being converted to a dessert business instead of a broader retail tenant, and she links this to broader worries about reduced retail choice for residents.
- Lang explains that, under current rules, traditional retail uses avoid parking in‑lieu fees that apply to restaurants and other more intensive uses, which in theory incentivizes property owners to retain retail; however, actual tenant mix is driven by market demand, rent structures, build‑out costs, and owner preferences.
- Chair Kasperzak comments that the city has a longstanding tension between restaurants and retail downtown and observes that Mountain View has, in practice, become an “Amazon community” where residents often shop online despite stating a preference for local retail.
- Member Yen asks how the city approaches prolonged vacancies and whether there is a threshold where the city encourages owners to shift uses; Chair Kasperzak responds that because these are private properties, council’s direct authority is limited to zoning changes and use regulations.
- Lang adds that the upcoming Downtown Precise Plan update provides a venue to reconsider allowable uses and that council has also asked staff to explore policy tools such as vacancy registration or a vacancy tax to incentivize activation of long‑empty spaces.
- Member Keith asks if staff has contact information for all Castro Street landlords, referencing the long‑vacant China Delight site, and Lang confirms staff has basic contact details and that this particular owner is out of the country.
- Chair Kasperzak notes that the China Delight building is unreinforced masonry, would require major structural work to reopen, and likely functions as a land‑value “savings account” for the owner, making it a prime example in vacancy‑tax discussions, even though the Chamber of Commerce is wary of that term.
- Member Cormier points out that retail tenants often must self‑fund tenant improvements, unlike in office or industrial settings, and suggests the city explore grant programs or similar incentives to help new retailers with build‑out costs, given high interest rates and challenging brick‑and‑mortar conditions; Vice Chair Baird supports that idea based on her own experience financing major improvements to a retail building in San Mateo.
- Chair Kasperzak interprets comments as informal staff direction for Lang to add potential economic incentives, including but not limited to retail grant programs, to the committee’s future discussion list, and several members indicate agreement.

#### Police update on downtown safety and homelessness outreach
Lieutenant Matthew Atkins from Mountain View Police Department provides a public safety update focused on auto burglaries, general crime trends, and responses to homelessness downtown. A prior collaborative effort involving a Downtown Ambassador program, increased lighting, and landscape modifications led to a significant drop in auto burglaries and overall crime. The department plans to staff officers, CSOs, and volunteers downtown during the holiday season and continue a holistic approach to unhoused individuals through a dedicated Community Services Outreach Officer, a Behavioral Services Unit, and emphasis on services rather than solely enforcement.
- Lieutenant Atkins reports that downtown auto burglaries rose significantly a few years ago, prompting a collaborative response involving volunteers, MVPD, and the Chamber to create a Downtown Ambassador program.
- Atkins explains that ambassadors focused on visible presence and education, while the city enhanced lighting and adjusted landscaping to reduce cover for criminal activity, resulting in a drastic reduction in auto burglaries and a general decrease in crime downtown.
- Because of the program’s success, Atkins says MVPD will staff officers, Community Service Officers, and volunteers in the downtown area again during the holiday season to deter theft and other crimes.
- Atkins describes the department’s approach to unhoused individuals in downtown, highlighting Community Services Outreach Officer McPherson, who connects people to housing and substance‑abuse resources, and the Behavioral Services Unit, which includes a clinician and crisis‑negotiation‑trained officers.
- He notes that several individuals who previously spent most of their time downtown have successfully obtained services and housing through this model, which focuses on underlying needs rather than only law enforcement.
- Atkins urges businesses and residents to report suspicious activity or people who may need help, emphasizing that if the department is unaware of a situation it is difficult to address it.
- Chair Kasperzak asks about handling a reported case of a street musician, likely unhoused and surrounded by trash, performing in the pedestrian mall in a way that concerned some residents; Atkins replies that MVPD’s typical first step is outreach, with Officer McPherson offering services and assessing needs.
- Atkins advises that residents witnessing concerning behavior should call the Police Department’s non‑emergency line for real‑time issues or use the online reporting system for less urgent situations, and he commits to posting the non‑emergency number in the meeting chat.
- Member Lange asks how to handle immediate issues like public defecation downtown, and Atkins reiterates using the non‑emergency line so officers can respond while the behavior is occurring.
- Lange inquires about a citywide anti‑loitering ordinance similar to signage she observed in Santa Cruz; Atkins states he is not aware of such an ordinance in Mountain View and notes that enforcement is limited when individuals are not impeding access or violating specific laws.
- Kasparzak confirms that jaywalking is no longer an enforceable offense in California, which affects some pedestrian enforcement dynamics downtown.
#### Pedestrian safety at Villa Street intersections
The committee briefly discusses pedestrian safety at the Villa Street signalized intersections intersecting Castro. Staff notes that traffic signals are being reactivated in phases after a period when drivers were used to all‑red or nonfunctional signals, requiring an adjustment period for both motorists and pedestrians. Transportation staff and police are monitoring near‑misses, posting signage, and planning a long‑term pedestrian‑mall design where people would cross mid‑block rather than at corners.
- Member Shaikh raises concerns about ongoing safety issues at the Villa Street intersection near 833–845 Villa, where a signal restored to operation has not fully changed pedestrian behavior and he has observed a near‑miss collision.
- Lang responds that Transportation and Public Works staff, along with MVPD, are actively monitoring the intersection and have placed signs to alert both drivers and pedestrians to the signal changes.
- Lang explains that after the signals were off or displaying all‑red for an extended period, both drivers and pedestrians developed new habits, so turning multiple lights back on simultaneously caused issues; as a result, staff is reactivating intersections one at a time and evaluating conditions after each change.
- Lang notes that the second intersection’s signal was turned on the day of the meeting, and staff will tackle additional signals once they understand safety impacts at the current locations.
- Lang outlines the long‑term vision for the pedestrian mall, in which intersections will be narrowed for vehicles and pedestrians will be encouraged to cross in the middle of blocks rather than at the corners, though this design is still years away.
#### 2023 Downtown Committee meeting schedule and format
The committee adopts a 2023 meeting schedule, maintaining the pattern of 10 AM meetings on the first Tuesday of selected months, while acknowledging possible changes in format as the city transitions out of emergency conditions. Members debate the merits of a 10 AM start versus the prior 8 AM schedule, noting impacts on business owners, parents, and commuting, and express general satisfaction with the current time and virtual format. Staff will confirm with the City Clerk and City Attorney how state law and council decisions will affect the committee’s ability to remain virtual or move to hybrid or in‑person meetings.
- Lang presents a proposed 2023 meeting schedule based on past practice: no meetings in January, July, or August, and meetings at 10 AM on the first Tuesday of February, March, April, May, June, September, October, November, and December.
- Chair Kasperzak recalls that pre‑pandemic meetings were held around 7:30 or 8:00 AM to avoid mid‑workday conflicts for business owners, and he questions whether 10 AM remains appropriate if the committee must resume in‑person sessions.
- Kasperzak suggests that staff seek clarification from the City Attorney and City Clerk about whether the committee can continue to meet virtually after the city and state rescind their emergency declarations, given that AB 361 authorization might phase out.
- Member Yen states that the 10 AM time works well for her as a parent due to school drop‑off schedules and notes that public participation has improved since the move from 8 AM to 10 AM, even if overall attendance still appears modest.
- Member Keith favors 10 AM or an early evening slot, arguing that early mornings are busy for many businesses and may suppress public engagement, and she notes that traffic is lighter at 10 AM than at 8 AM if in‑person meetings resume.
- Member Lin, a teacher, explains he must obtain substitute coverage for his classes during meetings and says earlier times are somewhat easier for him, but he is willing to adjust to the committee’s consensus.
- Lang recommends that the committee formally adopt the proposed dates and times now to comply with policy, with the understanding that staff can bring back modifications if legal or logistical constraints require changes later.
- After a brief discussion and no public comment, Vice Chair Baird moves to adopt the staff recommendation and member Yen seconds; Lang conducts a roll call and the schedule is approved unanimously.
