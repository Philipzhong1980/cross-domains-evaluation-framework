### Overview
The briefing, led by Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre with National Security Council spokesperson John Kirby, covers a wide range of domestic and foreign policy issues, anchored by responses to the Nashville school shooting, the Summit for Democracy, and global security developments. Jean-Pierre opens by announcing the First Lady’s trip to a Nashville vigil and touts the FDA’s over-the-counter approval of Narcan as part of President Biden’s broader effort to combat the opioid crisis, while also highlighting Vice President Harris’s Africa trip and major investments in women’s economic empowerment. Kirby then details new U.S. commitments of up to $690 million under the Summit for Democracy initiative, defends the summit’s invite list amid questions about participants like India, and underscores democratic values in upcoming engagements with Argentina, Iraq, Israel, and other partners. He offers a firm but measured line on Taiwan President Tsai’s routine transit through the United States, insisting China has no reason to overreact, and addresses broader U.S.–China dynamics, including prospects for a Biden–Xi call, cabinet-level visits, and support for a Xi–Zelenskyy conversation. Kirby also discusses Venezuela, Pakistan, the Mexico migrant fire, aid oversight in Ukraine, Senator Tuberville’s hold on military promotions, and questions about TikTok while frequently emphasizing accountability, readiness, and legal pathways for migration. In the latter portion, Jean-Pierre focuses on domestic debates over the debt limit, gun control, Nashville’s aftermath, the CBP One app, and immigration reform, strongly defending Biden’s record on gun violence and fiscal responsibility, backing repeal of outdated Iraq war AUMFs, and deflecting sensitive matters like access to classified documents and IRS actions to relevant agencies, giving absent observers a clear picture of the administration’s priorities and constraints across issues.
