### Overview
The briefing, led by Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre with extensive participation from Admiral John Kirby, covers several major themes: domestic emergencies, foreign policy crises, and a deep dive into the communications breakdown around Defense Secretary Lloyd Austin’s undisclosed hospitalization and prostate cancer diagnosis. Jean-Pierre opens by outlining severe weather threats across the U.S. and previewing President Biden’s upcoming economic trip to Allentown, Pennsylvania, while Kirby details Russia’s use of North Korean ballistic missiles in Ukraine, the resulting 47-nation joint condemnation, and the administration’s push for Congress to pass supplemental funding for Ukraine. The bulk of the session focuses on when President Biden and senior aides learned of Austin’s condition, with Kirby confirming that Biden was told of the hospitalization on January 4 and learned of the cancer only today, and Jean-Pierre describing a new memo from Chief of Staff Jeff Zients requiring all Cabinet agencies to submit and tighten delegation-of-authority protocols. Throughout contentious questioning, Kirby concedes the delay was “not optimal,” acknowledges damage to transparency and credibility, but stresses that Biden’s command of national security operations was never compromised and that Austin retains the President’s full confidence, while broader accountability will be handled by the Pentagon’s 30-day review. Reporters also probe U.S. positions on Israel-Gaza issues (including ceasefire, humanitarian pauses, journalist safety, and media access), rising Chinese military activity near Taiwan’s election, human rights concerns in Burundi, diplomatic efforts to avoid escalation with Hezbollah, aviation safety after the Alaska Airlines/Boeing incident, border security negotiations and Republican efforts to impeach DHS Secretary Mayorkas, economic outlook and “Bidenomics,” and assorted legal and political matters involving Donald Trump and Hunter Biden, with the White House largely sticking to established policy lines and referring legal specifics to the Department of Justice or White House Counsel.

Agencies must submit their existing written procedures by January 12 for review by Cabinet Affairs and the Chief of Staff. The administration presents this as implementing President Biden’s desire for stronger, standardized notification and transparency practices across the Cabinet.

#### Expectations and procedures for delegation of authority
The briefing examines what should occur when a Cabinet official, particularly the Defense Secretary, temporarily transfers authority due to incapacity. Kirby notes it is not uncommon for the Secretary of Defense to delegate authority to the Deputy Secretary, but he stresses that the notification process for such delegations needs review. He uses the analogy of a ship’s captain and executive officer to describe how authority can shift while preserving readiness. The administration signals that, going forward, it expects clearer notification up the chain of command when a Cabinet officer is hospitalized or temporarily unable to perform duties.
- Mr. Kirby explains that delegations of authority from the Secretary of Defense to the Deputy are not uncommon and sometimes occur for short periods.
- Mr. Kirby says one key lesson from this episode is the need to examine notification procedures when authority is delegated, particularly to ensure proper communication to the White House.
- Mr. Kirby states there is an expectation that if a Cabinet officer is hospitalized and cannot temporarily perform duties, that information will be communicated up the chain of command to the President.
- Mr. Kirby describes that if a national security emergency had occurred on December 22, Deputy Secretary Kathleen Hicks, who had full delegated authority and communications capability, would have been capable of acting as Acting Secretary of Defense.
- Mr. Kirby acknowledges he cannot prove how the White House would have learned of the delegation during a hypothetical crisis but underscores that the delegation itself ensured continuity of command.

At the same time, he acknowledges that officials retain some right to privacy.
