### Overview
  Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre and John Kirby lead the briefing, covering a White House trip to Milwaukee focused on Black-owned small-business growth and a stop at Hero Plumbing to highlight federal lead-service-line replacement funded by the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law. Kirby provides a humanitarian update on Gaza—reporting thousands of aid trucks and the first World Food Program deliveries from Jordan—while addressing related diplomatic issues including U.N. Security Council discussions, civilian casualty concerns, and serious but unconfirmed hostage-pause negotiations. The briefing stresses urgent congressional action on a national security supplemental that includes Ukraine, Israel, and border funding, with both officials urging prompt bipartisan agreement and warning of operational impacts if Congress delays. Other topics include concerns about election interference, Alexei Navalny’s detention, prisoner-return policy priorities, and administrative deferrals on legal and commercial matters such as the Colorado court decision, an Apple patent dispute (handled by USTR), and Nippon Steel’s planned purchase of U.S. Steel, while the White House highlights multi-platform outreach and refers space-policy questions to the Vice President’s team.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Milwaukee visit purpose
  Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre previews President Biden’s trip to Milwaukee to highlight gains for Black-owned small businesses under the administration’s economic agenda. She says the President will visit the Wisconsin Black Chamber of Commerce and announce new investments under the Investing in America agenda, tying progress to infrastructure and recovery legislation. The trip underscores the administration’s messaging on Bidenomics and targeted support for minority-owned firms.
- Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre said the President will visit the Wisconsin Black Chamber of Commerce to highlight how Bidenomics is driving a Black small-business boom.
- Karine Jean-Pierre said the trip will include announcements of new investments in Milwaukee and other communities under the Investing in America agenda.
- Karine Jean-Pierre said the visit demonstrates administration support for small-business growth and the impact of recent federal legislation.
  #### Hero Plumbing stop
  Jean-Pierre announces a brief stop at Hero Plumbing where President Biden will meet CEO Rashawn Spivey and be introduced by him at the main event. She frames Hero Plumbing as a Black-owned small business actively removing lead service lines in Milwaukee, tying the visit to the broader federal goal of lead pipe replacement. The stop is presented as both a local example and a policy demonstration of the administration’s infrastructure priorities.
- Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre said the President will make a brief stop at Hero Plumbing to meet CEO Rashawn Spivey and that Spivey will introduce the President at the event.
- Karine Jean-Pierre said Hero Plumbing is removing lead pipes in Milwaukee and is part of the effort to eliminate lead service lines nationwide.
- Karine Jean-Pierre said the stop highlights how federal investments translate to local, minority-owned business activity.
  #### Lead pipe replacement funding
  Jean-Pierre emphasizes the administration’s commitment to replace all lead service lines by the end of the decade, funded by $15 billion from the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law. She frames this as a historic investment that yields safer roads, cleaner water, and good-paying jobs. The statement also notes bipartisan opposition by some Republicans, but stresses continued executive action to implement the funding.
- Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre said the President is committed to removing all lead service lines in the nation by the end of the decade.
- Karine Jean-Pierre said $15 billion from the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law is funding that lead service line replacement effort.
- Karine Jean-Pierre noted that some Republicans, including Senator Ron Johnson, voted against the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, but said the administration will continue investments regardless.
  #### Gaza humanitarian access
  John Kirby provides a humanitarian update on Gaza, reporting a sustained flow of aid trucks through crossings including Kerem Shalom and Rafah and noting, for the first time, World Food Program trucks entering from Jordan. He quantifies deliveries—nearly 5,000 trucks since the conflict began—and frames the opening of Kerem Shalom as producing tangible benefits on the ground. Kirby emphasizes continued pressure to increase shipments while acknowledging more assistance is still needed.
- John Kirby said 60 trucks went through Gaza two days ago and that subsequent days saw larger convoys through Kerem Shalom and Rafah.
- John Kirby said World Food Program trucks entered Gaza from Jordan for the first time, which he described as a significant step forward.
- John Kirby said almost 5,000 trucks have entered Gaza since the conflict began and that the U.S. will keep pushing for more aid deliveries.
  #### Alex Saab and hostage questions

He characterizes current discussions about a pause and hostage exchanges as serious and ongoing but offers no operational specifics. Kirby commits to providing updates as information becomes available.
- A reporter asked what the United States received for the release of Alex Saab, and John Kirby said he could not confirm reports or provide further detail at this time.
- A reporter asked about efforts to secure a pause in exchange for hostages, and John Kirby said the negotiations are serious and ongoing but declined to provide specifics.
- John Kirby said the administration will update the public as it can regarding these discussions.
  #### Venezuela sanctions review

  #### U.N. Security Council resolution
  Kirby describes active discussions at the U.N. Security Council about a Gaza-related resolution and outlines the administration’s priorities for such a text. He says any Security Council language should condemn Hamas’s October 7 attack, recognize Israel’s right to self-defense, and commit members to facilitate humanitarian assistance to Gaza. Kirby defers to Ambassador Linda Thomas-Greenfield for further diplomatic detail but emphasizes these core elements as U.S. priorities.
- John Kirby said there are active discussions at the U.N. Security Council about another Gaza resolution and he would defer to Ambassador Linda Thomas-Greenfield for diplomatic specifics.
- John Kirby said the U.S. believes a Security Council resolution should condemn Hamas’s October 7 attacks, recognize Israel’s right to defend itself, and commit members to enabling humanitarian assistance.
  #### Civilian casualties in Gaza
  Pressed on mounting Gaza death tolls, Kirby reiterates that the acceptable number of civilian casualties is zero and underscores U.S. efforts to urge Israel to conduct more precise operations. He acknowledges Israel has adapted some operations in response to U.S. concerns but says civilian harm has not been eliminated. Kirby emphasizes continued U.S. engagement to reduce innocent casualties.
- John Kirby said the ‘right number’ of civilian casualties is zero and that the U.S. does not want to see civilians killed or wounded on either side.
- John Kirby said the U.S. has urged Israel to be more precise and deliberate in military operations and that Israel has been receptive and adapted some operations.
  #### China-Taiwan comments
  When asked about a reported exchange between Presidents Biden and Xi about reunification timelines, Kirby declines to disclose private conversations and restates U.S. policy. He reiterates adherence to the One China policy, opposition to Taiwanese independence, and opposition to unilateral changes to the status quo, especially by force. Kirby emphasizes that there is no reason for the situation to escalate to conflict.
- John Kirby declined to confirm specifics of private talks between President Biden and President Xi and said he would not read out private conversations.
- John Kirby reiterated that the United States adheres to the One China policy, does not support Taiwanese independence, and opposes unilateral changes to the status quo by force.
  #### Alexei Navalny concerns
  Kirby expresses concern about reports regarding Alexei Navalny’s whereabouts, calling the reports concerning though unconfirmed. He reiterates that Navalny should not have been detained and urges the Kremlin to release him and keep his family informed. The U.S. frames the issue as an ongoing human rights concern with expected diplomatic pressure.
- John Kirby said the reports about Alexei Navalny’s whereabouts are concerning and that he could not confirm them.
- John Kirby called on Moscow to release Navalny and to ensure his family is informed about his whereabouts.
  #### Hamas-Fatah talks and PA role

He stresses that the PA should be part of post-conflict governance but acknowledges it needs revamping to be credible and effective. The comment frames U.S. preference for PA involvement while noting governance shortcomings that must be addressed.
- John Kirby said the U.S. believes the Palestinian Authority should and can have a role in post-conflict governance in Gaza.
- John Kirby said the Palestinian Authority needs to be revamped and revitalized to be credible in that role.
  #### Ukraine aid urgency
  Kirby stresses continued U.S. support for Ukraine but says additional aid depends on Congress approving a supplemental request, noting one remaining opportunity this month to act. He warns of operational impacts if Congress delays, citing possible Russian offensive operations around Avdiivka and winter conditions that could favor Russian advances, and notes attacks on Ukrainian energy infrastructure. Kirby emphasizes the need for timely supplemental funding to sustain air defense and other critical materials.
- John Kirby said the U.S. will continue to stand with Ukraine but that new support depends on congressional action on the supplemental request.
- John Kirby said there is one more opportunity before the end of the month to move a security package and that delays will hinder materiel delivery times.
- John Kirby warned that Russian forces intend to conduct offensive operations—particularly around Avdiivka—and that winter conditions could favor such moves, increasing urgency for aid.
  #### Timing of security package
  When asked about timing for the last security package of the year, Kirby says it is expected before the end of the month but acknowledges uncertainty and that action may slide into early January. He reiterates the urgency and that quicker congressional action is better given logistical timelines for delivering equipment. The administration continues to press Congress for prompt supplemental approval.
- John Kirby said the security package is expected before the end of the month, but acknowledged it may move into early January.
- John Kirby said the sooner Congress acts the better, given that deliveries of materiel can take days or weeks.
  #### Red Sea coalition participation

He emphasizes that coalition operations are already in place with ships in the Red Sea and that some partners will be more public about participation than others. The U.S. respects each country’s domestic considerations in how they describe involvement.
- John Kirby said participating countries get to decide whether and how to announce their involvement in the Red Sea coalition.
- John Kirby said coalition ships are already operating in the Red Sea and the U.S. hopes additional partners can be added.

  Responding to the ODNI report naming Cuba among actors that attempted to influence the 2022 midterms, Kirby condemns foreign interference in U.S. elections and says the administration will call out and act against such activity. He frames foreign interference as unacceptable regardless of the perpetrator and commits to protecting electoral integrity. Kirby positions the U.S. response as unequivocal across actors.
- John Kirby said foreign interference in U.S. elections is unacceptable by any country and that the administration will call that out.
- John Kirby said the U.S. will not shy away from actions necessary to protect the integrity of U.S. elections.

He states the President will not make decisions damaging to national security but will take hard choices to secure Americans’ release, noting over 30 people have been returned under the administration. The statement frames detainee returns as a central policy aim with national-security safeguards.
- John Kirby said President Biden prioritizes the safety of Americans overseas and will do what is necessary to get wrongfully detained Americans home.
