### Overview
  The briefing highlights multiple coordinated efforts to accelerate vaccination and maintain pandemic progress. Acting Administrator Slavitt announces private-sector outreach—including major dating apps adding vaccination-status features—and a 'Digital Day of Action' promoting Vaccine.gov and the 438829 text line, with expanded support starting Monday to help people find vaccination sites and obtain free rides. Dr. Walensky presents encouraging CDC data showing declines in cases, hospitalizations, and deaths and improving county-level transmission tied to rising vaccination coverage, while Dr. Fauci summarizes real-world studies confirming high vaccine effectiveness across platforms and strong protection against severe outcomes. Dr. Murthy reviews survey evidence that overall vaccine confidence remains high but access barriers persist, highlights tax credits and employer roles in expanding access, and showcases community-driven outreach examples. In Q&A, CDC says it is working with states to improve race/ethnicity data reporting, experts acknowledge that public comfort with relaxed guidance will take time, the administration endorses incentive programs like lotteries as effective, confirms an 80 million dose commitment for international donation pending approvals, and discusses employer clinics and research needs for protecting immunocompromised people, including study of monoclonal prophylaxis.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Dating app vaccination push
  Acting Administrator Slavitt describes partnerships with major dating apps to encourage vaccination among young adults by adding features that display vaccination status, allow filters for vaccinated users, and help locate vaccination sites. This initiative targets platforms with more than 50 million U.S. users to leverage social incentives and reach people where they spend time. The announcement underscores the administration's effort to use private-sector outreach to raise vaccination uptake among younger populations.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt announces that dating apps including Bumble, Tinder, Hinge, Match, OkCupid, BLK, Chispa, Plenty of Fish, and Badoo will add features to highlight vaccination status.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt states that these apps will allow badges, vaccinated-only filtering, and premium features while also helping users find vaccination locations.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt highlights OKCupid data showing users who display vaccination status are 14 percent more likely to match as a rationale for the initiative.
  #### Digital Day of Action
  The administration highlights a coordinated 'Digital Day of Action' promoting Vaccine.gov and the 438829 text line to help people find vaccine locations and resources. Slavitt notes expanded support when texting 438829, including free rides and employer paid time off options starting Monday. This effort aims to simplify access and raise awareness using national and community outreach channels.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt announces a 'Digital Day of Action' promoting Vaccine.gov and the 438829 text line.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt says that starting Monday texting 438829 will show nearby vaccination sites and offer a free ride and additional support options.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt frames the outreach as a priority to reach people who have not yet made vaccination a top priority.
  #### Epidemiological data update
  Dr. Walensky presents recent CDC data showing sustained declines in cases, hospital admissions, and deaths, with a seven-day average of about 27,700 cases and hospital admissions around 3,400. She emphasizes county-level transmission improvements, noting increasing numbers of counties with low or moderate transmission and maps demonstrating marked geographic improvement since January. The data illustrate progress correlated with vaccination coverage but also reinforce the need to continue vaccinating remaining unvaccinated people.
- Dr. Walensky reports a seven-day average of roughly 27,700 new COVID-19 cases and a 19 percent decline from the prior week.
- Dr. Walensky states the seven-day average of hospital admissions is just over 3,400, down almost 15 percent, and daily deaths are at a new low of 498.
- Dr. Walensky notes that 307 counties (10 percent) have low transmission and 1,183 counties (37 percent) are in the moderate transmission category.
- Dr. Walensky attributes the improving county-level and national trends to increasing vaccination coverage, with more than 60 percent of adults having at least one dose and 126.6 million people fully vaccinated.
  #### Vaccine effectiveness evidence
  Dr. Fauci reviews multiple real-world studies confirming high vaccine effectiveness across platforms, including mRNA vaccines showing about 94 percent protection against symptomatic disease among healthcare personnel. He reviews nursing home data demonstrating substantial reductions in infections after full vaccination and J&J data showing strong protection against hospitalization and death after 14–28 days. Fauci concludes that vaccines protect individuals and interrupt transmission, reinforcing the central public-health message to get vaccinated.
- Dr. Fauci cites an MMWR analysis showing roughly 94 percent real-world effectiveness of Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna vaccines against symptomatic disease among healthcare personnel.
- Dr. Fauci describes a New England Journal of Medicine nursing home study showing near-zero infections more than 14 days after the second dose among vaccinated residents.
- Dr. Fauci summarizes data on the Johnson & Johnson vaccine showing approximately 72–74 percent effectiveness overall and 93–100 percent protection against hospitalization 14–28 days postvaccination.
- Dr. Fauci states that the combined evidence supports the conclusion that vaccines protect individuals, reduce severe outcomes, and interrupt transmission.
  #### Vaccine confidence and access
  Dr. Murthy reviews survey data indicating overall high vaccine confidence, with over 70 percent vaccinated, planning to, or likely to get vaccinated, while noting access barriers remain important among the unvaccinated. He highlights work-related constraints (time off, scheduling, eligibility confusion) and data showing employer policies and workplace vaccination can increase uptake and reduce equity gaps. Murthy spotlights tax credits for small employers, local partner initiatives, and the COVID-19 Community Corps as mechanisms to address access, motivation, and confidence.
- Dr. Murthy reports CDC survey data showing more than 70 percent of Americans are vaccinated, planning to get vaccinated, or likely to get vaccinated.
- Dr. Murthy cites a KFF survey finding that over a third of people who want vaccination report scheduling or time barriers, with 7 percent lacking information and 8 percent unsure about eligibility.
- Dr. Murthy notes that among employed unvaccinated adults, 28 percent would be more likely to vaccinate with paid time off and 20 percent would be more likely if the shot were given at work.
- Dr. Murthy highlights that tax credits for employers with fewer than 500 employees are intended to support paid time off for vaccination and recovery.
- Dr. Murthy gives examples of local efforts—employers offering time off, on-site vaccinations, and community organizations using peer outreach—to demonstrate the 'all-hands-on-deck' approach.
  #### Race/ethnicity data completeness
  A reporter asks whether CDC will provide more frequent state-by-state race and ethnicity vaccine data and how CDC addresses high rates of missing race/ethnicity information in vaccination records. Dr. Walensky says CDC updates its website on a weekly basis and has recently added a two-week rolling vaccine distribution view while actively working with states to improve data completeness. She acknowledges progress but emphasizes ongoing coordination with state and local public-health partners.
- Victoria Knight asks whether CDC will update state-by-state race and ethnicity data daily and address nearly 40 percent missing race/ethnicity data.
- Dr. Walensky responds that the CDC website is updated weekly and that CDC recently added a two-week rolling view of vaccine distribution.
- Dr. Walensky states CDC is actively working with states and localities to improve access to race and ethnicity data and that progress is ongoing.
  #### Public comfort and communication
  Reporters ask about public unease after images of a large, largely vaccinated crowd at the White House and whether lingering fears are irrational or reflect communication gaps. Dr. Fauci characterizes these fears as understandable and not irrational given sustained behavioral patterns over 16 months, and Dr. Walensky emphasizes that people may take time to adjust and that continued mask use by vaccinated people is acceptable. The discussion frames communication as needing to respect individual comfort while conveying the science behind new guidance.
- Steve Portnoy asks what experts say to people who remain fearful of close crowds despite vaccination guidance.
- Dr. Fauci says that lingering fears are understandable rather than irrational given long-standing precautions.
- Dr. Walensky echoes that adjustment takes time and clarifies that vaccinated people can continue to wear masks without shame while people move at their own pace.
  #### Incentive program effectiveness
  A reporter asks whether state-run vaccine lotteries and prize programs are useful and whether the administration encourages them. Acting Administrator Slavitt says the available data suggest these incentives are effective at motivating people who are not opposed to vaccination but have deprioritized it. The administration expresses enthusiasm for such measures as part of a broader strategy to draw attention and increase uptake among those still unvaccinated.
- Peter Sullivan asks about the value of state vaccine lotteries and whether the administration supports them.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt states that data indicate lotteries and similar incentives are working to motivate people who have not prioritized vaccination.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt expresses enthusiasm for incentive programs as part of outreach to increase vaccination.
  #### Impact of improving metrics & global donations
  Reporters ask whether declining cases and deaths will reduce people's motivation to vaccinate and request an update on U.S. plans to send doses abroad. Dr. Murthy cautions that improving metrics reflect vaccination success and warns against complacency, urging continued vaccination to sustain declines, while Slavitt reiterates that vaccine outreach and education must continue. Slavitt confirms a commitment to send 80 million doses overseas before July Fourth pending FDA approvals and says allocation decisions will be based on science, public health, and equity.
- Jeremy Diamond asks if declining cases and deaths will make people less likely to get vaccinated.
- Dr. Murthy cautions that improvements are the result of vaccinations and says continued vaccination is necessary to sustain progress and avoid future resurgences.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt confirms a U.S. commitment to send 80 million vaccine doses abroad before July Fourth pending FDA approval and says allocation decisions prioritize science, public health, and equity.
  #### Dating app origin question
  A reporter asks how the dating app initiative originated and whether it is a first for White House engagement with dating platforms. Slavitt explains that the apps initiated these features themselves in response to perceived audience needs and that the administration had conversations with them but characterizes the effort largely as private-sector action aligned with public-health goals. The exchange frames the initiative as a practical partnership rather than a direct White House program.
- Maureen Groppe asks how the dating app initiative came about and whether White House involvement is a first.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt explains that the companies took the initiative based on audience needs and that the White House had conversations with them but did not originate the program.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt frames the effort as private-sector activity that the administration applauds for reaching large audiences.
  #### Mask conflicts & worker safety
  A reporter raises concerns about confrontations and harassment of service workers enforcing local mask rules after CDC guidance eased mask use for vaccinated people. Slavitt emphasizes the stress and intensity of the past year and urges mutual respect, acknowledging the difficulty frontline workers face when policies differ by jurisdiction. The administration frames interpersonal civility as important while recognizing the challenges businesses and workers encounter enforcing rules.
- Tommy Christopher describes incidents of customers confronting and abusing workers over mask enforcement under differing local rules.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt responds by urging people to treat each other with respect and acknowledging the intense stress of the past year.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt recognizes the burden put on frontline workers when local policies differ and the need for civility.
  #### Employer clinics & immunocompromised options
  A CNBC reporter asks about employer on-site vaccination clinics and whether monoclonal antibodies could serve as preventive options for immunocompromised people. Slavitt and Murthy describe employer partnerships, matching processes, and tax incentives that facilitate on-site and near-site vaccination to increase access. Dr. Fauci acknowledges studies on immunosuppressed populations and says monoclonal antibodies are a plausible idea but require study for feasibility and duration, while also noting research into additional vaccine doses, antivirals, and prophylactic approaches.
- Meg Tirrell asks what employers can do to offer on-site vaccination clinics and what barriers exist.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt says employers are creating on-site clinics through partnerships with pharmacies and that the administration is helping match employers with providers.
- Dr. Murthy says employers can promote confidence, motivate vaccination, and improve access, and that tax credits and the COVID-19 Community Corps provide support and best practices.
- Meg Tirrell asks whether monoclonal antibodies could be used preventively for immunocompromised people.
- Dr. Fauci says monoclonal prophylaxis is a reasonable concept but requires study because of antibody half-life and logistical feasibility, and he notes ongoing research on additional vaccine doses, antivirals, and prophylactic strategies.
