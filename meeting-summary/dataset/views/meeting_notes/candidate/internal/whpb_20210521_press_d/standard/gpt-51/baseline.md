### Overview
Senior federal health officials hold a press briefing in which Acting Administrator Andy Slavitt emphasizes new public-private initiatives—particularly vaccination badges, filters, and perks across major dating apps—to reach younger adults, alongside a “Digital Day of Action” promoting Vaccines.gov, free ride offers, and text-based access tools as the U.S. surpasses 125 million fully vaccinated people and over 60 percent adult coverage. CDC Director Dr. Rochelle Walensky presents detailed national and county-level data showing substantial declines in cases, hospitalizations, and deaths, and a growing share of counties in low or moderate transmission, attributing this progress to rising vaccination rates while urging remaining unvaccinated individuals to seek shots. Dr. Anthony Fauci reinforces these trends with real-world studies confirming high vaccine effectiveness of mRNA and Johnson & Johnson vaccines against infection, hospitalization, and death, including in healthcare workers and nursing homes, and he later addresses ongoing research into better protection strategies—such as extra doses, monoclonal antibodies, and antivirals—for immunocompromised patients. Surgeon General Dr. Vivek Murthy reports that more than 70 percent of Americans are vaccinated or inclined to vaccinate and that access barriers, not just hesitancy, are now central—highlighting survey data on time, information, and workplace constraints, the importance of employer-led on-site clinics and paid time off (supported by tax credits), and community-driven campaigns through the COVID-19 Community Corps. In a Q&A, Walensky discusses efforts to improve race and ethnicity data reporting; Fauci and Walensky normalize lingering anxiety about unmasking despite strong safety data for vaccinated people; Slavitt endorses state vaccine lottery incentives and outlines U.S. plans to share 80 million doses abroad based on science and equity; Slavitt also explains the origins of the dating app collaboration and calls for respectful treatment of frontline workers amid mask rule transitions, while all three officials underscore that vaccines are safe, effective, increasingly accessible, and essential to sustaining progress against COVID-19 at home and abroad.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Business partnerships to boost youth vaccination
Acting Administrator Slavitt outlines how businesses that serve young people support vaccination efforts, focusing on incentives and outreach. He highlights dating apps such as Bumble, Tinder, Hinge, Match, OkCupid, BLK, Chispa, Plenty of Fish, and Badoo, which are rolling out features to promote COVID-19 vaccination among users. These initiatives matter because many remaining unvaccinated adults are younger and not opposed to vaccines but have not prioritized them. The current state is that multiple platforms are launching badges, filters, and premium perks while also directing users to vaccination sites.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt says numerous businesses and organizations have responded to the President’s call to help Americans get vaccinated, particularly young people.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt explains that dating apps serving over 50 million U.S. users will allow vaccinated people to display vaccination badges and filter for vaccinated matches.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt cites OkCupid data that users who display vaccination status are 14 percent more likely to get a match, framing vaccination as a socially desirable trait.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt notes that the participating apps will offer premium features such as boosts and super swipes to vaccinated users and will help them locate nearby vaccination sites.
#### Digital Day of Action and vaccine access tools
Slavitt introduces the administration’s “Digital Day of Action,” aimed at driving traffic to federal vaccine information and access tools. He emphasizes that vaccination has become far easier than earlier in the administration, with digital resources and transportation support available. The goal is to maintain vaccination momentum and reach those who have not yet prioritized getting a shot. Key enablers include the Vaccines.gov website, text-based location tools, and free rides to vaccination sites starting Monday.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt announces a “Digital Day of Action” where the White House, Michelle Obama, and others will promote Vaccines.gov and the 438829 text line.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt contrasts current conditions with four months earlier, noting that daily deaths and wait times for vaccines have dropped dramatically since President Biden’s first full day in office.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt reports that more than 125 million Americans are fully vaccinated and over 60 percent of adults have received at least one dose.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt stresses that cases, serious illness, and deaths have fallen sharply due to vaccines but can be reduced further if vaccination pace continues.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt notes that many unvaccinated people are younger and not opposed to vaccination; they simply have not prioritized it.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt explains that texting 438829 will soon provide nearby vaccine locations and an offer of a free round-trip ride, while more employers are offering paid time off to get vaccinated.
#### National COVID-19 trends and county-level risk
Dr. Walensky presents updated national COVID-19 metrics showing significant declines in cases, hospitalizations, and deaths, and then zooms into county-level risk trends. She describes how CDC’s Community Profile Report and COVID Data Tracker reveal shrinking areas of high transmission and growing areas of low or moderate transmission, reflecting vaccination progress. This framing matters for local decision-making, such as school guidance, and for contextualizing individual data points within broader national improvement. The current state is one of steady, widespread decline in community burden, though continued vaccination is needed to sustain it.
- Dr. Walensky reports a seven-day average of about 27,700 daily cases, down more than 19 percent from the prior week and at the lowest level since June 18, 2020.
- Dr. Walensky notes that average daily hospital admissions are just over 3,400, a nearly 15 percent weekly decrease, and daily deaths have declined to 498, a new low.
- Dr. Walensky explains that the CDC’s daily Community Profile Report and county-level COVID Data Tracker help communities monitor local progress and risk.
- Dr. Walensky states that some counties have seen case declines of 25 percent or more in the last week, underscoring heterogeneous but broad improvement.
- Dr. Walensky describes a steady month-long decline in counties classified as high transmission and an increase in counties classified as low or moderate transmission.
- Dr. Walensky reports that 307 counties (10 percent of the U.S.) now have low transmission and 1,183 counties (37 percent) have moderate transmission, defined as fewer than 50 cases per 100,000 in seven days.
- Dr. Walensky highlights CDC maps showing that areas with high and moderate burden (red and pink) are shrinking, while low-burden areas (light green) are markedly increasing across the country.
- Dr. Walensky attributes declining community risk to rising vaccination coverage, with over 60 percent of adults having at least one dose and 126.6 million Americans fully vaccinated.
- Dr. Walensky urges those not fully vaccinated to visit Vaccine.gov and obtain their first or second shot, emphasizing that progress is encouraging but work remains.
#### Real-world vaccine effectiveness and outcomes
Dr. Fauci reviews several new studies confirming the strong real-world effectiveness of COVID-19 vaccines, including mRNA vaccines and Johnson & Johnson’s adenovirus vector vaccine. He highlights high protection against symptomatic disease, infection, hospitalization, and death, including in high-risk settings like healthcare facilities and nursing homes. These data validate clinical trial results and underscore vaccines’ role in reducing transmission and severe outcomes. The current message is that vaccines work extremely well in practice and are essential to protecting individuals and communities.
- Dr. Fauci summarizes a CDC MMWR study showing 94 percent effectiveness of Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna vaccines against symptomatic COVID-19 among healthcare personnel at 33 U.S. sites, matching clinical trial performance.
- Dr. Fauci cites a New England Journal of Medicine study in nursing homes where only 1 percent of residents had infections within 0–14 days after their second dose and just 0.3 percent after 14 days.
- Dr. Fauci notes that 80 percent of infections among vaccinated nursing home residents were asymptomatic, consistent with other observations of milder outcomes after vaccination.
- Dr. Fauci contrasts vaccinated with unvaccinated nursing home residents, noting a 4.3 percent infection rate among unvaccinated within 0–14 days after the first clinic versus much lower rates among vaccinated peers.
- Dr. Fauci interprets lower infection rates among unvaccinated residents later in the study as a likely “mini” version of herd immunity within the nursing home setting.
- Dr. Fauci describes a Mayo Clinic Health System study showing that Johnson & Johnson’s vaccine has higher real-world effectiveness in the U.S. than the originally reported 72 percent efficacy.
- Dr. Fauci reiterates J&J trial data indicating 74 percent overall efficacy in the U.S., focusing on updated analyses of hospitalizations and deaths.
- Dr. Fauci reports 93 percent vaccine efficacy of J&J against hospitalization 14 days after vaccination and 100 percent efficacy against hospitalization at 28 days or later.
- Dr. Fauci notes there were no COVID-19-related deaths in the J&J vaccine group compared with five deaths in the placebo group.
- Dr. Fauci concludes that vaccines protect individuals and families and interrupt transmission chains, reinforcing the shared message: people should get vaccinated.
#### Vaccine confidence, access barriers, and employer role
Dr. Murthy discusses public attitudes toward vaccination and emphasizes that overall vaccine confidence remains high, but access barriers persist, especially among unvaccinated populations. He reviews survey data showing that time constraints, lack of information, and documentation concerns deter many willing individuals and underscores how employers and workplaces can help by providing time off and on-site vaccinations. This matters for both overall coverage and equity, as access barriers disproportionately affect Hispanic and Black adults. The current strategy focuses on an all-hands approach involving tax credits, community organizations, and employers to reduce barriers and close gaps.
- Dr. Murthy cites CDC survey data of over 14,000 people showing that more than 70 percent of Americans are vaccinated, plan to get vaccinated, or are likely to do so, indicating high vaccine confidence.
- Dr. Murthy notes that some unvaccinated people have questions, and the COVID-19 Community Corps is mobilizing trusted messengers to address concerns, but confidence overall is solid.
- Dr. Murthy references a Kaiser Family Foundation survey finding that over one-third of unvaccinated people who want shots as soon as possible cite lack of time or scheduling conflicts as the main barrier.
- Dr. Murthy explains that about 7 percent of this group lacks information on how to get vaccinated and 8 percent are unsure about their eligibility or required documentation, representing millions of people.
- Dr. Murthy says that among those not seeking immediate vaccination, access still matters: 28 percent of employed people would be more likely to vaccinate if given time off and 20 percent if they could get shots at work.
- Dr. Murthy stresses that employers can boost vaccination rates and narrow equity gaps because unvaccinated Hispanic (64 percent) and Black (55 percent) adults are more concerned about missing work than white adults (41 percent).
- Dr. Murthy highlights new tax credits for businesses and nonprofits with fewer than 500 employees that offer paid time off for workers to get vaccinated and recover from side effects.
- Dr. Murthy frames the next phase of the campaign as driven by people, organizations, and communities helping to vaccinate their social networks, calling for an all-hands-on-deck approach to access, motivation, and confidence.
- Dr. Murthy showcases examples such as Joe’s Kansas City Bar-B-Que providing time off, The Feeding Zoo hosting on-site vaccinations, Young Invincibles’ “Don’t miss out, LA” outreach, and ParentsTogether’s online engagement with parents about vaccines.
- Dr. Murthy asks the public to join the “Digital Day of Action,” directing people to Vaccine.gov and text codes GETVAX (438829) and VACUNA (882862), and reminds them that the vaccine is free and the best pathway out of the pandemic.
#### CDC race and ethnicity data reporting
In response to a media question, Dr. Walensky describes CDC’s current practices and efforts regarding race and ethnicity data on vaccination. She clarifies that updates occur roughly weekly and that CDC has added a feature tracking vaccine distribution over a rolling two-week period. Recognizing that missing race and ethnicity data remains a concern, she notes active work with state and local partners to improve completeness. The current state is incremental progress on data quality with ongoing efforts to reduce the approximately 40 percent missing data.
- Reporter Victoria Knight asks whether CDC will update state-by-state race and ethnicity vaccination data daily and how it plans to address nearly 40 percent missing race and ethnicity information.
- Dr. Walensky responds that CDC is updating its website approximately weekly and recently added a rolling two-week vaccine distribution view.
- Dr. Walensky says CDC is working actively with state and local public health authorities to obtain more race and ethnicity data and has already made some progress.
- Dr. Walensky indicates that improving completeness of race and ethnicity data remains an ongoing priority for CDC.
#### Public anxiety about returning to normal
A question about nervous reactions to a large, presumably vaccinated, indoor White House event leads to a discussion of psychological readiness to resume normal activities. Dr. Fauci and Dr. Walensky both validate lingering fears as understandable after months of caution. They emphasize that CDC guidance reflects strong scientific evidence that vaccinated individuals can safely be indoors and outdoors without masks but acknowledge that people will adjust at different paces. The current stance is that continued mask use by vaccinated individuals is acceptable and that social norms will shift gradually as people gain confidence.
- Reporter Steve Portnoy asks how officials respond to people who feel anxious seeing a large indoor gathering of vaccinated people at the White House after months of advice to avoid crowds.
- Dr. Fauci says such fears are not irrational and are understandable given the behavioral habits formed over a prolonged period of restrictions.
- Dr. Fauci reiterates that CDC guidance, based on science, indicates fully vaccinated people can feel safe indoors and outdoors without masks, even in crowds.
- Dr. Walensky notes that after 16 months of urging mask use, it is hard for many to immediately stop, and CDC expected that not everyone would feel ready to “rip off their masks.”
- Dr. Walensky stresses there is no shame or problem if vaccinated people continue to wear masks and anticipates a gradual transition as people adapt at their own pace.
#### State vaccine lotteries and incentives
Slavitt addresses questions about state-run vaccine lottery programs in places like Ohio, New York, and Maryland. He indicates that available data suggests these incentives are effective at increasing vaccination by drawing attention to the issue among those who are not opposed but unmotivated. The federal team views such programs positively and sees them as a useful tool in raising vaccine uptake. The current posture is supportive enthusiasm without a detailed federal directive to states.
- Reporter Peter Sullivan asks whether vaccine lottery programs in Ohio, New York, and Maryland are a good idea and if the administration encourages more states to adopt them.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt says data so far indicate these lottery incentives appear to work in increasing vaccination rates.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt explains that many unvaccinated people are not opposed to vaccines but have not prioritized them, so attention-grabbing incentives like lotteries can be effective.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt states that the administration is enthusiastic about such efforts, implicitly encouraging states’ use of incentives.
#### Impact of improving trends on vaccine motivation and global dose sharing
Responding to a question about whether declining cases and deaths might reduce motivation to vaccinate, Dr. Murthy and Slavitt argue that improvements result from vaccination and must be sustained by continued uptake. They caution against complacency and emphasize that millions still need full vaccination to prevent future surges. Slavitt also updates on U.S. plans to share 80 million vaccine doses abroad, noting pending FDA review and that allocation decisions will be guided by science, public health, and equity. The current state is ongoing domestic education campaigns and a firm commitment to international dose sharing before July 4th, with details forthcoming.
- Reporter Jeremy Diamond asks how declining cases and deaths may affect vaccine decisions among hesitant individuals and requests an update on U.S. plans to send doses overseas.
- Dr. Murthy warns that interpreting improving metrics as a signal to stop vaccinating would be the wrong conclusion, emphasizing that progress is due to vaccinations already administered.
- Dr. Murthy says continued vaccination is necessary to sustain declines in cases, hospitalizations, and deaths and avoid future infection spikes.
- Dr. Murthy urges people not to let their guard down but to remain cautiously optimistic and keep “foot on the accelerator” for vaccinations.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt notes that people still vaccinate against diseases like measles even when perceived risk is low, underscoring the role of public education.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt confirms the U.S. commitment to send 80 million doses abroad by July 4th, representing 13 percent of procured doses, in addition to a $4 billion COVAX pledge.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt says they await FDA approval for AstraZeneca doses and have no country allocation announcements yet.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt reiterates President Biden’s position that dose allocation decisions will be based solely on science, public health, and equity.
#### Origins of dating app vaccination initiative
A question about how the dating app vaccination features originated leads Slavitt to explain the collaboration with private companies. He frames the initiative as a response to people’s desire to safely resume social and romantic activities and links it to broader concerns about loneliness and social connection. Slavitt clarifies that companies themselves saw a need among their users and developed these features, with the administration engaging in conversations but not directing them. The current state is a public-private partnership that aligns vaccination with returning to valued social behaviors.
- Reporter Maureen Groppe asks how the dating app initiative came about and whether this is a first for the White House in engaging with dating sites.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt says the effort reflects recognition that people want to return to normal social and dating lives and that vaccines enable them to do this safely.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt references Dr. Murthy’s work on loneliness and social contact to underscore the seriousness behind what might seem like a light topic.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt credits the dating companies for taking the initiative based on their understanding of audience needs, noting that the White House had conversations with them but they led the effort.
#### Mask conflicts and respect for frontline workers
A question about confrontations over mask requirements, including viral videos and incidents in states with remaining mandates, prompts a brief response from Slavitt. He acknowledges the intense stress of the past year and a half and the difficulty of adjusting to changing rules. He does not delve into enforcement specifics but calls for mutual respect in interactions, particularly involving frontline workers. The current message is a general appeal for civility during the transition period.
- Reporter Tommy Christopher describes harassment and threats faced by workers enforcing mask rules, especially in states like New Jersey that have not yet lifted mandates, and asks for officials’ views.
- Reporter Tommy Christopher notes viral incidents such as actor Ricky Schroder berating a Costco employee and mentions customers threatening to get workers fired over mask requests.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt acknowledges the last 18 months as an intensely stressful period and recognizes that people are feeling strong emotions while adjusting to new guidance.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt urges people to treat each other with respect, implicitly supporting frontline workers tasked with implementing mask policies.
#### Employer on-site vaccination and immunocompromised protection
In response to questions about employer-hosted vaccinations and protection for immunocompromised people, Slavitt, Murthy, and Fauci outline current efforts and research. Slavitt and Murthy explain that many employers already host on-site or near-site clinics through partnerships with pharmacies and that the federal government is helping match employers with vaccinators, complemented by tax credits and community resources. Dr. Fauci addresses concerns about immunosuppressed individuals by describing ongoing NIAID studies on strategies such as additional doses and the potential, but yet untested, use of monoclonal antibodies or antiviral therapies. The current state includes active employer engagement and research into tailored protections for vulnerable patients, with future updates anticipated.
- Reporter Meg Tirrell asks what the administration wants from employers in offering on-site vaccination clinics and what barriers exist, and she inquires about using monoclonal antibodies preventively for immunocompromised people.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt says many employers are already arranging on-site and near-site clinics via state partnerships or pharmacies and that the government is helping match large employers with providers.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt strongly encourages employers to host vaccination clinics and provide paid time off, calling it a beneficial and increasingly common practice.
- Dr. Murthy outlines three key roles for employers: building vaccine confidence, motivating employees through encouragement and shared stories, and improving access by bringing vaccines closer to workers.
- Dr. Murthy identifies barriers for employers such as lack of awareness of their potential impact, uncertainty about logistics and partnerships, and questions about whether efforts are effective.
- Dr. Murthy says tax credits and the COVID-19 Community Corps aim to give employers tools, best practices, and peer support for workplace vaccination initiatives.
- Dr. Fauci notes that immunosuppressed individuals, including solid organ transplant recipients and some autoimmune patients on immunosuppressants, may respond less robustly to vaccines.
- Dr. Fauci reports that NIAID is conducting a major study on immunosuppressed patients to evaluate strategies such as additional vaccine doses.
- Dr. Fauci calls the idea of prophylactic monoclonal antibodies for immunocompromised individuals reasonable but points out it has not yet been studied in that context and would require repeated dosing due to antibody half-life.
- Dr. Fauci suggests that direct-acting antivirals, used either for immediate early treatment or as prophylaxis akin to HIV approaches, are another promising avenue under active pursuit to protect immunosuppressed patients.
- Acting Administrator Slavitt indicates they may devote a future briefing to update on antiviral clinical trials when the timing is appropriate.
