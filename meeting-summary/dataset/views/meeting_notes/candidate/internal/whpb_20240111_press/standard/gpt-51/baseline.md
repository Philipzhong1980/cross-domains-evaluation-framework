### Overview
National Economic Advisor Lael Brainard and Admiral John Kirby lead a White House briefing that pairs economic messaging with national security updates: Brainard previews President Biden’s trip to Allentown, Pennsylvania, using local job growth, infrastructure upgrades, and a surge in small-business applications to illustrate a broader nationwide entrepreneurship boom and improved inflation backdrop, while detailing ongoing efforts to cut household costs through drug pricing reforms, fee regulation, and pressure on corporations, amid questions on elevated car insurance, rent-driven inflation, Red Sea supply risks, banking stability, and public skepticism about ‘Bidenomics’; Kirby then condemns Iran’s seizure of the tanker St. Nikolas and addresses Houthi attacks in the Red Sea, reiterating that the U.S. will defend commercial shipping without pre-announcing military options as he simultaneously rejects ICJ genocide allegations against Israel, defends continued U.S. military support and oversight of arms transfers while acknowledging high civilian casualties and humanitarian shortfalls in Gaza, and notes that Ukraine aid has halted pending congressional approval of new funding, before Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre turns to domestic politics, pressing House Republicans to honor an existing budget agreement and avoid a shutdown, dismissing impeachment efforts against DHS Secretary Mayorkas as baseless stunts, defending Biden’s public engagement and donor access practices, reaffirming ongoing efforts to secure the release of detained Americans like Paul Whelan and Evan Gershkovich, backing Julie Su’s renomination as Labor Secretary, and highlighting the administration’s gun violence prevention, energy, and judicial nomination agendas as examples of continued policy activity despite partisan headwinds in Congress.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### President’s Allentown trip and local economy
Lael Brainard previews President Biden’s upcoming trip to Allentown, Pennsylvania, framing it as an example of a community recovering from past economic decline. She contrasts Allentown’s prior struggles under “trickle-down economics” with recent gains in jobs, infrastructure, and small-business activity. The discussion matters politically and economically, as the White House uses Allentown to illustrate the administration’s broader economic narrative. The current state is described as a robust local comeback supported by federal investment and community resilience.
- Ms. Brainard states that President Biden will travel to the Allentown area to meet small-business owners and hear about community economic comebacks.
- Ms. Brainard notes that Allentown, once a booming steel town, saw lagging employment and small-business opportunities before the pandemic.
- Ms. Brainard reports that since President Biden took office, 32,000 jobs have been created in Allentown and the unemployment rate has fallen from 6.4 percent to 3.9 percent, the lowest in 30 years.
- Ms. Brainard highlights that 17,000 more Allentown residents now have access to high-speed Internet and points to infrastructure and energy investments in airports, bridges, and manufacturing, including Pratt Industries.
- Ms. Brainard cites 4,000 new business applications in Allentown over three years as a sign of hope and confidence.

#### National small-business boom and entrepreneurship
Brainard broadens the lens from Allentown to national data showing a surge in small-business applications. She emphasizes that 16 million business applications have been filed since Biden took office, making each of the last three years a record for new applications. The administration presents this as evidence of economic dynamism and particular gains for minority entrepreneurs. The current state is characterized as a nationwide small-business boom with strong follow-through into actual business formation and job creation.
- Ms. Brainard says new data show a small-business boom across America, with 16 million business applications filed since President Biden took office.
- Ms. Brainard notes that each of the last three years has seen more business applications than any previous year on record.
- Ms. Brainard points out that there have been more applications in the first three years of the Biden administration than in all four years of the previous administration.
- Ms. Brainard explains that analysis shows these applications are translating into actual new business formation, which matters because small businesses are major job creators.
- Ms. Brainard highlights that Black business ownership has doubled and Hispanic business ownership is up 40 percent compared to pre-pandemic levels.

#### Inflation trends, supply chains, and prices
Brainard reviews new inflation data, arguing that inflation has fallen sharply as supply chain pressures ease. She links improvements in shipping and logistics to lower price growth and cites specific consumer categories—eggs, milk, toys, and gasoline—where prices have declined. This matters because the administration seeks to show progress on cost-of-living issues while acknowledging ongoing public concern. The current state is that inflation is down nearly two-thirds from its peak and key supply chain indicators have returned to pre-pandemic levels, but vigilance continues.
- Ms. Brainard states that inflation is down nearly two thirds as supply chain pressures have eased back to pre-pandemic levels.
- Ms. Brainard credits administration work with the private sector on shipping, trucking, semiconductors, and other areas for reducing supply chain pressures.
- Ms. Brainard notes that prices have come down for eggs, milk, toys, and that gas prices are now below $3 per gallon in much of the country.
- Ms. Brainard reiterates that the administration remains focused on lowering costs for American households despite recent inflation improvements.

#### Cost-lowering agenda and corporate pricing
Brainard outlines Biden’s policy agenda to reduce household costs, focusing on healthcare and consumer fees. She highlights actions on insulin, Medicare drug price negotiations, caps on seniors’ out-of-pocket costs, and efforts to curb junk fees like overdraft and baggage charges. She also calls on corporations that raised prices during supply chain disruptions to lower them now that input costs have fallen. The administration positions itself as actively fighting for consumers while contrasting its approach with congressional Republicans’ focus on tax cuts.
- Ms. Brainard emphasizes that President Biden is focused on lowering prescription drug costs, including capping insulin prices and negotiating Medicare drug prices.
- Ms. Brainard says the administration is reducing out-of-pocket costs for seniors and capping their annual drug expenditures at $2,000.
- Ms. Brainard highlights independent-agency actions on overdraft fees, bounced check fees, baggage fees, and family seating fees as examples of using regulatory authority to cut costs.
- Ms. Brainard calls on corporations that raised prices during snarled supply chains to bring those prices down and pass cost savings to consumers.
- Ms. Brainard contrasts the administration’s cost-lowering agenda with congressional Republicans, whom she says are instead pushing tax cuts for the wealthiest Americans and large corporations.

#### Tools to address elevated car insurance costs
In response to questions about car insurance driving CPI, Brainard outlines the administration’s limited but multifaceted toolkit for tackling specific price categories. She distinguishes between legislative authorities, independent regulatory agencies, and public pressure on corporations. While she does not detail a specific new initiative on auto insurance, she suggests relevant oversight may exist within independent agencies. The current state is general monitoring and reliance on existing authority rather than a targeted new policy announced at the briefing.
- A reporter notes that car insurance rate increases accounted for about 15 percent of the overall CPI increase and asks what leverage the administration has with insurers.
- Ms. Brainard explains that the administration uses legislative authorities to act in areas like insulin, Medicare drug price negotiations, and out-of-pocket healthcare costs.
- Ms. Brainard says independent agencies hold enforcement and rulemaking authorities, which have been used to curb overdraft and bounced check fees, as well as family seating and baggage fees.
- Ms. Brainard indicates that in the insurance area there may be relevant authorities, but she stresses these sit with independent agencies rather than the White House.
- Ms. Brainard reiterates that the administration also relies on public calls for big businesses to lower prices that were raised during supply chain disruptions.

#### Red Sea disruptions and U.S. economic impact
Brainard addresses concerns about Houthi-related attacks and disruptions in the Red Sea and their potential impact on global supply chains and U.S. prices. She separates national security management from economic monitoring and reports that, so far, U.S. economic effects are minimal. She notes some rerouting and longer shipping times but points to comprehensive supply chain indices that remain at pre-pandemic stress levels. The current state is close monitoring of shipping times, costs, and oil prices with no significant domestic economic impact yet observed.
- Ms. Brainard says the national security team is handling the security dimension of Red Sea attacks while her team monitors potential economic effects.
- Ms. Brainard reports that some shippers are using alternate routes, causing longer shipping times, but says there has been very little effect on the U.S. economy so far.
- Ms. Brainard notes that comprehensive supply chain pressure measures remain at pre-pandemic levels despite Red Sea disruptions.
- Ms. Brainard identifies oil prices and shipping costs and times as key real-time indicators the administration is watching, adding that retail gas prices remain down about $1.90 from their peak and below $3 per gallon for most Americans.
- Ms. Brainard says the administration will continue monitoring any possible effects on the U.S., while acknowledging the shipping lanes are more salient for other parts of the world.

#### Rent inflation and housing affordability
Brainard is pressed about rising rents as a major driver of inflation and outlines the administration’s approach to housing affordability. She underscores the importance of housing in household budgets and advocates for congressional action on housing tax credits and first-generation homebuyer support. She notes that mortgage rates have fallen somewhat in recent months but insists that forecasts of moderating rents are insufficient without policy intervention. The current state involves White House proposals stalled in Congress and continued calls for bipartisan legislative cooperation.
- Ms. Brainard acknowledges that rent and housing affordability are major concerns for households and are a significant driver of inflation.
- Ms. Brainard urges Congress to pass housing tax credits, which she says the President has proposed in every budget and which are critical for increasing affordable rental supply.
- Ms. Brainard says the administration has put forward proposals to help first-generation homebuyers and notes that mortgage rates have declined by about 1.25 percentage points in recent months.
- Ms. Brainard states that some forecasts anticipate average rents coming down but argues that forecasts alone are not enough and that congressional action is needed.
- Ms. Brainard expresses hope that Republicans in Congress will work with the administration to make housing more affordable.

#### Child tax credit and tax negotiations
Responding to questions on Capitol Hill tax talks, Brainard reiterates Biden’s strong support for expanding and making refundable the child tax credit for low- and moderate-income families. She cites the prior expansion’s effectiveness in reducing child poverty as a key economic and moral justification. The administration is open to a balanced package that might also include business tax items but prioritizes the child tax credit’s reach to poorer families. The current state is ongoing negotiations with no announced deal, but clear White House conditions for support.
- A reporter asks how the White House balances potential child tax credit and R&D tax benefits against concerns about inflation.
- Ms. Brainard stresses that President Biden’s expanded child tax credit was highly effective at lifting millions of children out of poverty.
- Ms. Brainard says the President wants to extend the child tax credit with a focus on refundability for low- and moderate-income families.
- Ms. Brainard states that the administration hopes to see a balanced tax package but that the critical element for the President is extending a child tax credit that reaches lower- and moderate-income households.

#### Regional banking sector stability
Brainard revisits last year’s regional banking turmoil and provides an updated assessment of the sector’s health. She notes that regional banks have strengthened their capital and liquidity positions and that overall resilience has improved. This matters for financial stability and credit conditions, which underpin the broader economy. The current state is a more robust regional banking sector, although the administration continues to monitor it closely.
- A reporter recalls the 2023 banking crisis and asks about the health of mid-size and smaller banks now.
- Ms. Brainard explains that regional banks have shored up capital and liquidity compared to a year ago.
- Ms. Brainard says most analyses indicate that the regional banking sector is now much healthier overall.
- Ms. Brainard emphasizes that the administration has been monitoring the sector closely and will continue to do so.

#### Food prices, wages, and perception of ‘Bidenomics’
Brainard addresses concerns that food prices remain high despite cooled inflation and questions about why the ‘Bidenomics’ narrative has not resonated. She notes declines over the past year in several grocery categories and points to strong macroeconomic indicators: job growth, real wage gains since pre-pandemic, higher median household wealth, and strong small-business formation. When challenged with data on real wages, she counters with updated figures showing real wage gains, especially for moderate-income workers, and increased purchasing power. The administration concedes work remains on costs but maintains that fundamentals are strong.
- A reporter asks what Brainard would say to Americans worried about still-high food prices and why the term ‘Bidenomics’ has not caught on.
- Ms. Brainard says the administration is very focused on grocery prices and notes declines in categories such as dairy, fruits, and vegetables over the last year.
- Ms. Brainard highlights that total U.S. employment is up 14.3 million since Biden took office, including 2.7 million in the last year, with low unemployment.
- Ms. Brainard states that real wages are up since pre-pandemic, median household wealth is higher in real terms, and small-business creation has surged.
- When confronted with figures suggesting real wages are down, Ms. Brainard responds that real wages are up over 1 percent for all workers and over 3 percent for moderate-income workers, and she claims households have about $1,700 in additional purchasing power compared to pre-pandemic.
- Ms. Brainard concludes that while economic indicators are strong, the administration still has work to do on lowering costs, which remains the President’s focus.

#### Iran’s seizure of tanker and U.S. response
Admiral John Kirby addresses the reported Iranian seizure of the oil tanker St. Nikolas in the Gulf of Oman. He condemns the action as provocative and unlawful, calling for the immediate release of the ship and crew. Kirby frames the seizure within a broader pattern of destabilizing Iranian behavior that the U.S. seeks to deter with allies. The current state is diplomatic condemnation and coordination with partners, with no specific new operational steps disclosed at the podium.
- Mr. Kirby reports that Iran has apparently seized the Marshall Islands-flagged, Greek-owned tanker St. Nikolas while it was lawfully transiting the Gulf of Oman.
- Mr. Kirby condemns the seizure as provocative and unacceptable and urges Iran to immediately release the ship and its crew.
- Mr. Kirby says the United States will work with allies and partners to deter and confront the full range of Iran’s destabilizing behavior in coordination with the international community.
- Mr. Kirby later dismisses Iranian claims that the seizure is retaliation for a prior U.S. confiscation, stating there is no justification for seizing a commercial vessel in international waters.

#### Media freedom, Israeli Supreme Court case, and ICJ proceedings
Kirby responds to questions about a case before the Israeli Supreme Court affecting journalists and about International Court of Justice (ICJ) proceedings alleging genocide in Gaza. He avoids commenting on specific court cases but strongly reaffirms U.S. support for media freedom and journalists’ safety. On the ICJ case, he calls the genocide accusations against Israel unfounded and rejects the notion that U.S. support for Israel violates international law. The current state is continued U.S. backing for Israel’s right to self-defense, ongoing private discussions with Israel on civilian protection and media freedom, and refusal to pre-commit to accepting potential ICJ penalties or sanctions scenarios.
- Mr. Kirby says he cannot comment on a case before the Israeli Supreme Court but affirms strong U.S. support for journalists’ work and safety in Gaza and elsewhere.
- Mr. Kirby notes that media freedom is a standing topic in U.S. discussions with Israeli counterparts, though he will not detail those diplomatic conversations.
- In response to ICJ arguments that Gaza is occupied and Israel bears legal responsibility for civilian protection, Mr. Kirby reiterates that the U.S. considers the genocide case against Israel unfounded and rejects the application of the term ‘genocide’ in this context.
- Mr. Kirby states that Israel has a right and responsibility to defend itself and that the U.S. will continue supplying it with necessary tools and capabilities.
- Mr. Kirby pushes back firmly against claims that the U.S. is violating international law by supporting Israel, answering “absolutely no” when asked directly.
- Mr. Kirby says he will not speculate on whether the U.S. would accept or block ICJ sanctions against Israel, emphasizing that the U.S. position on the case and on support for Israel is already clear.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that the U.S. continually presses Israel to protect civilians and civilian infrastructure and to minimize civilian casualties during operations.

#### Houthi attacks in Red Sea and potential U.S. action
Kirby fields multiple questions on Houthi attacks against commercial shipping in the Red Sea and possible U.S. responses. He echoes Secretary Blinken’s warning that the U.S. is prepared to act but refuses to detail potential military options or red lines. He emphasizes the need to protect international commerce and U.S. naval forces and notes the multinational nature of Operation Prosperity Guardian. The current state is heightened concern, ongoing consultations with partners, and a clear message that the Houthis must stop attacks or face consequences, without explicit disclosure of future operations.
- Mr. Kirby says he will not telegraph potential U.S. military actions against Houthi attacks but asserts the U.S. will do what is necessary to counter and defeat threats to commercial shipping in the Red Sea.
- Mr. Kirby reiterates that the U.S. continues to consult closely with allies and partners on appropriate next steps regarding Houthi attacks.
- In response to Houthi threats of retaliatory escalation, Mr. Kirby states that the Houthis will bear the consequences if they fail to stop attacks.
- Mr. Kirby later underscores that the Red Sea waterway is vital for international commerce and notes that over 20 nations are participating in Operation Prosperity Guardian.
- Mr. Kirby declines to outline any U.S. red lines or constraints publicly, arguing it is not helpful to discuss potential future military options from the podium.

#### Intensity of Israeli operations and congressional oversight of arms
Kirby addresses questions about U.S. efforts to reduce the intensity of Israeli military operations in Gaza and about congressional oversight of U.S. arms transfers. He notes that Israel has acknowledged the need to transition to a different phase of operations, and the U.S. has encouraged that shift, but insists Israel must speak to its own timelines. On oversight, he stresses that the administration respects congressional authority and legal requirements, while rejecting the premise that there is no oversight or accountability. The current state is sustained U.S. security assistance to Israel with stated expectations on conduct, debates on Capitol Hill over emergency arms transfers, and ongoing U.S.-Israel consultations about operational phases and civilian harm reduction.
- Mr. Kirby says Israel has publicly recognized the need to transition to a new phase of operations in Gaza, and he confirms the U.S. has encouraged such planning, but he declines to predict when changes will occur.
- Mr. Kirby declines to comment specifically on Senator Kaine’s reported effort to restrict future emergency arms transfers to Israel, citing a lack of detailed review and general caution about pending legislation.
- Mr. Kirby insists that there is congressional oversight of U.S. arms transfers and that the administration follows both the letter and spirit of the law, referring process details to the State Department.
- Mr. Kirby states that the U.S. remains in constant contact with Israeli counterparts and still sees no indication that Israel is violating the laws of armed conflict, even as he acknowledges there are too many civilian casualties.
- Mr. Kirby affirms that the U.S. holds Israel to clear expectations on the use of U.S.-supplied materiel and that these expectations are consistent with standards applied to other partners.

#### Tracking Cabinet officials and Secretary Austin hospitalization
Kirby responds to questions about how the White House Situation Room tracks Cabinet members’ locations in light of the delayed disclosure of Defense Secretary Lloyd Austin’s hospitalization. He describes a process based on daily check-ins that provide general location information and rejects the idea of using real-time GPS tracking or intrusive monitoring. He acknowledges that the failure to promptly learn of Austin’s hospitalization and delegation of authority was a problem that must not recur. The current state includes a directive from Chief of Staff Jeff Zients to review notification protocols and a commitment to improve transparency and internal communication, while maintaining privacy boundaries for senior officials.
- A reporter questions why, in 2024, the Situation Room relies on daily check-ins for Cabinet locations instead of modern GPS tracking, in light of the Austin episode.
- Mr. Kirby explains that the Situation Room tracks Cabinet officials’ general location through morning check-ins and logs, not by tracking their mobile phones or microchipping them.
- Mr. Kirby insists the administration will not use phone GPS or comparable tools to track officials in real time and quips that they will not be treated like “a poodle.”
- Mr. Kirby concedes that the White House did not know Secretary Austin was hospitalized and calls this a problem that should not happen again.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Chief of Staff Jeff Zients issued a memo directing Cabinet agencies to review protocols for notifying the White House when a principal is hospitalized or delegates authority, and he pledges that the administration will learn from the incident.

#### Accountability of U.S. weapons to Ukraine
Kirby is asked about a Defense Department inspector general report indicating incomplete accounting for tens of thousands of small, sensitive weapons supplied to Ukraine. He acknowledges the challenges of end-use monitoring in an active warzone while emphasizing ongoing improvements in oversight. Kirby cites the report’s findings that the Pentagon has enhanced mechanisms like handheld scanners and staffing and that there is no evidence of widespread diversion or black-market leakage. The current state is continued efforts to strengthen accountability within realistic constraints, with no indication to date of systemic illicit diversion of U.S. arms in Ukraine.
- A reporter notes that a Defense Department IG report found incomplete accounting for nearly 40,000 small, sensitive U.S.-supplied weapons to Ukraine and asks for the White House reaction.
- Mr. Kirby thanks the Defense Department IG for its detailed work and says the administration has long prioritized improving end-use accountability for materiel sent to Ukraine.
- Mr. Kirby highlights that the report acknowledges improvements such as handheld scanners and expanded embassy staff to better track arms.
- Mr. Kirby underscores the report’s conclusion that there is no evidence of large-scale diversion or illicit trafficking of U.S. materiel provided to Ukraine.
- Mr. Kirby stresses that wartime conditions limit the ability to count every round or weapon, and he notes the U.S. will not risk personnel by placing them near the front lines solely for tracking purposes.

#### NATO posture and Russian frozen assets
Kirby is asked to respond to former President Trump’s comments about NATO and to clarify U.S. policy on confiscating frozen Russian assets. He refuses to engage with campaign-trail rhetoric but points to Biden’s record of strengthening NATO, including expansion and enhanced capability in response to Russia’s war in Ukraine. On Russian assets, he offers no update, noting only that discussions with allies on possible confiscation and use of assets are ongoing. The current state is continued diplomatic work on asset policy and a reaffirmed commitment to NATO under Biden’s leadership.
- A reporter asks whether President Biden shares former President Trump’s view that Europe and NATO have taken advantage of the United States and owe it billions of dollars, and seeks comment on Trump’s reluctance to fully commit to NATO.
- Mr. Kirby declines to discuss campaign-trail statements or the former President’s remarks, calling such commentary inappropriate from his position.
- Mr. Kirby urges reporters to look at President Biden’s record, asserting that NATO is now larger and more capable due to Biden’s leadership and the Alliance’s response to Putin’s war in Ukraine.
- When pressed about confiscating roughly $300 billion in frozen Russian assets, Mr. Kirby says he has no update and notes that discussions remain ongoing with allies on this issue.

#### Domestic security threats, Hezbollah, and judges
Kirby fields questions about potential Hezbollah threats to Americans and increasing threats against judges, including swatting incidents. On Hezbollah, he says the U.S. is closely monitoring the situation in Lebanon and the Israel–Lebanon border and remains intent on preventing regional escalation, but he does not share specific threat intelligence. On threats to judges, he defers to the Justice Department while affirming that the administration wants judicial officials to operate safely. Later, Jean-Pierre reiterates that Biden condemns threats or violence against judges and any group. The current state is heightened vigilance across multiple threat vectors with limited public detail on specific countermeasures.
- A reporter cites a Politico report about rising risks of Hezbollah attacks on Americans in the Middle East or domestically and asks if the threat environment has changed.

- Mr. Kirby declines to discuss intelligence assessments or specific threats to Americans but pledges that the government works to ensure U.S. travelers have information needed to stay safe.
- Another reporter asks about an uptick in threats and swatting incidents targeting judges, including a judge in Trump’s civil fraud case.
- Mr. Kirby says that issue is outside his lane and refers questions to the Justice Department but stresses that officials should be able to do their jobs safely.
- Later, Ms. Jean-Pierre adds that President Biden condemns any threats or violence against judges or any group and will remain steadfast on this stance.

#### Humanitarian situation in Gaza and U.S. aid efforts
Kirby confronts questions about hunger and potential famine in Gaza amid limited humanitarian access. He acknowledges serious food insecurity, ongoing starvation risks, and an insufficient volume of aid trucks compared to pre-war levels. He notes some progress, such as Israel’s opening of the Kerem Shalom crossing, but concedes that current flows—reaching around 200 trucks on the best days—fall far short of needs. The U.S. continues to press Israel and regional partners to increase both the speed and quantity of aid, and Secretary Blinken has made this a focus of his regional diplomacy. The current state is persistent humanitarian distress in Gaza with ongoing U.S. efforts but no satisfactory solution yet.
- A reporter asks whether the administration has fresh concerns about famine in Gaza given the slow trickle of humanitarian assistance.
- Mr. Kirby states that the U.S. recognizes serious food security problems in Gaza and that many people are suffering from hunger and starvation.
- Mr. Kirby says food, along with water and medicine, is at the top of the list of items the U.S. is trying to get into Gaza via humanitarian aid.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Israel has opened the Kerem Shalom crossing, creating an additional entry point, but admits that the number of trucks entering Gaza remains inadequate.
- Mr. Kirby reports that on a maximum day roughly 200 trucks have gotten in, which is far below pre-war levels, and he stresses that the U.S. is not satisfied and will keep pressing to increase both volume and speed of aid.

He stresses that Russian attacks are intensifying, now including the use of North Korean ballistic missiles, and that Ukraine’s needs are particularly acute during winter. The administration frames congressional passage of the national security supplemental request as urgent to resume support. The current state is a pause in new U.S. security assistance packages for Ukraine pending congressional action, despite a deteriorating battlefield situation.
- A reporter asks for an update on military assistance to Ukraine and whether funding has truly run dry.
- Mr. Kirby confirms that the U.S. has issued the last drawdown package for which there was funding and that assistance has now ground to a halt.
- Mr. Kirby urges Congress to pass the national security supplemental request, calling additional funding critical.
- Mr. Kirby notes that Russian attacks are increasing and that Russia is using North Korean ballistic missiles against Ukraine, making Ukraine’s needs especially acute in the winter months.

#### Paul Whelan and Evan Gershkovich detention diplomacy
Jean-Pierre gives an update on President Biden’s meeting with Elizabeth Whelan, sister of detained American Paul Whelan.
