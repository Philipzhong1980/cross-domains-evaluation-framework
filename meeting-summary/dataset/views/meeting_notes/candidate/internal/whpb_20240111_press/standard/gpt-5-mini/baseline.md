### Overview
  National Economic Advisor Lael Brainard and National Security official Admiral John Kirby brief reporters on a range of economic and security issues. Brainard previews President Biden’s Allentown trip, highlights local recovery metrics, and announces record national small‑business application figures since the President took office; she presents new inflation data showing substantial easing, outlines the administration’s agenda to lower household costs—especially prescription drugs and insulin—and urges corporate price pass‑through while contrasting the administration’s priorities with Republican tax proposals. Reporters probed car insurance’s CPI impact, Red Sea shipping risks, rent-driven inflation, tax negotiations (emphasizing the child tax credit), banking-sector health, and wage trends, and Brainard described available legislative and agency tools and urged Congress to act on housing and family supports. Admiral Kirby condemned Iran’s apparent seizure of the St. Nikolas tanker, warned the Houthis to stop attacks on Red Sea shipping, defended U.S. support for Israel’s self‑defense while rejecting genocide allegations, and stressed efforts to expand humanitarian aid into Gaza; he also addressed oversight of arms transfers, accountability for materiel sent to Ukraine, gaps revealed by Secretary Austin’s hospitalization, and the need for supplemental Congressional funding for Ukraine. Karine Jean‑Pierre closed with domestic political items—urging House Republicans to honor a spending agreement to avoid a shutdown, noting judicial nominations remain a priority, calling impeachment efforts 'baseless' political maneuvers, reaffirming the administration’s work on hostage cases, and answering questions on topics from U.S.–Mexico relations to gun violence prevention—while emphasizing continued executive efforts to lower costs and defend U.S. national security interests.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Allentown presidential visit
  Lael Brainard previews President Biden’s trip to Allentown, Pennsylvania, where he will meet small-business owners and highlight local economic recovery. The administration frames the visit as demonstrating the comeback of communities previously hurt by trickle-down policies. Officials present local metrics showing job growth, lower unemployment, expanded broadband access, infrastructure investment, and new business applications.
- Lael Brainard says the President will travel to the Allentown area tomorrow to meet small-business owners and highlight community recovery.
- Lael Brainard reports that Allentown has added 32,000 jobs and reduced unemployment from 6.4 percent to 3.9 percent, the lowest rate in 30 years.
- Lael Brainard states that 17,000 more Allentown residents have gained access to high-speed internet and that investments are modernizing local infrastructure.
- Lael Brainard notes that 4,000 business applications have been filed in Allentown over three years as a sign of renewed confidence.
  #### Small-business boom nationwide
  The administration announces record levels of business applications since President Biden took office, portraying sustained entrepreneurship growth. Brainard emphasizes that 16 million business applications were filed across the three-year period, with each year setting new records and notable gains among Black and Hispanic entrepreneurs. The White House links small-business formation to job creation and broader economic resilience.
- Lael Brainard states that 16 million business applications have been filed since the President took office, making the last three years the strongest on record.
- Lael Brainard highlights that Black business ownership has doubled and Hispanic business ownership is up 40 percent compared to pre-pandemic levels.
- Lael Brainard asserts that analysis shows applications are translating into new business formation and that small businesses remain major job creators.
  #### Inflation data update
  Brainard presents new inflation data showing substantial easing, attributing much of the decline to reduced supply chain pressures. She points to falling prices for certain household items and lower gas prices as indicators of relief for consumers. The administration frames the trend as evidence of economic progress while acknowledging continued focus on lowering costs.
- Lael Brainard says inflation is down nearly two-thirds as supply chain pressures have eased over the past year.
- Lael Brainard reports price declines for household items such as eggs, milk, and toys and notes gas prices are below $3 in much of the country.
- Lael Brainard emphasizes ongoing administration focus on further lowering costs for American households.
  #### Lowering healthcare costs
  Brainard outlines the President’s agenda to lower healthcare costs, spotlighting prescription drugs and insulin pricing as priorities. She cites legislative authority used to reduce insulin prices and negotiated Medicare drug pricing, and underscores the goal of lowering seniors’ out‑of‑pocket costs. The administration also calls on corporations to pass along cost reductions to consumers.
- Lael Brainard says the President is focused on lowering prescription drug costs, including reducing insulin prices and negotiating Medicare drug prices.
- Lael Brainard highlights a $35 cap on insulin and a $2,000 cap on seniors' out-of-pocket drug costs as tangible outcomes.
- Lael Brainard calls on corporations that raised prices during supply‑chain disruptions to pass savings back to consumers.
  #### Political contrast on costs
  Brainard frames congressional Republicans as prioritizing tax cuts for the wealthy and corporations rather than policies to lower costs for households. The administration positions its legislative agenda (e.g., drug pricing, housing credits) as the alternative to Republican proposals. This contrast is used to underscore the administration’s focus on cost-of-living relief for families.
- Lael Brainard asserts that congressional Republicans have no plans to lower costs and continue to push tax cuts for the wealthiest Americans and large corporations.
- Lael Brainard presents the administration’s policy priorities as targeted toward lowering household expenses rather than broad tax cuts.
  #### Car insurance and CPI
  Reporters ask about car insurance increases contributing to CPI; Brainard explains the administration’s toolkit and limitations. She points to legislative authorities for areas like drug pricing and to independent agencies for enforcement and rulemaking on specific fee practices. Brainard also says agencies are generally focused on unfair or deceptive price practices and that some authorities relevant to insurance rest with independent agencies.
- A reporter notes car insurance rate increases accounted for about 15 percent of the recent CPI rise and asks what leverage the administration has.
- Lael Brainard responds that the administration uses legislative authorities where available and relies on independent agencies for enforcement and rulemaking.
- Lael Brainard says independent agencies are focused on addressing unfair and deceptive price practices that raise consumer costs.
  #### Red Sea shipping risks
  Brainard addresses monitoring of attacks in the Red Sea and their potential economic impact, stressing continued surveillance by the national security team. She reports only limited effects on the U.S. economy so far, with some shippers rerouting and longer transit times but no material U.S. impact. The administration highlights that overall supply‑chain pressure measures have returned to pre‑pandemic levels.
- Lael Brainard says the national security team is monitoring attacks in the Red Sea and their potential effects on the U.S. economy.
- Lael Brainard reports that shippers are taking alternate routes, causing longer shipping times, but that this has had very little effect on the U.S. economy so far.
- Lael Brainard notes that the comprehensive supply chain pressures measure is down to pre-pandemic levels due to administration efforts with the private sector.
  #### Housing and rent inflation
  Reporters question why rent remains a persistent inflation driver; Brainard emphasizes housing affordability as a top priority. She urges Congress to pass housing tax credits the President has proposed to increase affordable rental supply and notes proposals for first-generation homebuyers. Brainard also notes mortgage rates have come down recently, but stresses legislative action is still required to meaningfully improve affordability.
- A reporter points out rent rose 6.2 percent last year and asks why rent remains a key inflation driver.
- Lael Brainard urges Congress to pass housing tax credits in the President’s budget to increase the supply of affordable rentals.
- Lael Brainard says mortgage rates have declined about 1.25 percentage points in recent months and that forecasts expect average rents to come down, but legislation is still needed.
  #### Tax negotiations and child credit
  Reporters ask how the White House balances potential tax package elements against inflation concerns; Brainard reiterates the administration’s commitment to extending the child tax credit for low- and moderate-income families. She emphasizes the child tax credit’s effectiveness at reducing child poverty and expresses hope for a balanced package that preserves refundability for families in need. The White House seeks retention of priorities such as R&D credits while monitoring inflationary effects.
- A reporter asks how the administration balances child tax credit and R&D incentives against inflation risks in current tax negotiations.
- Lael Brainard emphasizes that the President wants the child tax credit extended and refundable for low- and moderate-income families because it significantly reduces child poverty.
- Lael Brainard says the administration hopes for a balanced package that preserves priorities for families and innovation.
  #### Regional banking health
  In response to questions about bank stability, Brainard says regional banks have strengthened capital and liquidity since last year and are in a more resilient condition. She reports that monitoring continues but overall analysis shows the sector is healthier. The administration frames regulatory and supervisory efforts as contributing to increased stability.
- A reporter asks about the current health of mid-size and regional banks after last year’s crisis.
- Lael Brainard says regional banks have shoredup capital and liquidity and are in much more resilient shape than a year ago.
- Lael Brainard says the administration continues to monitor the banking sector closely.
  #### Food prices and wages
  Reporters raise concerns about high food prices and public messaging around 'Bidenomics'; Brainard points to declines in several food categories and broader economic gains. She cites employment growth, real wage gains since pre‑pandemic, increased median household wealth, and small‑business creation as indicators of progress while acknowledging ongoing household cost pressures. The administration stresses continued focus on lowering weekly household expenditures.
- A reporter asks about persistent high food prices and why the term 'Bidenomics' has not gained traction.
- Lael Brainard notes several food categories, including dairy and produce, have come down over the past year.
- Lael Brainard says employment is up 14.3 million since pre‑pandemic and that real wages and median household wealth are higher than before the pandemic.
  #### Real wages clarification
  A reporter challenges Brainard on real wage trends since President Biden took office; Brainard corrects the characterization and provides updated metrics. She states that real wages are up over pre‑pandemic levels—over 1 percent for all workers and over 3 percent for moderate‑income workers—and that purchasing power overall has improved. The administration uses these figures to argue that wage gains have outpaced inflation since before the pandemic.
- A reporter cites numbers indicating real wages are down and challenges the administration’s claims.
- Lael Brainard responds that real wages are up since pre‑pandemic, with gains over 1 percent for all workers and over 3 percent for moderate‑income workers.
- Lael Brainard asserts that overall purchasing power has improved since pre‑pandemic and that employment gains are helping household finances.
  #### Seizure of St. Nikolas tanker
  Admiral John Kirby condemns Iran’s apparent seizure of the Marshall Islands-flagged St. Nikolas in the Gulf of Oman and calls for immediate release of the vessel and crew. He characterizes the action as provocative and unjustified and commits to working with allies to deter and confront destabilizing Iranian behavior. Kirby dismisses Tehran’s justifications and urges release without delay.
- Admiral John Kirby condemns the apparent Iranian seizure of the Marshall Islands‑flagged oil tanker St. Nikolas and calls for its immediate release.
- Admiral John Kirby says the United States will work with allies and partners to deter and confront Iran’s destabilizing actions.
- Admiral John Kirby dismisses claims from Tehran justifying the seizure and calls the action unjustified in international waters.
  #### ICJ and Israel questions
  Reporters press Kirby on the International Court of Justice (ICJ) proceedings and whether Gaza is 'occupied'; Kirby declines to provide legal opinions from the podium and reiterates the U.S. position rejecting genocide allegations against Israel. He affirms ongoing diplomatic discussions emphasizing media freedom and civilian protection and states the U.S. supports Israel’s right to defend itself while urging minimization of civilian harm.
- A reporter asks whether Gaza is an occupied territory under the ICJ judge’s comments; Admiral Kirby declines to render a legal opinion from the podium.
- Admiral John Kirby states the United States rejects accusations of genocide against Israel and cautions against using that term lightly.
- Admiral John Kirby says the U.S. discusses civilian protection and media freedom with Israeli counterparts and urges precision to minimize civilian casualties.
  #### Houthis and Red Sea response
  Kirby addresses the U.S. posture toward Houthi attacks in the Red Sea, declining to outline specific military options while signaling willingness to act. He says the U.S. will consult allies and partners and indicates that consequences will follow continued Houthi attacks on commercial shipping. Kirby notes multinational participation in Operation Prosperity Guardian to protect maritime commerce.
- A reporter asks how far the U.S. is willing to go against Houthi attacks; Admiral John Kirby says he will not telegraph punches but affirms the U.S. will do what is necessary to counter threats.
- Admiral John Kirby says more than 20 nations are now involved in Operation Prosperity Guardian to protect commercial shipping in the Red Sea.
- Admiral John Kirby warns the Houthis they will bear consequences if they fail to stop attacks on shipping.
  #### Gaza humanitarian assistance
  Kirby acknowledges severe food security issues in Gaza and expresses dissatisfaction with the current flow of humanitarian aid, noting that aid truck volumes remain far below pre‑war levels. He reports Kerem Shalom has reopened as an additional entry point but emphasizes more work is needed to increase speed and volume of deliveries. Kirby commits the U.S. to persist in diplomatic efforts to expand humanitarian access.
- Admiral John Kirby acknowledges significant food security problems in Gaza and says the U.S. is prioritizing food, water, and medicine deliveries.
- Admiral John Kirby reports that Kerem Shalom has been reopened as a second venue for aid but that truck volumes have been dramatically lower than pre‑war.
- Admiral John Kirby commits that the U.S. will keep working to increase the volume and speed of humanitarian assistance into Gaza.
  #### Arms transfers and oversight
  Reporters press Kirby on congressional oversight and whether the administration is assessing Israeli use of U.S. weapons; Kirby emphasizes ongoing consultations with Congress and with Israeli counterparts. He says the administration sees no indication of deliberate violations of the laws of armed conflict but reiterates concern over civilian casualties and expectations for responsible use of U.S.-provided materiel. Kirby defers detailed procedural questions to State and DOD.
- A reporter asks whether the administration has ensured Israel uses U.S. weapons in accordance with laws of war; Admiral John Kirby says the U.S. maintains expectations and is in constant touch with Israeli counterparts.
- Admiral John Kirby affirms the administration takes congressional oversight seriously and consults with Congress on foreign military assistance.
- Admiral John Kirby says there is no indication that Israel is deliberately and wantonly violating the laws of armed conflict, while acknowledging too many civilian casualties and urging steps to reduce them.
  #### Secretary Austin hospitalization
  Kirby admits the administration lacked timely awareness of Secretary Austin’s hospitalization and calls that lapse unacceptable, noting daily check‑in protocols exist for Cabinet officials. He references a White House memo from Jeff Zients urging agencies to review protocols and commits to improving situational awareness so the White House is informed when delegation decisions become necessary. Kirby says they will learn from the incident.
- A reporter questions how the Situation Room tracks Cabinet members after delays in knowing Secretary Austin’s status; Admiral John Kirby says there is a daily check‑in process but acknowledges the hospitalization should have been reported sooner.
- Admiral John Kirby references a memo from Jeff Zients advising agencies to review protocols and says the administration will get better at this.
- Admiral John Kirby states the White House needs to know promptly when a Cabinet official requires medical care that affects authorities.
  #### Ukraine materiel accountability
  On a Defense IG report about unaccounted weapons provided to Ukraine, Kirby says the administration appreciates the IG work and is focused on improving end‑use accountability with Ukrainian partners. He notes DOD has implemented improvements like handheld scanners and that the IG found no evidence of widescale diversion, while also saying wartime conditions limit perfect accounting. Kirby underscores continued efforts to enhance tracking without exposing personnel to undue risk.
- A reporter asks about a Defense IG report that nearly 40,000 weapons were not fully accounted for in Ukraine; Admiral John Kirby says the administration appreciates the IG’s work and is focused on improving accountability.
- Admiral John Kirby notes DOD has implemented improvements, such as handheld scanners, and the IG found no evidence of widescale diversion.
- Admiral John Kirby cautions that combat environments impose practical limits on perfect inventory accounting without risking personnel safety.
  #### Ukraine funding and drawdowns
  Kirby confirms the U.S. issued the last drawdown package it could under existing funds and urges Congress to act on the national security supplemental. He warns that assistance has slowed and that Russian attacks are increasing, intensifying the need for additional funding—particularly during winter months. The administration frames congressional action as essential to sustain Ukraine’s defense.
- A reporter asks whether military assistance to Ukraine has run dry; Admiral John Kirby confirms the last drawdown package that could be funded has been issued.
- Admiral John Kirby urges Congress to pass the national security supplemental to replenish assistance as Russian attacks continue and intensify.
- Admiral John Kirby highlights the acute need for funding during the winter months to support Ukraine’s defense.
  #### Domestic politics and spending deal
  Karine Jean‑Pierre addresses reports that Speaker Johnson might renege on a topline spending deal with Senate leadership and urges House Republicans to keep commitments to avoid a government shutdown. She frames the May agreement and the Speaker’s recent reaffirmation as the basis for continuing bipartisan cooperation. The White House emphasizes that keeping government open is Congress’s primary duty.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre says House Republicans previously voted for an agreement in May and that Speaker Johnson reaffirmed it on Sunday.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre urges Republicans to keep their word and stop playing games, warning that a shutdown is unacceptable.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre emphasizes the White House priority is to keep the government open and preserve the existing framework agreement.
  #### Judicial nominations status
  Reporters ask why multiple judicial nominees withdrew; Jean‑Pierre responds that each case is unique and the President remains committed to qualified nominations. She praises nominees like Julie Su, who was renominated, and frames the administration’s historical pace of confirmations as notable. The White House expresses desire to continue moving nominations through the confirmation process.
- A reporter asks about three judicial nominees withdrawing this week; Karine Jean‑Pierre says each situation is case‑by‑case and the President takes nominations seriously.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre defends the administration’s record of historic movement on nominations and urges continued Senate progress.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre says the President renominated Julie Su because he considers her highly qualified based on her performance as Acting Secretary of Labor.
  #### Impeachment and Mayorkas testimony
  Jean‑Pierre addresses planned impeachment proceedings against Secretary Mayorkas and says decisions about testimony rest with the Secretary, while the White House calls the efforts baseless political stunts. She urges House Republicans to focus on governance priorities like the budget rather than pursuing what she calls partisan spectacle. The White House defends cabinet officials and signals concern over repeated impeachment attempts.
- A reporter asks whether Secretary Mayorkas will testify before the impeachment committee; Karine Jean‑Pierre says that decision is for the Secretary to make.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre calls House Republican impeachment efforts 'baseless' and characterizes them as political stunts distracting from governing priorities.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre urges Republicans to concentrate on keeping the government open and addressing the national security supplemental rather than pursuing impeachment drives.
  #### Hostages and detainees efforts
  Jean‑Pierre outlines the administration’s continued work to bring home Americans held hostage or wrongfully detained, noting the President met with Paul Whelan’s sister and has prioritized these cases. She cites past successful returns of more than 30 Americans and describes ongoing diplomatic engagement led by the National Security Council and State Department. The White House declines to provide details on private discussions but affirms the issue remains a top priority.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre says the President met with Paul Whelan’s sister and takes hostage and wrongful detention cases very seriously.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre notes the administration has brought home more than 30 Americans held hostage globally and continues diplomatic efforts to secure additional releases.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre says the National Security Council and State Department remain engaged on these cases and will continue to work privately.
  #### Other policy questions
  Reporters cover a range of additional topics—oil production records, relations with Mexico and AMLO’s requests, donor Oval Office visits, Hunter Biden’s private‑citizen status, and gun violence prevention partnerships. Jean‑Pierre reiterates support for climate and EV transition alongside declining gas prices, describes U.S.-Mexico ties as non‑transactional, defers specifics on donor access while affirming rules are followed, and endorses state efforts to replicate the White House gun violence prevention office. She reiterates that Hunter Biden is a private citizen and refers inquiries to his representatives.
- A reporter asks about record U.S. oil production in 2023; Karine Jean‑Pierre says the administration remains committed to transitioning to EVs and clean energy while noting lower gas prices.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre describes the U.S. relationship with Mexico as a partnership rather than transactional and declines to negotiate specific requests from Mexico’s president at the podium.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre affirms the White House follows rules about hosting visitors and takes those rules seriously when supporters visit the complex.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre reiterates that Hunter Biden is a private citizen and refers questions about his legal matters to his representative.
- Karine Jean‑Pierre welcomes state-level gun violence prevention efforts and says the White House office will continue working with governors and localities on implementation.
