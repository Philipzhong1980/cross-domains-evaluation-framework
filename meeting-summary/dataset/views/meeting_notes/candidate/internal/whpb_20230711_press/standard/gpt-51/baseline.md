### Overview
Senior U.S. officials, led by National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan and Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre, brief reporters in Vilnius ahead of the NATO Summit, highlighting Türkiye’s agreement to forward Sweden’s NATO accession protocols after intensive U.S. and allied diplomacy, President Biden’s summit agenda, and a package of Alliance reforms that includes a new force model, treating 2 percent of GDP defense spending as a floor, and expanded cyber and global partnerships. Sullivan stresses that Ukraine is central to the summit through the inaugural NATO-Ukraine Council, additional support and interoperability measures, and a communiqué that offers a united, positive signal on Ukraine’s future NATO membership without setting explicit timetables or territorial preconditions, while also previewing Biden’s upcoming meeting with President Zelenskyy and longer-term security commitments that will be detailed later. He defends the temporary provision of U.S. cluster munitions as a necessary stopgap given low stocks of standard artillery ammunition and insists it does not undermine NATO unity, which he portrays as historically strong despite allies’ divergent legal positions on specific weapons. On regional and political issues, Sullivan reaffirms Biden’s long-standing support for Türkiye’s EU path while denying any formal linkage to Sweden’s bid, outlines Biden’s unconditional support for selling F-16s to Türkiye in consultation with Congress amid Greek concerns, and declines comment on domestic controversies involving alleged FBI-Ukraine social media cooperation and CEFC China Energy. Jean-Pierre complements this by underscoring ongoing congressional consultations on Ukraine aid without new long-term commitments, confirming FEMA’s response to severe U.S. Northeast flooding, offering no update on F-16s for Ukraine or the White House cocaine investigation, and framing Secretary Yellen’s China trip as part of a broader effort to sustain substantive economic dialogue with Beijing, all while repeatedly casting NATO’s expansion and cohesion as evidence of Biden’s successful alliance management in the face of Russian aggression.
  
### Detailed Summary
#### Sweden’s accession to NATO
National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan announces that Türkiye has committed to transmit Sweden’s NATO accession protocols to its parliament for ratification, clearing the way for Sweden to become NATO’s 32nd Ally. He explains that the agreement is the result of direct talks among Türkiye, Sweden, and NATO, but underscores intensive recent U.S. diplomatic engagement with all parties. This development is framed as a strategic response to Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, expanding the Alliance with another capable military. The current state is that Sweden’s membership is not yet formal but is expected in the near term pending Turkish parliamentary action.
- Mr. Sullivan states that President Biden welcomes the agreement between Türkiye, Sweden, and NATO’s Secretary-General that includes President Erdoğan’s commitment to transmit Sweden’s accession protocols for ratification.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that the President hosted the Swedish Prime Minister at the White House and spoke with President Erdoğan as part of U.S. efforts to support the deal.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that Secretary Blinken and he himself have been in regular contact with Turkish and Swedish counterparts to facilitate Sweden’s path to NATO membership.
- Mr. Sullivan emphasizes that the United States supports Sweden’s accession moving through the Turkish Parliament so Sweden can become NATO’s 32nd Ally.
- Mr. Sullivan later credits Sweden’s “effective work” in addressing Turkish concerns as having a significant impact on President Erdoğan’s final decision.
#### Biden agenda and NATO summit priorities
Sullivan outlines President Biden’s schedule in Vilnius and the broader objectives of the NATO Summit. The President will meet the Lithuanian President, U.S. congressional delegation leaders, and other allies, reaffirming Article 5 commitments and NATO’s eastern flank posture. Sullivan portrays NATO as larger, stronger, and more united than in recent memory, attributing this in part to Biden’s sustained diplomatic leadership since Russia’s invasion. The Alliance intends to address deterrence, defense modernization, and global challenges during the summit’s sessions.
- Mr. Sullivan says President Biden will meet the Lithuanian President to thank him for hosting and to reaffirm ironclad U.S. support for Article 5 and NATO’s eastern flank.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that U.S. forces have a bolstered posture in Europe after Russia’s invasion, including rotating military personnel in Lithuania.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that Biden will also meet with members of Congress at the summit, including Senate NATO Observer Group leaders, to ensure close coordination with the legislature.
- Mr. Sullivan asserts that the NATO Alliance will be “bigger and stronger than ever,” as well as more united and energized, when the summit begins.
- Mr. Sullivan credits President Biden with devoting substantial time to revitalizing alliances and rallying support against Russia’s invasion of Ukraine.
#### NATO defense spending and force posture reforms
Sullivan details expected summit deliverables on NATO’s deterrence, defense posture, and burden-sharing. Allies plan to adopt a new NATO Force Model to increase readiness and capacity, agree to higher defense spending, and treat the longstanding 2 percent of GDP benchmark as a minimum rather than a maximum. He notes nine consecutive years of increased defense spending by non-U.S. allies and efforts to strengthen defense industrial bases. The current state is that Allies are aligning on defense investment and industrial production to meet emerging security needs.
- Mr. Sullivan says Allies will adopt a new NATO Force Model designed to increase readiness and capacity.
- Mr. Sullivan indicates Allies will agree to increase defense spending and adopt a new Defense Investment Pledge.
- Mr. Sullivan states that more Allies are already above the 2 percent of GDP benchmark or on a path to reach it, and that leaders will agree 2 percent should be a floor, not a ceiling.
- Mr. Sullivan underscores that 2023 marks the ninth consecutive year of increased defense spending by non-U.S. NATO Allies.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Allies are working to strengthen their defense industrial bases, boosting production of critical weapons and equipment.
#### Cyber resilience and global partnerships
The briefing highlights NATO’s intention to strengthen cyber defenses and expand cooperation beyond the Euro-Atlantic area. Allies aim to bolster cyber resilience and defense through enhanced coordination mechanisms. Sullivan also notes plans to deepen global partnerships, including with Indo-Pacific partners and the European Union. The current state is one of active expansion of NATO’s functional and geographic cooperation to meet evolving security challenges.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that Allies will come together at the summit to bolster cyber resilience and cyber defense via increased coordination.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that the Alliance intends to expand global partnerships, including with Indo-Pacific partners and the European Union.
#### Ukraine support, NATO-Ukraine Council, and membership path
Ukraine is described as a central focus of the summit, with the first-ever NATO-Ukraine Council meeting to launch new support measures. Allies seek to provide a package of increased support, plan for Ukraine’s long-term needs, and enhance interoperability with NATO. Membership is framed as a future goal: Allies agree it is not feasible to bring Ukraine into NATO during active war, and they emphasize the need for further democratic and security sector reforms. The current position is that NATO will issue a “united positive signal” on Ukraine’s future membership while working with Kyiv on a reform path.
- Mr. Sullivan announces that the summit will host the first meeting of the NATO-Ukraine Council to coordinate support and interoperability.
- Mr. Sullivan says Allies will adopt a new package of increased support for Ukraine and examine long-term needs, including interoperability with NATO forces.
- Mr. Sullivan reiterates President Biden’s view that admitting Ukraine to NATO now would bring the Alliance into war with Russia.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that Ukraine still has further steps to complete on its democratic and security sector reform path before joining NATO.
- Mr. Sullivan states that Allies will send a united positive signal regarding Ukraine’s future membership path in the communiqué.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that Ukraine has an Annual National Programme detailing reforms, and the summit will both mark reform progress and outline further steps.
- Mr. Sullivan declines to set a timetable for Ukraine’s reforms when pressed, saying the focus is on the substance rather than a fixed duration.
- Mr. Sullivan avoids defining specific territorial conditions, such as full Russian withdrawal, that would define “at war” for NATO membership decisions, calling it a dynamic question for ongoing Alliance discussion.
- Mr. Sullivan later observes that both Allies and Ukraine increasingly converge that the key issue in Vilnius is defining the pathway toward future membership, not immediate accession.
#### Biden–Zelenskyy meeting and long-term security commitments
Sullivan previews a bilateral meeting between President Biden and President Zelenskyy and broader discussions on long-term security commitments to Ukraine. He says the U.S. and partners intend to support Ukraine’s defense now and deter future aggression through sustained assistance and commitments. The meeting will address military aid, economic support, and continued democratic reforms in Kyiv. Detailed formats for long-term guarantees are left for announcement the following day, but the administration signals readiness for multi-year commitments.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that President Biden and President Zelenskyy will meet in Vilnius and says Biden will always meet Zelenskyy when they are in the same city.
- Mr. Sullivan says the leaders will discuss how the U.S. and its partners can make long-term commitments to help Ukraine defend itself and deter future aggression.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that additional announcements on long-term commitments will come the next day, supplementing existing historic U.S. support over the past 16 months.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre reiterates that long-term security assistance will be a topic in Biden’s meeting with Zelenskyy and that the U.S. focus remains on sustaining security aid.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre says there are ongoing conversations with Congress about support for Ukraine but offers no new concrete, long-term congressional commitments.
#### Upcoming Biden speech on Ukraine and global challenges
Sullivan announces that President Biden will deliver a major speech in Vilnius before departing, framing U.S. and allied actions. The speech will cover support for Ukraine, defense of democratic values, and responses to global challenges. This is intended to communicate the broader strategic narrative of U.S. leadership and alliance cohesion. The speech serves as a public capstone to the summit’s outcomes.
- Mr. Sullivan says President Biden will give a major speech before leaving Vilnius, focusing on how the U.S. and partners support Ukraine, defend democratic values, and address global challenges.
#### Stoltenberg’s leadership and extended term
The U.S. expresses strong appreciation for NATO Secretary General Jens Stoltenberg’s leadership during a period of heightened European security threats. Sullivan credits Stoltenberg with helping secure the Türkiye–Sweden agreement and steering NATO through Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. Allies plan to concretely recognize this leadership by unanimously endorsing a one-year extension of his term. The current status is that broad political backing exists for Stoltenberg’s continued tenure.
- Mr. Sullivan praises Secretary General Stoltenberg’s leadership amid tests to European security and the rules-based international system.
- Mr. Sullivan credits Stoltenberg for his role in reaching the deal with Türkiye and Sweden on Sweden’s accession.
- Mr. Sullivan says Allies will unanimously endorse Stoltenberg’s extension as NATO Secretary General for another year.
#### U.S. support for F-16 sale to Türkiye
Questions focus heavily on whether U.S. support for transferring F-16s to Türkiye is linked to Ankara’s approval of Sweden’s NATO bid. Sullivan insists President Biden has for months clearly and unconditionally supported selling F-16s to Türkiye as being in U.S. and NATO interests, and he states Biden intends to move forward with the transfer in consultation with Congress. He stresses that the administration does not view the F-16 decision as directly transactional with Sweden’s accession, although he acknowledges ongoing Hill consultations and Greek concerns. Timing remains unresolved and subject to congressional processes.
- Mr. Sullivan says President Biden has been “clear and unequivocal” for months in supporting the transfer of F-16s to Türkiye as being in U.S. and NATO interests.
- Mr. Sullivan emphasizes that Biden has placed no caveats or conditions on F-16 support in public or private statements and intends to proceed in consultation with Congress.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms the administration has already sent the F-16 package to Congress and will work with lawmakers on the timing of the transfer but cannot predict specific dates.
- Mr. Sullivan acknowledges ongoing outreach to Senator Menendez and other House and Senate foreign relations leaders whose approval is needed for the transfer.
- Mr. Sullivan indicates the administration has engaged the Greek government, including the Greek Prime Minister, about security concerns and U.S. support for Greece’s defense, as well as de-escalation in the Eastern Mediterranean.
- Mr. Sullivan declines to detail whether Biden explicitly linked congressional attitudes on F-16s to Türkiye’s stance on Sweden, but he says Biden framed his call with Erdoğan as encouragement to take a positive step.
#### Türkiye’s EU aspirations and linkage to Sweden’s bid
Reporters probe whether Türkiye’s references to European Union membership formed part of a quid pro quo with NATO allies over Sweden’s accession. Sullivan reiterates that President Biden has long supported Türkiye’s path toward EU membership and that Washington favors constructive talks between Ankara and Brussels, including on democratic reforms. He firmly rejects the notion that NATO and EU issues are formally linked, stressing that the U.S. is not part of the EU and has held a consistent position on Turkey and the EU since long before Sweden’s bid. The current framing is that EU discussions and NATO accession are separate tracks, even if Erdoğan raised them politically.
- Mr. Sullivan confirms that President Biden has long publicly supported Türkiye’s membership path to the European Union.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. continues to support renewed EU–Türkiye talks that would include reforms on democratic resilience, as with other EU applicants.
- Mr. Sullivan states that the U.S. does not view Türkiye’s EU bid as part of a bargain over Sweden’s NATO accession and emphasizes that “NATO is NATO” and “the EU is the EU.”
- Mr. Sullivan stresses that U.S. support for Türkiye–EU talks predates Sweden’s NATO application and that, from Washington’s perspective, these issues are not connected.

#### NATO unity and cluster munitions decision
Sullivan defends the U.S. decision to supply cluster munitions to Ukraine and argues it does not undermine NATO unity. He notes that some allies, including the UK, have legal obligations under the Oslo Convention and have publicly discouraged cluster use, but says private conversations mirror public statements and maintain alliance cohesion. He stresses that while allies differ on the specific weapon, they are united on supporting Ukraine and that Putin’s expectation of a fractured NATO has been repeatedly disappointed.
- Mr. Sullivan says Prime Minister Sunak said nothing in private to President Biden about cluster munitions that differed from his public statements referencing UK legal obligations.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that several NATO Allies are signatories to the Oslo Convention and cannot support cluster munition use, but none argue that the U.S. decision threatens NATO unity.
- Mr. Sullivan argues that Allies understand the U.S. made the cluster decision thoughtfully and with the intent to protect Ukraine’s people and sovereignty.
- Mr. Sullivan underscores that differences over cluster munitions have not diminished what he calls historic, sustained NATO unity.
- Mr. Sullivan remarks that repeated predictions of NATO disunity have been proven wrong and that Putin has been “disappointed at every turn” by Allied cohesion.
- Ms. Jean-Pierre, when asked separately, affirms that alliances remain strong after the cluster decision and points to Sweden’s pending accession and NATO’s expansion as evidence of resilience.

He also resists giving a five- or ten-year horizon, underscoring that membership will depend on substantive democratic and security reforms and evolving security conditions. The current stance is deliberately flexible to preserve alliance discretion.
- Mr. Sullivan says there will not be explicit, detailed territorial or wartime conditions for Ukraine’s NATO membership spelled out in the communiqué or from the White House podium.

Sullivan concludes that Sweden’s concrete steps to respond to those concerns significantly influenced Erdoğan’s ultimate agreement.

#### Cluster munitions as temporary stopgap for Ukraine
In a closing exchange, Sullivan elaborates on the rationale and expected duration of U.S. provision of cluster munitions to Ukraine. He says Ukraine’s stocks of standard 155mm “unitary rounds” and allied supplies are running low, risking an inability both to sustain the counteroffensive and defend existing positions. The administration judged that the civilian risk of cluster munitions was outweighed by the risk of leaving Ukraine without sufficient artillery. He describes the policy as temporary, tied to ramping up U.S. unitary round production, though he cautions that exact timelines depend on both industrial output and battlefield usage rates and are measured in months rather than days or weeks.
- Mr. Sullivan explains that Ukraine’s and allies’ stockpiles of standard 155mm unitary rounds are running low, threatening both offensive and defensive capabilities.
- Mr. Sullivan says the U.S. was unwilling to leave Ukraine “defenseless,” even given the civilian harm risks associated with cluster munitions.
- Mr. Sullivan frames the decision as a tradeoff in which the risk of civilian harm from clusters was judged less than the risk of Ukraine lacking sufficient artillery ammunition.
- Mr. Sullivan describes the cluster munition provision as temporary, pending increased production of unitary rounds in the U.S. defense industrial base.
- Mr. Sullivan notes that unitary round production is ramping up but not as fast as desired, and he says the relevant timeframe is months, with the exact duration depending on usage rates and production progress.
