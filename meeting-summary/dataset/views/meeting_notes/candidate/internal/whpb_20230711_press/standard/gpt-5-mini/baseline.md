### Overview
  National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan and Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre brief from Vilnius on the NATO Summit, highlighting the agreement among Türkiye, Sweden, and NATO that advances Sweden’s accession and the active U.S. engagement that helped facilitate it. Sullivan outlines summit priorities including a new NATO Force Model, increased defense spending and a Defense Investment Pledge, stronger defense industrial bases, enhanced cyber coordination, expanded global partnerships, and the first NATO-Ukraine Council where Allies will agree a new support package and signal Ukraine’s pathway toward future membership while declining to set a timetable. The administration affirms support for transferring F-16s to Türkiye and says it will proceed in consultation with Congress without tying that transfer to Sweden’s accession, addresses concerns about Greece and congressional oversight, and frames the decision to provide a temporary cluster-munition variant as driven by urgent ammunition shortfalls while noting allied differences. The briefing emphasizes Allied unity and Secretary General Stoltenberg’s extension, confirms ongoing consultations with Congress about long-term security assistance, notes domestic responses to Northeast flooding, and underscores continued economic engagement with China, while declining to comment publicly on several pending legal and investigative matters.
  
  ### Detailed Summary
  #### Sweden accession agreement
  The briefing opens by highlighting the agreement among Türkiye, Sweden, and NATO that clears the way for Sweden’s accession to NATO, with President Erdoğan committing to transmit Sweden’s accession protocols for ratification. This development matters because it will expand NATO and send a strong signal of alliance cohesion amid Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. The current state is that the administration welcomes the deal and anticipates Sweden becoming NATO’s 32nd Ally in the near future.
- National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan says the President welcomed the agreement between Türkiye, Sweden, and the NATO Secretary-General.
- Jake Sullivan notes President Erdoğan has committed to transmit Sweden’s accession protocols for ratification.
- Karine Jean-Pierre states the administration views Sweden joining NATO as an important strengthening of the Alliance.
  #### U.S. diplomatic engagement
  Speakers describe an active U.S. role in the lead-up to the agreement, including direct engagement by President Biden, Secretary Blinken, and National Security Advisor Sullivan. The U.S. involvement matters because it helped facilitate communication among parties and reinforced allied coordination. The current state is sustained high-level contact, with Biden having recently hosted the Swedish Prime Minister, calling President Erdoğan, and planning to meet Erdoğan again during the summit.
- Jake Sullivan says the President hosted the Swedish Prime Minister at the White House and called President Erdoğan ahead of the summit.
- Jake Sullivan reports Secretary Blinken has regularly spoken with his Turkish counterpart throughout the process.
- Jake Sullivan says he personally had multiple conversations with Swedish and Turkish counterparts in the days before the agreement.
  #### NATO summit priorities
  Sullivan outlines summit priorities including adoption of a new NATO Force Model, increased defense spending, and a Defense Investment Pledge to boost readiness and capacity. These measures matter because they aim to strengthen NATO deterrence and defense posture in response to Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. The current state is that Allies plan to adopt these items during the summit and agree that 2 percent of GDP should be a floor rather than a ceiling for defense spending.
- Jake Sullivan says Allies will adopt a new NATO Force Model to increase readiness and capacity.
- Jake Sullivan says Allies will agree to increase defense spending and adopt a new Defense Investment Pledge.
- Jake Sullivan says the United States and Allies view the 2 percent defense spending target as a floor, not a ceiling.
  #### Defense industrial base and cyber
  The administration emphasizes strengthening Allies’ defense industrial bases to boost production of critical weapons and equipment and increasing coordination on cyber resilience and defense. This matters because higher production capacity and improved cyber coordination directly affect the Alliance’s ability to sustain long-term support for partners and operations. The current state is steady progress on increased defense spending and commitments to expand cyber cooperation and Indo-Pacific and EU partnerships.
- Jake Sullivan says 2023 marks the ninth consecutive year of increased defense spending by non-U.S. Allies.
- Jake Sullivan says Allies are working to strengthen their defense industrial bases and boost production of critical weapons and equipment.
- Jake Sullivan says the Alliance will work to bolster cyber resilience and expand global partnerships with Indo-Pacific partners and the European Union.
  #### Ukraine support and NATO-Ukraine council
  Allies plan to hold the first-ever NATO-Ukraine Council and agree on a new package of increased support, long-term needs, and interoperability plans for Ukraine. This matters because it signals sustained Allied backing and structures for coordination while addressing Ukraine’s immediate and future defense requirements. The current state is that Allies will discuss Ukraine’s path to future membership, provide a positive signal on that path, and the President will meet President Zelenskyy to discuss long-term U.S. commitments.
- Jake Sullivan says tomorrow will be the very first meeting of the NATO-Ukraine Council where Allies will agree on a new package of increased support for Ukraine.
- Jake Sullivan says Allies will expand plans for Ukraine’s interoperability with NATO and discuss Ukraine’s path to future membership.
- Jake Sullivan says the President will meet with President Zelenskyy to discuss U.S. and Allied long-term commitments to help Ukraine defend itself now and deter future aggression.
  #### Stoltenberg extension
  The briefing announces that Allies will unanimously endorse Secretary General Jens Stoltenberg’s extension for another year. This matters because continuity in NATO leadership provides stability during a period of heightened European security tensions. The current state is that Allies are prepared to recognize his leadership formally during the summit.
- Jake Sullivan says Allies will unanimously endorse Secretary General Stoltenberg’s extension as NATO Secretary-General for another year.
  #### F-16 transfers to Türkiye
  Reporters ask repeatedly about U.S. support for transferring F-16s to Türkiye, whether transfers were tied to Sweden’s accession, and the timing relative to Turkey’s parliamentary ratification. This matters because the transfers affect NATO capabilities and regional security sensitivities, including concerns from Greece and oversight by Congress. The current state is that the President supports the transfer, has submitted the package to Congress, intends to move forward in consultation with Congress, and will not specify precise timing or conditions publicly.
- Jake Sullivan says President Biden has been clear that he supports transferring F-16s to Türkiye and that the transfer is in U.S. and NATO interests.
- Jake Sullivan says the administration placed no public or private caveats on the F-16 transfer and intends to move forward in consultation with Congress.
- Jake Sullivan says he has been in contact with Senator Menendez and congressional leaders since any transfer would proceed through relevant committees.
- Jake Sullivan says the administration does not link the F-16 transfer to Sweden’s accession and treats NATO and EU processes separately.
  #### Ukraine reform timeline
  Reporters press for a timetable on Ukraine’s reform path required for future NATO membership, and Sullivan repeatedly declines to set fixed timelines. This matters because expectations about how quickly Ukraine can meet membership criteria influence political signaling and planning. The current state is that reforms are tracked through Ukraine’s Annual National Programme, the Alliance will work with Ukraine to define the pathway, and no explicit timetable is being announced at Vilnius.
- Jake Sullivan says he cannot put a timetable on how long Ukraine’s reforms will take and that the Alliance and Ukraine must work through the steps.
- Jake Sullivan says Ukraine’s Annual National Programme details many of the reforms Ukraine has embraced and is taking.
  #### Defining 'at war' and membership
  Reporters ask whether NATO will define conditions that would preclude Ukraine’s immediate membership while Russian forces remain on Ukrainian soil, and Sullivan says the Alliance will not issue explicit definitions from this podium. This matters because formal definitions could constrain future decisions and have legal and security implications. The current state is that Allies will continue internal discussions and consult with Ukraine to make decisions based on evolving conditions and European security interests.
- Jake Sullivan says the Alliance will not define 'at war' or lay down explicit conditions for membership from this podium.
- Jake Sullivan says Allies will continue to consult and make decisions based on what is best for European security and how circumstances unfold.
  #### Cluster munitions decision
  The U.S. explains its decision to provide a cluster-munition variant of 155mm rounds to Ukraine as a temporary measure driven by urgent ammunition shortfalls, while acknowledging allied differences on the weapon. This matters because the decision raises legal and humanitarian concerns and tests Alliance cohesion. The current state is that the administration considers the decision thoughtful and narrowly focused to avoid leaving Ukraine without necessary artillery, expects the use of cluster munitions to be temporary as unitary round production ramps up, and says the decision has not undermined NATO unity overall.
- Jake Sullivan says the United States decided to provide a cluster-munition variant because unitary round stockpiles were running low and Ukraine needed artillery to defend itself.
- Jake Sullivan says the administration views the cluster-munitions provision as temporary until unitary round production can meet Ukraine’s needs.
- Jake Sullivan says the British Prime Minister expressed nothing in private different from his public comments and that the decision has not disrupted U.S.-U.K. unity.
- Jake Sullivan says some Allies will not support the use of cluster munitions due to legal obligations, and the administration does not claim unanimity on their use.
  #### Allied unity and deterrence
  Sullivan frames the summit as a demonstration of NATO’s unity and purpose, asserting that repeated questions about transatlantic disunity are misplaced. This matters because strong public messaging is intended to deter Russian aggression and reassure partners. The current state is a confident administration stance that Allies are more united and energized than at many previous moments, with concrete steps on force posture and support for Ukraine.
- Jake Sullivan says rumors of NATO’s disunity are greatly exaggerated and that Allies repeatedly answer calls about unity with a forceful 'yes.'
- Jake Sullivan says Vladimir Putin has been counting on the West to crack and that Vilnius will disappoint that expectation.
  #### Press freedom and legal inquiries
  Reporters raise a House Judiciary report alleging FBI cooperation with a Ukrainian agency on disinformation and ask about an SDNY indictment tied to CEFC; Sullivan says he has not seen the report and reiterates support for press freedom, and he declines to comment on the indictment. This matters because allegations intersect with media freedom and potential legal scrutiny involving foreign actors. The current state is limited public comment from the NSC while the administration reasserts support for press and media freedom.
- Jake Sullivan says he has not seen the House Judiciary Committee report and emphasizes the administration’s strong support for press and media freedom.
- Jake Sullivan says he has not seen details about the SDNY indictment mentioned by reporters and declines to comment on potential legal liability for private individuals.
  #### Communiqué, congressional coordination, domestic issues
  Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre says Allies should expect a communiqué and additional deliverables from the summit, and she notes ongoing consultations with Congress about long-term U.S. security assistance for Ukraine. This matters because follow-on documents and congressional coordination will shape concrete assistance and commitments. The current state is that the White House is in regular contact with congressional members, FEMA has mobilized in response to Northeast flooding, and no additional substantive updates on topics like F-16s to Ukraine training or the Secret Service investigation are available.
- Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre says the administration expects a communiqué and deliverables to flow from summit discussions.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the White House is always in conversation with members of Congress on bipartisan support for Ukraine and that there are ongoing consultations about long-term assistance.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says FEMA personnel have mobilized to areas affected by flooding in the Northeast and that the White House is offering assistance.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says she has no new public details to provide on F-16 transfers to Ukraine, pilot training, or the Secret Service investigation into cocaine at the White House.
  #### U.S.-China economic contacts
  Reporters ask about Secretary Yellen’s trip to China and China’s proposed export controls on metals; Jean-Pierre says the trip was substantive and that the administration values open lines of communication on the global economy. This matters because economic measures and export controls have supply-chain and strategic implications. The current state is continued engagement and no additional policy details announced from this briefing.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says Secretary Yellen described her meetings in China as substantive and important for the global economy.
- Karine Jean-Pierre says the administration will continue open lines of communication with China and has no additional specifics to share at this time.
